New Era; Mobile Money Agents As A Service (MB#9)
 For years mobile money agents have been one of the biggest advantages in the mobile money industry. Sub Saharan Africa now has about 1.1 billion mobile money accounts transacting around $1.1 trillion. One of the biggest reasons people don’t even think about starting new mobile money services is simple: agent acquisition. It is hard, time consuming, and expensive. 
 Look at M-Pesa , Africa’s biggest mobile money, moving over $1 billion a day. The top use case for many customers is still withdrawing money, and that cannot happen without agents. Agents are king. They are 7 times more accessible than ATMs and 20 times more than bank branches. 
For years, mobile money companies treated agents as their most protected advantage. Something you build, control, and never share.
But something new and interesting is happening.
 Mobile money agents as a service.

 How it started 
 In 2023, Tigo , now Mix by Yas , Tanzania’s second largest telecom and mobile money operator with about 30 percent market share, opened its 200,000 agent network to DCB Bank . That allowed DCB Bank customers to cash in and cash out through Mix by Yas agents. 
At first, it looked like a pilot. A test.
 But in 2026, this week, Mix by Yas opened the same agent network to Equity Bank . Now it is clear this is not a pilot. It works. And this looks like the start of a new era. 
 What both sides get 
Banks get instant distribution through agent networks they did not have to build.
Mobile money companies get a new or expanded revenue stream from infrastructure they already invested in.

 The big question 
Will mobile money companies open agent networks to anyone?
Or only to players they do not compete with directly?
That answer will come when fintechs and neobanks, like Selcom Pesa or others, try to access these networks. If they get in, then agents stop being a protected competitive moat and start becoming shared infrastructure.
 What is good about this shift 
Mobile money operators are starting to treat agent networks not only as their exclusive advantage, but also as infrastructure that can generate value beyond their own products.
That is a big shift.
More players will be able to enter the market without worrying about building agent networks from scratch. Competition will move from who owns more agents to who delivers more value.

The new pattern 
Mobile money agents are slowly moving from protected advantage to monetized infrastructure. If this continues, more players will emerge, innovation will increase, and competition will shift from distribution control to product value.
That is a big structural change for financial services in Africa.
