Being a **Microsoft (****NASDAQ:MSFT****)** investor has been no fun for a while now. Against a backdrop of a raging bull market, the stock has underperformed many of its tech peers and even the S&P 500 across multiple timeframes (year-to-date, one-year, and three years).

However, according to one top investor, known by the pseudonym Oakoff Investments, buying the stock at current levels is not merely a sound idea but a “generational buying opportunity amid AI-driven catalysts and undervalued forward growth.”

According to Oakoff, who is among the top 3% of stock pros covered by TipRanks, the reasons for the stock’s lackluster performance are twofold. Microsoft remains one of the largest SaaS players, and the broader market repriced much of the software sector when investors began viewing advanced LLMs, including Anthropic’s latest models, as a potential threat to traditional software business models. Additionally, the company’s heavy investment in Copilot appears to have fallen short of expectations, especially given the scale of capital expenditure already committed.

But those issues do not deter Oakoff. “Despite this weakness and spoiled narrative around the stock, I believe that MSFT has become too discounted for the level of forward growth that should be coming in the next few years,” the 5-star investor said. “It is still a boring business that keeps delivering if we assess the most recent fundamentals, and I think it is smart to stay bullish.”

Going by the tech giant’s March quarter results, AI and cloud remain key growth drivers. Azure and related cloud services revenue rose 40% year-over-year, while Copilot adoption also showed meaningful improvement. Weekly engagement increased following a 250% rise in seats, suggesting adoption may finally be catching up to earlier expectations.

At the same time, concerns over a roughly $190 billion capital expenditure plan for calendar year 2026 are somewhat misplaced, with Oakoff pointing to expanding contracted revenue obligations, with RPO (remaining performance obligations) rising sharply to $627 billion, roughly doubling in the quarter, suggesting demand is already locking in much of the infrastructure spend.

While margins are expected to compress slightly in the near term, the investor expects eventual recovery as monetization from AI workloads scales over the next two to five years, improving return on invested capital. Oakoff also sees additional upside in Microsoft’s shift from fixed seat-based SaaS pricing toward usage-based models, which better captures compute demand and should support both revenue growth and cost efficiency over time.

Beyond Microsoft’s existing AI offerings, the investor also highlights agentic AI as an emerging long-term catalyst. Oakoff points to initiatives such as Project Solara and Microsoft Scout, unveiled at Microsoft Build 2026, arguing that these tools could deepen enterprise integration, raise switching costs, and reinforce Microsoft’s competitive moat.

“I understand the market’s skepticism around MSFT’s Copilot and CAPEX scaling, but I view it as a short-term pain point that will likely translate into longer-term business gains,” Oakoff summed up.
