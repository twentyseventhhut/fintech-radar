New Delhi: A Times Now investigation has uncovered a staggering new method being deployed by Pakistan-based terror outfit Jaish-e-Mohammed (JeM) to bypass global scrutiny and continue its operations.
Led by UN-designated terrorist Masood Azhar, JeM has allegedly built a parallel financial ecosystem through Pakistani digital wallets like EasyPaisa and SadaPay — a move that experts say amounts to a “digital hawala” system. Through this, JeM has already launched a PKR 3.91-billion fundraising drive (₹3,910 crore) to rebuild its terror infrastructure destroyed in Indian strikes and expand into 313 new markaz (terror centres) across Pakistan.
 Pakistan’s Deception Before FATF 
In 2019, to escape the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) grey list, Islamabad showcased a "crackdown" on JeM. It claimed to:
• Freeze bank accounts of Masood Azhar and his brothers.
• Shut traditional fundraising channels like animal hide donations and mosque cash drives.
• Place JeM’s Bahawalpur headquarters under government control.
This narrative worked — in 2022, Pakistan was removed from the grey list.
But the truth was different. Instead of eliminating JeM’s financial roots, Pakistan’s intelligence agency ISI helped the group migrate to fintech wallets. Bank scrutiny was shown to FATF, but behind the scenes, crores flowed seamlessly through mobile-based transactions, far away from international oversight.
 Operation Sindoor and the JeM Rebuild 
On May 7, 2025, India’s precision strikes under Operation Sindoor flattened JeM’s headquarters Markaz Subhanallah in Pakistan-occupied territory, along with four training hubs (Markaz Bilal, Markaz Abbas, Mahmona Joya, Sargal).
Within days, JeM launched a campaign to not only rebuild these facilities but construct 313 new centres across Pakistan. The campaign explicitly stated:
• Each markaz would cost PKR 12.5 million (₹1.25 crore).
• The total target was PKR 3.91 billion (₹3,910 crore).
• Donations were sought from supporters in Pakistan and abroad via digital wallets.
JeM-linked accounts on Facebook, WhatsApp, and proxy Telegram channels openly circulated appeals, videos, and even letters from Masood Azhar himself urging contributions.
 Digital Wallets: Terror’s New Banking System 
Investigators have traced hundreds of wallets linked to JeM leaders:
• Talha Al Saif (Masood Azhar’s brother): Using SadaPay linked to number registered under JeM’s Haripur commander Aftab Ahmad.
• Abdullah Azhar (Masood Azhar’s son): Running an EasyPaisa account for collections.
• Syed Safdar Shah (JeM’s Khyber Pakhtunkhwa commander): Operating another wallet in Mansehra.

Reports suggest over 250 EasyPaisa wallets are already active for this campaign, with 30 new ones created every month to escape tracking. JeM follows a rotation model — funds accumulate in one wallet, are split into 10–15 accounts, and then withdrawn in cash or re-routed, making tracing impossible.
"This is nothing short of a parallel digital banking system created for terrorism," a senior counter-terror analyst told Times Now.
 Weapons, Not Buildings: The Surplus Game 
While JeM claims billions are for markaz construction, intelligence inputs suggest a large portion is being siphoned for weapons.
• A mid-sized markaz costs just PKR 4–5 million, much lower than JeM’s inflated estimates.
• Even if 313 centres are built, the actual expense is closer to PKR 1.23 billion, leaving a surplus of ₹2,700 crore+.
• This surplus, experts fear, is being used to procure machine guns, rocket launchers, mortars, and possibly even attack drones, mirroring tactics of Hamas and TTP.
 Mosque Collections & Gaza Cover 
Despite the ban, JeM continues to collect funds inside Pakistan openly:
• Friday mosque collections are disguised as “donations for Gaza.” Videos accessed by Times Now show militants counting cash after prayers in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.
• JeM’s charity front Al Rahmat Trust funnels another PKR 10 crore annually through bank accounts under fake trustees.
By invoking Gaza, JeM taps into emotional sympathies to raise money while diverting it for jihad against India.
 JeM's Propaganda and Recruitment Machine 
Beyond fundraising, JeM has weaponised its markaz network:
• Markaz Subhanallah (destroyed by India) doubled as HQ, training centre, and lodging for cadres.
• Markaz Ifta in Karachi serves as a propaganda hub, brainwashing children, publishing Azhar’s speeches, and running social media campaigns.
• Other centres like Markaz Usman-o-Ali house Azhar’s family, offering them lavish safe houses under religious cover.
 Why 313 Markaz? JeM’s Masterplan 
Analysts believe JeM’s plan has two objectives:
1. Decentralisation of Terror – By creating hundreds of smaller centres, JeM ensures future Indian strikes cannot cripple its entire network.
2. Safe Havens for Leadership – At least 3–4 large markaz are designed as fortified safe houses for Masood Azhar and family, maintaining deniability about their whereabouts.
This structure mirrors Lashkar-e-Taiba’s vast spread, allowing JeM to survive long-term.
 The ISI Connection 
The investigation highlights the complicity of Pakistan’s ISI:
• Digital wallets remain unmonitored by the state.
• JeM gets access to black-market weapons at subsidised rates.
• Political leaders, including senators, have been documented meeting JeM cadres post-strikes.
This raises an urgent question: Is Pakistan truly fighting terror, or silently enabling it?
 
 The Bigger Danger 
JeM currently handles over 2,000 active wallets, moving PKR 800–900 million annually. Around 50% of this goes directly into arms purchases.
With cross-links to Hamas and TTP, JeM is now positioned to:
• Acquire advanced drones and quadcopters.
• Fund global jihadist propaganda online.
• Launch larger-scale terror attacks against India.
 Conclusion: A Global Threat 
This exposé proves that terrorism has gone digital. By exploiting fintech loopholes, JeM has built a shadow economy of jihad under Pakistan’s nose — or perhaps with its blessing.
For India, it underscores the urgency of pre-emptive counter-terror strikes and global diplomatic pressure on Pakistan. For the world, it raises alarms that digital wallets are the new frontier of terror financing, far harder to monitor than traditional banking.
