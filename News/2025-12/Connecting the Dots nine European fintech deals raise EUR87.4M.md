BlackFin Tech Weekly - December 8th, 2025
Every Week, we publish a short digest which sums up last week’s Fintech activity
Hello FinTech Friends,
 Welcome to another week of fintech insights. Let’s explore the news and trends shaping the industry! Over the last week, there were nine fintech deals in Europe , raising a total of €87.4 million , including five transactions in the United Kingdom, three in France and one in the Netherlands. 
Congratulations to the three largest rounds announced last week:
 Sokin , a UK-based company providing businesses with multi-currency accounts and fast international payments via its single platform, has raised €43m in a Series B round led by Prysm Capital . 
 Bitstack , a France-based App automating bitcoin savings with roundups, recurring investments, and BTC card rewards, has raised €12.8m in a Venture Round led by 13 Books Capital . 
 Ecair , a France-based company helping renovation companies fund projects by advancing public grants and offering simple customer loans, has raised €11m in a Venture Round led by Planet A , Inco Ventures , Kima Ventures and Enerfip . 
 Let’s dive in! 
 Sokin 
 Sokin , headquartered in the UK, provides businesses with multi-currency accounts and fast international payments through a unified platform and API. The company has raised €43 million in a Series B round led by Prysm Capital , with participation from Watershed. According to industry press, Sokin plans to deploy the funds to expand its presence in high-growth corridors such as the Middle East and Latin America, while deepening partnerships with financial institutions to strengthen its embedded-payments offering. The firm has also highlighted increasing enterprise demand for cost-efficient cross-border rails as a key growth driver.

 Bitstack 
 Bitstack , based in France, offers an automated Bitcoin savings app featuring roundups, recurring buys and BTC rewards. The startup secured €12.8 million in a Venture Round led by 13books capital , with AG2R La Mondiale, Serena, Stillmark, Y Combinator and Plug and Play also participating. Recent commentary from Bitstack indicates the company will expand beyond savings to broader neobank-like services, including payments and debit features, aiming to become a mainstream consumer finance app rather than a pure crypto tool. The new capital will also support compliance and licensing efforts under evolving EU MiCA regulations.

 Ecair 
 Ecair , headquartered in France, provides a banktech platform that enables renovation companies to finance projects by advancing public grants and offering simple customer loans. The company raised €11 million in a Venture Round involving Planet A , Inco Ventures , Kima Ventures and the group Enerfip . Beyond accelerating customer acquisition, Ecair is expected to invest heavily in underwriting automation, as rising renovation-related subsidies in France and the EU have created a surge in demand for compliant financing workflows. Analysts also note that Ecair’s model positions it strategically within the broader energy-efficiency transition ecosystem.

 In addition to this week’s fundraising activity, here is the M&A activity for the week: 
 Stripe , a US-based fintech providing payments infrastructure for the internet, has signed a definitive agreement to acquire Metronome , a US-based usage-based billing and monetization platform for SaaS and AI companies. Financial terms were not disclosed, though media reports suggest a price of around $1 billion. The deal aims to pair Stripe ’s global payments stack with Metronome ’s metered pricing, billing, and revenue tooling, capabilities that are becoming core for AI-native and consumption-based software businesses. Metronome counts leading AI and data companies among its customers (including OpenAI, Anthropic, and Nvidia) and is expected to continue accelerating its product roadmap within Stripe ’s ecosystem.

 iDPal , an Ireland-based provider of an identity verification platform, has announced the acquisition of NorthRow , a UK-based KYB specialist. The deal strengthens iDPal ’s regtech offering by combining its identity verification capabilities with NorthRow ’s business verification expertise, supporting regulated firms with more integrated end-to-end KYC/KYB workflows.

 Shawbrook Bank , a UK-based specialist bank providing lending and savings products to individuals and businesses, has announced the acquisition of Playter , a UK-based B2B BNPL and SME lending platform. The transaction will combine Playter ’s proprietary, AI-enabled credit and broker tooling with Shawbrook ’s existing digital capabilities, distribution, and funding base to broaden access to fast, flexible finance for UK SMEs. Since launching in 2021, Playter ’s platform has delivered over £100m of lending to SMEs across use cases such as VAT funding, working capital, and business expansion.

 And finally, we bring you four news stories that caught our eye last week: 
 Barclays CEO CS Venkatakrishnan said at the FT Global Banking Summit that Revolut has benefited from not holding a full UK banking licence, allowing it to avoid some consumer obligations faced by traditional banks. He acknowledged fintechs have improved service quality but argued banks are not competing on equal regulatory terms. Revolut , recently valued at $75bn and boosted by demand for its crypto services, now has 65mn customers worldwide, though regulators have yet to grant it a full licence, limiting deposits, lending, and excluding customers from the Financial Services Compensation Scheme. NatWest CEO Paul Thwaite said valuations do not concern him but agreed that Revolut has raised expectations for retail banking. Venkatakrishnan also endorsed the UK Budget’s growth focus and highlighted Barclays ’ plan to invest £45bn in the UK over the next three years.

 Automated wealth management firm Wealthfront plans to raise up to $485m in its Nasdaq debut, seeking a valuation of as much as $2.05bn. The offering includes 34.6m shares priced at $12–$14, with BlackRock and Wellington signalling interest in buying up to $150m of stock. Analysts say Wealthfront sits at the intersection of fintech and AI, helping it tap strong investor demand as the U.S. IPO market recovers. Founded in 2008, the company offers automated investing, cash accounts, planning tools and low-cost loans tailored to Millennial and Gen Z clients. Recent fintech listings such as Klarna , Chime and eToro have drawn robust interest, bolstering sentiment. Wealthfront will list under the ticker “WLTH,” with Goldman Sachs , J.P. Morgan and Citigroup leading the deal. The IPO marks its biggest valuation step since a planned 2022 sale to UBS collapsed.

 Ten major European banks, including ING , UniCredit and BNP Paribas , have created Amsterdam-based Qivalis to launch a euro-pegged stablecoin in the second half of 2026, aiming to challenge U.S. dominance in digital payments. Led by former Coinbase Germany CEO Jan-Oliver Sell , the firm is seeking an EMI licence and plans to hire up to 50 staff. The token will first serve crypto trading, offering near-instant, low-cost settlement, though demand for euro stablecoins remains small compared with dollar-backed giants like Tether . Regulators, concerned about reliance on U.S. fintech stablecoins, have shown support as the ECB works on its digital euro. BNP Paribas has joined the initiative, while other global banks are also exploring stablecoin issuance.

 The UK Treasury plans to introduce provisional 18-month FCA licences to help financial start-ups launch faster, allowing them to operate with lighter requirements before meeting full authorisation standards. The regime aims to ease barriers such as capital, compliance and audit thresholds that early-stage firms often struggle to satisfy, with City minister Lucy Rigby saying delays have hindered growth. The FCA will apply proportionate expectations and require less information, though firms will face limits on the volume or value of business they can conduct and must stop operating if they fail to secure full authorisation by the deadline. While regulators say the move will boost innovation and competition, consumer groups warn it could lower standards and increase risks. The licences will not apply to banks, insurers or firms offering long-term products, and the plan will proceed to consultation and legislation before taking effect.
