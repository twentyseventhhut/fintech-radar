Why Africa’s Fintechs are becoming Banks (MB#7) 
They say every fintech dreams of a banking license.In Africa, that dream has always felt distant, because the bar for a banking license is intentionally brutal.
Central banks are being asked a hard question: “Can we really trust fintechs with people’s money?”
The answer is possible but never easy. It takes courage, capital, governance, and patience.
 And regulators have scars 
 Global failures like FTX , which misused over $8 billion of customer deposits, remain fresh in the collective memory. Every time a fintech knocks on the door asking for a banking license, that history whispers, “Be careful.”

It didn’t happen in Africa but in today’s world, the world is a village.
 The Old Fintech Comfort Zone 
For over a decade, the gold standard Fintech licenses in Africa were:
 PSP (Payment Service Provider) 
 IMT (International Money Transfer Operator) 
If you had one of these, you were “safe.”
 Most of the African fintech giants you know lived comfortably here: Flutterwave, Paystack, Chipper Cash, LemFi, Nala and others. 
These licenses were good enough to:
 Partner with banks 
 Move money at scale 
 Appear highly compliant 
For years, that was the endgame.
 Something Is Shifting; Globally and Locally 
 This week alone, PayPal , a ~$50B fintech, applied for a banking license. 
That headline shook markets until you zoom out.
African fintechs have been quietly ahead of this curve.
 African Fintechs Are Becoming Banks 
 Moniepoint secured a microfinance banking license in Nigeria (2022), choosing to directly bank its 10M+ customers. In June 2025, it doubled down by acquiring 78% of Kenya’s Sumac Microfinance Bank . In the past 14 months Moniepoint has raised $200 Million and has been aggressively hiring top talent from Access Bank and Stanbic IBTC , deepening its bench as it scales banking operations, and it was also looking to acquire a commercial bank license. 
 In June 2024, Selcom , Tanzania’s biggest and oldest Fintech, acquired a licensed microfinance bank in Tanzania, Access Bank , rebranded it to Selcom Microfinance Bank and introduced Selcom Pesa , one of the cheapest neo-banking experiences in the country. 
 In June 2025, Lesaka Technologies acquired Bank Zero , which held ~$22M in deposits and 40,000+ funded accounts 
 In October 2025, Wave, Francophone Africa’s first Unicorn applied for a full banking license, committing $35M in capital; reframing its future around deposits, credit, and direct access to core infrastructure. 
This is no longer experimentation. It’s a pattern.
 So Why Not Just Partner With Banks? 
Yes, you can mention advantages on deposits, balance-sheet leverage, and margins matter.
But founders point to something deeper; Control. Independence. Survival.
 Sameer Hirji, CEO and Founder of Selcom , a fintech serving 100,000+ businesses and 60,000+ agents across Tanzania, which acquired a bank last year, revealed that: 
“The biggest change is independence from depending on third party enablers in the form of other banks, and in our case MoMo operators, to drive your strategy. These enablers can at the drop of a hat, at any time, pull the rug from under you in the name of margin pressure, or if you rub them the wrong way by posing a competitive threat, or they can simply circumvent you and cut you out of the value chain after they have learnt the ropes.
I’m not making any of this up, I have lived through it, every discussion and meeting fresh in my mind, even today”
