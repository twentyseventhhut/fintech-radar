Welcome to another week of fintech insights. Let’s explore the news and trends shaping the industry!
 Over the last week, there was one fintech deal in Europe, raising €4.6m million , in the United Kingdom: Sapi , a UK-based payment-linked financing platform that enables payment providers to offer flexible funding to small businesses, has raised €4.6 million in equity in a Venture Round led by Hudson Cove Capital Management . 
Let’s dive in!
 Sapi 
 Sapi , headquartered in London , provides a payment-linked financing platform that enables payment providers to offer fair, flexible funding to small businesses, with repayments taken directly from merchants’ payment flows. The company has raised ~€70 million in a debt and equity package, comprising €65 million in debt and a €4.6 million equity round led by Hudson Cove Capital Management , with participation from existing investors Triple A Capital and Passion Capital . The new capital will support Sapi ’s international expansion, particularly across Asia-Pacific, and further development of its technology platform. 
 Here is the European M&A activity for the week: 
 Cegid , a France-based provider of accounting, finance, payroll, and HR software, has agreed to acquire SHINE , a Denmark-based online business account offering a bank card, invoicing, and management tools for SMEs. The deal, valued at over €1 billion, would allow Cegid to integrate banking and payments features directly into its core financial suite, strengthening its position in the banktech segment and expanding its footprint in Northern Europe. The transaction is currently pending.

 Bridgepoint , a UK-based mid-market private equity firm, has announced the €228 million acquisition of ht.digital , a UK provider of audit, assurance, and accounting services for crypto and digital assets. The deal gives Bridgepoint exposure to a fast-growing segment of the financial services market, such as ht.digital supports exchanges, custodians, and digital asset providers facing increasing regulatory scrutiny. The acquisition is pending completion.

 POM , a Netherlands-based platform automating invoicing, payments, reminders, and accounting reconciliation, has reached an agreement to acquire FarPay , a Denmark-based provider of invoice delivery, customer payment automation, and debt collection tools.  And finally, we bring you four news stories that caught our eye last week: 
 Revolut has achieved a $75bn valuation following a secondary share sale, marking a 67% increase from its 2024 valuation of $45bn. The transaction, co-led by Coatue and Greenoaks , allowed employees to sell shares at premium pricing while positioning the company for aggressive global expansion. CEO Nik Storonsky plans to use the momentum to launch operations in India and Mexico, targeting 30 new markets by 2030. The valuation surge coincides with significant regulatory milestones, including final authorization to operate in Mexico and in-principle approval for payment licenses in the UAE. Revolut , which now serves 65 million customers, reported strong financial health with $4bn in revenue and $1.4bn in pre-tax profit last year. The move cements its status as a leading challenger bank as it integrates with major systems like India’s UPI .

 S&P Ratings has downgraded its assessment of Tether’s assets to “weak,” the lowest measure, citing increased exposure to high-risk assets and persistent transparency issues. The agency noted that risky holdings, including bitcoin, corporate bonds, and secured loans, rose to 24% of reserves, exposing the $184bn stablecoin to greater market volatility and potential under-collateralization. S&P also criticized the lack of disclosures regarding custodians and asset segregation to protect against insolvency. Tether strongly disputed the report, claiming its transparency exceeds banking standards and highlighting its resilience through past market shocks. Despite the downgrade, Tether reported $10.1bn in net profits this year, driven largely by interest from its massive US Treasury holdings. However, the downgrade arrives as the broader crypto market faces a downturn, with bitcoin prices falling significantly from recent peaks.

 Klarna has launched “KlarnaUSD,” a payment stablecoin on a Stripe -created blockchain, aiming to reshape its cross-border payment infrastructure. The move allows the Swedish fintech to bypass intermediaries like the Swift network, dramatically reducing costs for moving large sums globally. While initially focused on internal infrastructure, plans exist to roll the token out to merchants and consumers, following similar initiatives by PayPal and Stripe . This launch represents a significant pivot for CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski , who previously opposed crypto but now aims for Klarna to become a digital bank rather than just a lender. The strategy comes as Klarna shares have dropped 30% since its New York listing, prompting a need to unlock shareholder value amidst heating competition with other neobanks and crypto startups entering the banking space.

 The ‘digital asset treasury’ strategy is collapsing as a $1tn cryptocurrency market rout forces companies to dump holdings to prop up falling share prices. Shares in Strategy , the largest corporate bitcoin holder, have plummeted 50% recently, dragging down copycat firms and wiping $77bn from the sector’s market value since July. Analysts warn of a “vicious cycle” where falling token prices trigger stock sell-offs, forcing further crypto liquidation. Consequently, firms like FG Nexus , ETHZilla , and Sequans are reversing course, selling millions in tokens to fund share buybacks or service debt. While Strategy continues to buy despite potential index exclusion, analysts predict 95% of these digital treasuries could fail, particularly those holding niche tokens. The reversal marks a stark shift from the optimism fuelled by political pledges to make the US a “bitcoin superpower”.
