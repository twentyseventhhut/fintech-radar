 МТС продала 49,9% в своей башенной компании 
МТС объявила о продаже 49,9% в Башенной инфраструктурной компании (БИК) закрытому паевому инвестиционному фонду «Инвестиции 18» (ЗПИФ) под управлением УК «Доверительная». По состоянию на 2025 год, по данным СПАРК, МТС была учредителем УК «Доверительная», уточняет " Коммерсант ".
«Продажа позволит МТС не только значительно укрепить финансовую позицию за счет деконсолидации более чем 80 млрд руб. долга, но и привлечь денежные средства в размере 21,7 млрд руб.»,— заявили в компании. В МТС добавили, что у БИК есть долг в размере более 80 млрд руб., а в результате продажи доли в БИК доходы, обязательства и прочие финансовые показатели компании перестанут консолидироваться в отчетности группы МТС. «Сделкой также предусмотрен набор взаимных опционов участников, включая возможность выкупа МТС проданной доли в БИК при определенных обстоятельствах»,— сказали в компании. Условиями сделки предусмотрен совместный контроль МТС и ЗПИФ в отношении основной деятельности БИК, уточнили в МТС. 
По данным МТС, БИК принадлежит более 24,7 тыс. антенно-мачтовых сооружений (АМС) в России, а по данным агентства TelecomDaily, БИК находится на третьем месте среди инфраструктурных (башенных) операторов России по количеству АМС. В 2025 году общее количество эксплуатируемых АМС выросло на 9% — с 114,4 тыс. до 125,2 тыс.
Сделка выглядит не как классическая продажа стратегического актива внешнему инвестору, а как элемент финансовой и корпоративной реструктуризации, говорит руководитель проектов практики «Корпоративные финансы» Strategy Partners Владислав Карымов: «При этом такая структура сделки сохраняет для МТС стратегическую гибкость. Если БИК удастся увеличить эффективность использования инфраструктуры, нарастить денежные потоки и улучшить финансовые показатели, компания сможет рассчитывать на более высокую оценку актива. В дальнейшем это может расширить спектр возможных сценариев монетизации: от привлечения стратегического или финансового инвестора до публичного размещения компании при благоприятной рыночной конъюнктуре».

_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
MTS (PJSC, Russia's largest mobile operator, ~42% owned by AFK Sistema) agreed to sell **49.9%** of its tower subsidiary **Bashennaya Infrastrukturnaya Kompaniya (BIK / "Tower Infrastructure Company")** to a closed-end mutual investment fund (ZPIF) **"Investitsii 18"**, managed by UK "Doveritelnaya". Headline terms: **RUB 21.7bn cash** to MTS + **deconsolidation of >RUB 80bn of BIK debt** from MTS group accounts. Implied enterprise value of the tower business **>RUB 125bn** including debt (per Vedomosti/abireg). BIK owns **>24,700 antenna-mast structures (AMS)**, ~3rd-largest infrastructure (tower) operator in Russia by site count (TelecomDaily); total Russian AMS grew 9% in 2025, 114.4k→125.2k.

**+ Why structured exactly this way / what it reveals (the core red-team):**
- This is **not a classic strategic sale to an external investor** — it is a **financial / corporate restructuring**, explicitly framed as such by Strategy Partners' Vladislav Karymov in the source. Three structural tells:
  1. **MTS keeps 50.1% + joint control.** It sells *below* 50% and retains the controlling stake and joint control via the deal terms, so it never loses operational grip on its passive infrastructure (it remains the long-term anchor tenant on market-based lease terms).
  2. **The "buyer" looks related-party.** Per Kommersant citing SPARK (2025 data), **MTS was the founder of the management company UK "Doveritelnaya"** behind the ZPIF "Investitsii 18". A ZPIF is an opaque, tax-efficient wrapper; that the seller appears connected to the buyer's manager makes this read more like a **structured deconsolidation vehicle than an arm's-length disposal** — confirm/qualify, but it materially undercuts the "we sold half the tower business" framing.
  3. **Mutual options.** The deal includes a set of mutual options "including the possibility for MTS to buy back the sold stake under certain circumstances" — i.e. MTS can reverse it. A genuine permanent disposal does not usually need a built-in buy-back.
- **What MTS actually bought with this:** an accounting outcome. The >RUB 80bn of BIK debt leaves the MTS consolidated balance sheet, optically de-levering the group, while RUB 21.7bn cash comes in. The economic substance — MTS still controls the towers and still pays rent on them — barely changes. (analysis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
Russian tower-monetization wave (this is wave 2, not a first):
- **VEON / VimpelCom:** carved 14.5k sites into **National Tower Company** (2016); after a pulled sale, sold its ~**15,400-tower** Russian portfolio to **Service-Telecom for RUB 70.65bn (~EUR848m / ~$970m)** in 2021 — a *full external* sale (TowerXchange, dgtlinfra).
- **MegaFon:** carved ~17k towers into **First Tower Company**, then formed a **JV with independent towerco Vertical** — a middle path (asset separation + partner), not a clean sale.
- **Tele2 / Rostelecom:** allocated tower assets for sale (2022); state-controlled, expected to follow but no completed deal disclosed.
- **MTS:** spun towers into a wholly-owned subsidiary (BIK), and now this 49.9% transaction.
Independent Russian towercos that consolidated the market: **Service-Telecom, Russian Towers (Ivan Tavrin), Vertical**.

**+ Why the lay of the land is this way / second-order:**
- Western "tower-co" thesis (sell passive infra to specialist owner → higher infra multiple, operator de-levers and focuses on services) **broke after 2022 sanctions**: foreign tower majors (Cellnex, American Tower, IHS) and foreign capital exited Russia, so the **pool of credible external buyers collapsed**. (analysis)
- That structural absence of strategic buyers is *why* MTS' deal looks like an internal ZPIF restructuring rather than a Service-Telecom-style external trade sale — VEON could sell to a real towerco in 2021; in 2026 MTS arguably can't get the same arm's-length bid, so it engineers the de-leveraging instead. **This shifts the central question** from "did MTS monetize towers at a good multiple?" to **"is this a real sale at all, or balance-sheet engineering dressed as one?"** (analysis)

## [2] Company history / fit
- MTS has run an **ecosystem/de-leveraging narrative** for years: spinning out verticals (MTS Bank — IPO 2024; AdTech, media/KION, MTS Travel, fintech), targeting clearer SOTP value and a defined dividend (2024–2026 policy: ≥RUB 35/share). MTS Bank closed acquisition of ~99.9% of "Exy Bank" in Jan 2026 (ecosystem expansion).
- The towers are a **commodity, debt-heavy, low-growth passive asset** sitting inside a group whose equity story is "ecosystem + dividend". Carving them out is logically consistent: move the >RUB 80bn debt and the boring rent-yielding asset off the consolidated story while keeping economic control.

**+ Why MTS acts this way (structural pressure):**
- MTS pays a **high, policy-committed dividend to AFK Sistema (~42% owner)**, which is itself debt-laden and needs cash up from MTS. High payout + high Russian rates (CBR rate was elevated through 2025) = **balance-sheet stress**. Deconsolidating RUB 80bn of debt **cosmetically improves group net-debt/leverage ratios** that gate dividend capacity and borrowing cost — Sistema's cash need is the upstream driver, not a strategic re-think of infrastructure. (hypothesis — strong, given Sistema's well-documented leverage)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty: low.** Russian tower carve-outs began ~2016; the *external* monetization peak was VEON 2021. MTS' move is a late, **partial, related-party** version. The genuinely "new" element is the **ZPIF + mutual-option structure for deconsolidation**, which is a financial-engineering innovation, not an operational one.
- **Real value-add / who captures the margin:** the towers still earn rent; the question is **who owns the upside if BIK improves utilization/co-location**. Karymov flags exactly this: if BIK lifts efficiency and cash flows, a **higher valuation → later monetization (strategic/financial investor or IPO)** becomes possible. So the real "value" here is **optionality**, parked in a vehicle MTS still part-controls — MTS keeps the upside (50.1% + buy-back option) while exporting the debt. (analysis)
- **Traction to watch:** none yet on improved BIK economics; the RUB 21.7bn cash and debt deconsolidation are the only hard, near-term outcomes. Everything beyond that is "could/may".

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Near term:** RUB 21.7bn cash + >RUB 80bn off-balance-sheet debt → optically stronger MTS leverage, supportive of the dividend and of MTS Bank/ecosystem capital needs. Watch whether rating agencies / analysts treat the deconsolidation as *real* de-leveraging or see through the joint-control + buy-back option (IFRS may still require consolidation if MTS retains control — **a key open accounting question**).
- **Medium term:** possible second-step monetization of BIK (external investor or **BIK IPO**) "under favorable market conditions" — explicitly floated. Given sanctions and a thin domestic infra-investor base, a true external exit is uncertain.
- **Sentiment:** Russian press (CNews) framed it bluntly — "MTS lost almost half its tower business, with a RUB 80bn debt tail" — i.e. as much a **debt-offloading** story as a value-creation one.

**+ Why the market reads it this way / counterintuitive second-order:** a deal that *raises* reported financial strength (lower consolidated debt) can simultaneously **signal balance-sheet stress** — healthy operators don't need to ring-fence RUB 80bn into a ZPIF to hit leverage targets. If IFRS forces re-consolidation, the headline benefit partly evaporates and the deal collapses back toward "MTS raised RUB 21.7bn against its own towers". (analysis)

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
**Importance: 3/5** — A real, sizeable transaction (RUB 21.7bn cash, >RUB 80bn debt deconsolidated, >RUB 125bn EV) at Russia's largest mobile operator, and a clean case study in tower-asset carve-out + balance-sheet engineering. But: it is NOT a true external strategic sale (apparent related-party ZPIF, 50.1% retained, joint control, buy-back option), there is no operational novelty, the tower carve-out theme is years old in Russia (VEON 2021), and outside the Russian market the read-across is limited by sanctions/isolation. Substance is real but mostly financial-structuring, not value-creation — hence 3, not 4.

## Red-team / challenge questions
1. **Is the buyer actually independent?** Kommersant/SPARK indicate MTS founded UK "Doveritelnaya" managing ZPIF "Investitsii 18". If MTS controls or co-controls the buyer's manager, is this a sale at all, or a related-party restructuring? — *strongly suggests related-party; open on exact 2026 control chain.*
2. **Will IFRS even allow deconsolidation?** With 50.1% retained + "joint control" + a buy-back option, does MTS still control BIK under IFRS 10? If yes, the >RUB 80bn debt stays consolidated and the headline benefit largely evaporates. — *key open accounting question.*
3. **What is the cash actually for?** Does RUB 21.7bn go to dividends/AFK Sistema, MTS Bank capital, or debt service? Source doesn't say. — *open.*
4. **Is RUB 21.7bn a fair price for 49.9%?** EV >RUB 125bn incl. debt; equity value much lower after >RUB 80bn debt. Does 49.9% of equity ≈ RUB 21.7bn, and is that a discount/premium vs VEON's RUB 70.65bn for ~15.4k towers (BIK has ~24.7k)? — *implies a low per-tower equity value; debt load explains it; open on exact equity bridge.*
5. **What are the "certain circumstances" for the buy-back option?** If MTS can cheaply re-consolidate later, the asset never truly left. — *open; material.*
6. **Are the inter-company lease terms genuinely "market"?** MTS is the anchor tenant; above-market rent would transfer value back to BIK (and now its 49.9% owner). Who set them? — *open.*
7. **Why sell 49.9%, not 50%+?** Precisely to keep control + retain consolidation optionality + avoid loss of control — confirms the financial-engineering read. — *answered.*
8. **Has any external strategic/financial investor actually shown up?** No Service-Telecom-style buyer is named. Post-sanctions, is a real external towerco buyer even available in Russia? — *answered: market is thin; this is why it's a ZPIF.*
9. **How real is the floated IPO/strategic-exit path?** Karymov says "under favorable market conditions" — is there any precedent for a Russian towerco IPO post-2022? — *open; likely aspirational.*
10. **Does this materially move MTS group leverage and dividend capacity?** By how much does consolidated net-debt/EBITDA fall, and does it unlock the ≥RUB 35/share dividend? — *open; need MTS reported ratios.*
11. **Is the real driver AFK Sistema's leverage, not MTS strategy?** Sistema (~42% owner) is debt-heavy and dependent on MTS dividends — is this de-leveraging upstream-driven? — *strong hypothesis; open on direct evidence.*
12. **Did MTS's own tower portfolio underperform vs peers' (utilization, co-location/tenancy ratio)?** Third by site count — but is tenancy ratio competitive vs Service-Telecom/Russian Towers? — *open; decides whether "efficiency upside" is credible.*
13. **What's the prior art / how new is this?** Russian tower carve-outs since ~2016; VEON full sale 2021; MegaFon JV. MTS is late and partial. — *answered: low novelty.*
14. **Counterparty / governance risk in a ZPIF wrapper?** ZPIFs are opaque; could this obscure who ultimately benefits or enable later value transfer? — *open; governance flag.*
15. **What breaks the thesis?** (a) IFRS re-consolidation; (b) buy-back exercised → asset returns; (c) no external exit ever materializes → it stays a financing trick. Any one largely neutralizes the "monetization" narrative. — *analysis.*

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Global telecom-tower market ~$29–51bn revenue depending on definition; ~4.8–5.0mn towers worldwide, of which towercos own ~3.37mn (~70% penetration), operator-led towercos ~55.6% of towers, up from 52.7% YoY (per TowerXchange, 2024/25). Structure: capital-heavy, debt-funded, low-growth passive infrastructure with bond-like cash flows; value is created not by building masts but by **co-location / tenancy ratio** (multiple tenants per tower). Barriers = capital, planning/permits, anchor-tenant lock-in. The secular thesis — operators carve passive infra into a towerco to (a) de-lever, (b) refocus on services, (c) re-rate the infra at a higher infra multiple — is mature globally and ran in Russia ~2016–2021. **Why now (Russia-specific):** post-2022 sanctions removed the foreign tower majors (Cellnex, American Tower, IHS) and foreign capital as buyers, so the *external* monetization route that VEON used in 2021 is largely closed; high CBR rates through 2025 plus AFK Sistema's (~42% owner) leverage make deconsolidating >RUB 80bn of debt the operative driver, not a strategic re-think. Russian AMS count grew 9% in 2025 (114.4k→125.2k, TelecomDaily) — modest unit growth, consistent with a commodity asset.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: site count, **tenancy/co-location ratio**, EV per tower, EV/EBITDA. Russian players: independents **Service-Telecom** (bought VEON's portfolio), **Russian Towers** (Ivan Tavrin), **Vertical**; operator carve-outs — VEON/**National Tower Company**, MegaFon/**First Tower Company** (JV with Vertical), Tele2-Rostelecom (allocated, no completed deal), and MTS/**BIK** (~24,700 AMS, 3rd by site count per TelecomDaily). Basis of competition: scale and tenancy ratio (more tenants per existing mast = pure margin). MTS's position: **late and partial** — VEON did a clean external trade sale in 2021; MegaFon took a JV middle path; MTS does a 49.9% related-party ZPIF deconsolidation while keeping 50.1% + joint control + a buy-back option. So on the carve-out maturity curve MTS is the laggard, and its moat here is not the towers (commodity) but its own status as anchor tenant — switching-cost moat that accrues to whoever owns BIK, now 49.9% an apparently MTS-linked vehicle (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.**
- **VEON → Service-Telecom (2021), the anchor comp:** RUB 70.65bn (~$957–970m) for ~15,400 towers ⇒ **EV/tower ≈ $63k**; stated **EV/EBITDA ≈ 11.7x** (post-completion EBITDA) — per dgtlinfra/TowerXchange/VEON. [[Citigroup to sell remaining Russian operations at $1.2bn loss]] frames why a 2021-style external bid is no longer available.
- **MTS / BIK (this deal):** EV >RUB 125bn incl. debt for ~24,700 AMS ⇒ EV/tower ≈ RUB 125bn / 24,700 = **~RUB 5.06m ≈ ~$64k** at ~RUB 79/USD (Jun-2026). **This lands almost exactly on VEON's $63k/tower** — so on a headline EV/tower basis the asset is *not* obviously underpriced; the optics of a "cheap" deal come from equity, not EV: after >RUB 80bn debt, equity value is far lower, and RUB 21.7bn for 49.9% implies equity ≈ RUB 43–44bn for 100% (RUB 21.7bn / 0.499 = RUB 43.5bn), i.e. EV RUB 125bn − equity ~RUB 43.5bn ≈ RUB 81.5bn debt — internally consistent with the >RUB 80bn debt figure. EV/EBITDA for BIK = **no data** (BIK EBITDA not disclosed); cannot verify whether the 11.7x VEON multiple holds.
- **External public towercos (read-across, not direct comps — different geo/cost of capital):** American Tower trades ~18.5–20.4x EV/EBITDA (EV ~$132bn, 2025 Adj. EBITDA $7.13bn ⇒ $132bn/$7.13bn ≈ 18.5x, per multiples.vc/SEC/gurufocus, early 2026). Sanctioned Russian assets would price at a steep discount to this; the gap is a sanctions/risk discount, not an operational signal.
- **Internal comps:** [[Hunter Point Capital buys stake in Equitix from Tetragon]] — minority infra stake sold while seller keeps majority/realizes value (closest structural analogue); [[Telenor explores sale of 55% stake in Pakistan's Easypaisa Bank]] — telco shedding a capital-heavy unit to de-lever; [[Vodacom acquires controlling stake in Safaricom]] — telco-infra stake deal with state/related-party dynamics.
- Distribution not computed (only one clean comparable, VEON); qualitative read: **in-line on EV/tower vs VEON, in-line-to-cheap on equity given the debt load**, not a premium.

**Risk flags.**
1. **IFRS re-consolidation risk.** With 50.1% retained + "joint control" + a buy-back option, MTS may still control BIK under IFRS 10 — if so the >RUB 80bn debt stays consolidated and the headline de-leveraging benefit largely evaporates. Second-order: the entire "we cut group debt" thesis is contingent on an accounting determination not yet confirmed.
2. **Related-party / governance (ZPIF) risk.** Buyer's manager (UK "Doveritelnaya", ZPIF "Investitsii 18") appears MTS-founded per Kommersant/SPARK; an opaque ZPIF wrapper plus a buy-back option means this may not be an arm's-length disposal at all. Second-order: price discovery is absent, so "fair value" and any future intra-group lease/value transfer are unverifiable.
3. **No external exit + sanctions concentration.** Post-2022 the credible external/strategic buyer pool for Russian towers has collapsed; the floated IPO/strategic-exit ("under favorable market conditions") has no post-2022 Russian towerco precedent. Second-order: the "later monetization at a higher multiple" optionality may never crystallize, leaving a financing trick rather than a value-creation event.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This marks Russian tower monetization shifting from *external trade sales* (VEON 2021) to *internal balance-sheet engineering* — a re-rating of the playbook, not of the asset (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if MTS's auditors deconsolidate BIK and a genuine third-party investor or IPO appears within 12–18 months, this was real monetization; the trigger/what-breaks-it is the IFRS 10 control conclusion and whether the buy-back option is ever neutralized — if MTS keeps control or retains the buy-back, treat it as RUB 21.7bn raised against its own towers, not a sale.
