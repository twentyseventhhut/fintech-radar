Why Wall Street thinks US memory maker Micron is the next Nvidia
Micron, the Boise, Idaho-based memory chip maker, has captured Wall Street’s heart. Whether the love affair endures will heavily depend on how long the AI-driven supply crunch for memory chips lasts.
Micron promises that it has shored up its position for the long term, which would allow it to withstand a sudden drop in demand or overcapacity of supply. And Wall Street has become a believer, helping Micron briefly surpass the market valuation of Meta and Tesla for the first time on Thursday, though it floated back down by Friday to nearly match them.
Specifically Micron closed Friday’s trading with a market cap close to $1.27 trillion, while Meta was at $1.39 trillion and Tesla was at $1.42 trillion. Micron’s stock has soared over 236% in the past month alone, closing Friday at $1,132 a share. In comparison, it spent years upon years before mid-2025 at below $100 a share.
It’s a dizzying rise for a company that most consumers associated with the tiny memory cards that, back in the day, were commonly needed to boost PCs, smartphones, or other device storage.
Wall Street isn’t sweating over that product line. Micron is benefiting from the AI data center buildout boom that has created a shortage of system memory chips, both DRAM and NAND, which Micron makes, particularly High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM). A single AI server requires magnitudes more memory than a laptop.
AI system makers like Nvidia, as well as the hyperscalers building their own systems, are buying up large quantities of memory, such as Microsoft, Amazon AWS, Google, Meta and Oracle. This is forcing all the other companies who need memory to hoard it as well, from PC makers like Dell and HP, to other kinds of device makers.
This lack of supply, which has been dubbed RAMageddon , is predicted to persist  into 2027 . And it’s already driving up the price of consumer electronics like Apple products and Xbox consoles.
With the whole tech industry clamoring for more memory, Micron’s delivered blockbuster third-quarter earnings last week. Revenue quadrupled year-over-year to $41.45 billion, and profits skyrocketed from $1.88 billion to $28.2 billion over the same period. Micron also provided a positive outlook, forecasting fourth-quarter revenue of between $49 billion and $51 billion.
And Wall Street, which has been eager to find more public AI-related companies that may do as well as Nvidia, became even more enamored.
The historic problem for memory chip makers like Micron and Samsung is that building out manufacturing facilities to increase capacity is a time-consuming, expensive endeavor. And demand often falls just as companies can increase capacity, creating a glut and subsequent price drop.
Micron got ahead of any AI bust chatter by emphasizing a series of long-term supply agreements, including with Nvidia and AI lab Anthropic , that would presumably protect it. The company said in its earnings presentation that it has signed 16 strategic customer agreements across the data center, consumer, and auto market segments, which it expects to fundamentally transform its business model. 
That seemed to convince a number of analysts that this company could be another long-term, profitable investment. In a research note, William Blair tech analyst Sebastien Naji noted demand growth continues to outpace the rate that new cleanroom space can come online.
“Given the strong likelihood of continued ASP growth in the coming quarters and improving revenue visibility thanks to a rapidly expanding set of long-term agreements (SCAs) with key customers, we see potential for more durable earnings growth and reiterate our Outperform rating,” Naji wrote.
Whether Micron really can sustain itself for long-term without a bust cycle remains to be seen. But for a brief moment on Thursday, this U.S. company was more valuable than some of the industry’s giants.

_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

New figures, new reporting period, new "next Nvidia" analyst framing → fresh.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Micron's stock briefly topped Meta and Tesla by market cap for the first time on Thursday, then settled Friday at a ~$1.27tn cap (close $1,132/share); the stock is up ~236% in the past month, after spending years below $100 pre-mid-2025 (per TechCrunch, 2026-06-28). The driver is the fiscal-Q3 FY26 print: revenue $41.46bn (+346% YoY, +74% QoQ), net income $28.2bn vs $1.88bn a year ago, Q4 guide $49–51bn (per TechCrunch / Micron press release, week of 2026-06-22). DRAM was a record $31.3bn (76% of revenue), with DRAM ASPs up in the low-60s% range QoQ on tight supply (per S&P Global / IG earnings coverage).
→ Why this framing: the "next Nvidia" label is a Wall-Street demand for *another* liquid, US-listed, AI-infra long. The substance under the PR is real (a genuine HBM/DRAM shortage = pricing power), but the *durability* claim is the contested part. Micron pre-empted the bust narrative by disclosing 16 Strategic Customer Agreements (~$100bn minimum contracted revenue) across data-center/consumer/auto, including Nvidia and Anthropic ([[Micron and Anthropic announce memory partnership]]) — i.e. it is selling "visibility," not just chips, to earn a software-like multiple. (analysis)
→ What's silent: the SCAs are *minimum* commitments with undisclosed pricing floors; a pricing collapse in a glut could still compress margins even if volumes hold.

## [1] Competitors / peers
HBM is a 3-player oligopoly: SK Hynix leads (~50–55% share), Samsung ~35–40%, Micron ~5–10% (per Silicon Analysts / Counterpoint, 2026). UBS expects SK Hynix to take ~70% of HBM4 for Nvidia's Rubin platform in 2026. So Micron is the *smallest* of the three in HBM — the "next Nvidia" tag is about US-listing + AI leverage + earnings momentum, not dominance.
→ Why the re-rating still works: in a true shortage, even the #3 supplier prints record margins because all capacity is sold out; the bottleneck (cleanroom space, per William Blair's Naji) caps everyone's supply. SK Hynix has reportedly chosen DDR5 profits over a faster HBM4 ramp (per TechTimes, 2026-06-24), implicitly tightening HBM further. Second-order: the multiple gap vs SK Hynix/Samsung (both Korea-listed, lower multiples) is partly a US-listing/liquidity premium, partly that Micron's earnings inflection is the steepest. That premium narrows the moment ASPs roll over.

## [2] Company history / fit
Micron is the textbook memory cyclical: commodity DRAM/NAND, brutal boom-bust, years sub-$100/share. The structural pressure driving the SCA strategy is exactly this commodity history — to escape the "glut → price crash" trap it is locking in multi-year minimum-volume contracts to convert a take-price commodity into something with revenue visibility. HBM4 volume shipments began calendar-Q1 2026 for Nvidia's Vera Rubin platform (per IG/S&P coverage). The Anthropic deal (2026-06-23, equity + Claude design-in) fits the same logic: anchor demand + a strategic narrative.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Real and quantified, not announced: $41.46bn revenue, $28.2bn net income, DRAM ASPs +low-60s% QoQ, HBM capacity sold out through 2026 (much of 2027 too). That is traction, not a press release. HBM TAM is guided to grow ~40% CAGR to ~$100bn by 2028 from ~$35bn in 2025 (per Micron earnings call / BofA pegs 2026 HBM at ~$54.6bn).
→ Why the value-add may or may not be durable: the margin sits in *scarcity*, and scarcity is set by cleanroom build-out lead times vs AI capex. Who captures margin in the stack: today it's the memory triopoly because supply can't flex fast; if AI capex digests or all three add capacity into 2027–28, the same operating leverage reverses violently. The SCAs cushion volume but the *price* is the swing factor. Forward P/E ~9 vs trailing ~25 (per Yahoo/Simply Wall St) tells you the market already prices a peak-earnings cyclical, not a secular compounder.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Sentiment is euphoric: analyst targets doubled/tripled in a month — Needham $1,550, UBS $1,625, Aletheia $1,600, Stifel/TD Cowen $1,500, Wedbush/Bernstein $1,300, Wolfe $1,250, Citi $1,200. Morningstar projects Micron's CY26–27 net income to rank #2 in the PHLX Semiconductor Index, behind only Nvidia. "RAMageddon" shortage is expected to persist into 2027 (Samsung/SK Hynix warn beyond, per Tom's Hardware).
→ Why the second-order risk is *fragility, not safety*: the entire bull case rests on the shortage outlasting the capacity response. Memory is the most cyclical AI-infra exposure — when the cycle turns, the low forward P/E is a feature (it's discounting the down-leg), and a name that 3.3x'd in a month can de-rate just as fast. The "next Nvidia" comparison flatters: Nvidia has a software/CUDA moat; Micron's moat is a temporary supply gap.

**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: AI-infra / memory semiconductors (DRAM, NAND, HBM). HBM TAM ~$35bn (2025) guided to ~$100bn by 2028, ~40% CAGR (per Micron earnings call); BofA pegs 2026 HBM at ~$54.6bn, +58% YoY. Structure: consolidated triopoly — SK Hynix ~50–55%, Samsung ~35–40%, Micron ~5–10% of HBM (per Silicon Analysts/Counterpoint, 2026). Barriers are capital + lead time: new cleanroom space takes years and demand outpaces it (William Blair's Naji). Drivers/"why now": (1) AI data-center buildout consuming DRAM/NAND/HBM faster than capacity can ramp ("RAMageddon," persists into 2027 per Tom's Hardware); (2) DDR5 contract prices +90–95% QoQ in Q1 2026 (TrendForce). Second-order: scarcity is set by build-out lead times vs AI capex — the same operating leverage that drives record margins reverses hard if capacity catches demand.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: DRAM/HBM ASP trajectory, bit-shipment volume, HBM qualification share at Nvidia. Players & basis of competition: oligopoly competing on capacity allocation + qualification/yield, not price (everyone's sold out). Recent moves: SK Hynix reportedly favoring DDR5 profits over a faster HBM4 ramp (TechTimes, 2026-06-24), tightening HBM further; UBS sees SK Hynix ~70% of Rubin HBM4 in 2026; Micron began HBM4 volume shipments calendar-Q1 2026 for Nvidia's Vera Rubin. Micron's position: catching up / #3 in HBM but capacity-constrained and selling out, with a $100bn SCA book locking visibility. Moat: scale + intangibles (qualification/yield) — but thin vs Nvidia's software moat; the edge is a temporary supply gap (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** Micron: market cap ~$1.27tn, close $1,132/share, +236% in a month (per TechCrunch, 2026-06-28). Trailing P/E ~25.6, forward P/E ~9 (per Yahoo/Simply Wall St) → forward = `~$1.27tn / forward NI` implying the market discounts a steep earnings step-up AND subsequent normalization (a peak-cycle signature). Q3 FY26: revenue $41.46bn, NI $28.2bn → annualized run-rate NI well above trailing, hence the gap between trailing and forward multiples.
Peers: SK Hynix and Samsung are Korea-listed and trade at materially lower multiples (`[UNSOURCED]` exact P/E from a free source here) — the multiple gap is partly US-listing/liquidity premium, partly Micron's steeper inflection. Internal comps: [[Micron and Anthropic announce memory partnership]] (equity + design-in, 2026-06-23), [[AI memory-chip demand pushes Apple to raise iPhone prices]] (downstream ASP pass-through, 2026-06-20). Flag: forward P/E ~9 is "cheap" only optically — for a peak-earnings cyclical a single-digit forward multiple is the market pricing the down-leg, not a bargain. Distribution not computed (no ≥3 comparable free multiples); qualitative comparison.

**Risk flags.**
1. Cyclical reversal — memory is the most cyclical AI-infra exposure; the entire thesis is single-variable (shortage duration vs capacity response). When all three add HBM4/DRAM capacity into 2027–28, ASPs and the operating leverage reverse violently. Forward P/E ~9 already hints the market expects this.
2. Customer concentration — demand is concentrated in Nvidia + a handful of hyperscalers; a capex pause at even one materially dents the order book. SCAs are minimum-volume, not price floors.
3. Competitive share risk — Micron is #3 in HBM (~5–10%); SK Hynix may take ~70% of Rubin HBM4 (UBS). Today's print is supply-gap-driven; next-cycle share could squeeze Micron's premium.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** A genuine re-rating of US memory from deep-cyclical commodity to "AI-infra long," but it is sentiment-fragile: capital concentration into one name makes it fragile, not safe (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if HBM/DRAM contract ASPs keep rising through CY27, the SCA visibility narrative holds and the premium is defensible. Trigger that breaks it: the first quarter of flat/declining DRAM ASPs or a hyperscaler capex guide-down → the "next Nvidia" frame unwinds fast. Watch: TrendForce/contract-price prints and SK Hynix/Samsung capacity-add announcements.
