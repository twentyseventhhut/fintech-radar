# Context-enrichment: Alan Greenspan dies at 100
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Alan Greenspan, Fed Chairman 1987–2006, **died Monday, June 22, 2026, aged 100**, at his home in Washington D.C. of complications from Parkinson's disease; announced by his wife, NBC's Andrea Mitchell. Confirmed by an **official Federal Reserve press release (June 22, 2026)** plus obituaries (CNBC, NBC, CNN, CBS, NPR, Al Jazeera, Boston Globe). This is an obituary/commentary item, not a fintech transaction — its weight is in legacy and the monetary-policy lens, not breaking news.

**Why it matters / what it reveals:** The digest framed it as "legend of the financial market." De-PR'd, the substance is twofold: (1) Greenspan institutionalized the idea that a modern central bank manages **expectations**, not just the policy rate — "when he spoke, stocks, bonds and currencies moved" (Финтехно channel). (2) His laissez-faire, Ayn-Rand-influenced worldview (he was in Rand's "Collective," contributed to *Capitalism: The Unknown Ideal*, 1966) is precisely what the 2008 crisis put on trial — making this obituary a proxy for an ongoing debate about Fed independence, deregulation and asset-bubble responsibility that is **live again in 2026** (see [4]). One skeptical flag: the channel text is hagiographic ("legend," "symbol"); the harder story is the contested 2008 legacy, which the PR frame omits.

## [1] Competitors / peers (other Fed chairs / the institution)
The relevant "peer set" is the lineage of Fed chairs and the institution's evolving doctrine:
- **Paul Volcker** (1979–87, predecessor): broke 1970s–80s inflation with brutal rates — the credibility Greenspan inherited.
- **Ben Bernanke** (2006–14, successor, Feb 1 2006): inherited the housing bubble and ran the 2008 crisis response Greenspan's regime is blamed for seeding. Bernanke's tribute: "a great central banker… we are still learning from him."
- **Janet Yellen, Jerome Powell** — later chairs operating under the post-2008 macroprudential/regulatory regime that explicitly repudiated Greenspan-era hands-off finance.
- **Kevin Warsh** — per the corpus, the **current (2026) Fed Chairman** ([[Banks get more flexibility to display the FDIC Logo in digital tools]]), facing "an unusually high-stakes test" weeks into the job amid Trump-administration pressure.

**Why the lay of the land is this way (analysis):** Greenspan's 18.5-year tenure (second-longest ever) made the chairmanship a *personal* office — a "Maestro" (Woodward, 2000) whose word moved markets. Post-2008, the doctrine swung hard the other way: rules-based, macroprudential, committee-forward (the Dec 2025 FOMC statement is signed by a named voting roster — [[Federal Reserve cuts rates to 3.5-3.75% in December 2025]]). Second-order: the very personalization Greenspan pioneered is what makes today's politicized attacks on the chair (Powell/Warsh) possible — a strong-man Fed is a target.

## [2] Company history / fit (Greenspan's own trajectory)
Born March 6, 1926, NYC; Juilliard clarinet (1943–44); NYU economics (BA 1948, MA 1950, PhD 1977); ran consulting firm **Townsend-Greenspan & Co.** (1955–87); chaired the **Council of Economic Advisers under Ford** (1974–77); **Fed Chairman Aug 11, 1987 – Jan 31, 2006**, nominated by Reagan and reappointed by Bush Sr, Clinton and Bush Jr — i.e. he outlasted four presidents of both parties.

**Why he acted this way (analysis):** A career formed in private-sector forecasting plus Objectivist conviction → a genuine, not merely tactical, belief that markets self-regulate. That is the structural driver behind both his strengths (light touch let the 1990s expansion run) and his blind spot (he assumed banks would protect themselves). His own Oct 23, 2008 testimony — "I found a flaw… I was shocked" — is the rare case of an ideologue conceding the model failed.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction (what is genuinely durable in the legacy)
Not "new" — this is an obituary — so the question is **what of his record actually holds up**:
- **Durable:** expectations management / forward guidance as a core central-bank tool; a sustained price-stability era; the 1987 crash liquidity-backstop template (two months into his term, Black Monday, −22% Dow; he pledged liquidity). These are now standard practice — high "traction."
- **Contested:** the "Greenspan put" (2001–04 rates to ~1%), refusal to prick the dot-com bubble, and active opposition to regulating derivatives/subprime. Critics tie these to the 2007–08 crisis (which broke after he left). His famous Dec 5, 1996 "irrational exuberance" warning was prescient but un-acted-upon for 3+ years.

**Why the value-add is real-but-asymmetric (analysis):** The *technique* (managing expectations) survived and was adopted universally; the *ideology* (markets need no supervision) did not. Second-order: the post-2008 regulatory architecture — Dodd-Frank, stress tests, and even today's debates over stablecoin reserve rules and bank disclosure ([[Connecting the Dots stablecoins unlock economic growth and trade]], [[Fintech Wrap Up stablecoins move to the heart of global finance]]) — exists largely as a *correction* to the Greenspan model. His real legacy is thus dialectical: he is the thesis the modern regulatory regime was built to negate.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
No market-moving consequence (a 100-year-old retired official). The live thread is **Fed independence**: months before his death (Jan 2026), Greenspan co-signed a statement by 13 former Fed chairs/economists condemning the Trump administration's criminal investigation of Chair Jerome Powell as "an unprecedented attempt to use prosecutorial attacks to undermine [Fed] independence."

**Why this matters at second order (analysis):** Greenspan's death lands amid the most serious political pressure on Fed autonomy in a generation — current Chair Warsh "caught between Trump and the bond market" ([[Banks get more flexibility to display the FDIC Logo in digital tools]]), the Powell investigation, and a Fed mid-easing cycle ([[Federal Reserve cuts rates to 3.5-3.75% in December 2025]]). The counterintuitive effect: the obituary is being read less as nostalgia than as a *reference point in a fight over whether the Fed stays independent* — Greenspan, the apostle of a hands-off state, ended his life defending the institution's insulation from political hands. Sentiment in obituaries is "neither maestro nor villain": a measured reassessment, not the pre-2008 hagiography.
