Experian invests in Resistant AI to address APP fraud
Sinziana Albu
24 Jun 2026 / 5 Min Read
Experian has made a strategic investment in Resistant AI to launch an APP fraud prevention solution for the UK financial services sector.
The collaboration brings together Experian's data and technology capabilities with Resistant AI's AI modelling and anomaly detection tools, with the stated aim of strengthening the fraud and financial crime solutions available to financial institutions in the UK.
The partnership's first initiative will be an authorised push payment (APP) fraud prevention solution designed specifically for the UK market. According to Experian's Fraud and FinCrime report, APP fraud is the most commonly reported fraud type among UK businesses, identified by 47% of respondents as the primary threat they encounter.
Addressing a cross-disciplinary fraud challenge
APP fraud operates at the boundary between fraud and anti-money laundering (AML), exploiting money-mule networks and real-time cross-bank transfers to evade detection. Addressing it at scale requires visibility into transaction counterparties across multiple institutions, a process made more complex by existing privacy and data-sharing regulations.
In addition, the joint solution is expected to draw on Resistant AI's models to detect fraud and money laundering behaviours in real time. It is designed to complement existing transaction monitoring systems, enabling businesses to identify and stop suspicious transactions before they are processed. The product is also intended to surface previously unknown attack methods without disrupting current processes or requiring costly transformation programmes.
 David Bates , Managing Director of Credit and Verification Services at Experian UK&I, noted that the growing complexity of financial crime requires financial institutions to adopt technologies that improve their visibility and responsiveness. He stated that the partnership is aimed at helping institutions address sophisticated fraud and money laundering techniques while protecting both their operations and their customers.
 Martin Rehak , CEO of Resistant AI, pointed to the growing use of AI by financial criminals to scale attacks across a large number of victims, and noted the convergence of fraud and AML as a critical challenge for the sector. He stated that APP fraud can transition into money laundering in under five seconds, rendering traditional rule-based detection engines increasingly ineffective. It was also added that integrating AI into financial crime prevention is no longer optional, and that the joint solution is designed to combine broad data coverage with real-time prevention capabilities.
The investment reflects a broader industry shift in fraud prevention, driven by the increasing inadequacy of rules-based detection against AI-enabled criminal techniques. With APP fraud representing the most prevalent fraud type among UK businesses, the solution addresses a specific and documented gap in the market.

# Context-enrichment: Experian invests in Resistant AI to address APP fraud
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **This is a recycled announcement, not news.** The Paypers item (24 Jun 2026) describes Experian's strategic investment in Resistant AI and a "first initiative" APP-fraud solution for the UK — but Experian first announced this exact investment + APP-fraud plan on **22 July 2025** ([[Experian invests in fraud detection startup Resistant AI]], story s72c1975b). The 2026-06 text is near-verbatim to the July 2025 press release: same quotes (David Bates, Martin Rehak), same "47% of UK businesses cite APP fraud" stat, same "APP fraud can morph into money laundering in under 5 seconds." **The Paypers re-ran an 11-month-old story.** (The Paypers article itself is from Jun 2026; the underlying event is from Jul 2025.)
- **The actual product already went LIVE — and the news note misses it.** On **22 April 2026** Experian launched the joint product, **"Transaction Forensics"**, the first commercial output of the partnership (Experian PR, via fintech.global / Crowdfund Insider / City AM). The note's framing ("the partnership's first initiative WILL be…", "available later this year") is stale: the thing is shipping. → *Why this matters:* the genuinely citable, value-bearing fact (a live product with pilot metrics) sits OUTSIDE this note's source; the note carries only the year-old PR shell.
- **De-PR'd reality of "investment":** the equity piece was a strategic minority investment by Experian, later formalised when Resistant AI raised a **$25M (€21.6M) Series B in Oct 2025** led by DTCP, with Experian, GV and Notion Capital participating ([[Mastercard unveils Payment Optimization Platform to boost approval rates]] mentions the $25M raise). So "Experian invests" = corporate-VC stake + commercial JV, not an acquisition. Stake size / valuation undisclosed.
- **Why structured this way:** a credit bureau buying a small equity stake + co-building a product (rather than acquiring) lets Experian (a) bolt AI transaction-monitoring onto its UK fraud stack without M&A integration cost, and (b) keep optionality. The PR anchors to the flattering "47% = #1 fraud type" stat from Experian's own Fraud & FinCrime report to manufacture urgency.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Direct AI-native AML/fraud transaction-monitoring vendors:** Unit21 (rebuilt around configurable AI agents for the full fraud+AML lifecycle, [[Fintech Wrap Up Unit21 CEO on AI agents fighting financial crime]], [[Fintech Wrap Up why Unit21 rebuilt around AI agents]]); FIS + Anthropic AI agent for financial crime ([[FIS and Anthropic build AI agent to fight financial crime]], May 2026); ACI Worldwide on APP scams ([[Connecting the Dots ACI on tackling APP scams, part 1]]). Feature (Featurespace/Visa), NICE Actimize, ThreatMark and Hawk AI are the broader peer set (web).
- **Bank-side / point-solutions:** Starling Bank's customer-facing AI scam-spotting tool ([[Starling Bank launches AI tool for customers to spot purchase scams]], Oct 2025) attacks the same APP problem from the consumer end.
- **Experian's own adjacent moves:** it acquired KYC360 for UK fraud/financial-crime ([[Experian acquires KYC360 to boost fraud and financial crime solutions in the UK]], Oct 2025) and partnered Plaid on Plaid Protect ([[Plaid partners Experian, launches Plaid Protect fraud tool]], Jun 2025). → Experian is **stacking** fraud assets (Resistant AI + KYC360 + Plaid) into a UK financial-crime suite.
- **Position:** parity-to-catching-up. Resistant AI's edge is its "AI upgrade layer on top of existing rules engines" (80+ off-the-shelf models, no rip-and-replace) — a real differentiator vs full-platform replacers like Unit21. **+ Why:** the moat is distribution, not algorithm — Experian's proprietary UK consumer/commercial data + bureau relationships are what make the joint model better than Resistant AI standalone. The second-order dynamic: AI-native upgrade layers commoditise the detection model; the durable value migrates to whoever owns the data and the bank relationship — which is Experian, hence the equity stake.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Experian (FTSE 100, LSE: EXPN):** FY26 (to Mar 2026) total revenue +13%, organic +8% (top of guidance), Benchmark EPS +15%, +60bps margin (Experian FY26 results). It is mid-pivot from a pure credit bureau into a data+software+AI platform: ChatGPT marketplace integration, Snapchat financial-literacy AI ([[Experian and Snapchat partner on financial literacy for young users]]), Agent Operating System on Ascend ([[Experian launches Agent Operating System on Ascend platform]], Jun 2026), and a steady drumbeat of fraud assets (KYC360, Plaid, Resistant AI). Also publishes the 2026 Future of Fraud Forecast ([[This lines up with Experian's 2026 Future of Fraud Forecast, which estimates]]).
- **Resistant AI:** Czech-founded (2019, Prague/Nové Město), document-fraud + transaction-monitoring "AI upgrade for rules-based systems"; 80+ models, 150M+ documents verified; ARR up 10x since Series A, customer base 4x, **breakeven Sep 2025**; total funding $55.4M. Customers: Anna Money, Bank of Valletta, Moneta Money Bank, Finom.
- **Why Experian acts this way:** a credit-bureau take-rate is a slow-growth utility business; to defend its multiple Experian needs higher-growth software + AI lines. Fraud/financial-crime is a regulatory-forced spend category (PSR mandatory reimbursement) → reliable demand. Bolting on Resistant AI's models is a cheap way to own a fast-growing fraud-software line without building anomaly-detection from scratch. Logical fit, low risk.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty of THIS note: ~zero.** It is the re-issue of a July 2025 announcement. Nothing here is new as of June 2026.
- **Real value-add lives in the product (Transaction Forensics, Apr 2026 — outside this note):** pilot metrics of **+200% APP-fraud detection, −80% false positives, −50% total alert volume**; supports Faster Payments, BACS, CHAPS; covers APP fraud + money-mule + money-laundering at transaction/customer/company level. These are vendor-reported pilot numbers (not independently audited live results) — treat as "claimed," not proven at scale. (analysis)
- **Traction proof points that ARE real:** Resistant AI breakeven Sep 2025, ARR 10x, DTCP-led $25M Series B, and a shipped product with named payment-rail coverage. → unlike a pure "we plan to" PR, the underlying partnership has actually produced a live product and a financed company.
- **Who captures the margin:** Experian. The model is good because of Experian's data + bank distribution; Resistant AI supplies the engine but Experian owns the customer and the recurring software revenue. The equity stake + JV structure ensures Experian, not Resistant AI, banks the multiple. **+ What breaks it:** if banks standardise on full-platform replacers (Unit21 / FIS-Anthropic agentic stacks) rather than "upgrade-your-rules-engine" layers, the Resistant AI approach gets disintermediated.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **More products in 2026.** Experian PR says further Resistant AI joint products will launch across 2026; Transaction Forensics is just product #1.
- **Regulatory tailwind is the whole story.** UK PSR mandatory APP-scam reimbursement (live Oct 2024, up to £85k, 5-day payout, **50/50 sending/receiving PSP liability**) forced banks to eat scam losses — in year one (Oct 2024–Sep 2025) 88% (£173m) of in-scope losses reimbursed ([[PSR reports APP fraud reimbursement covers 88% of in-scope claims]]). UK Finance: £629.3m stolen H1 2025 (+3% YoY), 2.09m cases (+17%), 66% of APP fraud starts online ([[UK Finance reports 629 million pounds in fraud losses in H1 2025]]). → **Why the market goes this way:** reimbursement liability turns fraud-prevention from a cost centre into a P&L-defending must-buy, especially for receiving PSPs now on the hook — exactly the cross-bank-visibility problem Transaction Forensics targets.
- **Counterintuitive second-order effect:** because liability is now 50/50 and shared, the value of *cross-institution* visibility (which no single bank has) rises sharply — favouring data-network owners (Experian, Plaid, Mastercard/Featurespace) over single-bank tools. The note's "privacy/data-sharing complicates counterparty visibility" line is the real strategic crux, understated in the PR.
- **Sentiment:** neutral-positive but immaterial as a single-day item — this is a year-old story re-surfaced.
