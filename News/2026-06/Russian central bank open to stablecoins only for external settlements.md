иностранных стейблкоинов на национальном уровне. Две трети юрисдикций уже ввели такое

# РЕЗЮМЕ 

1.  Использование стейблкоинов в мире продолжает расширяться. При этом скорость 

такого расширения и его последствия различаются в зависимости от трех основных 

направлений использования стейблкоинов. 

1.1.  Средство расчетов в криптосфере 

Это исторически первое направление использования стейблкоинов, в рамках которого 

используется большая часть выпущенных в настоящее время стейблкоинов 1.

Преимущества стейблкоинов  при  данном использовании связаны с большей ликвидностью 

и меньшей волатильностью их стоимости по сравнению с криптовалютами, а также 

с их обращением в тех же открытых сетях, в которых обращаются криптовалюты. 

Риски такого использования стейблкоинов связаны с возможной потерей ликвидности 

и обесценением стейблкоинов, а также с используемыми  при  их обращении технологиями 

(например, возможные ошибки и взломы смарт ‑контрактов, утрата доступа  при  потере 

ключей). Также такому использованию стейблкоинов свойственны и общие риски, связанные 

с введением пользователей в заблуждение, мошенничеством, и другие вопросы защиты 

инвесторов. 

1.2.  Средство трансграничных расчетов 

Это динамически развивающееся направление использования стейблкоинов, 

востребованность и потенциал которого растут. 

Преимущества стейблкоинов в трансграничных расчетах связаны с относительно высокой 

скоростью расчетов  при  сравнительно меньших комиссиях, окончательностью расчетов 

и легкостью доступа, так  как  такие расчеты, в отличие от расчетов в резервных валютах, 

не требуют участия посредников из третьих стран. 

Риски такого использования стейблкоинов повторяют риски их использования в качестве 

средства расчетов в криптосфере, включая санкционные риски, так  как  эмитенты 

стейблкоинов,  как  правило, могут изымать их у законных владельцев, в том числе ссылаясь 

на односторонние ограничения,  без  решения суда. 

1.3.  Средство внутренних расчетов 

Это наиболее редкое направление использования стейблкоинов. Его востребованность 

ограничивается, с одной стороны, распространенностью платежей в национальных 

валютах, а с другой – угрозой долларизации, которую несет использование иностранных 

стейблкоинов во внутренних расчетах. 

Преимущества использования стейблкоинов, привязанных к национальной валюте, 

во внутренних расчетах неочевидны, так  как  пользователи могут использовать  для  расчетов 

саму национальную валюту, не принимая дополнительный риск на эмитента стейблкоина.

юрисдикций уже ввели такое регулирование или находятся в процессе его создания. При 

этом, как правило, страны регулируют выпуск стейблкоинов и требования к их эмитентам, 

устанавливая специальные усиленные требования или ограничивая использование

но не ограничивает выпуск и использование для инвестиций и в трансграничных расчетах 

цифровых финансовых активов (ЦФА) с характеристиками стейблкоинов. Проектируемое 

комплексное регулирование обращения криптовалют и цифровых прав в России 3

также предусматривает возможность использования ЦФА и иностранных стейблкоинов

во внутренних расчетах в России запрещено. 

4.  На практике выпуски ЦФА с характеристиками, аналогичными стейблкоинам, 

до настоящего времени не осуществлялись. Вместе с тем участники рынка предлагают 

обсудить возможную необходимость введения специального регулирования выпуска 

стейблкоинов в России, чтобы учесть особенность таких инструментов и повысить 

их востребованность в трансграничных расчетах. 

5.  Специальное регулирование выпуска стейблкоинов в России может предусматривать 

требования к достаточности и качеству обеспечения стейблкоинов, требования 

к их погашению по номиналу и сроку такого погашения, требования к финансовой 

устойчивости эмитента стейблкоинов и иные требования. При этом требует 

обсуждения вопрос о необходимости установления указанных требований в условиях 

невостребованности текущей возможности выпускать ЦФА с аналогичными 

характеристиками.

# Context-enrichment: Russian central bank open to stablecoins only for external settlements
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)

The trigger is **not a speech but a document**: a **Bank of Russia (CBR) consultation paper "Стейблкоины: направления развития в России" (Stablecoins: directions of development in Russia), dated 25.06.2026**, open for public comment until **01.09.2026** (`Consultation_Paper_25062026_52.pdf`, cbr.ru). The "Финтехно" channel's framing ("ЦБ готов обсуждать российские стейблкоины как инструмент для внешних расчётов, но не хочет пускать их во внутренние платежи") is an accurate de-PR of the paper's own résumé.

What the paper actually says (primary-source text, not PR):
- Russian law has **no concept of "stablecoin"**, but does **not prohibit** issuing/using ЦФА (digital financial assets) with stablecoin-like characteristics **for investment and cross-border settlements**. Domestic-payment use of such instruments **is already prohibited** (§3, §6).
- **In practice, no ЦФА with stablecoin characteristics have ever been issued in Russia** (§4) — so this is regulation of a market that does not yet exist domestically. The CBR explicitly questions whether special rules are even needed "in conditions where the current ability to issue analogous ЦФА is unclaimed" (§5).
- The paper's recommendation (§7): **discuss special regulation for issuing stablecoins specifically to enable cross-border settlements**, while keeping the **domestic ban** — analysis "found no significant advantages" to domestic stablecoin use absent regulatory arbitrage, and lifting the ban is deemed "unjustified" given monetary-fragmentation risk (§6).

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** This is a **monetary-sovereignty document, not a crypto-friendly one.** The CBR's logic ladder: USD-pegged coins are 89% of the market (USDT+USDC) → dollar stablecoins threaten "долларизация" and the monetary transmission mechanism → therefore domestic use is dangerous, but a *Russian* cross-border instrument is useful to "не требуют участия посредников из третьих стран" (avoid third-country intermediaries) i.e. sanctioned correspondent banking. The "openness" is **narrowly instrumental and sanctions-driven**, not a liberalization. It is a *consultation* (comments to 01.09.2026), so even the cross-border framework is **proposed, not law**.

**Skeptical correction to the headline frame:** this position is **not new.** Governor Nabiullina stated the identical "yes for international settlements, no for domestic" line at the **State Duma on 30.10.2025** ("I will *reiterate* our position…", TASS), and it sits on a draft federal law **№1194918-8 "О цифровых валютах и цифровых правах"** (cited in the paper's footnote 3) that passed first reading **21.04.2026 (327–13)**. The June paper **operationalizes a year-old stance** into a specific stablecoin-rulemaking consultation.

Direct peers are **other central banks setting cross-border crypto/stablecoin policy** — and the contrast is sharp:
- **Brazil (02.05.2026):** central bank **banned** stablecoin/crypto settlement in cross-border payments for fintechs/PSPs (effective 01.10.2026) — exact-opposite stance on the same use case [[Brazil's central bank bans crypto settlement in cross-border payments]].
- **Russia's own prior move (10.2025):** MinFin + CBR agreed to legalize crypto for foreign-trade payments — the policy this paper now extends to stablecoins specifically [[Russia to legalize crypto for cross-border trade payments]].
- **US GENIUS Act (signed 18.07.2025):** federal payment-stablecoin framework, pro-issuance, **dollar-reinforcing** — strategically the antithesis of Russia's de-dollarization aim.
- **Hong Kong / EU MiCA / UAE / Japan:** the paper itself cites these as jurisdictions that regulate issuance and *restrict domestic use* — Russia explicitly models its "restrict internal use" approach on them (§2) [[Hong Kong plans first stablecoin licenses by March]].
- **Private/market peers** building the cross-border-settlement rail Russia wants a sanctioned version of: [[Anchorage Digital launches stablecoin settlement for banks]], [[ARP Digital and BBK partner on cross-border stablecoin settlement]], [[Coinbase partners with Standard Chartered for multi-currency fiat rails]].

**Why the lay of the land is this way (2nd-order):** Western and Western-aligned regulators (US, Brazil) are converging on *blocking* crypto from settlement rails or channeling it into *dollar*-reinforcing issuance. Russia sits in the inverse quadrant: it needs a **non-dollar, sanction-resistant cross-border rail**, so it permits exactly what Brazil bans. The peer split is not technical — it is geopolitical alignment.

## [2] Company history / fit

CBR trajectory: ELR enabling law (01.09.2024) → crypto **mining legalized** (11.2024) → foreign-trade crypto-settlement pilot (09.2025) → **digital ruble live for federal government** (01.01.2026), retail rollout scheduled 01.09.2026 (twice delayed) → CBR to **study a national stablecoin in 2026** (Chistyukhin, 19.02.2026) [[Bank of Russia to explore feasibility of a national stablecoin]] → this consultation paper (25.06.2026).

**Why the CBR acts this way:** structurally it is a **monetary-sovereignty hawk** running two parallel tracks: (1) the **digital ruble** as the sovereign *domestic* instrument (a CBR liability), and (2) **private cross-border stablecoins** as a *foreign-trade* tool it does not want competing with the ruble at home. The paper fits this logic perfectly — it carves stablecoins into the cross-border lane only, protecting the digital ruble's domestic monopoly. Adjacent context: a **ruble-backed stablecoin (A7A5)** already exists privately (state-linked Promsvyazbank 49%), but it is OFAC/EU-sanctioned and is **not** the CBR's instrument [[Sanctioned Russian crypto network expands across Africa]].

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction

**What is genuinely new:** a formal CBR rulemaking consultation on a **stablecoin-specific** issuance framework (reserve adequacy, redemption-at-par, issuer solvency requirements — §5, Appendix 5). What is **not** new: the underlying "external-only" policy (Oct 2025) and the cross-border-crypto enabling law (draft, first reading Apr 2026).

**Traction — near-zero domestically, by the CBR's own admission:** "выпуски ЦФА с характеристиками… стейблкоинов до настоящего времени не осуществлялись" (no stablecoin-like ЦФА have ever been issued). So this regulates a **non-existent domestic market**, and the cross-border corridor's actual volumes, participant list, and asset mix are **not public**. The only high-volume Russian-linked stablecoin (A7A5, >$100B cumulative on-chain per Elliptic) operates **outside** this regime and saw daily volume fall ~$1.5B→~$500M after OFAC/EU sanctions (Aug/Oct 2025) — evidence that the cross-border value-add is **real but sanctions-capped**.

**Who captures the value (2nd-order):** for a Russian cross-border stablecoin to matter, *foreign* counterparties, exchanges, and custodians must accept it — exactly the chokepoint OFAC/EU used to throttle A7A5. So the binding constraint is **not Russian regulation but foreign willingness to touch the asset.** The consultation can build a pristine domestic framework and still produce no usable cross-border rail if the foreign leg is sanctioned. The real question shifts from "will Russia regulate stablecoins?" to **"can any Russian-controlled stablecoin survive foreign-side sanctions enforcement?"**

## [4] What's next / market sentiment

Path: comments to **01.09.2026** → CBR position paper → feeds the **2nd/3rd readings of law №1194918-8** and a **separate MinFin stablecoin bill** (both targeting ~01.07.2026 effect, now likely slipping) → CBR national-stablecoin feasibility findings. Parallel: **retail digital ruble** scheduled 01.09.2026 (watch for a third delay); deputy FinMin Chebeskov (09.06.2026) floated **fees/limits on "unfriendly" coins USDT/USDC/BNB** (~0.5–3%) — a *tightening* that would push usage toward "friendly"/ruble-backed coins.

**Why the market goes this way / counterintuitive 2nd-order:** The CBR is deliberately engineering a **two-tier digital-money system** — sovereign digital ruble at home, sanctioned-resistant private stablecoins abroad. The counterintuitive effect: by **fee-penalizing the most liquid global stablecoins** (USDT/USDC) precisely to favor domestic alternatives, Russia raises the cost and lowers the liquidity of its *own* cross-border rail (foreign partners want USDT, not a ruble coin), potentially **defeating the trade-facilitation goal it is pursuing.** Sanctions risk makes the strategy fragile, not safe.

## Red-team / second-order questions

1. **Is this a new policy or a recirculation?** → **Recirculation + operationalization.** The "external-only" stance dates to Nabiullina's Duma remarks of 30.10.2025 ("I will *reiterate* our position"). What is new is the 25.06.2026 *consultation paper* turning it into stablecoin-specific rulemaking. Not a U-turn.

2. **Announced or live?** → **Consultation only.** Comments accepted to 01.09.2026; the enabling law (№1194918-8) has passed only first reading (21.04.2026). Nothing is in force.

3. **Does a domestic Russian stablecoin market actually exist?** → **No.** The paper itself states no stablecoin-like ЦФА have ever been issued in Russia. This regulates a non-existent market.

4. **What is the precise mechanism delta vs the digital ruble?** → Digital ruble = CBR liability, sovereign, domestic + BRICS rails. Stablecoin = private/credit-institution issued, reserve-backed, **cross-border only**. The paper deliberately keeps them in separate lanes to protect the ruble's domestic monopoly.

5. **Is "external only" really about sanctions evasion?** → **Yes, stated openly.** The paper's rationale ("no third-country intermediaries") and Duma deputies' framing ("bypassing sanctions restrictions") make the trade/sanctions motive explicit.

6. **Can any Russian-controlled stablecoin survive foreign-side enforcement?** → **Open — the decisive question.** A7A5 (>$100B cumulative volume) shows it CAN scale but volume fell ~3x after OFAC/EU sanctions. Foreign acceptance, not Russian law, is the binding constraint.

7. **Will the proposed USDT/USDC/BNB fees (0.5–3%) help or hurt the corridor?** → **Likely hurt (2nd-order).** Foreign trade counterparties want liquid USD coins; penalizing them to favor ruble-backed coins raises friction and may defeat the trade-facilitation goal. Open whether fees are adopted.

8. **Is the contemplated national stablecoin ruble- or gold-backed?** → Ruble-pegged, reserve-backed, privately issued per Chistyukhin (19.02.2026). **Gold-backing: unconfirmed** — no source.

9. **Was the June news said at SPIEF 2026 or a press conference?** → **No — it is a written consultation paper (25.06.2026), not a speech.** SPIEF venue: unconfirmed/unsupported.

10. **Who is silent about what?** → The paper gives **no cross-border corridor volumes, participant list, or asset mix** — the data that would prove traction. It also avoids naming A7A5 (the sanctioned state-linked coin that actually moves volume).

11. **What special requirements are proposed?** → Reserve adequacy/quality, redemption-at-par and timing, issuer financial soundness; for any *domestic* use — issuance only by credit institutions with reserving (Appendix 5, §6). Domestic use deemed "unjustified."

12. **Does this contradict any "domestic ruble stablecoin launch" headlines?** → **Yes** — any "2026 domestic stablecoin launch" claim contradicts the CBR's stated domestic ban; treat as low-confidence.

13. **How does this compare to peers' direction of travel?** → **Diverging.** Brazil bans this exact cross-border use (02.05.2026); US GENIUS Act builds dollar-reinforcing issuance; Russia uniquely frames stablecoins as a state-controlled sanctions-resistant trade tool.

14. **Could the consultation just be a stalling/box-ticking exercise?** → **Open.** Given zero domestic issuance and the paper's own "advantages unclear" language, plausible it formalizes the status quo rather than enabling a real market near-term.

15. **What would raise importance above 3/5?** → A binding law adoption, disclosed corridor volumes, or a foreign jurisdiction agreeing to settle trade in a Russian stablecoin. None present today.

Importance: 3/5 — A genuine, well-sourced primary-document signal of Russia's monetary-sovereignty + sanctions-resistance strategy, with clear peer contrast (Brazil/US). But it is a *consultation, not law*, regulates a *non-existent domestic market*, restates an Oct-2025 stance, and shows no public traction — so it is directional context, not a market-moving event.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Global stablecoin market cap ≈ **$315–321bn** (mid-2026), up ~55% YoY (per Business Standard/IMF corpus + bitcoinfoundation.org). The relevant sub-segment here is **cross-border settlement**, not crypto trading: B2B stablecoin payments grew **+733% YoY** in 2025 to ~$390bn (Asia ~$245bn / NA ~$95bn / EU ~$50bn, per OpenFX 2026 report), yet stablecoins are still **~1% of global payment flows** — unchanged 2023→2026 despite the absolute surge. Structurally the market is **extremely consolidated and dollar-pegged**: USDT+USDC ≈ **92%** of cap; **non-dollar stablecoins are ~0.24%** of the market (~$771m, per CoinDesk 20.05.2026). The structural barrier the CBR is fighting is precisely this: non-USD coins lack the deep liquid Treasury collateral and FX liquidity that make USD coins usable abroad — so a ruble coin's "why now" (sanctions, de-dollarization, monetary-sovereignty) collides with the **why-it-can't**: foreign counterparties want the liquid dollar leg, not a ruble token (2nd-order: same trap context-enrichment flagged for the corridor's economics).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for a cross-border rail: **on-chain volume, daily settlement volume, holder/wallet count, corridor count, share of non-dollar market**. The protagonist (CBR) discloses **none** of these for any Russian instrument — it regulates a domestic market that, by its own admission, has **zero issuance**. The only Russian-controlled coin with real KPIs is **A7A5** (outside the CBR regime): >**$110bn** cumulative on-chain, ~**43% of the global non-dollar stablecoin market**, ~29,000 wallets (May 2026) — but daily volume fell **~$1.5bn → ~$500m** after sanctions (Bloomberg 22.01.2026 / Elliptic / CertiK). Recent regulatory peer moves on the **same use case**: Brazil **banned** crypto/stablecoin cross-border settlement (02.05.2026, effect 01.10.2026) [[Brazil's central bank bans crypto settlement in cross-border payments]]; **EU 20th package** (adopted 23.04.2026, applies **24.05.2026**) blanket-banned all Russian/Belarusian crypto providers and — notably — added **the digital ruble and RUBx to the banned-asset list (Annex LIII)** alongside A7A5 (Elliptic/TRM/Morgan Lewis). Protagonist position: **niche and structurally cornered** — building rules for a non-existent domestic market while its only working cross-border instrument is sanctioned and its sovereign instrument (digital ruble) is now itself EU-blacklisted `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round/deal attaches to a central-bank consultation, so trading multiples = **not applicable / no data**. Internal comps frame the *market the CBR is regulating around*, not a target to value: [[Stablecoin market crosses $300 billion]] (sector size), [[IMF top two stablecoins reach combined $260bn market cap]] (USD concentration), [[Trace Finance raises $32 million Series A led by CoinFund]] ($10bn+ cross-border volume, regulated EM↔US stablecoin infra — the *permitted* version of what Russia wants), [[Coinbase partners with Standard Chartered for multi-currency fiat rails]] and [[Noah and Nala launch instant stablecoin settlement network]] (Western-aligned cross-border rails). Sanity note: A7A5's ~43% non-dollar share is an **outlier driven by a sanctions corridor, not organic demand** — a flag to read down, not a valuation signal. EV/Revenue, P/S etc. → no data (no issuer financials, no priced entity).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Foreign-leg sanctions are the binding constraint, not Russian rulemaking** — the EU 20th package now blacklists even the *digital ruble* (24.05.2026), so the CBR's two intended rails (private cross-border stablecoin + sovereign CBDC) are *both* foreign-blocked; a pristine domestic framework can still yield no usable corridor (2nd-order: regulation cannot buy foreign acceptance).
2. **Liquidity/structure works against a ruble coin** — non-dollar = 0.24% of a 92%-USD market; penalizing USDT/USDC (proposed 0.5–3% fees, Chebeskov 09.06.2026) raises the cost of the *very liquidity* foreign trade partners want, plausibly defeating the trade-facilitation goal.
3. **Regulating a non-existent market = execution/relevance risk** — zero domestic issuance + the CBR's own "advantages unclear" language means this may formalize the status quo rather than enable a real rail near-term; importance stays directional, not market-moving.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This sharpens the **global regulatory divergence** into a clean three-way split: US (GENIUS Act) builds dollar-reinforcing issuance, Brazil/EU **block** crypto from settlement rails, Russia uniquely tries to engineer a **state-controlled sanctions-resistant** ruble rail — and the EU's move to blacklist the digital ruble itself shows the West is now pre-emptively closing that lane. Falsifiable thesis: *the cross-border ambition fails on the foreign leg regardless of domestic rules.* **Trigger that would break it:** a non-aligned jurisdiction (e.g. a BRICS/EAEU trade partner) publicly agreeing to settle real trade volume in a Russian-controlled stablecoin, with disclosed corridor volumes — none exists today. Watch: comments close 01.09.2026 → 2nd/3rd readings of law №1194918-8; retail digital-ruble launch (01.09.2026, watch a third delay).
