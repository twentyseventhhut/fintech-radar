## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On ~**2026-06-23/24**, Yape (Credicorp/BCP's Peruvian super-app) added **"Yape Bolivia"** as a remittance destination — wallet-to-wallet sends from Peru to its sister app in Bolivia. This makes Bolivia Yape's **9th outbound corridor**, after the Feb–May 2026 push (Colombia→Nequi, then Argentina/Chile/Mexico/Italy = 8th in [[Peru's Yape expands remittances to Argentina and Chile]]).

- **Rails (the key de-PR'd fact):** this is a **closed-loop, intra-group, app-to-app** corridor — Yape Peru → Yape Bolivia, BOTH owned by Credicorp/BCP (Yape Bolivia is the former BCP-Bolivia "Soli/Soli Pagos", rebranded 2023). **No external partner** (unlike Colombia via Nequi, Argentina via Mercado Pago). It works only when sender AND receiver are both on Yape. → Why this matters: it's ecosystem lock-in, not an open remittance network — it lights up demand Yape already owns on both ends rather than acquiring a new corridor.
- **Limit — corpus vs reality conflict (flag):** the aggregated note text says **S/500/day**. External sources (Infobae, La República, 2026-06-23) report the standard Yape outbound caps of **S/3,000/op, S/3,500/day, S/12,000/month** (~US$945/day) — identical to other Yape corridors. The "S/500/day" in the digest appears to be an error; treat S/3,500/day as the verified figure. Either way it is a small-ticket retail rail, commission-free (FX margin unstated).
- **Scale, de-PR'd:** Yape's OWN projection is only **~4,000 remittances/month** year one — deliberately marginal. The "S/75M+ sent" PR figure is **cumulative across ALL corridors to date**, not Bolivia-specific.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Peru→Bolivia corridor:** thinly served — Western Union/MoneyGram agents, banks, informal cash. Yape ([[Peru's Yape partners with Western Union on remittances]]) is effectively building the first wallet-to-wallet rail here purely because it owns apps on both ends.
- **Peru wallets:** Plin (BCP-consortium, interoperable with Yape), Bim (unbanked focus), Izipay Ya. Yape+Plin ≈ ~90% of in-person wallet txns.
- **Alt cross-border value rails:** [[Bybit launches Bybit Pay in Peru via Yape and Plin]] (stablecoins→soles, Jan 2026) competes for the same cross-border-value user on cost/speed.
- **Prior outbound corridors via 3rd-party rails:** [[Nequi integrates with Yape for Peru-Colombia transfers]] (Feb 2026). → Why intra-group here but partners elsewhere: BCP already owns a Bolivia bank/app, so no partner needed (analysis).

## [2] Company history / fit
- 2017 Yape launches (Peru). **Aug 2023** enters Bolivia (rebrands Soli, ~800K users) — first intl market. Oct 2024 TerraPay (inbound). ~2024 breakeven >12.3M users. Aug 2025 Western Union (inbound). Jan 2026 Bybit Pay. Feb 2026 Colombia outbound. May 2026 Argentina/Chile/Mexico/Italy. **Jun 2026 Bolivia** — roughly one new corridor every 1–2 months in 2026.
- **Fit:** logical — Bolivia is the one foreign market where Yape owns the receiving app (>4M users by mid-2026, up from 800K). Cross-corridor expansion monetizes existing two-sided footprint. ([[EBANX integrates Yape for cross-border merchant payments]] shows the parallel cross-border-merchant push.)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Yape scale (Credicorp Q1 2026):** ~**19.4M users** (+8% YoY), ~16.4M MAU, revenue +101% YoY, operating profit ~S/205M (~US$59M), profitable. ~57% of Peru's ~34M population. Yape Bolivia >4M users.
- **What's genuinely new:** first **own-app, both-ends closed-loop** corridor in Yape's remittance set — cheaper/faster (no intermediary) but limited to the Yape↔Yape graph. **Traction is unproven and the company's own target (~4k/month) is tiny.** This is a feature launch, not a corridor win.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Expect more outbound corridors on the ~monthly cadence; deeper Yape Bolivia monetization (credit/payments, Peru playbook); crypto rails (Bybit) as parallel channel. Credicorp (Ferrari) frames a regional super-app + financial-inclusion strategy.
- **Skeptic's central question:** Peru→Bolivia is NOT a major remittance lane (Bolivia's inbound flows come mostly from Spain/Chile/US/Brazil/Argentina; the big Peru flows are INBOUND US→Peru). So the real value is **ecosystem lock-in across the BCP-owned LatAm footprint**, not corridor economics. Bolivia's FX/dollar-scarcity (parallel rate, capital controls) is an unmodeled risk for a soles→boliviano send.

## Freshness verdict
**FRESH.** Genuinely new corridor (Bolivia, 9th destination, intra-group rails) — NOT a re-run of [[Peru's Yape expands remittances to Argentina and Chile]] (8th, May 2026, different countries/partners). Same multi-month expansion campaign, but a distinct new event.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Intra-LatAm digital-wallet remittances — wallet-to-wallet transfers replacing cash/agent rails. LatAm cross-border payments generated ~$34.6bn in revenue in 2024, tracking to ~$52.7bn by 2030 at ~7.3% CAGR (per TransFi/secondary citation, via globenewswire, 2026-05) — note: that is the broad cross-border revenue pool, not the Peru→Bolivia retail-remittance corridor specifically, for which there is no public sizing ("no data"). Structure: fragmented, with three competing stacks — incumbent MTOs (Western Union, MoneyGram, Ria, Remitly), wallet/super-app rails (Yape, Mercado Pago, Nequi), and stablecoin entrants (dLocal+Félix, El Dorado, MoneyGram-Crossmint). "Why now": Yape already ran a Western Union receive-side alliance in Peru (550k users, usage tripled YoY, top sources US/Chile/Spain/Italy, per Yape via businesswire, 2025-09) and in 2026-05 added Argentina/Chile/Mexico/Italy send corridors (8 destinations, Argentina landing in Mercado Pago). The Bolivia leg is distinctive because Yape operates on BOTH ends — Peru (16.4M MAU Q1 2026, ~82% of EAP) and Yape Bolivia (3M+ users Q1 2025, replaced Soli Pagos, targeting 5.5M in 2026 and ~50% of Bolivia's population by 2028, per The Fintech Times/Financial IT). Closed-loop wallet-to-wallet, not agent network.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: corridor TPV, active senders/receivers, take rate per transfer, FX spread captured. Players & basis of competition: Western Union/MoneyGram/Ria/Remitly compete on reach + agent density; Mercado Pago/Nequi/Yape on owned distribution and zero/low fee in-app UX; stablecoin players (dLocal+Félix, El Dorado) on speed/cost in "underserved corridors." Recent moves: Yape↔Western Union receive (2025-09); Nequi↔Yape Peru-Colombia transfers (2026-02); Yape +4 destinations (2026-05); now Peru→Yape Bolivia (2026-06). Protagonist position: **ahead** on this specific corridor because it controls both wallets (no third-party payout rail, no FX-counterparty needed on the Bolivia side if internal) — a closed-loop moat from network effects + owning distribution on both ends. The S/500/day cap (~$130) signals retail/P2P family flows, not merchant or high-value remittance — economics per transfer undisclosed `[UNSOURCED]`.

**Comps & multiples.** Parent Credicorp (NYSE: BAP) — market cap ~$29.1bn (per public.com, Jan 2026); forward P/E ~12.4x, trailing P/E ~15.9x; ROE 19.0% FY2025, record 21.1% Q1 2026 (per BAP IR/Yahoo Finance). Yape is not separately listed; it broke even in 2024 (monthly rev/user S/6.5 vs cost S/5.3), reached 7.2% of Credicorp's risk-adjusted revenue in Q4 2025 (doubled YoY), with payments ~52% and lending ~23% of Yape revenue (per Credicorp Q4 2025). Internal comps (corridor/wallet-remittance): [[El Dorado raises $9 million led by Paradigm for LatAm stablecoin payments]] (round, not market cap), [[dLocal and Félix launch stablecoin WhatsApp remittances in LatAm]], [[MoneyGram launches stablecoin remittance app in Colombia]], [[Rappi and AstroPay launch Latin America’s first super-app wallet integration to pay]]. Distribution not computed — comps are private rounds/product launches, not a comparable multiple set (qualitative only). Credicorp's ~12x fwd P/E at ~20% ROE is in-line-to-cheap for a high-ROE EM bank; Yape upside is embedded, not separately re-rated.

**Risk flags.** (1) **Bolivia FX crisis is the dominant risk.** Official rate frozen at 6.96 BOB/USD since 2011 vs parallel ~9–15 BOB/USD; central-bank liquid reserves fell to ~$30M mid-2025; Fitch CCC-, Moody's Ca (per CEIC/Marketplace/IMF, 2025–26). A closed-loop S/-denominated transfer into a boliviano wallet exposes users to a parallel-market gap and convertibility/settlement risk — the "send" headline hides WHO bears the FX conversion and at WHICH rate (de-PR: silent on FX mechanics). (2) **Regulatory/AML** on a new cross-border corridor into a sanctioned-risk, capital-control jurisdiction — Bolivia's FX rationing can trap or delay settlement. (3) **Disintermediation/scale** — corridor is structurally small (S/500/day cap) and Bolivia user base (3M) is early; revenue contribution to Credicorp is immaterial near-term, so this is strategic optionality, not a P&L event.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is the first corridor where a single super-app owns both endpoints, turning remittances into an internal ledger entry rather than an MTO/FX transaction — if it works, it's a template to disintermediate Western Union/Ria on owned-wallet pairs across the Andes. Falsifiable thesis: closed-loop economics let Yape undercut MTO fees on the corridor. What breaks it: Bolivia's parallel-rate gap forces Yape to either eat FX losses or price at the parallel rate (killing the cost advantage), OR central-bank FX rationing blocks settlement. Trigger to watch: disclosed corridor TPV/fee and whether transfers settle at official vs parallel BOB.
