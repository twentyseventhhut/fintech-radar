NEW YORK , June 24, 2026 /PRNewswire/ --  Trovy , the consumer fintech replacing high-interest debt with home equity-powered financing, today announced a $15 million Series A led by   Left Lane Capital , bringing total funding to $25 million. Existing seed investors Kleiner Perkins , DCM Ventures , and Camber Creek also participated. The funding will accelerate Trovy's national expansion, deepen its product platform, and grow the team building the definitive financial home base for America's 85 million homeowners.
 Homeowners Are Sitting on a Fortune – And Needlessly Paying a Fortune in Interest 
Household debt has reached record levels, with nonmortgage consumer debt in the United States exceeding $5 trillion, costing Americans an estimated $550 billion in interest annually, much of it at APRs above 20%. 1  For the roughly 85 million homeowners sitting on trillions of dollars in untapped home equity, a far cheaper source of capital is hiding in plain sight.
Trovy was built to close that gap by giving homeowners a smarter, faster way to put their equity to work and dramatically reduce their borrowing costs. Its flagship product is a home equity–backed credit card that gives homeowners on-demand access to low-cost capital they can use anywhere – replacing the high-rate credit cards, personal loans, and specialty financing that most homeowners cobble together to cover everyday needs.
"As a homeowner, you've spent years building equity and you deserve a better way to put it to work," said TJ Milani, Trovy Co-Founder and CEO. "Trovy gives you the low interest rates of a home equity line of credit with the everyday flexibility of a credit card, unlocking smarter financing that works the way your life actually does."
 More Than a Lender – A Platform Built for Homeowners 
Trovy is building what it describes as a financial home base for homeowners: a unified platform combining smarter financing with a suite of tools built around the home. The financing side gives homeowners on-demand access to their home equity to cover life's expenses at a fraction of the cost of conventional debt, whether that's a home renovation, a dental bill, a family vacation, or everyday purchases. In addition, a homeowner hub – a home management platform with maintenance reminders, expert tips, document storage for insurance policies, warranties, and home records, and insights to help homeowners protect and maximize the value of their home – and a rewards program built around the things homeowners actually spend on and care about.
"We are building the platform homeowners have never had – one that helps them manage and enjoy their home, and leverage their equity for low-cost financing, whether that's handling the costs of homeownership or funding the rest of their lives," said Ashley Harris, Trovy Co-Founder and COO. "We want Trovy to be the home base for every homeowner. Once you have it, you won't want to own a home without it."
Trovy is launching its second financial product this summer: the 1Loan, a HELOC purpose-built for home purchases and refinances. Unlike a traditional mortgage, the 1Loan gives homeowners flexible, on-demand access to equity for whatever comes next, from home improvements to debt consolidation and beyond. The 1Loan meets homeowners at the moment of purchase or refinancing and grows with them from there.
 Built Differently From the Ground Up 
Less than 18 months after founding, Trovy is live in 27 states and licensed in 30, having launched its first product in June 2025, roughly eight months after formation. Unlike fintech platforms that layer on top of bank partnerships, Trovy is a licensed consumer lender, giving it direct control over its product, underwriting, and the full borrower experience. The Trovy card is issued by Cross River Bank pursuant to a license from Mastercard.
"TJ and Ashley have built something rare, and they have the backgrounds to match," said Henry Toole, Partner at Left Lane Capital. "The team has a wealth of fintech experience from Figure, SoFi, and JPMorgan. Home equity is one of the largest and most underutilized categories in consumer finance, and we believe Trovy is building the definitive modern platform for it. We're proud to lead this round as they scale."
"TJ, Ashley, and the Trovy team are building for a simple but enormous reality: for most Americans, the home is their largest asset, yet the financial products around homeownership still feel fragmented, slow, and expensive," said Leigh Marie Braswell, Partner at Kleiner Perkins. "Trovy is turning home equity into something homeowners can actually use in everyday life. We've believed in this team from the beginning, and we're thrilled to continue supporting them."

# Context-enrichment: Trovy raises $15M Series A for home equity-backed credit card
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Trovy, a NYC consumer fintech, raised a **$15M Series A led by Left Lane Capital** (Henry Toole, Partner), with existing seed investors **Kleiner Perkins, DCM Ventures, Camber Creek** participating. Total raised to date: **$25M** (so ~$10M seed prior). Announced 2026-06-24 via PR Newswire.
- The flagship product is a **home equity-backed credit card** (Mastercard, **issued by Cross River Bank**), pitched as "the low interest rates of a HELOC with the everyday flexibility of a credit card." Per NerdWallet, APR is **5.59%–14.24%** (June 2026), capped at 18%; credit limits **$10K–$250K**; balances of $100+ convertible to fixed-rate installment plans of **5–25 years**.
- Founders: **TJ Milani (CEO)** and **Ashley Harris (COO)**; team backgrounds from **Figure, SoFi, JPMorgan** (per the lead investor). Company is **~18 months old** (founded 2024; PR claims "two-year-old" via StrictlyVC but PR Newswire says "less than 18 months after founding" — minor discrepancy, treat founding as late 2024). First product launched **June 2025**.
- Traction stated: **live in 27 states, licensed in 30**. Trovy is a **licensed consumer lender** (NMLS #2676733) — i.e., it owns underwriting and the borrower experience directly, not just a bank-partnership wrapper (the card is still issued via Cross River). A second product, **"1Loan"** — a HELOC for home purchase/refi — is slated for "this summer" (announced, not live).
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The card is a HELOC dressed as a card. The economics are a secured-lending spread, not interchange — so the "card" framing is a distribution/UX wrapper around a second-lien mortgage product. The PR anchors to a flattering macro base ($5T nonmortgage consumer debt, ~$550B/yr interest, 85M homeowners) to make the TAM look enormous, but says nothing about **loan volume, balances drawn, charge-offs, or warehouse/forward-flow capacity** — the numbers that actually matter for a lender. $15M Series A is small for a balance-sheet lender; absence of a debt facility in the announcement is the loudest silence.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Aven (the key comp)** — the category leader for home-equity-backed cards. Aven raised a **$110M Series E at a $2.2B valuation (Sept 2025)** [[Aven raises $110M Series E at $2.2B valuation]], offers a home-equity Visa with **2% cashback**, APR ~**7.49–14.99%** (cap 18%), and has expanded into mortgage and even a **Bitcoin-backed Visa up to $1M (Apr 2026)** [[Aven Bitcoin Visa card offers BTC-backed credit up to $1M]]. Aven also partners with banks to distribute the product [[Aven and Banner Bank offer home equity-backed cards]]. Aven is **years and billions of valuation ahead**; Trovy is a clear late, sub-scale challenger.
- **Figure** — the **#1 non-bank HELOC originator in the world**, embedded across 150+ partners, passing ~125bps in savings [[This Week in Fintech Figure becomes world's top non-bank HELOC lender]], [[This Week in Fintech Figure conversion case study]]. Notably, **Trovy's founders came from Figure** — Trovy is partly a Figure-alumni bet on the consumer card front-end. Figure's funding partner Cross River is also Trovy's card issuer [[Cross River extends $250M forward-flow for Figure crypto loans]].
- **Splash Financial** — raised **$70M Series C and launched a HELOC** (Sept 2025) [[Splash Financial raises $70M Series C, launches HELOC]] — another well-funded entrant into home-equity consumer lending.
- **Yendo** — adjacent "secured card on a hard asset" model (vehicle-secured), $200M facility from i80 Group (Feb 2026) [[Yendo lands $200M facility from i80 Group for vehicle-secured cards]]. Shows the broader "collateralize an asset to cut card APR" pattern.
- **Position:** parity on product *mechanics*, but **behind on scale, capital, and distribution**. Trovy's one differentiator vs Aven is being a **directly-licensed lender** (claimed tighter control of underwriting) — but it still relies on Cross River for issuance, so the "no bank dependency" framing is partial.
- **Why the lay of the land is this way / second-order:** Home-equity cards are a *capital-intensive lending* business, not a software business. The winner is whoever has (a) the cheapest, deepest debt funding and (b) the lowest-cost origination/distribution. Aven won early by combining a slick card UX with scale and a $2.2B war chest; Figure won the rails/origination layer. Trovy entering now with $15M of equity and no announced debt facility means the **central question is not "is the product good" but "can it fund originations at competitive cost before Aven/Figure lock up distribution"**.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Founded ~late 2024; first product live June 2025; Series A June 2026 — a **fast 18-month cadence** (seed → live → Series A). Fits the founders' pedigree (Figure/SoFi/JPMorgan) — they know HELOC origination and consumer credit.
- Strategy = land with the card, then build a **"financial home base"**: a homeowner hub (maintenance reminders, doc storage, warranties), a rewards program, and the 1Loan HELOC for purchase/refi. This is the **Aven playbook** (card → mortgage → platform) one stage behind.
- **Why the company acts this way:** A single secured-lending product is a thin, commoditizable spread. To justify a venture (software) multiple rather than a lender (book-value) multiple, Trovy must layer software/platform stickiness on top — hence the "home base" framing. Whether that is real value-add or PR padding is the open question (see [3]).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Not novel as a category** — Aven productized the home-equity card years earlier and is at $2.2B; banks (Banner Bank) have offered HELOC-linked cards too. Trovy's mechanism delta in one sentence: a directly-licensed consumer lender offering a home-equity card with **fixed-rate installment conversion up to 25 years** and very large limits ($250K), vs Aven's cashback-led card.
- **Traction is thin and announcement-grade:** "live in 27 states" is a licensing/coverage metric, **not** loan volume, balances, customers, or loss rates — none disclosed. The 1Loan is **announced, not launched**.
- **Why the value-add is fragile, deeper:** In secured consumer lending, **margin is captured by whoever holds the cheapest capital and the borrower relationship**. Trovy holds the relationship (licensed lender) but, on current disclosure, **not** scaled cheap capital — Aven and Figure do. The "homeowner hub / rewards" software is undifferentiated and unlikely to command a software multiple on its own. The durable question: can Trovy build a **funding cost advantage or distribution moat** before incumbents commoditize the card? On evidence so far, unproven.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Plans:** national expansion, launch 1Loan HELOC this summer, build out platform/team. Investor sentiment is constructive (Left Lane: "definitive modern platform"; Kleiner: backed from the beginning) but the **round size ($15M) and the absence of any debt facility** signal this is still an early, prove-the-unit-economics bet, not a scale round.
- **Regulatory / credit-risk backdrop (the real risk):** This is **lending against a primary residence via a credit card** — the borrower can lose their home on default, and the impulse-spend dynamic of a card on a second-lien is exactly the consumer-protection concern reviewers flag for Aven. (No public CFPB action against Aven was found in research — do **not** assume one exists.) The category sits in **HELOC/TILA/RESPA territory plus card disclosure rules**; a directly-licensed lender carries the compliance burden directly. A **housing-price downturn** would compress tappable equity, credit limits, and recovery values simultaneously — the second-order risk that makes this book pro-cyclical.
- **Why the market may go this way / counterintuitive:** Record consumer debt at 20%+ APR makes "swap your card debt for HELOC-priced credit" a genuinely attractive value prop in a high-rate environment — real demand tailwind. But the same macro that creates demand (stretched consumers) is what makes **home-collateralized card lending dangerous at scale**: the most eager borrowers are the most distressed, and a card UX encourages drawing equity for consumption, not investment. Capital concentration in Aven/Figure may make the *category* look safe while making a **sub-scale, thinly-funded entrant like Trovy fragile** in a downturn.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: home-equity-backed consumer credit (HELOC + HELOC-as-a-card), inside US consumer lending. Size/structure: tappable home equity is ~$11tn against ~$17tn total homeowner equity (per ICE Mortgage Monitor, via The Mortgage Reports / Bankrate, Mar 2026) — the macro backdrop the PR leans on. The actual addressable flow is much smaller: 2025 HELOC originations were ~1.5m units / **$271bn** (up from $230bn in 2024), and full home-equity origination (HELOC + closed-end) ~$311bn; HELOC outstanding balances hit **$446bn** in Q1 2026, +10.9% YoY, with aggregate HELOC limits past $1tn (per MBA Home Equity Lending Study + NY Fed Q1 2026, via hel.news / Wolf Street). Lenders model ~9.5% HELOC-debt growth for 2026 (MBA). So the category is **growing double-digit but is a mature, bank-dominated $300bn-origination market**, not a greenfield — the "$11tn untapped" framing conflates the stock of collateral with the (far smaller) annual lending flow. Structure: fragmented at the front-end (banks, credit unions, and a thin layer of fintech challengers) but **capital-intensive** — the binding barrier is cheap, deep debt funding, not software. Drivers / why now: (1) record ~$5tn nonmortgage consumer debt at 20%+ card APRs (NY Fed, per the note) makes "swap card debt for HELOC-priced credit" a genuine value prop; (2) rates still elevated, so refinancing the first lien is unattractive and second-lien/HELOC tapping is the path of least resistance — a real tailwind for the whole category in 2025–26.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs (none of which Trovy discloses): origination volume / balances drawn, net-interest spread, charge-off / 90+ delinquency rate, and cost of funds. Benchmark: HELOC 90+ delinquency rose to **0.95%** in Q1 2026 and has been creeping up since late 2024 (NY Fed) — low in absolute terms but rising, and these are seasoned bank books, not an 18-month-old fintech book. Key players & basis of competition: **Aven** (category leader, secured card on HELOC, 2% cashback, competes on scale + brand); **Figure** (#1 non-bank HELOC originator, competes on origination rails / partner distribution, ~125bps savings passed through); incumbent banks/credit unions (87% of home-equity lending, compete on cost of funds). Recent moves with dates: Aven $110M Series E at $2.2B (Sept 2025) and expansion into mortgage refi; Aven BTC-backed Visa (Apr 2026); Cross River extended $250M forward-flow to Figure (Jun 2026). Protagonist's position: **niche / late challenger** — product mechanics near-parity with Aven, but behind on scale, capital and distribution. Moat lens: claimed switching-cost/relationship moat via directly-licensed lending and a "homeowner hub," but **(analysis)** the hub/rewards layer is undifferentiated and the card is still issued by Cross River, so the moat is thin and unproven on current disclosure.

**Comps & multiples.** This is a private-round event, so comps are last-round valuations, not market caps — **no public EV/Revenue or P/E computable; valuation multiples = no data** (Trovy disclosed no revenue, balances, or post-money).
- **Aven** — $2.2B post-money (Sept 2025); had issued **>$3.0bn aggregate credit lines** (Aven press / Fast Company). Crude price-to-cumulative-credit-lines proxy = $2.2bn / $3.0bn = **~0.7x** (note: numerator is equity value, denominator is cumulative origination, not a balance or revenue — directional only, not a real multiple). [[Aven raises $110M Series E at $2.2B valuation]]
- **Splash Financial** — $70M Series C (Sept 2025), launched HELOC. [[Splash Financial raises $70M Series C, launches HELOC]]
- **Figure** — #1 non-bank HELOC originator; $250M forward-flow from Cross River (Jun 2026) — shows the *debt-funding* scale incumbents command. [[This Week in Fintech Figure becomes world's top non-bank HELOC lender]], [[Cross River extends $250M forward-flow for Figure crypto loans]]
- **Trovy** — $15M Series A, $25M total raised; **post-money not disclosed → [UNSOURCED]**. Distribution not computed (only 1 valuation data point, and across mismatched stages: Series A vs Series E vs Series C). Read: Trovy is **~2 orders of magnitude smaller in equity, and crucially has NO announced debt facility** vs Aven's $2.2B war chest and Figure's $250M forward-flow — the gap that matters for a balance-sheet lender. Not "rich vs cheap" on a multiple (uncomputable), but structurally **sub-scale on the only axis that decides this category: cost and depth of capital.**

**Risk flags.**
1. **No debt funding stack disclosed.** A home-equity lender funds originations off warehouse / forward-flow, not $15M of equity. Without a competitive cost of funds, Trovy cannot win a spread business where peers (Aven $2.2B, Figure $250M forward-flow) already have it — second-order: it caps how fast it can originate before incumbents lock up distribution.
2. **Pro-cyclical, untested credit book against primary residences.** HELOC 90+ delinquency is rising (0.95%, NY Fed Q1 2026) and home prices are softening in some markets; a downturn simultaneously compresses tappable equity, credit limits and recovery values. A card UX encourages drawing equity for *consumption* by the most stretched borrowers (adverse selection) — and default can mean foreclosure. An 18-month book has never seen a cycle.
3. **Commoditization / no moat + regulatory surface.** Mechanics are near-parity with Aven and banks already offer HELOC-linked cards (87% market share in home-equity lending); the spread is thin and the "homeowner hub" is undifferentiated, so margin accrues to whoever has cheapest capital, not the newest UX. Layered on top: HELOC (TILA/RESPA) + card-disclosure compliance carried directly as a licensed lender — a card on a home-collateralized line is exactly the consumer-protection profile regulators scrutinize. (No public CFPB action against Aven was found — do not assume one exists.)

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) Not a re-rating of the category — it's a **second VC-backed entry validating the Aven/Figure thesis that home equity is the cheapest under-tapped consumer-credit pool**, but at a stage (small round, no debt facility) that signals "prove unit economics," not "scale." Falsifiable thesis: Trovy is a *late, capital-disadvantaged* challenger that will struggle to reach competitive funding cost before Aven/Figure commoditize the card. Trigger that would break it: announcement of a sizeable warehouse / forward-flow facility (e.g. $100M+) and disclosed origination volume — that would convert it from a UX wrapper into a real lender. Watch next: the 1Loan HELOC actually launching (announced, not live), any debt facility, and whether disclosed loss rates hold as HELOC delinquencies trend up sector-wide.
