## Mark Zuckerberg Directed Meta to Create a Prediction Markets App

The experimental app, internally called “Arena,” would be independent of Facebook and Instagram. It could compete for attention with Polymarket and Kalshi, the biggest prediction markets.

Listen

· 6:10 min

Mark Zuckerberg, Meta’s chief executive, is making a broader push to create new types of apps based on emerging social behavior online.Credit...Jason Henry for The New York Times

By Mike Isaac and David Yaffe-Bellany

Mike Isaac reports on tech companies and Silicon Valley from San Francisco. David Yaffe-Bellany covers financial technology from New York.

June 23, 2026

Polymarket and Kalshi, prediction markets where users can bet on outcomes as varied as the Super Bowl and the length of the State of the Union address, have been some of the fastest-growing destinations on the internet.

Mark Zuckerberg has noticed — and he wants in on the action.

Mr. Zuckerberg, the chief executive of Meta, recently dispatched a small team at his company to create a smartphone app similar to Polymarket and Kalshi, two employees with knowledge of the matter said. Users would not wager money, and the app would probably rely on a video-game-like points system instead, one person said, though the company had not ruled out the eventual use of real money betting.

The app is internally referred to as “Arena” and would function independently from Meta’s social networking apps, which include Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp and Messenger, said the employees, who spoke on the condition of anonymity to discuss confidential plans. Meta aims to grow the app by leveraging its large social networking audiences and directing them toward using it, they said.

The effort, which insiders characterized as experimental but a top priority, is part of a broader push by Mr. Zuckerberg to create new types of apps based on emerging social behavior online. More than 3.56 billion people visit one or more of Meta’s apps every day, an amount that has raised questions about whether those platforms have reached a saturation point.

Arena is one of a handful of apps that Meta is trying out. Others include one called Meta Photos, another stand-alone app, which would create new types of media using artificial intelligence, the employees said.

Meta declined to comment.

For years, Mr. Zuckerberg has chased growth by looking for how user behavior on the internet is changing and then quickly following fast-growing competitors such as Snap and others by cloning their apps and features.

Those efforts have had mixed success. Meta has struggled with new stand-alone apps before, largely because it has been difficult to get people to find and download them. In 2019, under a team called “New Product Experimentation,” employees tried creating various social apps, including those focused on podcasts and travel, as well as music and matchmaking. Few gained traction, three people familiar with the projects said.

But as Facebook and Instagram increasingly serve video-focused content, Meta executives believe there are fewer areas inside the apps to test new product ideas, the people said. That has pushed the company to set its sights on separate apps.

This is not the first time Meta has experimented with prediction markets. In 2020, it released Forecast, a crowdsourced prediction market app that prompted people to make guesses about the world in the early days of the Covid-19 pandemic. The app was positioned as a way to share crowdsourced knowledge. It used a points system to make predictions about the future. Meta shuttered the app in 2022.

Since then, prediction markets have exploded into a cultural phenomenon, featured during major sports events and in the Golden Globes telecast. In 2025, Kalshi and Polymarket drew a combined $50 billion in online trades. This year, the total has already surpassed $130 billion.

That success has drawn attention from other companies. A prediction market operator can make money by collecting fees on every bet, a potentially enormous source of revenue. Traditional gambling firms like FanDuel and DraftKings have started offering them, as has Gemini, a cryptocurrency exchange. Trump Media & Technology Group, President Trump’s social media business, has also rolled out prediction market plans.

Image

A subway advertisement for Kalshi, a prediction market that, along with its rival Polymarket, has become increasingly popular. Credit...Karsten Moran for The New York Times

Kalshi declined to comment. Polymarket did not respond to a request for comment.

The surge of betting on prediction markets has brought intense legal scrutiny. Because these markets offer odds on virtually everything, they create new opportunities for people to use inside information to make money.

A pattern of suspicious trading on Polymarket in particular has generated concerns in Washington. In April, federal prosecutors in New York City charged a member of U.S. Special Forces with using confidential information to place bets about the top-secret plan to capture Nicolás Maduro, the president of Venezuela. The soldier made more than $400,000 betting on the operation, according to prosecutors.

Concerns about insider trading and other possible abuses have put a spotlight on the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, the obscure federal agency that oversees prediction markets. The agency, never particularly large, has shrunk under the Trump administration, leaving it with its smallest staff in years, just as its responsibilities have rapidly expanded.

Senator Richard Blumenthal, Democrat of Connecticut, criticized Meta’s plans on Tuesday in a social media post: “Meta copied slot machines to addict kids to Instagram. Now Zuckerberg is turning his company into a prediction market.”

He added that Meta’s business model was “profiting from addiction” and directed people to support two bills he was cosponsoring in Congress, the Kids Online Safety Act and the Prediction Markets Security and Integrity Act.

Meta insiders have cautioned that Arena remains in development and may not be released.

But as executives search for ways to keep the world’s largest social media sites thriving, Mr. Zuckerberg appears to be relying on his well-worn product development strategy: Follow the users.

Mike Isaac is The Times’s Silicon Valley correspondent, based in San Francisco. He covers the world’s most consequential tech companies, and how they shape culture both online and offline.

# Context-enrichment: Meta builds standalone prediction markets app "Arena"
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 2026-06-23 the **NYT (Isaac / Yaffe-Bellany)** reported that Mark Zuckerberg has dispatched a small team to build a stand-alone smartphone prediction-markets app, internally codenamed **"Arena"** (other internal names cited by NPR: "Antwerp", "FBForecast"). It is **NOT launched** — it is "experimental but a top priority" and Meta insiders explicitly cautioned it "may not be released." Meta declined to comment. Source-grade: leak from two anonymous employees + internal documents seen by NPR — not a Meta announcement.
- **Mechanism (de-PR'd, the most important under-reported fact):** Arena uses a **points / play-money** system, not real-money wagering ("money could be added later"). But the real delta vs the NYT framing is the **AI layer**: per NPR and TechTimes (citing internal docs), **Llama auto-generates the markets** from trending topics, makes "personalized market recommendations", and — critically — **Meta's AI resolves the markets in near-real-time** (the AI decides whether an event happened). Arena is described in the docs as a **rebuild of Forecast** (Meta's 2020 crowdsourced prediction app, shuttered 2022 due to the "operational cost of manual question curation") with Llama replacing the human editors. → This reframes the central question: Arena is **not** "Meta copies Kalshi"; it is "Meta runs an AI-curated engagement loop" where the LLM both authors and adjudicates — closer to an attention/feed product than to a CFTC-regulated exchange.
- **Why structured this way:** points-not-money lets Meta sidestep the CFTC / state-gambling regime that is currently strangling Kalshi/Polymarket ([[Minnesota becomes first US state to ban prediction markets]], [[Spain blocks prediction markets Polymarket and Kalshi]]). And standalone-not-in-feed is the deliberate choice because Meta "believes there are fewer areas inside the apps to test new product ideas" — but distribution is the catch (see [2]).

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Kalshi** — US leader, ~91% US market share ([[Kalshi holds 91% of US prediction market, BofA data shows]]), $17.9bn monthly volume, regulated (CFTC), raising at **$40bn** ([[Kalshi in talks to raise at $40 billion valuation]]).
- **Polymarket** — crypto-native, ~$7.1bn monthly volume, seeking ~$15bn ([[Polymarket in talks to raise at 15B valuation]]), ICE-backed ([[ICE invests $600 million into prediction market Polymarket]]).
- **Distribution challengers (Arena's truest peer set):** X (Polymarket partner), Robinhood ([[Robinhood prediction market hits 9 billion contracts, 1M users]]), Coinbase ([[Coinbase rolls out Kalshi prediction markets across US]]), Gemini ([[Gemini moves to enter prediction markets]]), Stocktwits, Crypto.com/Underdog, plus **Trump Media** plans. Most ride Kalshi/Polymarket rails; Meta uniquely intends to **build its own.**
- **Institutional / "no-money" model converging:** **Cboe** launched "Cboe Predicts" S&P 500 binary event contracts on **2026-06-23** (same day) via Interactive Brokers / Schwab — shows even regulated incumbents now want in.
- **Position:** Meta is **late and behind on the product**, but **#1 on distribution** (3.56bn DAU). It is the only entrant whose model is points + AI-resolution rather than real-money CFTC contracts. **+ Why this lay of the land:** the value of a prediction-market platform = liquidity × distribution × regulatory clearance. Kalshi has liquidity+regulation; Meta has distribution but neither liquidity nor (yet) real money → it can flood the top of funnel but cannot capture the **fee economics** that make Kalshi worth $40bn.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Forecast (2020–2022):** Meta's direct prior art — same idea, points-based, killed for cost. The Llama-resolution design is the literal attempt to fix what killed v1.
- **Pattern of failed standalone apps:** NYT lists New Product Experimentation (podcasts, travel, music, matchmaking 2019) — "few gained traction." Meta's historic standalone-app failure mode is **discovery/download friction**, which is precisely why it now wants to funnel from Facebook/Instagram/WhatsApp.
- **Adjacent fintech moves:** [[Meta plans stablecoin comeback in second half of year]], [[Meta begins paying creators in USDC via Stripe]] — Meta is re-entering money-movement broadly, so a real-money pivot for Arena later is structurally plausible. **+ Why Meta acts this way:** core apps near saturation ("questions about whether platforms have reached a saturation point") → Zuckerberg's well-worn "follow the users / clone the fast-growing competitor" playbook (Snap→Stories, etc.). Prediction markets are simply the current fast-growing social behavior to clone.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Traction = zero.** Nothing is live; no users, no volume, no launch date; may be cancelled. Treat as a **signal of intent**, not a product.
- **Genuine novelty:** the AI-author + **AI-resolver** loop. No incumbent lets an LLM adjudicate outcomes "in near-real-time" — Kalshi/Polymarket resolve via defined data sources / human/UMA-style oracles. **+ Why the value-add is real-but-fragile:** an LLM resolver removes the human-curation cost that killed Forecast and lets markets scale to any trending topic instantly → that IS the unlock. BUT it imports a **truth-arbitration problem**: an AI deciding "did X happen" on contested events is a liability and trust magnet, and with money it becomes a disputes/fraud surface. **Who captures the margin:** with points there is **no fee margin at all** — Arena's value is purely **engagement/ad-inventory** (more time-on-app), not transaction take-rate. So unless/until real money is added, Arena is an engagement feature, not a fintech P&L line. The thing that makes Kalshi a $40bn business (fees on $17.9bn/mo) is exactly what Arena, by design, does not have.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Immediate market reaction (2026-06-23):** sports-betting incumbents fell — **DraftKings -2%**, **Flutter (FanDuel)** dipped ~2% intraday, **Robinhood** declined — on fear Meta's distribution could disrupt sportsbooks/prediction rails. (CNBC / Yahoo Finance.)
- **Political/regulatory heat:** Sen. Richard Blumenthal attacked it day one ("Meta copied slot machines to addict kids to Instagram. Now Zuckerberg is turning his company into a prediction market"), pushing the **Kids Online Safety Act** and **Prediction Markets Security and Integrity Act**. Backdrop: CFTC understaffed; insider-trading scandals (Special-Forces soldier +$400k on the Maduro op; George Santos under investigation; [[WSJ finds 0.1% of Polymarket accounts capture 67% of profits]]); state bans ([[Minnesota becomes first US state to ban prediction markets]]). **+ Why the market goes this way:** the sector is simultaneously **exploding** (2025: $50bn combined Kalshi+Polymarket; 2026 YTD: >$130bn; World Cup alone >$5bn — [[Over $5 billion bet on World Cup across Polymarket and Kalshi]]) **and de-rating on regulatory/integrity risk**. Counterintuitive second-order: by using points + AI, Meta is **explicitly engineering around** the very regulation that bounds incumbents — which is also why it draws the **child-safety/addiction** attack instead, a Meta-specific liability the pure-play exchanges don't carry.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

Second-order red-team (each answered from sources or marked "open"):

1. **Is this a product or a press leak?** A leak. Two anonymous employees + internal docs (NPR); Meta declined to comment; insiders said it "may not be released." Not announced, not live, no date. → weight is "intent signal," not "launch."

2. **Is the NYT "Meta copies Polymarket" frame the real story?** No. The load-bearing fact, buried by NYT and absent from the note's aggregated text, is that **Llama auto-generates the markets and Meta's AI resolves them in near-real-time** (NPR, TechTimes). That changes the category from "exchange clone" to "AI-curated engagement loop."

3. **Where is the margin?** Open/None under the announced design. Points = play money = **no fee take-rate**. Kalshi's $40bn rests on fees over $17.9bn/mo volume; Arena, by design, has none of that. Value accrues only as engagement/ad inventory unless real money is added.

4. **Will real money come?** Open — "could be added later" (NYT). Plausible given Meta's broader money push ([[Meta plans stablecoin comeback in second half of year]], USDC creator payouts), but unconfirmed and the gating regulatory event.

5. **Can an LLM credibly resolve contested markets?** Open and the biggest design risk. "AI decides what is true" on trending/political events invites disputes, manipulation, and — with money — fraud-liability. No incumbent delegates resolution to an LLM; Kalshi/Polymarket use defined data sources / oracle processes.

6. **Did Meta's prior attempt fly?** No — Forecast (2020) was shuttered 2022 on manual-curation cost. Arena is explicitly a "rebuild" with Llama replacing editors (NPR). So the novelty is "automate the thing that killed v1," not the concept.

7. **Does standalone fix Meta's discovery problem?** Partly. Stated solution = funnel from 3.56bn DAU. But every prior NPE standalone (podcasts/travel/music/matchmaking, 2019) died on download friction. Cross-promo is unproven for this category and is the whole bet.

8. **Did the market take it seriously?** Yes, asymmetrically: DraftKings -2%, Flutter ~-2% intraday, Robinhood down (CNBC/Yahoo, 2026-06-23). The Street priced a distribution threat to sportsbooks/rails, not to Kalshi's regulated moat.

9. **Does it dent Kalshi/Polymarket valuations?** No evidence — Kalshi was reported chasing **$40bn the very next day** (CoinDesk 2026-06-24, [[Kalshi in talks to raise at $40 billion valuation]]). Meta has no liquidity; the threat is to distribution rails, not the exchange.

10. **What regulatory axis does this actually trigger?** Not CFTC/gambling (points sidestep it) but **child-safety/addiction** — Blumenthal's day-one attack + KOSA + Prediction Markets Security and Integrity Act. A Meta-specific liability pure-plays don't carry.

11. **Why now, structurally?** Core-app saturation + a >$130bn-YTD fast-growing behavior to clone (sector data: 2025 $50bn → 2026 YTD >$130bn; World Cup >$5bn, [[Over $5 billion bet on World Cup across Polymarket and Kalshi]]). Classic "follow the users."

12. **Is the data/ad/Llama-training angle the hidden prize?** Open — not disclosed, but prediction behavior across 3.56bn DAU is a plausible ad-targeting/training asset; flagged as hypothesis only.

13. **Who's silent about what?** Meta: monetization, launch date, AI-resolution accuracy and dispute handling. Kalshi & Polymarket: declined to comment. The silence is concentrated exactly on the economics and the resolver-risk.

14. **Is incumbent convergence relevant?** Yes — Cboe launched "Cboe Predicts" S&P 500 binary contracts the **same day** (2026-06-23) via IBKR/Schwab. The "no-money / simple yes-no" framing is spreading from both Big Tech and regulated exchanges simultaneously, signaling the category is mainstreaming.

15. **Biggest second-order risk if it works?** Normalizing "an AI adjudicates what is true" for stakes-bearing bets is a governance/trust story larger than the betting product — open, but the most consequential downstream effect.

Importance: 3/5 — rationale: Pre-product leak from a serial standalone-app failer, no traction and (under points) no fee economics — that caps it. Clears 3 because the entrant is Meta (3.56bn DAU, real distribution threat priced in: DKNG/Flutter/HOOD down day one), the AI-author-and-resolver mechanism is genuinely novel and reframes the story, and it lands in a white-hot, heavily-covered corpus. Not 4: no launch, no money, no margin yet.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.**
- **Size / growth:** Combined Kalshi+Polymarket volume ~$50bn in 2025 → **>$130bn YTD 2026** (note text/NYT). Monthly volume scaled from ~$1.2bn (2025) to **>$20–21bn/mo** by early 2026 (per TRM Labs); analysts forecast the category could process **~$1.3tn annual volume / ~$25bn weekly** in 2026 (per Gambling Insider, via secondary citation). MAU hit **8.58mn in Jan 2026, +400% YoY**. Fee TAM is the gating number: ~**$0.5–1bn** addressable fee pool near-term, with one forecast of **~$10bn annual revenue by 2030** (Gambling Insider) — note: forecast, not verified.
- **Structure:** Consolidating fast — Kalshi holds **~91% US share** ([[Kalshi holds 91% of US prediction market, BofA data shows]]). Value in the stack sits at **(a) the regulated exchange / liquidity** (Kalshi via CFTC) and **(b) distribution** (X, Coinbase, Robinhood, brokers). Entry barriers: regulation (CFTC + state gambling law) and liquidity network effects — NOT technology. Meta clears neither today.
- **Drivers + "why now":** (1) cultural mainstreaming — World Cup drew **>$5bn** across the two ([[Over $5 billion bet on World Cup across Polymarket and Kalshi]]); (2) incumbent convergence — **Cboe Predicts** launched S&P 500 binary event contracts on **2026-06-23** (same day) via IBKR/Schwab. Why-ladder: explosive volume → every distribution owner wants a cut → Meta (3.56bn DAU) is the largest untapped funnel → it builds rather than partners → but with points it monetizes engagement, not fees → second-order: it expands the **top of funnel** for the category without capturing the **fee margin** that defines the sector's enterprise value.

**Competitive landscape.**
- **Sector KPIs:** monthly trading volume, **take rate** (Kalshi ~0.9–1.14% blended), MAU/active traders, and (for an engagement model like Arena) DAU × time-on-app.
- **Key players & basis of competition:** Kalshi (regulated liquidity), Polymarket (crypto-native, ICE-backed), and distribution layers — X/Polymarket, Robinhood ([[Robinhood prediction market hits 9 billion contracts, 1M users]]), Coinbase ([[Coinbase rolls out Kalshi prediction markets across US]]), Gemini ([[Gemini moves to enter prediction markets]]), Cboe/Schwab (institutional). Basis of competition = **liquidity + distribution + regulatory clearance**, not product UX.
- **Recent moves:** Kalshi raising at **$40bn** (2026-06-24, [[Kalshi in talks to raise at $40 billion valuation]]); Polymarket seeking **~$15bn** ([[Polymarket in talks to raise at 15B valuation]]); Cboe Predicts live 2026-06-23.
- **Protagonist position:** **niche/late on product, #1 on distribution.** Moat = distribution scale + (novel) AI-author/AI-resolver loop; but moat on **economics is absent** under points (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.**
- **Kalshi (private):** ~$2bn annualized revenue (Sacra est., Jun 2026) at a target **$40bn** round → **~20x EV/Revenue** = `$40bn / $2bn`. On 2025 actuals ($260mn rev, $22.88bn volume → **1.14% take rate**) the trailing multiple is far richer; ~20x forward is the defensible read for hyper-growth (revenue scaled ~2.7x H2'25→'26). Internal: [[Kalshi raises over $1 billion at $22 billion valuation]] (prior $22bn round = ~11x on ~$2bn run-rate).
- **Polymarket (private):** ~$15bn target ([[Polymarket in talks to raise at 15B valuation]]); annualized revenue ~$338–390mn (Sacra/DefiLlama, began monetizing Q1'26) → **~38–44x EV/Revenue** = `$15bn / ~$0.37bn`. Trades at a discount to Kalshi on absolute valuation despite the higher multiple — explained by lower US share + a planned token clouding volume stickiness (Fortune).
- **Public read-through (sentiment, not Arena comps):** DraftKings **-2%**, Flutter ~-2% intraday, Robinhood down on 2026-06-23 — the market re-priced incumbents on Meta's distribution threat.
- **Flag:** Kalshi ~20x and Polymarket ~38–44x EV/Rev are **above the ~0.5–20x sanity range**, but **justified by growth** (Kalshi rev ~2.7x; category volume ~10x+ YoY) — rich-but-not-mispriced for hyper-growth; Polymarket the richer/riskier name. **Arena itself = no multiple (pre-product, points-based, no revenue).**

**Risk flags.**
1. **No fee economics under points** — Arena monetizes engagement, not transactions; the sector's entire enterprise value (Kalshi $40bn) rests on the take-rate Arena lacks. Second-order: it's an ad/engagement feature, not a fintech P&L, unless real money is added.
2. **AI-resolution liability** — Llama adjudicating "what is true" in near-real-time is a novel trust/dispute/manipulation surface no incumbent carries; becomes a fraud surface if money is added.
3. **Meta-specific regulatory axis** — sidesteps CFTC but invites **child-safety/addiction** scrutiny (Blumenthal day-one, KOSA, Prediction Markets Security and Integrity Act) — a liability pure-plays don't face.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Meta validates prediction markets as a mainstream **social format**, not just a trading product — the threat is to **distribution rails** (X/Robinhood/Coinbase intermediation) more than to Kalshi's regulated-exchange moat (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if Arena ships AND adds real money, it re-rates the **distribution layer** and pressures incumbent rails; **trigger** = a confirmed launch date + a money/CFTC pathway. **What breaks the thesis:** Arena follows Forecast and prior NPE standalones into the graveyard on download friction — the base-rate outcome.
