# Context-enrichment: Coins.ph selects Clear Junction for European payment corridors
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Coins.ph (Philippine crypto-native payments/remittance app, founded 2014, ~5M+ verified users) has appointed **Clear Junction** (UK/EU/Canada-licensed payments-infrastructure provider, est. 2016) as a banking-rails supplier for its European corridor activity. De-PR'd, this is a **Banking-as-a-Service / correspondent-access deal**, not a product launch:
- Clear Junction issues **named virtual IBANs (vIBANs)** to Coins.ph's corporate/FI clients.
- Provides access to **EUR rails (SEPA Credit + SEPA Instant)** and **GBP rails (Faster Payments)**, plus Clear Junction's internal settlement network for in-ecosystem transfers.
- Purpose: "collections, disbursements, treasury operations, and local payment access" for corporate and financial-institution clients operating across Europe.
- **Why structured this way:** Coins.ph is renting regulatory/banking access rather than obtaining its own EU/UK licences and bank relationships — the classic emerging-market fintech move to plug into European rails fast. The "selects/partners" framing dresses a vendor-procurement decision as strategic expansion. There are **no go-live date, no client names, no volumes, no fees** disclosed → this is an enablement announcement, not adoption.
- **Note:** The PR leads with the corporate/FI B2B angle, NOT consumer OFW remittances — so this is about Coins.ph building out a treasury/payments-infra business line, distinct from its retail Philippine wallet.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Clear Junction's own book** shows this is a commodity service: it runs near-identical vIBAN + SEPA/Faster Payments deals with **Ripple** (2024, GBP/EUR payout coverage), **Flutterwave** (Sep 2025, African remittances into UK/EU), and **Agant** (Jun 2026, GBP stablecoin GBPA). Coins.ph is one more client on a productized rail, not a bespoke arrangement.
- **Coins.ph's own corridor stack (from corpus):** HashKey PH–HK corridor ([[Coins.ph and HashKey launch Philippines-Hong Kong remittance corridor]], Jul 2025), Higlobe stablecoin US–PH rail ([[Higlobe and Coins.ph launch stablecoin US-Philippines payment rail]], Aug 2025), Circle peso-payout network ([[Coins.ph joins Circle network for peso payouts]], Feb 2026), Veem cross-border ([[Veem and Coins.ph expand cross-border payments partnership]], Jan 2026), QRPh stablecoin checkout ([[Coins.ph launches stablecoin payments via national QRPh standard]], Apr 2026). Europe is the missing geography being filled in.
- **Rail rivals for Coins.ph in Europe:** any EU/UK EMI-as-a-service (Currencycloud/Visa, Banking Circle, Modulr, ClearBank, Nium). Clear Junction's edge is crypto-friendliness + correspondent reach, which matters for a crypto-native client that mainstream BaaS may de-risk away.
- **Position:** parity / catching up. This brings Coins.ph to baseline European connectivity that peers already have; not a differentiator.

## [2] Company history / fit
Trajectory: Coins.ph has been systematically stitching corridors and rails over 2025–26 — HK (Jul'25), US stablecoin (Aug'25), all-in-one app (Jan'26), Circle/USDC peso payouts (Feb'26), QRPh stablecoin checkout (Apr'26). It is moving from a retail Philippine crypto wallet toward a **multi-corridor payments/remittance network** and now an institutional treasury offering. **Why:** OFW remittance fees (1–6%) are the prize, but Coins.ph is also building a B2B/FI rails business where margins on collections/disbursements/treasury are stickier than retail crypto trading. Europe is a logical add: UK + UAE each = ~4.6% of the record **$35.63B 2025 OFW cash remittances** (BSP), and the EU hosts large Filipino diaspora populations. So the Europe corridor both serves remittance inflows and lets Coins.ph sell rails to other FIs. The deal fits the strategy cleanly.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty: low.** Renting vIBANs + SEPA/Faster Payments from a BaaS provider is standard plumbing; the same vendor does it for Ripple/Flutterwave/Agant. The only "new" element is that *Coins.ph specifically* now has European reach — a NEW geography for this company, but not a new capability for the market.
- **Traction: none disclosed.** No live date, clients, or volumes. Announced ≠ live.
- **Where the margin sits:** Clear Junction captures the rail/settlement take; Coins.ph captures the FX/remittance spread and any treasury fees on top. The structural risk for Coins.ph is that it is a **reseller of someone else's regulated access** — it does not own the European licence, so its economics and continuity depend on Clear Junction's correspondent network and risk appetite (crypto-linked flows are precisely what gets de-banked). That keeps Coins.ph a margin-taker, not a rail owner, in Europe. (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Watch for: first disclosed EUR/GBP volumes, named corporate/FI clients, and whether Coins.ph layers stablecoin (USDC/EURC) settlement onto these rails — given its Circle and Higlobe ties, a stablecoin-into-SEPA bridge is the plausible next step. (hypothesis)
- **Backdrop:** record $35.63B OFW remittances in 2025 (+3.3% YoY, ~7.3% of GDP); US 39.7%, UK and UAE ~4.6% each. Europe is a secondary but non-trivial inflow source, and a corridor where stablecoin-rail cost advantages (vs 1–6% legacy fees) are the real lever.
- **Risk:** de-banking / compliance risk on crypto-linked flows; dependence on a single infra vendor; commoditized, low-moat capability. Sentiment: incremental positive, not market-moving.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Two stacked markets meet here: (1) the Philippine cross-border remittance pool — record **$35.63bn OFW cash remittances in 2025, +3.3% YoY, ~7.3% of GDP** (per BSP, via BusinessWorld, Feb 2026); source mix is US 39.7%, Singapore 7.3%, Saudi 6.6%, Japan 5.0%, UK and UAE ~4.6% each — so Europe is a real but secondary inflow; and (2) the Banking-as-a-Service / vIBAN infrastructure layer Coins.ph is renting — global BaaS sized at **~$28.96bn in 2026, projected ~$65.78bn by 2031 at ~17.8% CAGR** (per Mordor Intelligence; note: BaaS market sizing varies 2–30x across firms, treat as order-of-magnitude only). Structure: the remittance corridor business is highly fragmented and price-competitive (send cost on $200 still ~5.7%, per Munify/Circle PR, Jun 2026); the EU/UK rail-access layer is concentrated among a handful of licensed EMIs (Clear Junction, Banking Circle, Currencycloud/Visa, Modulr, ClearBank, Nium). Entry barrier is regulatory (EMI licence + correspondent banking), which is exactly why Coins.ph rents rather than builds. **Why now:** stablecoin settlement (USDC/EURC) is collapsing corridor costs vs 1–6% legacy fees, and EU/UK open-banking rails (SEPA Instant, Faster Payments) make "rent-a-rail" expansion cheap — driving a wave of emerging-market fintechs plugging into European corridors.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: for the corridor business — TPV/volume, take rate / FX spread, cost-to-send %; for the BaaS supplier — vIBAN accounts issued, settlement volume, per-transaction fee. None disclosed in this deal. Players & basis of competition: Clear Junction competes on crypto-friendliness + correspondent reach (it serves crypto-native clients mainstream BaaS de-risks away); rivals (Banking Circle, Modulr, ClearBank, Nium) compete on price/scale/coverage. Recent peer moves (de-PR'd): Clear Junction runs near-identical vIBAN+SEPA/Faster Payments deals with Ripple (2024), Flutterwave (Sep 2025), Agant GBPA stablecoin (Jun 2026) — confirming this is a productized commodity rail, not bespoke. On the Coins.ph side, the corridor stack is being assembled fast: HashKey PH-HK (Jul 2025), Higlobe US-PH stablecoin (Aug 2025), Veem (Jan 2026), Circle peso payouts (Feb 2026), QRPh stablecoin checkout (Apr 2026). **Protagonist's position: parity / catching up, niche.** This brings Coins.ph to baseline European connectivity peers already have; the moat is its PH-side licence + ~5M users, not this rail (commodity, non-exclusive, switchable). **Risk flags.**
1. **Dependence on someone else's rails / disintermediation.** Coins.ph resells Clear Junction's regulated access; it owns no EU/UK licence here, so its European economics and continuity hinge entirely on a single vendor's correspondent network and risk appetite — a margin-taker, not a rail-owner. Second-order: pricing power and survival of the corridor are not in Coins.ph's hands.
2. **De-banking / compliance risk on crypto-linked flows.** Crypto-native flows are precisely what correspondent banks de-risk; if Clear Junction's upstream banks tighten, the corridor can be throttled overnight. Second-order: fragile, not entrenched, European presence.
3. **Commoditization / no moat.** Identical vIBAN+SEPA pattern is sold to Ripple/Flutterwave/Agant; non-exclusive, easily replicated or switched. Second-order: zero defensibility — value accrues to whoever owns the PH-side licence and FX spread, which here is Coins.ph only on the demand side.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Incremental, not a re-rating: this is a geography-fill, not a new capability for the market — one more emerging-market fintech renting European rails. Watch-thesis (falsifiable): *if* Coins.ph layers stablecoin (USDC/EURC) settlement onto these SEPA/Faster Payments rails — plausible given its Circle/Higlobe ties — it could convert a commodity vendor deal into a cost-advantaged stablecoin-into-SEPA bridge. Trigger/catalyst: first disclosed EUR/GBP volumes or a named EURC/SEPA settlement integration. What would make the thesis wrong: the deal stays B2B treasury plumbing with no stablecoin layer and no disclosed volumes — i.e. it remains a "selected/signed" line item that never goes live at scale.
