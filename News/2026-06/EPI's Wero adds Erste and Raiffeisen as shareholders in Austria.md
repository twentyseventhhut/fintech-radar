EPI adds Erste and Raiffeisen as Wero shareholders
Sinziana Albu
22 Jun 2026 / 5 Min Read
The European Payments Initiative has welcomed Erste Bank Oesterreich and Raiffeisen as shareholders to advance Wero's rollout in Austria.
Raiffeisen's participation spans Raiffeisen Bank International (RBI) and three regional Landesbanken: Raiffeisenlandesbank Niederösterreich-Wien, Raiffeisenlandesbank Oberösterreich, and Raiffeisen-Landesbank Steiermark. The formal admission of the three Landesbanken as shareholders remains subject to their respective supervisory board approvals.
Wero's pan-European expansion
Austria, with a population of approximately nine million, represents a new market in Wero's expanding European footprint. The platform operates as a real-time A2A payment solution and had surpassed 55 million users ahead of its Austrian entry, with rollouts also progressing in Luxembourg and the Netherlands. With this in mind, the support of Erste Bank Oesterreich and the Raiffeisen network, which together serve the majority of Austrian banking customers, is expected to provide a foundation for market adoption in the country.
EPI was established to build a pan-European payment network, offering consumers and merchants an account-to-account alternative to card-based international payment schemes. Wero launched initially across Germany, France, and Belgium, where A2A capabilities have progressively gained traction among retail users. Austria represents the latest addition to this rollout, and the participation of major domestic banking institutions reflects EPI's strategy of embedding Wero within established financial relationships in each target market.
 Martina Weimert , CEO of EPI, noted that the participation of Erste and Raiffeisen further strengthens EPI's mission to build a resilient, pan-European payment network, and described the development as part of a broader new phase of growth for Wero across Europe.
In addition, Gerda Holzinger-Burgstaller , Chief Executive Officer of Erste Bank Oesterreich, stated that the bank's commitment to offering customers secure and flexible payment solutions underpins its decision to add Wero to its services, and that as an EPI shareholder, Erste intends both to offer the solution to clients and to actively contribute to its development.
Moreover, Johann Strobl , CEO of Raiffeisen Bank International, said that the Raiffeisen group's EPI shareholding reflects a commitment to shaping the future of payments in Austria and across Europe, and that providing customers and merchants with alternatives such as Wero is intended to advance greater choice and efficiency in the payments landscape.
With the Austrian expansion now confirmed, EPI's shareholder base continues to broaden, reinforcing Wero's ambition to establish itself as a widely accepted payment solution across Europe.

# Context-enrichment: EPI's Wero adds Erste and Raiffeisen as shareholders in Austria
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 2026-06-22 EPI (operator of the Wero wallet) admitted **Erste Bank Oesterreich** and the **Raiffeisen** network — Raiffeisen Bank International (RBI) plus three regional Landesbanken (Niederösterreich-Wien, Oberösterreich, Steiermark) — as **shareholders**. This is a *governance/capital* event, not a live product launch. Key de-PR points:
- The three Landesbanken's admission is **still subject to supervisory-board approvals** (not closed).
- Wero is **not yet live for Austrian consumers** — the press frames this as the "foundation for market adoption", i.e. banks bought in, rollout follows later. "Welcomes as shareholders" ≠ "Austrians can pay with Wero today".
- The "55 million users" figure is **pan-European (DE/FR/BE) ahead of Austrian entry**, not Austrian traction. → Why anchored this way: it borrows the incumbent core-market base to make a still-unlaunched 9M-person market look pre-validated.
- **Why structured via shareholding rather than mere membership** (cf. [[Van Lanschot Kempen becomes Principal Member of EPI, adds Wero]], [[Airwallex becomes EPI Principal Member to bring Wero]]): shareholders sit on EPI's cap table and governance, signalling deeper, harder-to-reverse commitment than the membership tier — Erste + Raiffeisen together cover the **majority of Austrian retail banking**, so this is a near-complete domestic distribution lock-in rather than a single-bank pilot.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Cards (Visa/Mastercard):** the real target. Mastercard alone has >900M branded cards in EU circulation; Wero ~47–55M users. In Germany — Wero's first and most mature market — A2A is still only ~5% of transaction volume (reported), so card displacement is early. (analysis) Austria is a card/contactless-heavy market; Wero must win habit, not just bank shelf-space.
- **Domestic schemes:** Austria has **Bluecode** (QR A2A, cf. [[Bluecode expands QR payment reach to 60 markets via Alipay+]]) already operating locally — Wero enters a market that is not greenfield.
- **EuroPA alliance:** EPI signed a 2026-02 MoU linking Wero with Bizum (ES, ~30M), Bancomat (IT), SIBS (PT) and Vipps MobilePay (Nordics) — ~130M users / 13 countries interoperability target (cf. [[Bancomat, EPI and SIBS demonstrate cross-border payment interoperability]]). Austria slots into this widening A2A bloc.
- Position: Austria is **catching up** infill within Wero's DACH-plus expansion (DE/FR/BE live → LU/NL/AT in progress), not a frontier.

## [2] Company history / fit
EPI's playbook is consistent: secure the **dominant domestic banks per country** before consumer launch. Trajectory — Germany/France/Belgium P2P live (cf. [[Belgian banks join EPI to expand Wero wallet across Europe]], [[Wero launches e-commerce payments in Belgium]]), Luxembourg via Payconiq (cf. [[Wero to launch in Luxembourg with Payconiq and EPI]]), Netherlands replacing iDEAL, plus e-commerce enablement via Nexi (cf. [[Nexi implements Wero for eCommerce in Germany]]) and ACI Worldwide (cf. [[ACI Worldwide and EPI to power instant payments in Europe]]). → Why this way: A2A only works when the buyer's *and* seller's banks both support it, so EPI must onboard whole national banking systems, not sign individual fintechs — hence the shareholder route for Austria's two-pillar (Erste + Raiffeisen) banking landscape.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Novelty here is **low** — it is geographic expansion of an existing wallet, executed via the same shareholder-onboarding template EPI already used. Real value-add (analysis): Austria's two largest banking groups joining means near-universal *potential* reach from day one, which is the precondition (not proof) of network effects. Traction caveat: **no Austrian usage numbers exist yet** (nothing is live); the citable traction is pan-European (~55M users; ~EUR 7.5bn / 43M users reported in Wero's first year, France-heavy). Who captures the margin: A2A bypasses card interchange, so value shifts from schemes to the bank consortium — but only if merchants adopt; until POS/e-commerce volume materialises, this is bank-side cost-saving theatre, not a consumer win.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Next: supervisory-board sign-offs for the Landesbanken, then a consumer rollout date for Austria (P2P first, POS/e-commerce later, mirroring DE). EPI roadmap: cross-border P2P in 2026, cross-border e-commerce/POS in 2027. Sentiment is "sovereignty" framed (European alternative to Visa/Mastercard/PayPal), heavily politician-driven — sceptics note the sovereignty narrative oversells a still-low-adoption product. → Counterintuitive second-order (hypothesis): adding more shareholder banks broadens distribution but **dilutes governance and slows decision-making**; the bigger the consortium, the harder a unified, fast consumer experience becomes — the classic bank-consortium coordination tax that sank earlier pan-European schemes (Monnet).

## Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Live or announced?** Is Wero actually usable by Austrian consumers as of 2026-06, or only shareholder onboarding? → **Open/No**: sources frame it as "foundation for market adoption"; no consumer launch date or live confirmation. This is capital + governance, not a product launch.
2. **Is the deal even closed?** The three Raiffeisen Landesbanken's admission is "subject to supervisory-board approvals" → **partially open** — not fully consummated at announcement.
3. **What share of Austrian banking do Erste + Raiffeisen actually cover?** Press says "majority"/"most" — credible (the two dominate Austrian retail) but no exact % cited → distribution potential high, but unquantified.
4. **Any Austrian usage/volume numbers?** → **None**. All figures (55M users; EUR 7.5bn / 43M first-year) are pan-European, France-heavy. No Austrian traction to assess.
5. **Is 55M users real adoption or registered accounts?** Likely registered/activated, not MAU; intensity of use unknown. In Germany A2A is reportedly only ~5% of transaction volume despite being the launch market → low engagement.
6. **What did the same before in Austria?** Bluecode (domestic QR A2A) already operates; Austria is not greenfield → Wero must displace an incumbent local scheme, not just cards.
7. **Mechanism delta vs cards in one line?** Real-time A2A bypassing card interchange/rails — but only valuable once merchant POS/e-commerce acceptance exists, which is not yet live in Austria.
8. **Who is silent on economics?** No interchange/merchant-fee, fraud-liability, or revenue-split detail disclosed → the actual unit economics for banks/merchants are unstated.
9. **Is "shareholder" materially different from "member"?** Yes — cap-table/governance stake vs distribution membership; signals deeper commitment but adds consortium coordination cost.
10. **Does the sovereignty framing hold up?** Politically driven; critics (EUobserver, philippdubach) argue it oversells a low-adoption product and is more infrastructure arbitrage than sovereignty.
11. **What breaks the network effect?** Two-sided adoption (consumer + merchant) must both clear; bank shelf-space ≠ consumer habit. Card/contactless inertia in Austria is the main risk.
12. **Governance-dilution risk?** Each new shareholder broadens reach but slows unified product decisions — the coordination tax that historically sank pan-European bank schemes (Monnet). (hypothesis)
13. **Timeline to actual Austrian consumer launch?** **Open** — no date published; likely P2P first then POS/e-commerce, mirroring Germany (2027 for cross-border POS per EPI roadmap).
14. **Who needs whom more?** EPI needs Austria's two banking pillars for credible coverage; the banks need a card alternative to cut interchange — roughly mutual, but EPI gains distribution it can't build alone.
15. **Second-order: does broader EuroPA/EPI scale help or fragment?** Interoperability with Bizum/Bancomat/SIBS/Vipps (~130M) strengthens the bloc, but interop ≠ unified UX; cross-border features only ship 2026–27.

Importance: 3/5 — A meaningful expansion: Wero locks in Austria's two dominant banking groups (Erste + Raiffeisen) as cap-table shareholders, near-completing domestic distribution for a 9M market and reinforcing the pan-European A2A/anti-card thesis. But it is governance/capital, not a live launch (still pending board approvals), with zero Austrian traction, low engagement even in mature Germany, and an existing local rival (Bluecode). Not novel — same playbook as prior EPI onboardings. Solid building block, not a breakthrough.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** European account-to-account (A2A) payments — the subvertical Wero plays in. Size/growth: A2A reached ~17% of European e-commerce transaction value in 2024 and is projected to exceed €850bn in transaction value by 2026 (per coverage citing market trackers, via europeanbusinessmagazine.com, as of 2026); consumer A2A globally is forecast at ~$5.7tn by 2029 (per Juniper Research, as of 2025). Europe digital-wallet + A2A rails are cited compounding at ~17.7% CAGR (per Mordor Intelligence, as of 2026) — treat the single-source CAGR as indicative, not gospel. Structure: the consumer-payments value chain is consolidated at the scheme layer (Visa/Mastercard) but A2A is a fragmented overlay of national bank consortia (Wero/EPI, Bizum, Bancomat, Vipps) stitched via the EuroPA MoU (~130M users / ~72% of EU population target). Entry barriers are extreme — you must onboard whole national banking systems on both buyer and seller sides, which is regulation- and network-effect-gated, not capital-gated. Drivers + "why now": (1) the EU Instant Payments Regulation (in force Jan 2025) mandates 10-second euro transfers at no extra cost, supplying the free rail A2A rides on; (2) PSD2 + a UK Competition Appeal Tribunal ruling (Jun 2025) that card interchange breaches competition law pressure the 0.3–1.5% European interchange Wero bypasses; (3) politically-pushed "European sovereignty" vs US schemes. Second-order: the rail being a regulated public utility means Wero's edge is distribution/UX, not infrastructure — i.e. arbitrage, not a moat (cf. philippdubach.com critique).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: registered vs active users, TPV (transaction value), transactions/user, merchant acceptance breadth, and per-transaction cost vs card interchange. Players + basis of competition: incumbents = Visa/Mastercard (network + acceptance ubiquity; Mastercard alone >900M EU cards per note); the A2A bloc = Wero/EPI, plus Austria's domestic Bluecode (QR A2A, already live — Austria is NOT greenfield, cf. [[Bluecode expands QR payment reach to 60 markets via Alipay+]]); processors that ride either rail = Nexi, Worldline. Basis: distribution (bank shelf-space) + consumer habit, not price alone. Recent moves: EuroPA sovereign-network MoU (2026-02, cf. [[Pan-European payment providers sign sovereign network MoU]]); ACI Worldwide infra deal (2026-06, cf. [[ACI Worldwide and EPI to power instant payments in Europe]]); Nexi e-commerce enablement (2026-04, cf. [[Nexi implements Wero for eCommerce in Germany]]); Wero e-commerce live in Germany late-2025 (Lidl, Decathlon, Rossmann). Protagonist position: Wero is the front-runner of the A2A bloc by scale (>55M users pan-EU pre-Austria) but is catching up vs cards on actual usage — in Germany, its most mature market, A2A is reportedly only ~5% of transaction volume (per note). Moat: nascent network effects from near-universal bank coverage (Erste + Raiffeisen ≈ majority of Austrian retail banking), but two-sided adoption is unproven — bank shelf-space ≠ consumer habit `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** Wero/EPI is private — no market cap, no disclosed Austrian revenue/TPV, so a direct Wero multiple = no data. EPI cumulative development cost is cited at ≥€1.5bn ("probably much higher", per Datos Insights / EPC, as of 2026) — a sunk-cost signal, not a valuation. External comps are the listed European card-exposed processors, used to frame the A2A re-rating risk, NOT to value Wero:
- **Nexi** (EPI shareholder, Wero e-commerce enabler): EV €5.08bn / Rev (ttm) €6.27bn = **0.81x EV/Revenue** (per companiesmarketcap / Yahoo, as of 2026-06-22). EV/EBITDA: €5.08bn / €1.9bn EBITDA = ~2.7x by these figures, though one source cites 4.6x "lowest in Nexi's history" (down from ~17–18x in 2021) — the two are inconsistent, so flag EV/EBITDA as `[UNSOURCED]`/check, but the directional read is unambiguous: cheap.
- **Worldline** (EPI shareholder): market cap ~€0.57bn (per Yahoo, as of 2026) — distressed, sub-1x revenue territory; exact EV/Rev = no data (revenue not captured).
- Distribution not computed (only one internally-consistent EV/Rev figure). Qualitative read: European card-acquiring/processing peers trade at sub-1x revenue — well below the EV/Rev ~0.5–20x range floor and far under their own 2021 highs. This is a **structurally re-rated, "cheap vs history" cohort**, and the de-rate is itself partly the market pricing the A2A/interchange-compression thesis Wero embodies. Internal comps confirm the playbook cadence: [[Wero launches e-commerce payments in Belgium]], [[Wero to launch in Luxembourg with Payconiq and EPI]], [[Bancomat, EPI and SIBS demonstrate cross-border payment interoperability]], [[Mollie joins EPI Company to accelerate Wero]] — all member/shareholder/geo expansion, none disclosing economics.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Announced ≠ live, deal not closed** — this is a cap-table/governance event; Wero is not usable by Austrian consumers, no launch date, and the three Raiffeisen Landesbanken remain subject to supervisory-board approval. Why: the entire "near-universal Austrian coverage" thesis is contingent, not banked.
2. **Adoption/engagement risk** — the ~55M figure is pan-EU registered (France-heavy), and even mature Germany sits at ~5% of transaction volume; Austria adds a live local rival (Bluecode) and card/contactless inertia. Why: two-sided habit must clear on both consumer and merchant sides or the bank shelf-space is stranded.
3. **Consortium coordination / governance-dilution tax** — each new shareholder broadens reach but slows unified product decisions; this is the failure mode that sank prior pan-European bank schemes (Monnet). Why: more owners → slower, compromised consumer UX, the opposite of what's needed to beat single-stack schemes `(hypothesis)`.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is incremental consolidation of the A2A bloc, not a re-rating catalyst on its own — Wero buys distribution it cannot build alone, completing DACH-region coverage. Falsifiable thesis: if Wero cannot move past P2P into merchant POS/e-commerce volume in a launched market (German A2A share rising materially above ~5%, or first credible Austrian TPV figures post-launch), the sovereignty narrative is infrastructure arbitrage, not displacement. Trigger to watch: an actual Austrian consumer go-live date + the first POS/e-commerce acceptance numbers; what breaks the thesis is sustained sub-10% transaction-volume share in mature markets despite near-universal bank coverage.
