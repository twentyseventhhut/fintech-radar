Waymo and Uber quietly part ways in Phoenix
Waymo robotaxis are no longer available on Uber’s ride-hail app in Phoenix, Arizona, ending a nearly three-year partnership in the city, both companies confirmed to TechCrunch on Monday. 
Uber said it is readying the launch of a separate autonomous vehicle partnership in the city, but did not name the partner. Waymo told TechCrunch that the vehicles Uber used for this “pilot” program have already been integrated into its own Phoenix fleet, available through its app. 
Waymo users started noticing that the company’s vehicles were absent from Uber’s network in recent days. Waymo’s vehicles are still available on Uber in Austin and Atlanta, for instance. Uber told TechCrunch the two companies decided to end the deployment in Phoenix as it was the contracted end date.
The quiet end to this partnership in Phoenix, which Waymo said happened in May, comes as the Alphabet-owned company is starting to put its newest robotaxis — the Zeekr-made van it calls Ojai — on the road. It’s also happening as the Uber-Waymo relationship appears to be wearing in some places , with the two companies poised to directly compete against each other in London as early as this year.
Still, both companies praised the collaboration in Phoenix as a successful jumping-off point for their respective robotaxi plans, which have gotten increasingly ambitious since 2023. 
“This was a productive pilot that paved the way for future expansions and partnerships across the globe. After hundreds of thousands of trips with Uber, we have integrated these vehicles back into our Phoenix fleet, where they will continue to serve riders through Waymo, including our public transit integration with Via, and delivery with DoorDash,” Waymo told TechCrunch. “We’re grateful to all of the Uber customers who took fully autonomous trips with us, and we look forward to continuing to serve the Phoenix community.”
“Phoenix was our first pilot market with Waymo and was an intentionally limited deployment, reaching just over a dozen vehicles dedicated to the program. We learned a lot from that collaboration, which helped us to quickly scale Austin and Atlanta, where hundreds of Waymo AVs are available exclusively on Uber and our  coverage area continues to expand ,” Uber said.
The robotaxi landscape looks much different than it did when these two companies kicked off this collaboration in 2023. Back when it was first announced, the idea of Uber and Waymo partnering up still seemed unlikely given their messy legal battle that ended in a settlement in 2018. Robotaxis as a technology were in a far more uncertain place, as no operator had reached scale yet. Cruise was still seen as a viable competitor, as it had not yet gone through its own scandal and been absorbed into General Motors.
In the three years since, Waymo has grown its fleet to around 4,000 vehicles, and Uber has inked deals to add dozens of autonomous vehicle partners to its network. 
This Phoenix partnership remained an unusual one, as it was the only city where Waymo operated directly and through Uber. Waymo is in the process of launching in around 20 new cities this year, is operating in 11 major U.S. metro areas, and the company offers more than 500,000 trips every week.

# Context-enrichment: Waymo and Uber quietly part ways in Phoenix
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Freshness: FRESH.** This is a genuine new development (the wind-down of a 2023 partnership + Uber readying an unnamed Phoenix replacement), not a re-report. No prior note in the corpus covers the Waymo–Uber Phoenix deal or its end; adjacent notes cover Uber's *other* AV partners and the broader robotaxi race (see [1]/[4]), so they are context, not duplicates.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Waymo robotaxis disappeared from Uber's ride-hail app in Phoenix; the removal actually happened in **May 2026** and was **confirmed by both companies on 2026-06-29** (surfaced only after riders noticed). Both frame it as a graceful, pre-planned expiry: Uber calls it "the contracted end date" of an "intentionally limited pilot" of "just over a dozen vehicles"; Waymo calls it "a productive pilot." Original tie-up: announced **2023-10-26**, part of a multi-year deal; it let Uber riders be matched to a Waymo on UberX/Green/Comfort, plus Uber Eats delivery (the delivery leg had already ended ~mid-2025).

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** Phoenix is Waymo's oldest, deepest market where it runs its own direct app at scale — so it does **not need Uber's demand funnel** there. The ~12 cars aren't leaving Phoenix; they were reabsorbed into Waymo's own fleet (own app + DoorDash delivery + Via transit). So this is Waymo **pulling supply out of a competitor-partner's marketplace in a city it owns**, not a retreat. The "contracted end date" framing conveniently hides who declined to renew — the leverage sat with Waymo. Uber simultaneously says it is "readying" a new **unnamed** Phoenix AV partner (EV press floats Lucid/Nuro — unconfirmed). Trade press frames it as "tensions deepening," not amicable.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Waymo (the leader):** ~500,000 paid rides/week (doubled from ~250k in Apr 2025; targeting ~1M/week by end-2026), ~11 US metros, fleet cited at "~4,000" (independent trackers say ~3,000–3,500 — treat 4,000 as upper-bound/PR). New **Ojai** van (Zeekr-built in China, cheaper-to-produce; passenger service began May 2026) is the unit-economics play.
- **Tesla Robotaxi:** unsupervised only in Austin (~20–42 cars, third-party counts), supervised elsewhere; added Miami Jul 2026. Still tiny vs Waymo (see [[US proposes axing AV brake-pedal rule in boost for Tesla]], [[Tesla settles FSD crash lawsuit as federal probes continue]]).
- **Uber's own multi-partner AV stack:** the real story — Uber has no in-house AV (sold ATG to Aurora, 2020) so it aggregates: Wayve (London — see [[Wayve launches $85M employee tender at $8.5B valuation]]), Momenta (see [[Chinese autonomous-driving firm Momenta prepares US IPO]]), Pony.ai, WeRide, Nuro+Lucid, May Mobility. Ecosystem plays like [[Aseon Labs raises $10M to service robotaxis between rides]] show the buildout.

**Why the landscape is this way / second order:** Uber's aggregation is both strength and hedge born of weakness — because it owns no AV tech, Waymo can walk from any market once Waymo's direct app matures there. Waymo keeps Uber only where it lacks its own demand (Austin, Atlanta).

## [2] Company history / fit
The relationship was always a **frenemy** arrangement grafted onto litigation scar tissue: the 2017 Waymo v. Uber trade-secret suit (Levandowski) settled Feb 2018 with Uber paying Alphabet **~$245M in equity (~0.34% stake)** — so Alphabet has been an Uber shareholder throughout the "partnership." The fit today: Uber = distribution; Waymo = the only at-scale driverless tech. It works where Uber brings demand Waymo lacks (Austin/Atlanta, hundreds of AVs exclusive to Uber); it stops working where Waymo already owns the market (Phoenix) or where they'll compete (London).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
The *event* itself is low-novelty (a ~12-vehicle pilot expiring), but the **signal** is real: it's the first concrete case of Waymo **disintermediating Uber** in a mature market. Genuine traction underneath: 500k rides/week, ~10x growth in <2 yrs, 11 metros — Waymo is the clear commercial leader; the Ojai is the credible cost-down lever. Value-add question: who captures the robotaxi margin — the tech owner (Waymo) or the aggregator (Uber)? Phoenix is a data point that **Waymo captures it once its own app scales**, leaving Uber the marginal/hard markets.

**Why it matters (deeper):** The central question shifts from "is Waymo–Uber fraying?" to "in the endgame, is Uber a durable demand layer or a temporary crutch Waymo discards city-by-city?" Phoenix suggests the latter pattern — worth watching whether Austin/Atlanta exclusivity survives.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **London 2026 showdown:** first true head-to-head — Waymo's own app (Jaguar I-Pace testing since Apr 2026) vs Waymo-rival Uber+Wayve; both hedge on UK regulation, no firm commercial date.
- **Phoenix:** watch for Uber's named replacement partner (Lucid/Nuro rumored, unconfirmed).
- **Economics:** no operator has confirmed robotaxi profitability as of Q1 2026; break-even models push real margins past ~2030. One analyst set $100 on Uber (~39% upside), treating Waymo as "free optionality." Bull case rests on future cost curves + utilization, not current margins.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Robotaxi / autonomous ride-hailing. The sector is pre-consolidation and capital-intensive: Waymo raised $16bn in Feb 2026 at a $126bn post-money (up from a $45bn Series C in Oct 2024) — per Waymo/CNBC. Alphabet's Other Bets (Waymo is the main revenue line) posted ~$411mn revenue against a ~$2.1bn operating loss in Q1 2026, i.e. the whole segment is still burning ~5x its revenue (per Alphabet 8-K via CNBC/Yahoo) — a signal that AV economics are not yet self-funding at scale. Structure: two distinct value-chain positions are crystallizing — (a) full-stack owned networks that carry the capex/liability (Waymo, Tesla), and (b) demand/aggregation layers that avoid vehicle ownership (Uber). Barriers on the owned side are extreme (capital, regulatory approvals city-by-city, safety-validation data); barriers on the aggregation side are demand network effects and merchant/rider distribution. Why now: Waymo has crossed a scale threshold (~500k+ paid trips/week, ~4,000 vehicles per the note; ~15mn rides in 2025 per Waymo), which is exactly what lets it internalize demand it previously rented from Uber.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: paid trips/week, fleet size, cost-per-mile / per-vehicle utilization, geographic coverage (metros live), safety miles. Key players & basis of competition: Waymo (owned full-stack, tech + safety-record lead, ~11 US metros live, ~20 new cities targeted 2026 per note); Tesla (owned, betting on low-cost FSD but stalled — only ~20 active unsupervised vehicles in Austin a year in, per Electrek Jun–Jul 2026, geofence expanded but fleet not); Uber (pure aggregator, 20+ AV partners incl. Lucid, Nuro, Stellantis, Wayve, WeRide, launched an "Autonomous Solutions" division Feb 2026, targeting 15+ cities by end-2026 per Uber IR/Yahoo). Recent moves: Waymo–Uber ended the Phoenix deployment in May 2026 (note); the two are set to compete head-to-head in London later this year (note); Uber signed a non-binding L4 MoU with Stellantis+Wayve and a Nvidia deal to scale to 100k vehicles from 2027 (per Uber IR). Protagonist position: this note is really about the *relationship* — Waymo is **ahead** on owned-network scale and is now pulling in-house the vehicles Uber used to distribute (Phoenix fleet reintegrated). Uber's moat is demand aggregation / lowest wait-times across many suppliers (analysis); Waymo's moat is intangibles — its autonomy stack + safety data + manufacturing (Zeekr "Ojai" van "built to make money"). The Phoenix split shows the structural tension: Waymo doesn't need Uber where it already has density.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Disintermediation of the aggregator (Uber).** The core signal here: where Waymo reaches its own density (Phoenix), it reintegrates the fleet and drops off Uber's app. If owned networks keep pulling supply in-house city-by-city, Uber's AV story degrades from "the network everyone rides on" to "the network only where owners lack density" — margin/relevance risk to Uber's autonomy thesis. Second-order: Uber's ~2.9x multiple already reflects skepticism.
2. **Capital-intensity / loss-funding on the owned side.** Other Bets ~$2.1bn quarterly operating loss and Waymo's $16bn raise show owned networks are years from stand-alone profitability; a funding-market tightening would hit the expansion pace (20+ cities) hardest.
3. **Execution/scaling gap (Tesla, and by extension "cheap AV" bulls).** Tesla stuck at ~20 active unsupervised cars a year in despite geofence expansion (Electrek) — the sector's cost-curve promises are unproven at scale; "announced cities" ≠ "vehicles on the road." De-PR: both Waymo and Uber called Phoenix a "successful pilot," but the substance is a contract *ending* and a partner *going quiet* on who Uber's new Phoenix AV partner is.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The Phoenix exit reframes Waymo–Uber from partnership toward *frenemy/competition* (London head-to-head this year) — a re-rating trigger for the "Uber-as-AV-aggregator" thesis. Falsifiable thesis: if Uber's next AV wins are only *non-Waymo* suppliers (Lucid/Nuro/Wayve) in cities where owners lack density, Uber is being pushed to second-tier supply — watch (a) whether Waymo comes off Uber in Austin/Atlanta next (currently still on), and (b) who Uber's unnamed Phoenix partner turns out to be. Thesis breaks if Waymo *re-lists* on Uber or signs fresh multi-city aggregation deals — that would signal owners still need Uber's demand even at density.
