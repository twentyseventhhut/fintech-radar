# Context-enrichment: Bank of England sets out draft rules for systemic stablecoins
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

The aggregated digest text ("supports innovation while ensuring stability... backing assets, prompt redemptions, strong consumer protections") is pure press-release framing. The real story is the **end of a 8-month regulatory climbdown**: the BoE on 2026-06-22/23 published its **final Policy Statement + draft rules** for sterling-denominated *systemic* stablecoins, and the headline is what it **dropped**, not what it added.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **De-PR'd fact:** This is the BoE's response-to-consultation + draft Code of Practice closing the loop on its **November 2025 Consultation Paper** "Sterling-denominated systemic stablecoins" (CP closed 2026-02-10). It is a *policy statement + draft rules for further feedback (to ~2026-09-22)*, NOT a live regime. No sterling stablecoin has been designated systemic by HM Treasury; the GBP-stablecoin market is ~$12m (vs a ~$300bn+ global, USD-dominated market) — the regime is effectively theoretical for years.
- **The substance = the U-turn on holding limits.** The Nov 2025 CP proposed **temporary per-coin holding limits of £20,000 for individuals and £10m for businesses** (with an exemptions regime for large businesses/exchanges/supermarkets), justified by an explicit Financial Stability Paper modelling bank-deposit flight (the £20k threshold would have covered ~94% of consumers, ~2.1x monthly income). Industry called these "unworkable" / an "innovation blocker." The June 2026 draft **scraps the individual/business holding limits** and (per reporting) replaces them with a **temporary per-coin issuance cap (~£40bn) and loosened backing (reported ~70% short-dated gilts / 30% unremunerated BoE deposits, up from 60/40)**. _Flag: the holding-limits removal + softer backing are well-corroborated across reporting (Decrypt/CoinDesk/fintechfutures); the exact £40bn cap and 70/30 split are **medium-confidence** (legal-alert/press summaries, not a direct read of the BoE PDF, which blocks automated fetch) — treat as reported, not confirmed verbatim._
- **What carries over (confirmed from Nov 2025 primary source):** no interest/yield to coinholders; **par redemption by end of business day**; backing split between short-dated UK gilts and **unremunerated** deposits at the BoE; possible **central-bank liquidity backstop** for issuers under stress; "systemic" designation made by **HM Treasury** (Banking Act 2009 criteria), after which the issuer moves from FCA-only to joint BoE (prudential/stability) + FCA (conduct) oversight. Non-systemic / crypto-trading stablecoins stay with the FCA.
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** the BoE's *original* design was unusually conservative by global standards — it is the only major regime to have floated **per-person holding caps** and to **force a chunk of reserves into zero-yield central-bank money**. That reveals the BoE's true priority is **protecting commercial-bank deposit funding** (mortgage/credit creation), not stablecoin competitiveness. The June climbdown reveals the second-order constraint: **regulatory tax competition**. Once the US GENIUS Act (signed 2025-07-18) imposed *no* holding limits and the EU MiCA regime went live, the BoE's caps risked pushing sterling-stablecoin activity offshore — so it swapped a *demand-side* control (how much a user can hold) for a *supply-side* control (an aggregate issuance ceiling), which is less hostile to users while still throttling systemic deposit migration. Deputy Governor Sarah Breeden's own framing shifted from "harsh but beneficial" (Nov 2025) to conceding the rules may have been "overly conservative" (2026 H1).

## [1] Competitors / peers (regulatory regimes, not companies)
- **US — GENIUS Act** (signed 2025-07-18): payment-stablecoin federal framework. **1:1 reserves** in cash / insured deposits / short-dated Treasuries / repos / govt MMFs; **no yield to holders**; **no holding limits, no issuance cap**; holders get bankruptcy priority. → The most issuer- and user-permissive of the three.
- **EU — MiCA** (EMT/ART rules applied 2024-06-30): 100% reserves and **par redemption** for e-money tokens; but a **€200m/day (or 1m tx/day) cap on "significant" non-euro stablecoins** used as a means of exchange — a deliberate brake on USD-stablecoin penetration. → Caps by *transaction volume on foreign-currency coins*, not by holding size or aggregate issuance of the domestic coin.
- **UK — BoE systemic regime:** even post-climbdown the UK remains the **most restrictive on issuer economics** (forced zero-yield BoE deposits) and uniquely uses an **aggregate per-coin issuance cap**. Position: **catching up / deliberately laggard**. The BoE insists it will move "just as quickly as the US," but the US has a *law*; the UK still has *draft rules*.
- **Why the lay of the land is this way (analysis):** each jurisdiction is optimising a different fear. US = growth + Treasury-bill demand (Standard Chartered projected up to $1tn new T-bill demand by 2028); EU = monetary sovereignty vs the dollar; UK = financial-stability / bank-disintermediation. The UK's structural problem is real (analysis): a large migration into stablecoins drains the deposit base that funds UK lending — hence caps. But the cure (zero-yield reserves at the BoE) **structurally taxes the GBP-stablecoin business model** relative to a GENIUS coin that can hold ~100% in yielding T-bills. That is why no one is rushing to issue.

Prior corpus on regimes: [[This Week in Fintech Hong Kong Stablecoins Ordinance overview]] (HMT/BoE/FCA developing fiat-referenced rules; overseas issuers need FCA authorisation), [[Bank of England considers stablecoins for wholesale markets]] (BoE "open-minded" on wholesale use, the DSS carve-out).

## [2] "Company" history / fit (the BoE's trajectory)
Dated trajectory (all confirmed in corpus):
- **2023-11-06:** BoE Discussion Paper on systemic stablecoin payment systems (+ FCA DP23/4). Originally proposed **all reserves as BoE deposits** — even more conservative.
- **2025-07:** [[Bank of England considers stablecoins for wholesale markets]] — Sasha Mills "open-minded" on wholesale.
- **2025-09-16:** [[Bank of England proposes stablecoin holding caps]] — the £20k/£20,000 retail cap first leaks; industry backlash begins.
- **2025-10-13:** [[Bank of England plans stablecoin cap exemptions]] — BoE plans waivers for exchanges/large businesses (per Bloomberg).
- **2025-11-07/10:** [[Bank of England consults on systemic stablecoin rules]] — formal CP confirms £20k individual / £10m business limits, **60% gilts / 40% unremunerated BoE deposits** (95% gilts step-up for new entrants), CP open to 2026-02-10.
- **2026-03-12:** [[Bank of England open to revising stablecoin caps]] — senior official signals willingness to reconsider after backlash.
- **2026-05-15:** [[Bank of England softens proposed stablecoin holding limits]] — Breeden tells the FT the BoE is "actively exploring alternatives," admits it may have been too cautious (SVB-stress assumptions reassessed).
- **2026-06-22/23:** final Policy Statement + draft rules — limits dropped.
- **Why the BoE acts this way (analysis):** it started from the deposit-stability frame (it is a *central bank*, not a growth regulator), softened only because of two structural pressures — (1) **international tax competition** (GENIUS/MiCA already live) and (2) **a thin sterling-stablecoin market** that gave the BoE room to relax without near-term stability cost. The climbdown fits a clear pattern of progressive concession (2023 all-BoE-deposits → 2025 60/40 → 2026 ~70/30 + caps gone), each step trading stability conservatism for competitiveness as the political cost of looking like a laggard rose.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new in the June draft:** the **abandonment of per-person holding limits** — the BoE's most distinctive and most criticised feature — and the substitution of an **aggregate issuance cap** as the deposit-flight guardrail. That is a real policy-design innovation (a supply-side rather than demand-side throttle) and the main reason this is newsworthy vs the Nov 2025 CP.
- **Traction = essentially none (this is the anti-PR core).** There is **no live systemic sterling stablecoin and no HMT systemic designation**. The GBP-stablecoin sector is ~$12m (one notable token, tGBP, ~$1.5m). The whole regime is a draft for a market that does not yet meaningfully exist. The FCA's *separate* non-systemic regime (CP25/14, issued 2025; PS expected ~summer 2026) is where real near-term issuance would happen — and even there the UK is testing in a **sandbox** ([[Revolut among firms testing stablecoins in UK FCA sandbox]]).
- **Why the value-add is real or not (analysis, 2 levels deep):** the *regulatory* value-add is real (removing caps materially de-risks building a UK sterling stablecoin). But **who captures the margin** is the deeper question: with a chunk of reserves forced into **zero-yield BoE deposits** and **no yield passable to holders**, the issuer's net interest margin is structurally thinner than a GENIUS-Act issuer's. So the regime still **commoditises sterling-stablecoin economics** — the BoE captures the float benefit (free funding), the issuer carries the cost. The central question is therefore **not "is the UK open for stablecoins?" but "can a sterling stablecoin make money under forced zero-yield reserves when a dollar coin keeps the T-bill yield?"** Until that math improves, dropping holding limits removes a *demand* ceiling but leaves the *supply-side* economics unattractive — which is why traction stays near zero.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Next steps:** feedback on the draft rules (~to 2026-09-22), then BoE + FCA finalise **Codes of Practice / joint approach document later in 2026**; regime expected operational ~2027. HMT must designate a coin "systemic" before any of this binds.
- **Sentiment:** industry broadly welcomes the climbdown (Coinbase's Tom Duff Gordon, the Payments Association's Riccardo Tordera, UK Cryptoasset Business Council had called caps "bad for savers/the City/sterling" and "nearly impossible to enforce without digital IDs"). But criticism persists on the **30% zero-yield BoE-deposit drag** — the House of Lords Financial Services Regulation Committee (2026-06-02) warned it "could have a significant impact on the business viability of stablecoin issuers in the UK." Circle (FCA-registered EMI) is positioned to pursue UK authorisation; Tether's natural route stays offshore. _(Lords/Breeden quotes are external-research-sourced; treat as reported.)_
- **Why the market goes this way + second-order effect (analysis):** the BoE has solved the *political* problem (looking competitive) without solving the *economic* one (issuer margin). Counterintuitive second-order effect: **removing holding limits may matter less than it looks**, because the binding constraint on a sterling stablecoin was never user demand caps — it was the **zero-yield reserve tax**. So the headline "BoE drops caps" could prove a soft win: it clears a symbolic obstacle while the real economic obstacle (and the dominance of USD coins) remains. The genuine UK competitive lever is the **FCA non-systemic regime + sandbox**, not this systemic regime, which only bites at a scale (£tens of bn) no GBP coin is close to.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Stablecoins are now a converging-regulation, scale-driven sub-sector of crypto/payments. Global supply ~$300bn+ and growing (~$282bn estimated end-2025, per Citi/Visual Capitalist via coincentral/visualcapitalist, as of 2026-06), with sell-side forecasts of ~$1.9bn base / ~$4tn bull by 2030 (Citi GPS, 2026) and ~$2tn by 2028 (Standard Chartered) — treat as analyst projections, not facts. ~99% of circulating value is USD-pegged (per FinTech Weekly, 2026-06); sterling stablecoins are effectively a greenfield market today. Structure: globally concentrated (USDT ~$186-190bn and USDC ~$74-75bn together ≈93% of total cap, per coinlaw/coindesk, as of 2026-06), but the UK sterling segment is unbuilt — the FCA only selected its first sandbox issuers (Revolut, Monee, ReStabilise, VVTX) in Feb 2026 [[Revolut to test GBP stablecoin in FCA sandbox]]. Barriers are now primarily regulatory/capital, not technical: authorization-gated issuance, 1:1 segregated reserves, at-par redemption — the same core architecture across MiCA, the US GENIUS Act and the UK regime [[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]]. **Why now:** this BoE policy statement (2026-06-23) closes the systemic-tier piece of the UK's two-regime design (FCA for non-systemic, BoE + FCA jointly once HMT designates a coin "systemic"). Notably, the BoE *softened* its earlier stance: it dropped the proposed individual (£10-20k) and corporate (£10m) holding caps and instead set a temporary £40bn (~$53bn) issuance ceiling per systemic coin, and raised the permitted share of reserves in short-term UK gilts to 70% from 60% (remainder in non-interest-bearing BoE deposits) (per CoinDesk/The Block/Payment Expert, 2026-06-22). Consultation closes 2026-09-22; Code of Practice to be finalized end-2026; regime live from 2027.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: circulating supply / market cap, reserve composition & yield retained by issuer (issuers keep the float income; holders are barred from interest under all three regimes), redemption reliability, and venue distribution (regulated CASPs vs self-custody/DeFi). Key players: incumbents are USD issuers — Tether/USDT (~$186-190bn) and Circle/USDC (~$74-75bn, public as CRCL); USDC has out-grown USDT in % terms for two straight years on regulated-venue demand (CoinDesk, 2026-01). In the UK specifically there is no incumbent — the protagonist here is the *regulator* (BoE), and the would-be issuers are sandbox-stage fintechs, with Revolut the most credible given scale (though still in UK banking "mobilization phase," deposits capped at £50k) [[Revolut to test GBP stablecoin in FCA sandbox]]. Basis of competition: distribution and regulatory standing, not price. Recent moves with dates: FCA sandbox cohort named Feb 2026; House of Lords committee urged BoE to reconsider restrictions (2026-06-02); BoE softened rules (2026-06-22/23). Position: the UK is *catching up / deliberately building* a sterling market from zero rather than defending share — the policy softening is an explicit response to fintech-hub-flight criticism (New York/Singapore/Dubai). Moat for a future systemic GBP issuer = regulatory license + network effects, but unproven `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** This is a regulation event, not a deal — no valuation/round/metrics attach to the BoE, so issuer multiples are "no data" for the protagonist. Reference comp: Circle (CRCL) is the only public pure-play stablecoin issuer; its market cap and USDC float are publicly observable but a clean EV/Revenue requires verified revenue — `[UNSOURCED]` here (no audited 2026 revenue figure confirmed in sources). Price-per-dollar-of-float as a sanity gauge: Circle equity vs ~$74-75bn USDC float — not computed without a verified market-cap figure (sources gave float, not a same-dated CRCL cap) → "distribution not computed, qualitative comparison." Internal comps (regulatory/market precedents, not trading comps): [[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]], [[Connecting the Dots comparing MiCA and the GENIUS Act]], [[FDIC proposes first stablecoin rules under GENIUS Act]], [[Trump signs GENIUS Act stablecoin law]], [[Kenya drafts higher capital rules for stablecoin issuers]]. Read-across: every major regime mandates 1:1 segregated reserves + redemption + no holder yield; the UK is the strictest on reserve form (mandated unremunerated BoE deposits for ≥30% of backing), which directly compresses issuer economics — a real, not nominal, difference vs MiCA/GENIUS.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Economics at systemic scale (margin compression).** Forcing ≥30% of reserves into non-interest-bearing BoE deposits, plus a ban on paying holders interest, caps the float yield exactly when a coin gets large enough to matter. Second-order: a sterling stablecoin may be structurally less profitable than a USD peer the instant it succeeds, blunting the incentive to build one in the UK — the "cliff" the BoE just tried (partly) to soften by widening the gilt allowance to 70%.
2. **The £40bn issuance cap as a growth ceiling.** A per-coin circulation limit means a UK systemic stablecoin cannot scale into the $100bn+ league USD peers already occupy. Second-order: caps UK issuers' relevance in any global settlement use-case and entrenches USD dominance (~99% of supply) by default.
3. **Two-regime "cliff" / regulatory uncertainty.** A firm grows under lighter FCA rules, then crosses into the heavier BoE systemic regime at success — and the cap/threshold is explicitly "temporary" and revisable. Second-order: planning risk discourages the very scaling the regime says it wants; flagged publicly by the House of Lords and industry (fintech-hub flight to NY/Singapore/Dubai).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` The softening (caps dropped, 70% gilt allowance) signals the BoE prioritizing UK competitiveness over maximal prudence — a marginal re-rating *toward* a viable sterling market, but still bounded by the £40bn ceiling and no-yield rule. Falsifiable thesis: if the regime is genuinely attractive, expect ≥1 sandbox issuer (most likely Revolut) to commit to a live GBP stablecoin launch ahead of the 2027 go-live. Trigger to watch: consultation responses by 2026-09-22 and whether the final Code of Practice raises or removes the £40bn cap. What would break the thesis: issuers publicly conclude the reserve economics still don't work and route sterling ambitions through MiCA EMTs or US GENIUS structures instead.
