OpenAI unveils its first custom chip, built by Broadcom
On Wednesday, OpenAI unveiled its first custom-built inference processor, designed and manufactured in collaboration with Broadcom. Named Jalapeño, the new processor was designed specifically for the unique needs of OpenAI’s inference systems. OpenAI’s own AI models assisted in the development of the chip, the company said.
While the chip is still being tested, OpenAI says early results show significantly better performance-per-watt than current state-of-the-art alternatives.
The partnership was officially announced in October , but OpenAI’s chip plans have long been rumored as a way to reduce the company’s dependence on Nvidia’s GPUs. Google and Amazon have both built custom chips to serve a similar purpose, often called “AI accelerators” — silicon designed specifically to speed up machine learning workloads.
OpenAI president Greg Brockman explained the company’s approach to chip development on its in-house podcast , shortly after the Broadcom partnership was announced.
“We have a deep understanding of the workload,” Brockman said in the episode. “We’ve really been looking for specific workloads that are underserved, [and asking] how can we build something that will be able to accelerate what’s possible?”
Jalapeño is specifically designed for inference, the process of running pre-built AI models in response to user commands. In the announcement, OpenAI emphasized the chip’s low operating cost when running real-time coding models. It’s likely that more performance-intensive tasks like pre-training will still rely on Nvidia hardware, but even small reductions in inference costs could do a lot to improve the company’s bottom line.
Optimizing that inference system may prove to be a crucial factor in the economics of AI going forward — and it’s likely to take place at every level of the stack. OpenAI is already building agentic products like Codex and the models that power them, as well as data centers to run those models. Moving into purpose-built chips lets the company go even further in that process, as the company explained in its announcement.
“OpenAI is not only developing frontier models or building products on top of them; it is designing the infrastructure underneath them: chip architecture, kernels, memory systems, networking, scheduling, deployment systems, and product experience,” the company wrote. “Because OpenAI operates across the stack, each layer can be optimized around the same goal: making its models faster, more reliable, and more affordable for users.”

# Context-enrichment: OpenAI unveils Jalapeño, its first custom inference chip, with Broadcom
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- On 2026-06-24 OpenAI and Broadcom **unveiled** "Jalapeño" — OpenAI's first custom-built **inference** accelerator (OpenAI brands it an "Intelligence Processor"). It is NOT in production: engineering samples are "running ML workloads in the lab at production target frequency and power" (ran GPT-5.3-Codex-Spark); first servers/platforms targeted **online by end of 2026** (TechCrunch, OpenAI/Broadcom releases, SiliconANGLE).
- This is the **product reveal of an already-announced relationship**, not a new tie-up. The Broadcom collaboration was officially announced **2025-10-13**: 10 gigawatts of OpenAI-designed accelerators, deployment H2-2026 → end-2029 (corpus: [[Seven European fintech deals raise EUR 95 million in a week]] cites the ~$350–500bn order figure; OpenAI/Broadcom Oct releases). So "unveils its first custom chip" = first silicon out of a deal that is ~8 months old. **Why this framing**: PR anchors to "first chip" (novelty) rather than "first deliverable of a previously-priced megadeal" — the latter is less of a headline and invites the "is it on schedule / on spec" question.
- **Claimed (vendor, unverified) numbers**: "significantly better performance-per-watt than current state-of-the-art" (OpenAI/Broadcom); **~50% lower cost per inference token vs current GPU alternatives** and **~840 mm² reticle-sized die on TSMC 3nm with 8 HBM stacks, systolic-array** (single-source / secondary spec reporting — treat as `(unverified)`); **nine-month design→tape-out**, claimed among the fastest high-performance ASIC cycles ever, sped up by OpenAI's own models (OpenAI; Tom's Hardware).
- Stack of partners: OpenAI designs the accelerator + system architecture; **Broadcom** does silicon implementation, networking (Tomahawk Ethernet) and connectivity; **TSMC** fabs; **Celestica** does board/rack/system integration; **Microsoft** + others host the gigawatt-scale deployment. **Why structured this way**: OpenAI owns the high-margin/high-IP layer (architecture, kernels, serving) and outsources the capital-heavy commodity layers (fab, packaging, racks) — same playbook as Google (TPU+Broadcom) and Amazon (Trainium+Marvell).
- **Who's silent about what**: no absolute throughput, no TCO $/token absolute, no volume/units, no capex split, no statement on what share of OpenAI inference Jalapeño will carry vs the Nvidia fleet. "Performance-per-watt" with no baseline named is a classic un-falsifiable claim.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **The real comparison is hyperscaler custom silicon, not Nvidia head-on.** Google **TPU** (longest track record, now sold externally), Amazon **Trainium/Inferentia** (in talks to sell racks externally — [[AWS in talks to sell Trainium AI chip racks to outside companies]]), Meta **MTIA**, Microsoft **Maia**, plus China's self-silicon push (Alibaba/Baidu — [[China orders tech giants to halt Nvidia AI chip purchases]]) and [[ByteDance develops custom CPUs to power AI infrastructure]]. OpenAI is the **last of the megascale labs to ship its own inference ASIC** → catching up, not leading, on the "build your own silicon" axis.
- **On the design-partner axis OpenAI sits inside Broadcom's book**, alongside Google, Meta, Anthropic. Broadcom + Marvell control ~95% of custom-ASIC co-design; Broadcom alone ~70%+ (per Tom's Hardware/hashrateindex, May–Jun 2026). So the named "competitor" Nvidia loses share at the margin, while **Broadcom is the structural winner** regardless of which lab wins.
- **Mechanism delta vs Nvidia (one sentence):** Jalapeño trades GPU general-purpose flexibility for a fixed-function inference datapath co-designed to OpenAI's exact kernels/serving patterns → claimed perf/W and $/token wins on the *predictable, high-volume* slice (live ChatGPT/API/Codex), while training and experimentation stay on Nvidia. **Why the landscape is this way**: inference is becoming the dominant, repetitive, cost-sensitive workload (corpus: [[Baseten reportedly raising at $400 million valuation for AI model infrastructure]], [[This Week in Fintech Semafor on the rise of an AI futures market]]) — exactly the regime where custom ASICs beat GPUs on TCO; training stays heterogeneous, where GPUs hold.

## [2] Company history / fit
- OpenAI has been vertically integrating down the stack: frontier models → products (Codex, ChatGPT) → data centers (Stargate/Microsoft) → now **chip architecture**. The Oct-2025 Broadcom 10GW deal sat on top of ~$1tn of recent chip+DC commitments and the Roi/agentic-product moves ([[OpenAI acquires personal investing app Roi]]). **Why OpenAI is doing this**: at ~$25bn revenue run-rate (Jun-2026) but ~$14bn projected 2026 loss and inference cost ~$14.1bn in 2026, the **largest controllable cost line is inference compute**. Owning the inference chip is the most direct lever on gross margin and on reducing single-supplier (Nvidia) pricing power. Brockman: "We have a deep understanding of the workload… specific workloads that are underserved."
- Fit check: logical and on-trend — every lab with scale and a stable workload eventually co-designs silicon (Google did it years ahead). The risk is **timing/IP**, not strategy: OpenAI is paying a premium to compress the learning curve (nine-month cycle, AI-assisted design) rather than build a chip org over years.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty**: OpenAI's *first own silicon* is genuinely new for OpenAI, but the *category* (lab inference ASIC) is not — Google/AWS/Meta did it first. The credible novelty is the **nine-month design→tape-out using OpenAI's own models** — if real, that's a meta-story (AI accelerating its own hardware) bigger than the chip itself `(analysis)`.
- **Traction = essentially zero today.** No units shipped, nothing in production, no live cost data — only lab samples and end-2026 intent. By the pipeline's "announced ≠ live" rule, this is an **announcement**, not adoption. The credible signal of seriousness is the *prior* commitment (10GW, multi-year, TSMC capacity, Celestica racks) — money is committed even if silicon isn't deployed.
- **Who captures the margin**: even if Jalapeño works, **value leaks to Broadcom + TSMC** (they get paid on volume regardless), and the perf/W gain accrues to OpenAI only if it actually displaces enough Nvidia inference to move COGS. The chip does not touch training (Nvidia keeps that), so the GPU dependence narrative is **reduced, not removed** — Jalapeño carves the highest-volume, most-predictable slice, not the whole fleet.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Roadmap: "first step in a multi-generation compute platform"; first servers online end-2026, scaling into the 10GW Broadcom program through 2029 with Microsoft + partners. Watch for: actual deployment date slippage, a *named* perf/W baseline, and the first disclosed $/token or % of inference served on Jalapeño.
- Sentiment: Broadcom is the consensus structural winner — AI semi revenue $10.8bn in Q2-FY26 (+143% YoY), Q3 guide ~$16bn, FY27 reiterated >$100bn AI; OpenAI reveal read as further validation ([[Cadence stock gains 24% in three months]] shows the EDA/semi-AI tailwind in-corpus). **Counterintuitive second-order**: the more labs build custom ASICs, the more **Broadcom/TSMC become the chokepoint** — concentration shifts from Nvidia (GPU) to the design-services + fab duopoly, which is not obviously better for buyers. For OpenAI, success here is necessary but not sufficient: it improves unit economics but the company still loses ~$14bn/yr; chips help the denominator, not the demand.
- Regulatory backdrop: US-China silicon decoupling ([[China orders tech giants to halt Nvidia AI chip purchases]]) makes domestic/own-silicon strategically valued; TSMC concentration is the systemic risk.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

_Red-team / challenge questions (second-order). Each: answer from sources or "open"._

1. **Is this actually a new event or a re-announcement?** Mostly a re-announcement. The Broadcom partnership was announced 2025-10-13 (10GW, deploy H2-2026→2029). 2026-06-24 is the *first silicon reveal* of that deal, not a new tie-up. Weight comes from "first chip exists" not "new relationship".

2. **Is Jalapeño live or announced?** Announced/in-lab only. Engineering samples run in the lab (ran GPT-5.3-Codex-Spark at target freq/power); first servers targeted *end of 2026*. Zero production traction today.

3. **Are the performance-per-watt and ~50% cost-per-token claims verifiable?** No. Perf/W has no named baseline (un-falsifiable as stated). The ~50% $/token and the 3nm/840mm²/8-HBM die specs are single/secondary-source — marked `(unverified)`. Treat all as vendor claims pending a named benchmark.

4. **Does this actually reduce OpenAI's Nvidia dependence?** Partially, and only for inference. Jalapeño targets the high-volume, predictable inference slice (live ChatGPT/API/Codex); training and experimentation stay on Nvidia GPUs. Dependence is reduced at the margin, not removed.

5. **Who actually captures the margin?** Broadcom + TSMC capture guaranteed volume-based margin regardless of outcome; OpenAI captures value only if Jalapeño displaces enough Nvidia inference to move COGS. The structural winner of the whole custom-ASIC wave is Broadcom (~70%+ co-design share), not any single lab.

6. **Did OpenAI's prior infra bets "fly" (traction precedent)?** OpenAI's compute commitments (Stargate, ~$1tn chip+DC, 10GW Broadcom) are real money committed; product traction is strong (~$25bn revenue run-rate Jun-2026). But none of that proves a *self-designed chip* will hit spec on schedule — different risk (silicon execution vs demand).

7. **What's the precise mechanism delta vs an Nvidia GPU?** Fixed-function inference datapath co-designed to OpenAI's exact kernels/serving patterns, trading GPU general-purpose flexibility for perf/W and $/token on repetitive inference. Open: by how much, on which model sizes, at what batch/latency.

8. **Is the "nine-month cycle, AI-assisted" claim the real story?** Possibly bigger than the chip. If genuine, AI accelerating its own hardware design is a meta-signal `(analysis)`. Open: no independent confirmation of which steps were AI-assisted or the counterfactual cycle time.

9. **What is conspicuously not disclosed?** Absolute throughput, absolute $/token, unit volumes, capex split, and what share of OpenAI inference Jalapeño will serve. Silence on share = can't size the COGS impact.

10. **Is there a conflicting account of the deal (Nexus / $18bn)?** Yes — some reports reference "Project Nexus / $18bn first phase" and training+inference XPUs, vs the Oct-2025 framing of 10GW inference accelerators, H2-2026→2029. Treat the 10GW Oct deal as the confirmed anchor; "Nexus/$18bn" = single-thread, `open`.

11. **Does this fix OpenAI's economics?** No, it helps one cost line. Inference cost ~$14.1bn in 2026; even a large per-token saving improves gross margin but OpenAI is still projected to lose ~$14bn in 2026. Chips touch the denominator (cost), not the demand/loss problem.

12. **Does concentration risk improve or worsen?** Shifts, not improves. Less single-supplier exposure to Nvidia GPU pricing, but more exposure to the Broadcom design-services + TSMC fab duopoly — the new chokepoint. Counterintuitive second-order: more labs building ASICs entrenches Broadcom/TSMC.

13. **Why now (timing)?** Inference is becoming the dominant, cost-sensitive workload (the regime where custom ASICs beat GPUs on TCO), OpenAI's loss profile forces COGS action, and US-China silicon decoupling raises the strategic value of owned/domestic silicon.

14. **What would falsify the bullish read?** Deployment slips past end-2026; no named perf/W baseline ever published; Jalapeño serves a trivial % of inference; or Nvidia's next-gen perf/W erases the ASIC TCO gap. Watch the first disclosed $/token or % of inference served.

15. **Is OpenAI ahead or behind peers?** Behind on "build your own silicon" (Google/AWS/Meta shipped first), but the credible differentiator is the compressed design cycle. On the design-partner axis it's just another name in Broadcom's book.

Importance: 4/5 — High because it is a flagship signal in the dominant 2026 theme (custom inference ASICs eroding Nvidia's grip, value accruing to Broadcom/TSMC), from the most-watched AI company, with a credible meta-angle (AI-designed silicon, nine-month cycle). Held below 5 because there is zero production traction (lab samples only, end-2026 deployment), the headline perf/W and cost claims are unverified/un-baselined, and it is the silicon reveal of an 8-month-old deal rather than a brand-new development.
