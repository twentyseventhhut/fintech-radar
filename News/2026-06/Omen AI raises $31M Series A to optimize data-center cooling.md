Omen AI’s plan to optimize data centers is all wet
The AI-driven demand for compute power has data centers looking to squeeze more from every rack of GPUs. One consequence? Bacterial outbreaks.
The liquid for liquid-cooled chips is a mixture of water and a substance that inhibits bacteria growth. To run the chips hotter, data center managers can change the mix to include more water, which absorbs heat better, but leads to nasty contamination that clogs the flow. To solve that, they flush the system, which can mean shutting down a rack for five or six hours at a potential cost of millions of dollars. 
 Omen AI has a solution: A tiny spectrometer that can monitor that fluid health in real time, spotting bacterial growth before it becomes a massive problem. “You’re not risking huge amounts of downtime because you have no insight into what’s going on chemically,” explains CEO and founder Zach Laberge.
Today, Omen AI said it raised a $31 million Series A round, led by Nava Ventures and including participation from CRV, Vanderbilt University, Mann+Hummel, Starhill Holdings, and Hard Launch Capital, as well as personal investments from executives at Bridgestone, GM, Johnson Controls, and TensorWave.
Laberge founded his first company in 2020 when he was 14, raising $3 million to install sensors on construction equipment and ultimately dropping out of high school. (His father and mother, a former Minister of Education for Ontario, were supportive of his plan to carve his own path.)
After that startup shut down, Laberge started Omen in 2024, with the idea of focusing on fluid systems as the key to enabling construction machinery to be smart enough to know when it needed to be fixed. The idea was to replace the time-consuming process of extracting samples and sending them to a lab with real-time awareness. Besides bacterial growth, the device can spot pumps and pumps wearing out if it sees copper or chromium, or seals if it sees silicon.
Caterpillar dealerships were a key early customer for Omen’s heavy vehicles business, but Cat is also a major supplier of gas-powered turbines and generators to provide on-premises power for data centers. It didn’t take long for Omen to see where the wind was blowing.
“That was kind of the transition,” Laberge told TechCrunch. About six months ago, “a lot of the dealerships were saying, ‘Hey, we’re starting to put sensors on our turbines, can you guys do anything on the building side of things?’”
Omen discovered that those buildings are full of fluid, from their HVAC systems to their chip cooling. Spotting a new, fast-growing group of potential customers, Omen began to focus on data centers. 
“It’s rare to see such a young founder who has the respect of established, large corporations in a space that moves a bit more slowly,” said Cory Rellas, a partner at Nava Ventures who sits on Omen’s board. “For Omen in particular, much of our diligence came through our introductions with large customers which quickly validated their approach.”
Omen, which has raised $40 million since its founding in 2024, is working with a dozen data center customers as they build out their offering, including TensorWave, a company building an AI compute cloud on AMD chips.
“The fluid running through these massive systems is a critical variable that most of the industry is flying blind on,” Piotr Tomasik, TensorWave’s president, said in a statement. “Omen … see the future of infrastructure exactly the way we do, better monitoring to optimally support compute customers.”
While many organizations rely on mailing fluid samples to labs for insight, Omen isn’t alone in developing on-premises analytics — Pyxis, an established water-monitoring firm, rolled out its data center coolant monitoring product earlier this month.
The key tech advances that unlocked this approach are recent improvements in both optical technologies and signal processing software. “Hardware is just cheap enough that it makes sense to play at scale, and then signal processing lets us make more sense out of the noise,” Laberge said.

# Context-enrichment: Omen AI raises $31M Series A to optimize data-center cooling
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**FRESHNESS: fresh.** No prior Omen AI note in the corpus (first coverage; single TechCrunch mention 2026-06-29). Not a duplicate/re-run. Adjacent prior notes exist on the data-center power/cooling theme — see [[CME suffers multi-hour outage from data center cooling failure]], [[Datacentres use 6% of UK and US electricity, says IDCA]], [[Scala Data Centers secures $328 million for Chile expansion]] — but none cover this deal.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Omen AI (San Francisco, founded 2024, CEO Zach Laberge, 20) raised a **$31M Series A**, led by **Nava Ventures**, with CRV, Vanderbilt University, Mann+Hummel, Starhill Holdings, Hard Launch Capital + angels from Bridgestone, GM, Johnson Controls, TensorWave. **~$40M total raised** since 2024 (TechCrunch says $40M; some outlets cite $41.5M — treat as rounding, ~$40M).
- **Central correction — the title mis-frames the product.** Omen does **NOT** "optimize cooling" in the PUE/energy sense (no RL setpoints, no chiller control, no digital twin). It sells a **tiny inline optical spectrometer + signal-processing software that monitors the *health of the coolant fluid* in real time** — spotting bacterial growth, and wear metals (copper/chromium → pumps; silicon → seals). It is a **fluid-condition sensing / predictive-maintenance** play, i.e. "used-oil analysis for the liquid-cooling loop", not a cooling-efficiency play. → Why it matters: this moves the item out of the "AI cuts data-center energy" bucket (Phaidra/DeepMind) into a much smaller "loop chemistry sensor" niche, which changes both the comps and the weight.
- The problem is real and specific: to run chips hotter, operators dilute the biocide with more water (better heat transfer) → bacterial contamination clogs flow → a flush means shutting a rack **5–6 hours at a cost of "millions"**. Omen catches it early. → Why framed this way: the "millions of downtime" number is a generic cost-of-problem pitch (leans on an ITIC downtime survey), **not** a measured customer saving. No PUE/energy % exists to report — any such figure would be fabricated.
- Traction claims are **all self-reported**: "a dozen data-center customers" (only **TensorWave** named — and TensorWave is *also an investor*, a conflict worth flagging), monitors operators representing ~$200B assets / 10–14 GW, "2M+ machines" across the legacy industrial fleet, "21+ elemental signatures". None independently audited; "deployed/monitoring" is company-asserted, live-vs-pilot not itemized.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Direct (fluid/coolant chemistry monitoring):** thin field. **Pyxis** (established water-monitoring firm) rolled out a data-center coolant-monitoring product *earlier in June 2026* — so Omen is **not first** in on-prem coolant analytics. CDU/loop chemistry probes (pH/conductivity) from Schneider Electric, Dober, Ecolab already exist; Omen's claim is multi-element **optical spectroscopy** vs. basic probes — superiority not independently benchmarked (open).
- **Adjacent, NOT direct (cooling *optimization/control*):** Phaidra (ex-DeepMind, Jim Gao) — **$50M Series B Oct 2025**, NVIDIA tie, claims ~25% cooling-energy cut; Google DeepMind's in-house RL (40% cooling-energy / ~15% PUE, 2016-18); Vigilent; BrainBox AI (buildings HVAC). These optimize energy; Omen monitors fluid — **a different layer**. Do not treat them as head-to-head. → Why the confusion: the note's title imported the wrong frame.
- **Biggest structural threat — NVIDIA/OEM native sensing.** GB200/GB300/Rubin reference designs embed loop sensors (flow, pressure, temp, coolant chemistry) feeding DCIM. → Second-order: if the rack ships with sensing built in, a third-party bolt-on spectrometer risks commoditization. Omen's bet is that multi-element optical detail beats OEM probes — durability of that moat is the whole question.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Laberge is a repeat teen founder: first company at 14 (2020, construction-equipment sensors, ~$3M raised, later shut down; dropped out of HS). Started Omen in 2024 on **fluid systems for construction machinery** (Caterpillar dealerships early customers). → Why the pivot: Cat also supplies gas turbines/generators for on-prem data-center power; ~6 months ago dealerships asked "can you do the building side?" Omen saw a faster-growing customer base and re-pointed at data centers. Logical, opportunistic pivot riding the AI-datacenter capex wave — but the DC focus is <1 year old, so DC "traction" is nascent.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real novelty:** continuous inline multi-element spectroscopy of the loop, replacing mail-a-sample-to-a-lab. Enabled by cheaper optics + better signal processing (Laberge's own framing). Genuinely useful vs. periodic sampling.
- **But value-add is narrow and unproven at the margin:** it's a monitoring/telemetry layer, not control. → Who captures the margin: the durable value in liquid cooling is likely to accrue to whoever *controls* the loop (OEM/CDU makers, cooling-optimization software with SLAs), while a sensor that merely *reports* risks being (a) commoditized by OEM-embedded sensing or (b) a feature inside a DCIM suite. Omen must prove its spectrometer is both hard to replicate and worth a standalone contract. No audited savings, one named (and conflicted) customer, and a fast follower (Pyxis) already in the coolant niche → traction is announcement-stage.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Backdrop is favorable: liquid cooling is now standard for H100/Blackwell (GB200 NVL72 ≈ 140 kW/rack; Rubin = 100% liquid, closed 45°C loop), microbial/corrosion fouling can cut heat transfer ~14-30% — so the *problem* is real and growing. Liquid-cooling market ~$5-6B (2026) → ~$27-29B by 2033-35 (~18-26% CAGR, third-party vendor estimates, directional).
- → Why the market may still not reward this specific play: sensing is the thin, easily-bundled slice of that TAM. Counterintuitive second-order effect — the very rack-density surge that creates the contamination problem also pushes OEMs to *build in* the sensor, so the tailwind that opens the market can also close Omen's standalone window. Risks: OEM commoditization, customer-investor circularity (TensorWave), one-source announcement coverage, very young founder/short DC track record.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Omen sits not in data-center *cooling* but in the narrower adjacent niche of coolant/fluid *health monitoring* — inline spectrometry that watches liquid-cooling loops for bacterial growth, wear-metal (copper/chromium) and seal (silicon) contamination. The host sector, data-center liquid cooling, is ~$4–6bn in 2026 depending on the firm, projected to ~$27bn by 2033–35 (GM Insights / MarketsandMarkets / Precedence, via GlobeNewswire/GVR press releases, cited as of May 2026) — CAGR estimates range wildly, ~18% to ~31.5%, which itself is a caution: vendor market-sizing here is soft, treat the TAM as directional, not precise. "Why now": AI GPU racks are being pushed to 60–100+ kW density, forcing direct-to-chip and immersion liquid cooling; operators run hotter (more water in the mix, better heat absorption) which raises bacterial-contamination risk — the exact failure Omen instruments. The monitoring sub-segment is a small slice of that cooling TAM, not the whole thing (analysis). Structure: fragmented and early — few dedicated players, adjacent to legacy industrial water-treatment vendors; barrier is mainly optical-sensor + signal-processing IP plus enterprise/hyperscaler trust, not capital.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for a hardware-sensor+SaaS play: installed sensor units, monitored fluid-loops / rack coverage, recurring (analytics) revenue attach, and avoided-downtime as the ROI pitch (a flush = ~5–6 h rack shutdown "at a potential cost of millions" per CEO — vendor framing, unaudited). Players: **Pyxis Lab** (Holliston, MA; est. 2012) — established water-monitoring firm that rolled out a data-center coolant-chemistry product in May 2026 (per PitchBook profile / company site); PitchBook lists it as bootstrapped/unfunded, i.e. a credible incumbent on tech but not VC-scaled. Legacy path = mailing fluid samples to labs (the incumbent workflow Omen displaces). Recent moves: Omen Series A led by Nava Ventures announced 2026-06-29/30; Pyxis DC product May 2026. Protagonist position: niche first-mover on *real-time on-prem* fluid analytics, but not alone and not clearly ahead of Pyxis on product — its edge is customer access (Caterpillar-dealer origin, TensorWave design-partner) more than defensible tech (analysis). Moat: intangibles (optical/signal-processing IP) + nascent switching costs once embedded in loops; thin today (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** Round: $31M Series A; total raised $40M (TechCrunch/note) vs $41.5M (company PR) — the two figures disagree; use the reported round, treat totals as approximate. No revenue, ARR, valuation or post-money disclosed → **EV/Revenue, P/S, EV/EBITDA all = no data** for Omen; any multiple would be pure invention `[UNSOURCED]`. External comp **Pyxis** — private, no disclosed funding/valuation → no data. Internal corpus comps (thesis-support, not valuation): [[CME suffers multi-hour outage from data center cooling failure]] (2025-12) — a real exchange outage caused by DC cooling failure, direct evidence the downtime-cost pitch is grounded; adjacent DC-capital deals [[Scala Data Centers secures $328 million for Chile expansion]], [[Mastercard invests €250 million in three France data centres]] show the capex wave Omen rides but are not fluid-monitoring comps. **Distribution not computed — <3 comparable figures; qualitative only.** A $31M Series A for a pre-revenue-disclosed sensor startup is inside normal frontier-AI-infra ranges, so no over/under-valuation flag can be responsibly made (no data).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Not-alone / low moat.** Pyxis, an established water-monitoring incumbent, shipped a competing DC-coolant product the same month — second-order: if the moat is really "cheap optics + signal processing" (CEO's own words), the barrier to fast-following (including by cooling OEMs and lab incumbents) is low, pressuring pricing before Omen scales.
2. **Customer & category concentration / early stage.** "A dozen" design-partner customers, several disclosed only via PR (e.g. TensorWave, itself a young AMD-GPU cloud); the "$200B assets / 10–14 GW monitored" figure appears only in the company PR, is unaudited and likely counts pilots — reliance on a handful of AI-cloud early adopters means demand tracks the AI-capex cycle and could reverse with it.
3. **Pivot / execution risk.** Company pivoted from construction/heavy-equipment sensors to data centers ~6 months before the round; young 20-yr-old founder, prior startup shut down. Second-order: a monitoring-only wedge risks being commoditized or absorbed into cooling-hardware or CDU vendors' own telemetry, capturing the value one layer up the stack.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is a *new-entry* signal, not a re-rating: capital is now flowing into the "picks-and-shovels of picks-and-shovels" — instrumenting the cooling loops rather than building the cooling. Falsifiable thesis: fluid-health monitoring becomes a standard line-item in liquid-cooled AI buildouts, and Omen (or Pyxis) converts pilots to recurring analytics revenue. Watch: whether Omen discloses paying-customer count and recurring revenue at the next round, and whether a cooling-OEM/CDU vendor (or a hyperscaler) bundles fluid telemetry natively — that would break the standalone thesis by disintermediating the monitoring layer.
