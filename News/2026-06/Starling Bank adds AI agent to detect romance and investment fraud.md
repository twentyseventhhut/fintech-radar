Starling Bank adds AI agent to detect romance and investment fraud
Sinziana Albu
25 Jun 2026 / 5 Min Read
Starling Bank has launched a Scam Intelligence AI agent within its assistant, targeting more than ten fraud types for five million customers.
The feature is now available to the bank's five million customers. Its development drew on input from fraud advocate Cecilie Fjellhøy, who lost nearly GBP 200.000 to a romance scam later documented in Netflix's 'The Tinder Swindler'. The launch coincides with UK Finance figures showing that authorised push payment (APP) fraud losses rose 19% in 2025 to GBP 576.4 million. Romance fraud losses increased 23% over the same period, while investment fraud losses grew 40% year on year.
The Scam Intelligence AI agent forms part of Starling Assistant, which the bank launched in March 2026. The assistant enables customers to use voice or natural language to manage finances, plan transfers, and ask questions about their spending. When a customer indicates they are about to make a payment, the function analyses the surrounding context and poses a series of targeted questions to surface potential fraud indicators before any funds are transferred.
In addition, if a customer states they intend to send GBP 3.000 to cover a new partner's flight ticket, for example, the agent will ask how they met, how long the relationship has been ongoing, and why the partner cannot cover the cost independently. It then assesses whether the situation matches a recognised fraud pattern and, if so, recommends that the customer speak with the bank's support team.
The tool runs on Google Cloud's Gemini model. Participation is opt-in, and all customer data is stored within Starling's Google Cloud environment and is not used for model training. An earlier version of the Scam Intelligence tool, introduced in 2025 and designed to identify fraudulent online marketplace listings, increased the rate at which customers cancelled suspicious marketplace payments by 300%.
Sector and government context
The UK Minister for Fraud, Lord Hanson , welcomed the launch and noted the government's GBP 250 million commitment under its Fraud Strategy to address fraud at a structural level. Starling's Chief Moreover, Customer and Banking Officer, Bernadette Smith , noted that in the absence of sufficient action from social media platforms, where many scams originate, the burden of fraud prevention falls on individuals and financial institutions. Harriet Rees , the bank's Chief Information Officer and the UK government's AI champion for financial services, said the tool is intended to drive behavioural change by prompting customers to pause and question their spending intentions before completing a transfer.
 Cecilie Fjellhøy, who advised the development team based on her personal experience, noted that a bank is often better placed than friends or family to help a victim recognise fraudulent behaviour, as direct personal intervention can sometimes reinforce a scammer's influence rather than dispel it.

# Context-enrichment: Starling Bank adds AI agent to detect romance and investment fraud
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **24–25 June 2026** Starling rolled a **"Scam Intelligence" AI agent** into **Starling Assistant** (the Gemini-powered agentic app it launched 20 Mar 2026 — see [[Starling launches UK's first agentic AI assistant]]). It is **live** to ~5M personal customers, **opt-in**, running on **Google Cloud Gemini**, with data kept inside Starling's Google Cloud tenancy and not used for model training. Mechanism: when a customer signals a payment, the agent asks **probing conversational questions** (for romance: how/when you met, relationship length, why the partner can't pay), matches against recognised fraud patterns, then **advises** and **recommends speaking to support** before funds move. Covers romance + investment + ~10 other types (pension, deepfake/phishing). Developed with input from **Cecilie Fjellhøy** (Netflix "The Tinder Swindler"; lost ~£200k).

**Why framed this way / what it reveals.** The PR leans on two anchors that need de-PR'ing:
- The **"300% increase in cancelled suspicious payments"** belongs to the **2025 marketplace Scam Intelligence tool**, NOT this new romance/agent feature. **Starling published ZERO efficacy data for the new agent** — accuracy, false-positive rate, fraud prevented, adoption. That gap is the single biggest tell (analysis).
- The shock figures (**APP fraud £576.4m, +19%; romance +23%; investment +40% YoY**) are correct but sourced from the **UK Finance Annual Fraud Report 2026** (2025 data, published 15 Jun 2026) — not "last year" loosely. Verified. (https://www.ukfinance.org.uk/news-and-insight/press-release/fraud-report-2026-press-release)
The real driver isn't novelty — it's **liability**: since the PSR reimbursement regime (below), APP-fraud losses land on banks, so an opt-in "we made you pause" tool is as much a **liability/defence narrative** as a customer-protection one (analysis).

## [1] Competitors / peers
Conversational, point-of-payment AI scam intervention already exists — **Starling is not the category first**:
- **Revolut** — launched **AI card-scam intervention + in-app "break the spell" educational flow in Feb 2024**, claimed **30% drop in investment-scam card losses**. Direct prior art, ~16 months ahead. (https://www.revolut.com/news/revolut_launches_ai_feature_to_protect_customers_from_card_scams_and_break_the_scammers_spell/)
- **Lloyds** — "Scam Check" (marketplace screenshots + Q&A) announced for 2026, **"later this year" = likely not yet live**; staff-facing agentic fraud-ops live; **blocked >£1bn in 2025**. See [[Lloyds expands AI fraud defences after blocking £1bn in scams]].
- **Monzo** — strong control suite (Call Status, Trusted Contacts, Known Locations) but **not conversational AI scam-questioning**.
- **NatWest** — Featurespace lifted scam detection **+135%** (back-end ML, not consumer dialogue).
- Internal peer: marketplace-focused, point-of-payment Q&A is the emerging UK norm — see [[Starling Bank launches AI tool for customers to spot purchase scams]] (Oct 2025) and [[SEON and Domaine launch Shopify-native fraud prevention app]].

**Why the lay of the land is this way (analysis).** All UK banks face the same PSR liability shock simultaneously → near-simultaneous "interrogate-the-customer-at-payment" tools. Differentiation collapses to **execution + UX integration**, not concept. Starling's edge is bundling it inside a full **agentic assistant** + a **romance-specific** script + the Fjellhøy halo — narrative differentiation more than capability differentiation.

## [2] Company history / fit
Coherent trajectory: **call status indicator** (Oct 2024, bank-impersonation) → **Scam Intelligence marketplace tool** (2025, the 300% claim) → **Starling Assistant** (Mar 2026, [[Starling launches UK's first agentic AI assistant]]) → this agent (Jun 2026). Also expanding business tooling ([[Starling launches business tools ahead of HMRC digital tax rules]]) and weighing a US/NY listing ([[Starling Bank weighs New York listing for US expansion]]).

**Why Starling acts this way (analysis).** Two structural pressures: (1) as a **profitable digital-only bank eyeing a listing**, it needs a defensible "AI bank" story and clean fraud-loss optics; (2) the **agentic assistant is the platform**, and each new "Intelligence" feature is cheap marginal content that deepens engagement and justifies the Gemini bet. Anti-fraud is the most PR-friendly use case to debut.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Genuinely new?** Partly. Romance-specific conversational questioning inside an agentic assistant is fresh in UK retail banking, but the core "AI questions you / breaks the spell" pattern is **Revolut, Feb 2024**. Not a first.
**Traction:** **None disclosed for this agent.** Only proxies: old marketplace tool +300% cancellation rate (base/sample/period undisclosed); Revolut's −30% (a competitor's tool). No adoption/uptake numbers for Starling Assistant or the agent.

**Why value-add is uncertain, deeper (analysis).** (1) **Opt-in penetration risk**: the most scam-vulnerable users (older, less tech-savvy) are least likely to opt in, so reach may be inverse to need. (2) **Behaviour-change evidence is thin** — does an LLM prompt actually stop a customer mid-spell? Only indirect proxies, no independent eval. (3) **LLM reliability**: a Gemini agent issuing fraud-risk verdicts can hallucinate/be inconsistent; no accuracy or false-positive disclosure. (4) **False-reassurance / liability risk**: an opt-in tool could later be invoked in "customer caution"/gross-negligence reimbursement disputes — value could partly accrue to the bank's defence, not the customer.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Regulatory backdrop is the whole story:
- **PSR mandatory APP-fraud reimbursement**, in force **7 Oct 2024**: **£85,000 cap** (cut from a proposed £415k), **50/50 cost split** between sending and receiving PSPs, 5-business-day reimbursement. (https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/app-fraud-mandatory-reimbursement-uk-psr-publishes-final-policy-for-7-october-2024-go-live-date)
- **UK Fraud Strategy 2026–2029** (9 Mar 2026): **>£250m over 3 years**, £31m Online Crime Centre. (https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/fraud-strategy-2026-to-2029/fraud-strategy-2026-to-2029-disrupting-crime-supporting-economic-resilience-and-delivering-justice-accessible)
- **Origin sits with platforms**: ~70–80% of APP fraud starts on social media / online marketplaces; **Online Safety Act 2023** obligations on fraudulent ads bite from **2027** — but **platforms carry no reimbursement liability**; banks still pay. Starling execs (Smith, Rees) explicitly flag this asymmetry.

**Why the market goes this way (analysis).** Liability without source-control: banks bear the loss while the fraud originates on platforms they can't police → expect every UK bank to ship customer-interrogation tools as **loss mitigation**, and to lobby hard to push liability upstream to social platforms. **Second-order:** if these tools genuinely cut losses, they strengthen banks' negotiating hand for a platform-liability regime; if they don't, the £85k cap + 50/50 split keeps compounding cost and the central question shifts from "whose AI is best" to **"who ultimately pays for platform-originated fraud."**

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This sits at the intersection of (a) UK APP-fraud loss-mitigation and (b) the AI-fraud-detection software market. **Market size:** UK payment fraud losses reached **£1.28bn in 2025** (+4% YoY), of which **APP fraud = £576.4m (+19%)** with 248,070 cases (+7%); investment scams are the largest APP category by value at **£221.5m (+40% YoY)**, romance +23% (per UK Finance Annual Fraud Report 2026, pub. 15 Jun 2026). Banks reimbursed **£354.3m = 61% of APP losses** in 2025 — this reimbursement bill IS the addressable cost the tool targets, not a software TAM (analysis). The broader AI-fraud-detection software market is sized very differently across vendors — ~$6.8bn (2025)→$49.5bn (2034) at ~24.6% CAGR for "AI-powered fraud detection," vs $40-82bn (2026) for total "fraud detection & prevention" — **wide dispersion, treat as directional only**, and note this is the *vendor* market (SEON, Featurespace, Resistant AI), not where Starling plays. Starling is a *consumer* of the category building in-house on Gemini, not a seller. **Structure:** UK retail-bank fraud-prevention is not a market with a take-rate — it is a cost centre forced by regulation. Barrier to entry = the bank licence + customer base, not the AI. **Drivers / "why now":** the PSR mandatory-reimbursement regime (live 7 Oct 2024; £85k cap, 50/50 PSP split) moved APP loss onto banks → every UK bank now ships customer-interrogation tools as loss mitigation. Second-order: ~70-80% of APP fraud originates on social/marketplace platforms that carry **zero reimbursement liability** (Online Safety Act ad-fraud duties bite only from 2027) — banks pay for fraud they cannot police.

**Competitive landscape.** This is not a revenue-KPI sector for the bank; the operative KPIs are **fraud £ prevented / blocked, payment-cancellation uplift, false-positive (friction) rate, and opt-in adoption %** — Starling discloses **none** of these for the new agent. Basis of competition = **execution + UX + distribution**, not concept (the "interrogate-at-payment / break-the-spell" pattern is now table stakes). Players & dated moves: **Revolut** — AI card-scam "break the spell" live **Feb 2024**, claims **−30% in card-scam losses tied to investment opportunities** (per Revolut; verified wording, ~16 months ahead of Starling). **Lloyds** — blocked **>£1bn in 2025**, "Scam Check" announced for "later this year" = **likely not yet live**; see [[Lloyds expands AI fraud defences after blocking £1bn in scams]]. **Monzo** — strong controls (Known Locations, Trusted Contacts) launched **Jul 2024** + real-time fraud platform, but **not** conversational AI questioning; also note Monzo's reimbursement reliability was itself challenged — see [[Monzo wrongly denied refunds to thousands of fraud victims]]. **NatWest/Featurespace** — back-end ML (+135% detection), not consumer dialogue. New entrant: [[Falkin raises funding for AI scam prevention]] (£2m, Nov 2025). **Starling's position: among leaders, not the leader** — fast-follower on concept, differentiated only by (i) bundling inside a full agentic assistant (Gemini), (ii) a romance-specific script, (iii) the Fjellhøy/"Tinder Swindler" halo. Moat = customer base + the assistant platform (switching costs), **not** the fraud feature itself (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** Starling is private; comparison is to its own corporate valuation, not a fraud-tool multiple (no standalone revenue for the feature → multiple = **no data**). Corporate frame: Starling reported **FY26 (May 2026) pre-tax profit £217.1m on revenue £887.4m** — a fifth straight profitable year, but **profit FELL** from £280.0m (FY25) and £301.1m (FY24) *while revenue rose* £714m→£887m, i.e. **margin compression** (PBT margin 42%→39%→24.5% across FY24/25/26 — arithmetic: 217.1/887.4 = 24.5%; per Wikipedia/press citing Starling annual reports). Last marked valuation is the reported **secondary/share-sale target of ~£3.5-4bn** (2026; mark as a secondary-sale/round figure, NOT a public market cap) — implying **P/S ≈ £4bn / £0.887bn ≈ 4.5x** revenue (round-valuation basis, not market cap). Earlier primary round: **£130.5m at £2.5bn (2022)** per FintechFutures. Internal comps (corpus, as `[[wikilink]]`): [[PSR reports APP fraud reimbursement covers 88% of in-scope claims]], [[UK Finance reports 629 million pounds in fraud losses in H1 2025]], [[Starling Bank launches AI tool for customers to spot purchase scams]], [[Lloyds expands AI fraud defences after blocking £1bn in scams]]. Verdict: corporate P/S ~4.5x is **in-line** for a profitable neobank, but the **falling absolute profit** is the number that matters more than any fraud-feature optics — fraud reimbursement is one of several cost pressures behind the margin slide (analysis). Standalone feature multiples: **not computed — no standalone economics disclosed.**

**Risk flags.**
1. **No disclosed efficacy = unverifiable value (and possible mis-selling tail).** Zero accuracy / false-positive / fraud-prevented / adoption data for the new agent; the only hard numbers belong to other tools (Starling's 2025 marketplace tool +300%; Revolut −30%). Why it matters: if the tool doesn't materially cut losses, it is a PR/liability narrative carrying ongoing Gemini cost, not loss mitigation — and an opt-in "we made you pause" tool could be cited against the *customer* in future gross-negligence/reimbursement disputes.
2. **Liability without source control (structural, sector-wide).** Banks bear APP loss under PSR (£85k cap, 50/50, £354.3m reimbursed in 2025) while ~70-80% of fraud originates on platforms with no reimbursement liability until at least 2027. Why it matters: a customer-facing chatbot cannot fix an upstream-origination problem — the cost compounds regardless of tool quality, and competes for spend with the reimbursement bill itself.
3. **Adoption inverse to need (opt-in) + LLM reliability.** Opt-in design means the most vulnerable (older, less tech-savvy) are least likely to enable it; reach may be inverse to need. Compounding: an LLM (Gemini) issuing fraud-risk verdicts can hallucinate / be inconsistent or generate false-positive friction on genuine new relationships — no guardrail or eval published.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** For the sector this is **convergence, not a re-rating**: conversational point-of-payment interrogation is now the UK norm, so the contest moves from "whose AI is best" to **"who ultimately pays for platform-originated fraud."** Falsifiable thesis (analysis): if banks publish hard efficacy data showing these tools meaningfully cut APP losses, it strengthens their hand to lobby liability upstream to social platforms; **watch for the first independently-audited efficacy number (catalyst) — its continued absence across all banks is itself the tell.** Thesis breaks if a bank discloses that customer-interrogation tools do NOT move loss rates, which would shift the whole debate to platform liability and reimbursement-cap economics.
