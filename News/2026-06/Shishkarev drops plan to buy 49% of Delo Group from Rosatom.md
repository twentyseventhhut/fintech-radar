 Сергей Шишкарев не будет выкупать 49% ГК «Дело» у «Росатома» 
Бизнесмен Сергей Шишкарев не станет выкупать 49% основанной им группы компаний (ГК) «Дело» у госкорпорации «Росатом». Об этом предприниматель объявил в Telegram.
«Вынужден констатировать: макроэкономическая ситуация и рыночная конъюнктура не позволяют сформировать конфигурацию сделки, которая устроила бы все заинтересованные стороны и гарантировала устойчивое будущее группы», – написал он.
Шишкарев пояснил, что доступный формат сделки «не обеспечивал гарантий стабильной деятельности и развития» ГК. Теперь право определять структуру владения группой переходит к «Росатому».
Как напоминают " Ведомости ", партнерство между ГК «Дело» и «Росатомом» началось в декабре 2019 г. Тогда структура госкорпорации «Атомэнергопром» выкупила 30% в уставном капитале ООО «Управляющая компания «Дело» за 29,9 млрд руб. Исходя из этого компания была оценена примерно в 100 млрд руб. В 2022 г. «Росатом» реализовал опцион и увеличил свою долю до 49%.
Предполагалось, что Шишкарев и «Ростех» создадут совместное предприятие для управления ГК «Дело». Об этом говорил сам Шишкарев в апреле. Уточнялось, что стратегическое управление СП будет осуществляться сторонами совместно, а оперативное – самим Шишкаревым либо его представителем.
Доля «Росатома» в УК «Дело» (49%) оценивалась в 74 млрд руб.

# Context-enrichment: Shishkarev drops plan to buy 49% of Delo Group from Rosatom
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 26 June 2026 Sergei Shishkarev, founder and co-owner (~51%) of Delo Group — Russia's largest transport-and-logistics holding — announced via Telegram that he will NOT buy out Rosatom's 49% stake in the management company OOO "UK Delo". His stated reason: "the macroeconomic situation and market conditions do not allow forming a deal configuration that would suit all interested parties and guarantee the group a stable future." Crucially, he ceded the next move: "the right to determine the group's ownership structure now passes to Rosatom."

This is a reversal, not a fresh event. The buyout was already deep in the pipeline:
- The 49% was valued at ~74bn rub (mergers.ru, Vedomosti).
- Shishkarev had publicly intended to buy out via a bridge loan; he approached VTB for financing (VTB head Kostin, ~April 2026: no decision yet).
- FAS (anti-monopoly service) had already approved the purchase in April 2026, with 10-year provisions on key operating assets (pricing/sales-policy disclosure).
- A JV with Rostec was planned for the strategic management of Delo post-Rosatom.

**+ Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The trigger upstream was Rosatom launching a "Russian roulette" (mutual buy-sell) procedure in Feb 2026 — a shotgun clause in the partnership agreement, activated because Rosatom's and Shishkarev's visions for the group's development diverged. Under it, one side names a price; the other must either buy at that price or sell at it (60 days to choose, then ~120 days for approvals). Shishkarev chose to buy — secured FAS clearance, lined up a Rostec partner, sought VTB money — and then, after months, walked. The "macro conditions" line is the polite cover; the substance is that he could not assemble ~74bn rub of financing on terms that "guaranteed a stable future" (read: debt service at 2026 Russian rates would have crippled the asset). By the rules of the shotgun, declining to buy hands the initiative back to the counterparty — hence "Rosatom now decides the structure." (analysis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
Delo Group is itself the dominant Russian player, so peers are about ownership/control of strategic logistics, not market share:
- **Global Ports** — Delo's listed container-terminal arm; Delo raised its stake to >90% (2024), having gone from 30.75% (2018) to 61.5% (2022, buying out APM Terminals). Largest sea-container-terminal operator in Russia (~23% of national container turnover, 2022). This is the crown jewel inside the disputed structure.
- **State-control consolidation peers:** Rosatom (nuclear + Northern Sea Route logistics), Rostec, Transmashholding (Rosatom explicitly said it was NOT selling the Delo stake to TMH), R-Alliance (Shishkarev regained 1% of Delo MC from it). These show the same pattern: strategic transport assets gravitating toward state-linked holdings.

**+ Why the lay of the land is this way:** Since 2022 sanctions and the loss of Western partners (APM Terminals exited), Russian ports/logistics have been re-domesticated and pulled into state-corporation orbits (Rosatom for the Arctic/NSR, Rostec for industrial). The Delo case is the second-order tell: even a private founder cannot cleanly re-privatize a strategic logistics asset once a state corporation sits at 49% with a shotgun clause. The "competition" is really over who controls the chokepoint, not who serves customers better. (analysis)

## [2] Company history / fit
- 1993: Delo founded by Shishkarev.
- Dec 2019: Rosatom's Atomenergoprom buys 30% of UK Delo for 29.9bn rub → implied ~100bn rub valuation.
- 2022: Rosatom exercises option → 49%.
- 2018-2024: Delo consolidates Global Ports (30.75% → 61.5% → >90%).
- Feb 2026: Rosatom triggers "Russian roulette" over strategy disagreement.
- Apr-May 2026: FAS clears Shishkarev's purchase; Rosatom "sees no obstacles"; Rostec JV and VTB financing in play.
- 26 Jun 2026: Shishkarev declines to buy; ball returns to Rosatom.

**+ Why the company acts this way:** Shishkarev built Delo as a private operator but accepted a state co-owner in 2019 for capital/political cover for the Global Ports roll-up. That bargain embedded a state veto. When visions diverged, the shotgun forced a binary: buy out the state at full market value in a high-rate, capital-scarce 2026 — or exit. The valuation gap is telling: Rosatom paid 29.9bn for 30% in 2019 (~100bn implied); its 49% is now marked at ~74bn (~151bn implied for 100%). Funding that buyback with bridge debt at Russian 2026 rates was the structural pressure that broke the plan. (analysis/hypothesis)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
There is no new operational capability here — this is a control/ownership story. The "traction" that matters is the real, cash-generating asset base: Global Ports (#1 container terminals), rail/intermodal, and stevedoring that handle a large share of Russian container throughput. That base is genuine and was never in question; what changed is who owns the upside.

**+ Who captures the value:** By withdrawing, Shishkarev likely keeps operating control short-term but loses the chance to own the 49% upside outright; Rosatom now sets terms. Plausible outcomes: Rosatom buys Shishkarev out (state consolidates to majority/100%), or a new state-linked partner (Rostec was already the intended JV partner) is slotted in. Either way the margin/strategic value tilts toward the state corporation that held the optionality — the shotgun clause, not the operating business, is what captured the value. (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
The note explicitly hands "the right to determine the ownership structure" to Rosatom. Watch for: Rosatom exercising the other side of the shotgun (buying Shishkarev's stake), a Rostec/Rosatom carve-up, or a fresh financing structure. Key risks/unknowns: Shishkarev is sanctioned (named so by portseurope), which constrains external financing and complicates any sale to a non-state buyer; 2026 Russian rates make any leveraged buyout punishing; FAS provisions (10-yr pricing-disclosure) bind whoever ends up in control.

**+ Why the market goes this way (second-order):** This is a data point in the broader re-statisation of Russian strategic logistics. A sanctioned private founder being unable to buy back his own company from a state corporation signals that, in 2026 Russia, control of port/rail chokepoints increasingly flows one direction — toward state holdings — regardless of who founded the asset. Counterintuitively, "macro conditions" is doing real work here: capital scarcity is itself the mechanism of consolidation. (analysis)

## Top challenge / red-team questions (second-order)

1. Is "macroeconomic conditions" the real reason, or a face-saving cover for failed financing? Evidence points to the latter: VTB never confirmed the bridge loan (Kostin, ~April: "no decision"). — likely financing failure (analysis).
2. What exactly does "the right to determine the ownership structure passes to Rosatom" mean operationally — does Shishkarev now have to SELL his 51% under the shotgun? — open; the shotgun's binary implies Rosatom can now force a buy/sell.
3. Was a binding price already set under the Feb 2026 "Russian roulette," and at what number? ~74bn rub for 49% is cited, but is that the shotgun strike price or a press estimate? — open.
4. Does FAS approval (April 2026) lapse now that Shishkarev declined? Do its 10-yr pricing provisions bind Rosatom instead? — open.
5. What happens to the planned Rostec JV — is it dead, or does Rostec slot in as Rosatom's co-owner/operator? — open (Rostec was the intended strategic-management partner).
6. How does Shishkarev's sanctioned status constrain any sale to a non-state buyer or external financing? — material constraint; flagged by portseurope.
7. Who controls Global Ports day-to-day if ownership flips to the state? Delo holds >90% of Global Ports — that's the real asset at stake.
8. Is there a forced-seller discount risk for Shishkarev's stake if Rosatom now exercises the buy side? — open.
9. Valuation drift: 2019 implied ~100bn rub; 2026 49% at ~74bn (~151bn implied). Is that real appreciation or rouble nominal inflation? — needs deflating; likely largely nominal (analysis).
10. Could a third state-linked buyer (e.g. Transmashholding) re-enter despite Rosatom's earlier denial? — open; Rosatom explicitly denied a TMH sale earlier.
11. Does Shishkarev retain operational management even after ceding ownership initiative, per the April JV framing? — partly: April terms had operational control with Shishkarev — but those were pre-withdrawal.
12. What is the debt-service math that "didn't guarantee a stable future"? ~74bn rub at 2026 Russian rates implies a crushing annual coupon vs Delo's cash flow. — analysis; no published figures.
13. Is this a one-off or part of systematic re-statisation of Russian strategic logistics? Corpus + portseurope ("Russia consolidates state control over transport and logistics") suggest the latter.
14. How reliable is the single-source primary (mergers.ru, 310 words)? Corroborated by Interfax/TASS/Vedomosti chain — well-supported on facts, thin on the "why."
15. What is the fintech/finance relevance at all? Limited — this is infrastructure/M&A; financial angle is the bridge-loan/VTB financing and large-cap Russian deal flow. Hence importance capped.

**Importance: 3/5** — A genuinely large (~74bn rub / strategic) M&A reversal involving Russia's #1 logistics holding and two major state corporations (Rosatom, Rostec), with clear second-order signal on re-statisation of strategic assets and the limits of leveraged buyouts in 2026 Russia. ## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: Russian ports / transport-and-logistics M&A and state-asset consolidation — not core fintech; the financial angle is the (failed) leveraged buyback. Market sizing: Russia's container throughput ~5.19m TEU (Dec 2024, +13% YoY from 4.59m), per CEIC, having clawed back from the 2022 sanctions low (St Petersburg still ~450k TEU short of 2021); Global Ports (Delo's >90%-owned terminal arm) handles ~23% of national container turnover (2022, per note). Structure: highly **consolidated** post-2022 — Western operators (APM Terminals) exited, and strategic gateways were re-domesticated into state-corporation orbits (Rosatom/Arctic-NSR, Rostec/industrial). Barriers: capital, regulation (FAS), sanctions exposure, and state veto rights baked into ownership. **Why now / the real driver = the cost of money.** Bank of Russia key rate was 14.25% in June 2026 (cut 19 Jun, from a 21% peak in 2025; baseline avg 13–15% for 2026, per cbr.ru). A ~74bn rub bridge-financed buyback at those rates is the structural reason the deal collapsed — "macro conditions" is doing real work, not just cover. Second-order: in a high-rate, capital-scarce 2026, leveraged re-privatization of a strategic asset from a state co-owner is arithmetically near-impossible — so capital scarcity itself becomes the mechanism of state consolidation. (analysis)

**Competitive landscape.** This is a control/ownership story, not a market-share contest — Delo is already the dominant domestic player. KPIs that matter: container TEU throughput / national share; for the deal itself, the buyback price (~74bn rub for 49%) vs financing cost (key-rate-linked coupon) vs the asset's cash generation. Players are co-owners/state holdings, not customers: Rosatom (49%, holds the shotgun), Shishkarev (~51% founder), Rostec (intended strategic-JV partner), with Transmashholding explicitly ruled out by Rosatom as a buyer. Recent moves: Feb 2026 Rosatom triggers "Russian roulette" (shotgun buy/sell); Apr 2026 FAS clears Shishkarev's purchase with 10-yr pricing-disclosure provisions; ~Apr 2026 VTB approached, "no decision yet" (Kostin); 26 Jun 2026 Shishkarev declines to buy, initiative passes to Rosatom. Protagonist's position: founder is the **forced/constrained party** — sanctioned (per portseurope), unable to assemble financing, now exposed to the sell side of the shotgun. Moat is the asset base (Global Ports #1 container terminals, rail/intermodal), but the *control* premium sits with whoever holds the optionality (Rosatom), not the operator. (analysis)

**Comps & multiples.** No public Delo Group financials (revenue/EBITDA), so EV/Revenue and EV/EBITDA = **no data**; only the implied equity valuation is observable, computed from disclosed stake prices:
- 2019: Rosatom paid 29.9bn rub for 30% → implied 100% ≈ `29.9 / 0.30 = ~99.7bn rub`.
- 2026: 49% marked at ~74bn rub → implied 100% ≈ `74 / 0.49 = ~151bn rub`.
- Implied appreciation `~151 / ~100 = ~1.5x` over ~7 years (`~6%`/yr nominal) — likely **largely rouble nominal inflation, not real value creation** (analysis); deflating by 2019–26 RUB CPI would compress most of it. Cross-deal multiples not computed: distribution not computed, qualitative comparison only.
- Internal comps (corpus, analogous Russia/state-exit/buyout dynamics, NOT direct sector peers): [[Citigroup to sell remaining Russian operations at $1.2bn loss]] (Western exit → state-linked buyer, Putin sign-off), [[JPMorgan explores new financing structures for leveraged buyouts]] (LBO financing engineering backdrop), [[Russia's A7 cross-border payments firm plans global expansion]] (2026 sanctioned-Russia state-corporate finance backdrop). **Risk flags.**
1. **Financing / rate risk (the binding constraint).** At a 14.25% key rate, debt-servicing a ~74bn rub bridge would have crushed Delo's cash flow — VTB never confirmed the loan; this is why the deal died and would re-break for any leveraged buyer. Russian corporate bankruptcies reportedly +20% in 2026 on rate/liquidity stress (Moscow Times), underlining the squeeze.
2. **Sanctions / forced-seller risk.** Shishkarev is sanctioned (portseurope), which blocks external/Western financing and narrows buyers to state-linked parties — pushing toward a discounted sale of his 51% if Rosatom now exercises the buy side of the shotgun.
3. **Re-statisation / control-capture risk.** The shotgun clause, not the operating business, captured the value; ownership of a strategic chokepoint (Global Ports) is flowing one-way toward state holdings regardless of who founded it — concentration of strategic-infrastructure control in state corporations.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** A data point that, in 2026 Russia, a private founder cannot re-privatize a strategic logistics asset from a state co-owner once high rates make leverage unaffordable — capital scarcity *is* the consolidation mechanism. Falsifiable thesis: Rosatom ends up consolidating to majority/100% (possibly slotting Rostec as operator) rather than Shishkarev regaining the 49%. Trigger to watch: Rosatom exercising the buy side of the shotgun, or a fresh financing structure; thesis breaks if a non-state buyer or a low-coupon state-backed facility lets Shishkarev re-enter. (analysis)
