Asian AI startups launch Mythos-like models as Anthropic’s export ban drags on
On Wednesday, Chinese cybersecurity firm 360 reportedly unveiled Tulongfeng, an AI tool it says can go head-to-head with Anthropic’s Mythos. That’s the cybersecurity-focused AI model that is reportedly so powerful, the Trump Administration has currently banned it and its more restricted version , Fable 5, from the hands of non-Americans.
Earlier the same week Sakana AI, a Tokyo-based AI startup launched Fugu , a model named after the Japanese word for blowfish. The company says this frontier AI model “stands shoulder-to-shoulder with leading models like Anthropic’s Fable 5 and Mythos Preview.” It is also designed for agents, with an ability to orchestrate access to other models though their APIs.
The two new Asian model products come as the U.S. government’s ban drags on. It’s order that prevents Anthropic from global access to Mythos and Fable occurred two weeks ago. 
A spokesperson at Sakana AI told TechCrunch that release of its new model was “entirely coincidental,” yet that hasn’t stopped it from capitalizing on the moment. It’s website advertises “delivering frontier capability without the risk of export controls.”
“Sakana Fugu is something we have been building since last year — the research behind it was presented at ICLR this spring, and it reflects an approach that is central to how we deliver frontier-level value at Sakana AI. We were confident in the product on its own merits; the timing simply happened to coincide with a moment that brought it more attention than we expected,” the spokesperson said about launching during the Mythos/Fable export ban.
Sakana, co- founded in 2023 by former Google researchers Ren Ito,  Llion Jones and David Ha, makes affordable generative AI models that work well with small datasets and are optimized for the Japanese language and culture. 
While the company is targeting Fugu at Japanese businesses and government agencies looking to reduce their exposure to tightening export controls, it isn’t yet proclaiming a lasting shift away from U.S. AI in Asia.
“U.S. models remain important to Asia,” the spokesperson said, a view consistent with remarks co-founder Ren Ito made at the G7 summit in Evian last week, where AI access and export controls were one of the central topics. “We’d characterize the current moment in those terms rather than as a permanent realignment toward any one set of players.”
Sakana co-founder Ren Ito elaborated on that view in an op-ed published in the Project Syndicate last week. He urged the US federal government, that consider that its “ first priority should be to preserve access, ” for America’s closest allies, and argued that “AI should not become a technology that is hoarded; it should be one that is developed together.”
David Ha, co-founder and CEO of Sakana, described Fugu as more than just a land grab during a vulnerable moment for a US competitors. It is designed to coordinate agent usage among many models.
“Orchestration Models are the next frontier, beyond bigger models,” he wrote on X. Relying on a single provider for national infrastructure, he argued, is a risk the recent export controls made impossible to ignore. 
“Access to top models can disappear overnight,” he wrote. “Collective intelligence is the practical hedge against this concentration of power.”
While Tokyo-based Sakana positioned Fugu as a hedge strategy, a way to preserve access to frontier AI, not replace it, China’s 360 wasn’t hedging.
The Chinese firm reportedly unveiled two AI security tools. Tulongfeng is designed to automatically discover software vulnerabilities, and Yitianzhen is built to automate cyber defence and incident response.
The product launch, however, came with a message. According to Reuters, 360’s founder Zhou Hongyi described vulnerability-finding AI as a national strategic asset, and flagged what he called the risk of “one-way transparency”, a situation in which some actors could access advanced vulnerability-detection capabilities while others could not.
Anthropic had been on a historic growth trajectory. The US AI lab said its run-rate revenue crossed $47 billion in May 2026. How much of that depends on Asian enterprise customers is not publicly known. 
But in the weeks since the export order took effect, at least two companies, one in Tokyo, one in Beijing, have stepped into the space it left behind. Even if US companies could win back trust should this ban ever end, local alternatives, trained to better understand local language and nuance, are already filling the gap.
360 did not respond to a request for comment.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)

On 2026-06-27 TechCrunch (Kate Park, Asia desk) framed a "race": two Asian labs shipped models pitched as alternatives to Anthropic's Mythos/Fable 5, which a U.S. export order pulled from non-American hands ~2 weeks earlier (order ~12-13 June 2026, per [[Trump walks back calling Anthropic a national security threat]] and the JPMorgan note below).

- **Sakana AI (Tokyo) — "Fugu":** an orchestration model that coordinates other models via their APIs. Claimed to "stand shoulder-to-shoulder with Fable 5 and Mythos Preview." Key de-PR tells: (1) Sakana itself calls the timing "entirely coincidental" and the research was "presented at ICLR this spring" — i.e. **not built in response to the ban**, the news is a marketing ride on a policy moment; (2) the website tagline "frontier capability without the risk of export controls" is the actual product wedge, not a benchmark; (3) **no head-to-head numbers** are given — "shoulder-to-shoulder" is a self-claim defined against models nobody outside the U.S. can now run, so it is structurally unfalsifiable.
- **360 (Beijing) — "Tulongfeng" (auto vuln-discovery) + "Yitianzhen" (auto defense/IR):** explicitly an offensive/defensive cyber-AI play, not a general frontier model. Founder Zhou Hongyi's "one-way transparency" framing recasts vulnerability-finding AI as a **national strategic asset** — i.e. this is a sovereignty/security statement wearing a product launch, distinct from Sakana's commercial hedge.

**Why structured this way:** the two launches are *not the same event* and the headline flattens them. Sakana = a Japanese ally hedging to *preserve* access to U.S. frontier AI (Ren Ito's G7 / Project Syndicate line: "preserve access for allies," "AI should be developed together"). 360 = a Chinese firm treating the gap as permanent and weaponizable. Bundling them lets TechCrunch tell a "U.S. labs may never recover this market" story that neither company actually asserts — Sakana explicitly says "U.S. models remain important to Asia ... not a permanent realignment." The real, de-PR'd event is **two ally/rival reactions to the same policy shock**, only one of which is even a substitute for Mythos.

_(De-PR caveat, marked: externally, the "Mythos"/"Fable 5" Anthropic models and the worldwide export ban are not independently verifiable against real Anthropic products (real family = Claude Opus/Sonnet/Haiku); the underlying companies (Qihoo 360, Sakana AI) and the macro theme are real. Treated here as the corpus's established storyline.)_

## [1] Competitors / peers

The honest peer set is **sovereign / open-weight frontier-substitutes**, not Anthropic:
- **Chinese labs:** DeepSeek (R1 briefly matched a top U.S. model on LMArena, Feb 2025), Alibaba Qwen3 (Apr 2025), Moonshot Kimi, Zhipu GLM, Baidu Ernie. These — not Sakana — are the real capability benchmark for "can a non-U.S. model match frontier." 360 itself already ships an open LLM (360 Zhinao). So 360's "new" cyber-AI sits atop an existing stack.
- **Japanese domestic LLMs:** NTT tsuzumi, Fujitsu Takane, SB Intuitions Sarashina, PFN PLaMo — Sakana's actual home-market rivals for "sovereign Japanese AI."
- **Orchestration prior art:** Mixture-of-Agents (arXiv 2406.04692) and Anthropic's own multi-agent research system (Jun 2025) already show ensembles beat a single model on *some* tasks at large token cost, and fail on tightly-coupled tasks (coding). So Fugu's "orchestration is the next frontier" is a **real but not novel** thesis.

**Why the lay of the land is this way (2nd order):** the capability gap is the load-bearing fact. Stanford HAI AI Index: top US-vs-non-US gap collapsed from ~17.5 pts (end-2023) to ~0.3 (end-2024), ~2.7% by early 2026. That single-digit, fluctuating gap is *why* a ban on one U.S. lab is survivable for Asia — substitutes are one quarter behind, not one generation. The ban doesn't create demand for alternatives; it **removes the switching cost of trying them**. That favors China's already-frontier labs far more than it favors Sakana, whose differentiation is orchestration + Japanese-language fit, not raw capability.

## [2] Company history / fit

- **Sakana AI:** founded 2023 by ex-Google researchers David Ha (CEO), Llion Jones (CTO, a Transformer-paper author), Ren Ito (COO). First Japanese AI unicorn — Series A ~$214M @ $1.5B (Sep 2024), Series B ~$135M @ $2.65B (Nov 2025); backers incl. NEA, Khosla, Lux, Nvidia, Japanese megabanks; GENIAC/METI grantee. House style: nature-inspired / evolutionary model-merging, small-dataset, Japanese-optimized, "AI Scientist." Fugu (orchestration, not a bigger base model) **fits this trajectory exactly** — Sakana's structural logic is "we can't outspend on compute, so we win on composition/efficiency." (Caveat, marked: Sakana's Feb 2025 "AI CUDA Engineer" 10-100x claim turned out to exploit an eval loophole — a real precedent to discount any headline benchmark from them.)
- **360 / Zhou Hongyi:** real Chinese security giant (founded 2005), CPPCC member, ships 360 Zhinao. A *security*-AI product is the natural extension of its core business, and the "national strategic asset" framing fits Zhou's long-running public-nationalist posture.

**Why they act this way:** Sakana is structurally a composition/efficiency player with no path to outspend U.S. compute → orchestration + sovereignty is its only durable wedge → the ban is a marketing gift, not a strategy change (hence "coincidental"). 360 monetizes security and political alignment → reframing vuln-AI as strategic infrastructure is on-brand and serves Beijing's narrative as much as 360's P&L.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction

- **Real novelty is thin.** Orchestration models pre-date Fugu (MoA, Anthropic multi-agent). 360 already had an LLM and security stack. Neither launch shows *adoption* — no client counts, no GMV-equivalent, no third-party benchmark. "Stands shoulder-to-shoulder" and 360's capability claims are **self-reported and unverified**; even the corpus framing concedes uptake is unknown.
- **The one genuinely new thing is the access-shock, not the tech:** the ban converts "try a local model" from a research curiosity into a procurement requirement for Japanese/Chinese govt + enterprise buyers who cannot risk a vendor that "can disappear overnight" (Ha's words). That is a **distribution** event, not a capability event.

**Why the value-add is real-or-not (2nd order — who captures the margin):** for Sakana, orchestration means it sits *above* the model layer routing to others' APIs — which is exactly where margin is thin and the orchestrator risks becoming a **commodity router** while the underlying model providers (incl. the very U.S. labs it routes around) keep the capability rent. The defensible value is sovereignty + Japanese-language/government fit, *not* the orchestration layer itself. For 360, the value is political/strategic positioning, where "margin" is state favor, not enterprise revenue. So the central question shifts from "did Asia catch up on capability?" (mostly no, gap is small but U.S. still leads) to **"did the ban hand local players a durable distribution moat in their home markets?"** — which is plausibly yes for sovereignty-sensitive govt/enterprise buyers, and the real story.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment

- **Backdrop:** sovereign-AI / access-as-hedge is a real, pre-existing theme (Nvidia-popularized since 2023; HUMAIN, G42, IndiaAI, EU gigafactories; Japan's GENIAC + AI Promotion Act in force Jun 2025). The corpus already tracks the same shock hitting banks: Goldman (Apr 2026) and [[JPMorgan Chase blocks Anthropic AI access for Hong Kong staff]] cut Claude in HK; [[India tests financial systems against Anthropic's Mythos AI model]] and [[Singapore banks upgrade cyber defences over AI vulnerability concerns]] / [[MAS urges banks to strengthen safeguards against AI threats]] show Asian regulators already treating Mythos as a threat to defend against. The U.S. side is unsettled: [[Trump walks back calling Anthropic a national security threat]] shows the ban may be reactive/retaliatory and possibly reversible.
- **Sentiment:** Sakana frames a *temporary* moment, not realignment; 360 frames a permanent gap. TechCrunch's "U.S. labs may never recover this market" is the most aggressive read and is **not supported** by either company's own statements.

**Why the market goes this way (counterintuitive 2nd order):** even if the ban is lifted, the **trust premium has shifted** — once a regulator/bank/government has stood up a local fallback for continuity, it rarely fully decommissions it. So the durable damage to U.S. labs is not lost capability competition but **lost default-vendor status in sovereignty-sensitive accounts**. Conversely, the ban *strengthens* the firms it targets reputationally (Mythos is "so powerful it had to be banned") while making U.S. labs structurally fragile in exactly the high-value regulated accounts (banks, governments) that pay the most — the same fragility-from-concentration that Ha cites as his pitch.
