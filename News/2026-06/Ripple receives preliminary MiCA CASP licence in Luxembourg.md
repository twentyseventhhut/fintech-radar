Ripple receives preliminary MiCA CASP licence in Luxembourg
Sinziana Albu
23 Jun 2026 / 5 Min Read
Ripple has secured preliminary MiCA CASP licence approval from Luxembourg's CSSF to serve financial institutions across the EEA.
Subject to final conditions, the approval will enable the company to offer regulated cryptoasset services to financial institutions and businesses across all 30 countries of the European Economic Area (EEA).
The CASP licence, once fully approved, will work in conjunction with Ripple's existing EU Electronic Money Institution (EMI) licence, also held in Luxembourg. Together, these authorisations will allow European banks, fintechs, and corporates to access Ripple's cryptoasset and stablecoin payments infrastructure through a single integration for the first time, covering the complete payment cycle from collection and exchange through to payout.
In addition, the development is also intended to position Ripple to extend into broader cryptoasset activities across the EEA. Europe represents an established market for the company's products, with a client base spanning a number of larger financial institutions across the region, reflecting growing demand for digital asset services and infrastructure.
The CASP approval is issued under MiCA, the EU's regulatory framework for cryptoasset services. Full approval of the licence will render Ripple fully MiCA-compliant. Cassie Craddock , Managing Director for the UK and Europe at Ripple, noted that MiCA has contributed to a new wave of institutional digital asset adoption, with demand continuing to increase across the region. Craddock observed that financial market infrastructure is transitioning onchain, covering areas such as cross-border payments, settlement, collateral management, and tokenised assets, and that banks and fintechs are actively building digital asset capabilities to remain competitive.
Regulatory context and global licensing footprint
The Luxembourg CASP approval follows Ripple's receipt of an EMI licence and Cryptoasset Registration from the UK's Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in January 2026, extending the company's regulatory reach across both the EU and the UK. The company currently holds more than 75 regulatory licences globally.
 Matthew Osborne , Head of Policy for the UK and Europe at Ripple, acknowledged the constructive approach taken by the CSSF throughout the licensing process. Osborne noted Luxembourg's established position as a centre for financial services regulation in Europe, pointing to the jurisdiction's supervisory experience and proportionate framework for digital assets as key factors in selecting it as Ripple's European regulatory base.

# Context-enrichment: Ripple receives preliminary MiCA CASP licence in Luxembourg
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- On 2026-06-23 Ripple disclosed it received a **preliminary** CASP approval from Luxembourg's CSSF — a "Green Light Letter," **subject to final conditions** (ripple.com; coindesk.com). This is NOT a granted/passportable licence yet: it is a conditional pre-approval. The note's own primary source (The Paypers) is precise: "preliminary… subject to final conditions"; only "full approval… will render Ripple fully MiCA-compliant."
- De-PR: the digest headline "secures a preliminary MiCA CASP License, enabling full-scale payment services across Europe" front-runs reality. EEA-wide passporting activates only on **final** approval, not on the green-light letter. Live-vs-announced: announced/conditional, not yet live.
- **Why structured this way:** the timing is regulatory, not commercial — MiCA's transitional grandfathering window for CASPs **closes 1 July 2026, no extensions** (ESMA; [[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]]). Firms that want to keep serving EEA crypto clients had to be in the pipeline by the deadline. Ripple's June disclosure is a "we made the cut-off" signal: it pairs the new CASP with its **existing EMI licence (also Luxembourg)** so that, once final, banks/fintechs/corporates get "collect → exchange → pay out" through a **single integration for the first time." The EMI is what lets Ripple's clients issue/redeem **RLUSD** (an e-money token, the only authorized stablecoin category under MiCA) under the stablecoin framework.
- Numbers stated: 30 EEA countries; Ripple holds **>75 regulatory licences globally** (PR claim, unverified independently). RLUSD circulation ~$300m as of Q1 2026 per press; reached ~$1bn market cap by Nov 2025 per earlier coverage — directionally large, mark exact figure as press-sourced.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Coinbase** — first US exchange to win a MiCA licence, also via **Luxembourg as EU hub, Jun 2025** ([[Coinbase secures MiCA license in Luxembourg]]). Ripple is ~12 months behind Coinbase on the same jurisdiction choice and is still only at preliminary stage; Coinbase is fully authorized.
- **Fipto (France/AMF)** — first European stablecoin payment player to hold **both a Payment Institution licence and a full MiCA CASP licence**, Jan 2026 ([[Stablecoin player Fipto achieves dual authorisation status]]; [[Ten European fintech deals raise €79.3M in a week]]). Fipto's "manage the entire payment value chain / single compliance framework" framing is **identical** to Ripple's "single integration / full cycle" pitch — and Fipto got there ~5 months earlier (and with a final, not preliminary, CASP).
- **Triple-A (France/AMF, Jun 2026)** ([[Triple-A secures MiCA CASP licence from France's AMF]]), **Nuvei (EU CASP, Jan 2026)** ([[Nuvei secures EU CASP licence under MiCA]]), **BVNK (Malta, Feb 2026)** ([[BVNK secures MiCA licence in Malta]]), **Crossmint (Jan 2026)** ([[Crossmint secures MiCA authorization to expand across EU]]), **Zodia Custody (Luxembourg PI, Jun 2026)** ([[Zodia Custody secures Luxembourg license to expand EU stablecoin services]]), **dtcpay (Luxembourg EMI, Jul 2025)** ([[dtcpay gets green light for Luxembourg EMI licence]]). ~204 firms held full CASP authorization by May 2026 (web).
- **Position: parity/catching up, not ahead.** The dual-rail (EMI + CASP) structure is genuinely strong, but it is no longer novel — Fipto pre-empted the exact pitch. Ripple's edge is **scale and brand** (institutional client base, RLUSD, prime brokerage via Hidden Road/Ripple Prime), not regulatory firstness.
- **Why the lay of the land is this way:** Luxembourg's CSSF has become the **passporting hub of choice** (Coinbase, Bitstamp, Clearstream, dtcpay, Zodia, now Ripple) — proportionate digital-asset framework + EU-wide reach from one filing. The competition has shifted from "who gets a licence" to "who can monetize a single-integration full-stack rail across the EEA."

## [2] Company history / fit
- Dated trajectory: Luxembourg **LEI registration + EU operations launch, Jul 2025** ([[Ripple launches European operations in Luxembourg]], stated goal: EMI under MiCA to issue RLUSD + cross-border payments across 30 EEA) → **preliminary EMI clearance from Luxembourg, Jan 2026** (coindesk.com) → **FCA EMI + Cryptoasset Registration (UK), Jan 2026** → **preliminary CASP, Jun 2026.** The CASP is the logical capstone of a plan telegraphed a year earlier; it fits cleanly.
- Broader context: Ripple deployed >$2.7bn in 2025 M&A — **Hidden Road $1.25bn** (prime broker, now Ripple Prime, closed Oct 2025), **GTreasury ~$1bn**, **Rail ~$200m** (stablecoin platform), plus Standard Custody (2024); reportedly bid $4–5bn for Circle (USDC) pre-IPO (web). 
- **Why Ripple acts this way:** post-SEC, Ripple is rebuilding from "the XRP company" into a **vertically integrated institutional crypto-payments + custody + prime + stablecoin stack**. A take-rate/transaction business needs a regulated moat to sell to banks; MiCA + EMI is the EEA entry permit for that stack. The structural pressure: stablecoins are commoditizing (USDC/EURC dominate MiCA-authorized supply) → Ripple must win on **distribution and integration breadth**, not on the token itself.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new vs press:** the *combination* (EMI for stablecoin issuance/redemption + CASP for crypto services, both Luxembourg) giving clients one integration for the full payment cycle. That is real and useful for institutional buyers who hate stitching multiple licensees together.
- **What's NOT new:** the dual-licence pitch itself (Fipto, Jan 2026, did it first with a *final* CASP). And critically — **this is preliminary**, so there is **zero new live traction** here: no new clients, GMV, or RLUSD EEA volume disclosed at this milestone. "Established market / larger financial institutions" is unquantified PR.
- **Who captures the margin (analysis):** the durable value sits with whoever owns the **client integration + settlement rail**, not the stablecoin (which is a near-zero-margin, reserve-yield business dominated by Circle). Ripple's bet is to be the integration layer so RLUSD rides its own rails — but if banks adopt the rail and route USDC/EURC over it, Ripple captures rail/FX fees but loses the issuer economics. The single-integration moat is real only if switching costs build up before EMT issuance commoditizes further.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Next: convert preliminary → **final** CASP (final conditions unspecified), then passport across 30 EEA states; scale RLUSD issuance/redemption via EEA clients. Watch for the ESMA register entry as the live confirmation.
- Sentiment: XRP-press framed it as a price catalyst ("reclaim $3?" — yahoo/ccn), i.e. retail-narrative driven, not earnings-driven. Regulatory backdrop is the real driver: the **1-July-2026 hard deadline** ([[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]]) forces a shakeout — >80% of EU crypto firms were still unlicensed mid-2026 (web) → consolidation toward ~200 compliant players. 
- Risks: (a) "final conditions" could slip past the deadline, creating a gap; (b) RLUSD is a small EMT vs USDC/EURC incumbency under MiCA; (c) the value-add depends on bank adoption that is announced-not-proven. **Counterintuitive second-order (analysis):** being one of ~200 licensed firms is a moat *now*, but as more banks build in-house digital-asset capability (Craddock's own quote), the licensed-rail premium compresses — Ripple's licence is a ticket to the game, not a durable lead.

## Top challenge / red-team questions (second-order)

1. **Is "secures a preliminary CASP licence" actually a licence?** No — it is a CSSF "Green Light Letter," explicitly subject to final conditions. No EEA passporting until final approval. The digest verb "secures/enabling full-scale payments" overstates a conditional pre-approval. (anti-PR gate: announced ≠ live)

2. **Is the dual EMI+CASP "single integration" genuinely novel?** No. Fipto (FR/AMF, Jan 2026) was the first European stablecoin payment player to hold *both* a Payment Institution licence and a *full* CASP, marketed identically ("entire payment value chain / single compliance framework"). Ripple is ~5 months later and still preliminary on the CASP leg. Novelty = scale/brand, not firstness.

3. **What new traction does this milestone add?** None disclosed — no new clients, no RLUSD EEA volume, no GMV. "Established market / larger financial institutions" is unquantified. The only hard new fact is a regulatory step. Importance is capped by zero new adoption.

4. **Why now — commercial or deadline-driven?** Deadline. MiCA's transitional grandfathering closes **1 July 2026, no extensions** (ESMA). This is a "made the cut-off" disclosure days before the wall, not a product launch.

5. **Could "final conditions" slip past 1 July and create a service gap?** Open — conditions are unspecified. If final approval lags the deadline, EEA crypto-service continuity is at risk; the preliminary letter alone may not be sufficient cover. Worth watching the ESMA register for the live entry.

6. **Does Ripple capture issuer economics or just rail fees?** Open/analysis — durable margin sits with the client-integration + settlement rail, not the EMT (stablecoin). If banks route USDC/EURC over Ripple's rail, Ripple earns FX/rail fees but loses RLUSD issuer yield. The moat is integration switching costs, not the token.

7. **How does RLUSD stack up vs MiCA-authorized incumbents?** Weak on size — USDC/EURC dominate authorized EMT supply; RLUSD ~$300m (Q1 2026, press) / ~$1bn peak (Nov 2025, press). RLUSD is a challenger EMT, not a leader, inside the EEA.

8. **Why Luxembourg specifically?** CSSF = the EU passporting hub of choice (Coinbase, Bitstamp, Clearstream, dtcpay, Zodia). Proportionate digital-asset framework + supervisory experience + one-filing EEA reach. Ripple's own quotes (Osborne) confirm it as the strategic regulatory base. Answered.

9. **Is the >75 global licences claim verifiable?** Open — it is a Ripple PR figure repeated by press; no independent registry tie-out. Treat as company-stated, not independently confirmed.

10. **Who is silent about what?** Economics — no take-rate, no client names, no RLUSD EEA targets, no fee model for the "single integration." The PR sells breadth of permission, not unit economics. Standard regulatory-PR opacity.

11. **Does this re-rate XRP/Ripple fundamentally?** No (analysis). XRP-press framed it as a price catalyst ("reclaim $3"), i.e. retail narrative. The licence is a permission ticket; ~204 firms held full CASP by May 2026. Being licensed is table stakes for the EEA, not a durable competitive lead.

12. **What is the second-order risk to the licensed-rail premium?** Bank disintermediation — Craddock's own quote notes banks/fintechs are "actively building digital asset capabilities." As in-house capability grows, the licensed-rail premium compresses. Capital/licence concentration looks like safety but can become a low-margin commodity rail. (analysis)

13. **Is this a beat vs Ripple's own telegraphed plan?** In-line. The plan was set in Jul 2025 (LEI + EU launch, stated EMI-for-RLUSD goal) and advanced via preliminary EMI (Jan 2026) + FCA EMI/registration (Jan 2026). The CASP is the expected capstone, executed on schedule — no surprise.

14. **Does the UK FCA leg add EEA value?** No directly — post-Brexit the UK is outside the EEA passport; the FCA EMI/registration extends UK reach separately. It signals execution breadth but does not strengthen the MiCA/EEA position.

15. **What would make the thesis wrong (falsifier)?** If, within ~12 months of final approval, Ripple discloses named EEA bank integrations with material RLUSD/rail volume, the "permission-not-traction" read flips to a genuine distribution moat. Absent that, the milestone stays a regulatory checkbox.

Importance: 3/5 — Material regulatory milestone for a large, well-capitalized institutional player that completes a year-long, clearly-telegraphed EEA strategy just before the hard 1-July MiCA deadline; the EMI+CASP single-integration stack is genuinely useful. But it is **preliminary** (not a final licence), **not novel** (Fipto did the dual-licence first), and carries **zero new disclosed traction** — so it sits above routine single-jurisdiction CASP grants but below a true market-moving event.

Entry barrier is now **regulatory** (capital + licence) far more than tech. Why now: the **1-July-2026 MiCA hard deadline, no extensions** ([[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]]) forces a one-time shakeout — >80% of EU crypto firms were still unlicensed mid-2026 (web), so the licensed cohort gets a temporary scarcity premium. Absent disclosed volume, it stays a regulatory checkbox.
