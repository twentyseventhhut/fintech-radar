FINOS announces plan to launch open source resiliency alliance
Sinziana Albu
29 Jun 2026 / 5 Min Read
FINOS has announced its intent to establish OSERA, a global, vendor-neutral open source resiliency alliance for financial services.
The initiative follows a successful pilot phase involving Deutsche Bank, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Royal Bank of Canada (RBC), and TD Bank Group. During that phase, critical Java project versions were hardened by Moderne and released to a Sonatype Nexus repository neutrally hosted by FINOS.
The core premise behind OSERA is that financial institutions depend on broadly overlapping sets of open source components and versions, meaning a flaw in a shared dependency poses a simultaneous risk to multiple firms. Rather than each institution independently addressing the same vulnerability at its own cost, the alliance seeks to centralise that remediation in a neutral, governed environment, where fixes are produced once and made available to all members.
OSERA is designed as a downstream complement to Akrites, the Linux Foundation's recently announced cross-industry programme for coordinated disclosure and upstreaming of vulnerability fixes. Alongside the Open Source Security Foundation, OSERA intends to represent the financial sector's interests in defining industry-wide remediation standards.
 Gabriele Columbro , executive director of FINOS, noted that AI-assisted scanning has reduced the time needed to identify serious vulnerabilities from weeks to minutes, creating the prospect of a significant increase in newly disclosed vulnerabilities across both current and legacy software versions. Furthermore, Columbro stated that FINOS began exploring mutualised backpatching and common supply chain standards in late 2025, and that AI-driven threats have since made this approach a priority at scale.
Regulatory alignment and pilot outcomes
According to the official press release, the alliance is being developed with structured compliance as a central objective. OSERA aims to provide a shared, auditable framework for meeting regulatory obligations under the EU's Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), the Network and Information Security Directive 2 (NIS2), and the EU Cyber Resilience Act, whose enforcement duties are set to begin in 2026.
During the pilot, four widely used Java frameworks were backpatched and released in a member-only repository, with end-to-end consumption validated at three member banks without requiring changes to existing CI tooling. A shared prioritisation tool, the Risk Navigator, was also developed to allow member firms to collectively manage backpatch sequencing.
 Dov Katz , Managing Director and Distinguished Engineer at Morgan Stanley, stated that at the scale large financial institutions operate, producing fixes represents only part of the challenge, with reliable consumption across a complex, regulated software estate being equally important. At the same time, Katz indicated that OSERA is intended to align the ecosystem around practical, implementation-led standards for how open source fixes are produced, validated, and consumed.
The alliance plans to govern backpatches on a time-bound basis, typically spanning 12 to 24 months, with vendors contracted under service level agreements.

# Context-enrichment: Goldman Sachs hires for potential Marcus launch in Ireland
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

> ⚠️ NOTE-INTEGRITY FLAG: The aggregated text and the only cached primary source in this note are entirely about **FINOS / OSERA open-source resiliency alliance** — the Marcus/Ireland headline appears only as a "News on Fintech" sidebar link in The Paypers newsletter. So the note's body does NOT actually cover the Marcus story; enrichment below is reconstructed from external reporting (Bloomberg, Irish Times) plus the internal corpus. ## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **The fact:** Goldman Sachs has begun posting LinkedIn job ads in Dublin for a handful of roles focused on its **consumer-deposits business and regulatory interactions** in Ireland, to "assess the viability" of launching Marcus there (Bloomberg, 25 Jun 2026). This is **hiring to explore**, not a committed launch — no licence granted, no live product, no date. The Central Bank of Ireland "won't confirm or comment on individual applications."
- **This is NOT new news — it's a 2026 escalation of a 2025 story.** Bloomberg first reported Goldman was "in talks with regulators" about an Irish Marcus launch on **13 May 2025**; the Irish Times ran "déjà vu as Goldman Sachs eyes Irish household savings" on 14 May 2025. The June 2026 item is the **"talks → first hiring"** follow-up — a real incremental step, hence fresh, but a low-drama one.
- **Why structured this way (the real story, de-PR'd):** This is **deposit-funding / treasury strategy, not a retail-consumer comeback.** Marcus today = a savings-deposit gatherer whose cheap deposits fund the wider Goldman group's trading and lending. The trigger is explicit in reporting: the **UK caps how much Goldman can raise from depositors** before it can no longer use that cash to fund the investment bank — so GS needs a **new euro-denominated deposit pool**. Ireland is attractive because (a) Irish household deposits earn **below the euro-area average**, leaving margin to undercut, (b) the market is **heavily concentrated** (only ~3 major banks after the post-crisis exodus of NatWest/Ulster, KBC, Danske), and (c) Ireland gives an EU/euro funding base. Bloomberg frames the prize as **"tens of billions of euros in cheap funding."**

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Irish/EU deposit & neobank field:** **Revolut** (banking via Lithuanian licence, very large Irish user base, now a primary account for many) is the standout disruptor; **N26**, **Bunq**, and deposit-marketplaces (**Raisin**) also gather euro savings. The three Irish incumbents (AIB, Bank of Ireland, PTSB) pay low rates — the gap Marcus would target.
- **The more telling peer is the parallel US-giant-into-EU-deposits move:** **JPMorgan Chase launched a digital savings bank in Germany in May 2026** with a ~4% promotional rate ([[Chase launches digital savings bank in Germany]]), explicitly a deposit-product entry like Chase UK (2021). Goldman's own Germany ambition (planned for 2019) was on the same list as Ireland. So Marcus-Ireland is **one instance of a broader pattern: US wholesale banks chasing cheap, sticky euro retail deposits to diversify funding**, not a strategic re-embrace of consumer banking.
- **Position:** Goldman is a **late, narrow entrant** — savings-only, no current account, no lending. Against Revolut's full-stack app and Chase's marketing muscle, Marcus competes on rate, not product breadth.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Marcus arc (dated):** Launched US 2016 (savings + unsecured personal loans); **UK savings 2018** (still live today, deposits held in **Goldman Sachs International Bank**, FSCS-protected to £85k/£120k); Apple Card 2019; GM card. Then the **retreat**: ~$3bn+ consumer losses accumulated by 2022 (cumulative consumer-unit losses later cited well above that); David Solomon's **partial retreat announced Jan 2023** (scrapped the broad checking account, mothballed US personal loans); sold GreenSky; exited the GM card; and handed the **Apple Card to JPMorgan** ([[JPMorgan to take over Apple Card from Goldman Sachs]]; first reported 2025, transfer 2026). What survived the cull is **exactly the savings-deposit franchise** that Ireland would extend.
- **Why GS acts this way:** The Ireland push is **continuous with the survivor, not a reversal.** Goldman kept Marcus deposits precisely because cheap, stable retail funding is valuable to a wholesale bank that lacks a deposit base. Reframing the central question: this is **not "is Goldman getting back into consumer banking?" (no) but "can a wholesale bank profitably run a thin euro deposit-gathering utility under EU/Irish regulation?"**

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty is low.** Same product (online savings) GS has run in the UK since 2018, now ported to a new jurisdiction. The genuinely new element is the **EU/Irish regulatory perimeter** and the funding-diversification logic.
- **Traction so far = zero in Ireland** — job ads only. No licence, no customers, no balance. UK Marcus is the proof-of-concept that it can work; the open question is whether Irish economics (deposit-guarantee costs, low-rate but sticky base, competition from Revolut on UX) clear Goldman's funding-cost hurdle.
- **Who captures the margin:** the value is **internal transfer pricing** — cheap euro deposits feeding the group's trading/lending book — not a standalone consumer P&L. That's why it can be rational even though "consumer banking" as a business failed for Goldman.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Next steps:** licence/authorisation discussions with the Central Bank of Ireland; a savings-only launch (no current account expected near-term); Germany is the parallel target. Timeline unverified — "potential" launch.
- **Sentiment:** Irish commentary is **sceptical/"déjà vu"** — Goldman has eyed Irish/German deposits before and pulled back; and its overall consumer-banking record is poor. Counterintuitive second-order point: an entrant that competes purely on rate with no relationship product is **funding-cost sensitive and exit-prone** — if euro rates fall or the group's funding need eases, Marcus-Ireland could be wound down as quickly as the US consumer products were. **Risk: another half-built consumer foray that retreats.**

8. **"Tens of billions of euros" — sourced or speculative?** Bloomberg's framing of the addressable prize, not a Goldman target. Treat as illustrative.
9. **Who are the real competitors?** Revolut (Lithuanian licence, large Irish base), N26, Bunq, Raisin; plus US-giant deposit entrants — JPMorgan Chase Germany (May 2026). Verified.

## Earnings Review

> [!warning] Not an earnings release — financial backdrop only. The news ("Goldman Sachs begins hiring for potential Marcus launch in Ireland", The Paypers, 2026-06-29) is a hiring/expansion item with **no figures**. Below is the latest reported Platform Solutions / consumer-banking segment context as the backdrop for a possible Marcus Europe push. Numbers from Goldman's own filings (Q1 2026 8-K/10-Q) and 2024–2025 releases. Anti-staleness: latest reported quarter is **Q1 2026 (released 2026-04-13)** — fresh (<3 months from 2026-06-29).

**Backdrop read (not a beat/miss).** The Marcus consumer franchise sits in a segment Goldman is actively *winding down*, not scaling: **Platform Solutions Q1 2026 net revenues $411M, down 33% YoY** (vs $610M Q1 2025), dragged by Apple Card held-for-sale markdowns; the Apple Card program is being handed to JPMorgan Chase. A Marcus *re-expansion into Ireland/Europe* would run counter to the reported retreat from U.S. consumer finance — the deposits gathering is the one piece Goldman is keeping (inside Asset & Wealth Management), so an Ireland deposit play is consistent with "deposits stay, lending/cards go."

**Key figures (Goldman's own filings).**
- Firmwide Q1 2026: net revenues **$17.23bn**, net earnings **$5.63bn**, diluted EPS **$17.55**, annualized ROE **19.8%**, ROTE **21.3%** (record-revenue quarter).
- **Platform Solutions Q1 2026: net revenues $411M, −33% YoY** (Q1 2025 $610M per the Q1'26 8-K; note a separate aggregator cited $676M for Q1'25 — treat the Goldman-stated −33%/$610M base as primary, the $676M figure [UNSOURCED-DISCREPANCY]). Pre-tax earnings **$75M**; provision for credit losses **$1M**. Management attributes the YoY revenue drop to "net markdowns … related to the Apple Card loan portfolio, which was transferred to held for sale in 2025."
- **Q4 2025 Platform Solutions: net revenue LOSS of $(1.68)bn**, driven by **$2.26bn of Apple Card markdowns** (held-for-sale + contract-termination); partly offset elsewhere by a **$2.48bn loan-loss reserve release** in provision. This was the segment's trough quarter.
- FY2024 Platform Solutions net revenues **$2.43bn (+2% vs 2023)**; average gross loans **$20.48bn** (2024) vs $21.48bn (2023) — a shrinking book even before the Apple Card exit. Goldman's standing target was **pre-tax profitability for Platform Solutions by end-2025**.
- **Marcus deposits:** reported inside Asset & Wealth Management (Private banking and lending), historically **>$100bn**; Q1 2026 commentary flags "lower deposit spreads related to Marcus deposits, partially offset by higher deposit balances" — i.e. balances growing, spread/NIM compressing.

**By segment / driver.** Platform Solutions is the smallest and only loss-prone segment, now defined by the Apple Card unwind: the Q4'25 markdown ($(1.68)bn print) and the Q1'26 residual drag (−33% YoY). Underlying consumer *deposits* (the Marcus product an Irish launch would sell) sit in AWM and are growing in balance terms while spreads compress. So the consumer-banking economics Goldman would carry into Ireland are: cheap-ish deposit gathering (margin-pressured) decoupled from the card/lending losses it is exiting.

**vs expectations / prior period.** No consensus line for the Platform Solutions sub-segment (not separately forecast by the Street) → qualitative only. YoY momentum: Platform Solutions revenue −33% (Q1'26 vs Q1'25); the FY trajectory ran ~$2.43bn (2024, +2%) → Q4'25 $(1.68)bn loss print → Q1'26 $411M recovery as markdowns roll off. Firmwide Q1'26 was a beat-flavored, record-revenue quarter (EPS $17.55, ROE 19.8%), so the consumer drag is immaterial to the group P&L — which is exactly why Goldman can afford an opportunistic, low-cost Ireland hiring probe.

**Guidance / forward.** No segment guidance tied to this news. Standing target: Platform Solutions pre-tax profitability by end-2025; the Apple Card-to-Chase transition is expected to take ~24 months, during which Goldman keeps operating (and reporting) the program. Implication for a Marcus Ireland launch: a *deposit-led, balance-sheet-light* European entry would fit the post-Apple-Card strategy (keep low-cost funding, drop consumer lending). (analysis)

**Thesis-flags.**
1. **Strategic dissonance** — Goldman is publicly *exiting* U.S. consumer finance (Apple Card → Chase, GreenSky already sold), yet hiring for a Marcus *Europe* launch. Fact → why: the kept piece is deposit-gathering, not lending. Why it matters: an Ireland deposit play is cheap funding diversification, not a return to the loss-making consumer-credit model. Second-order: low execution risk to group ROE, but also low strategic conviction signal — a "potential" launch staffed by a few hires, not a committed roll-out.
2. **Deposit economics, not credit economics** — Marcus deposits already >$100bn with *compressing spreads*; an EU deposit base helps funding/liquidity optics but is NIM-dilutive in a rate-cut path.
