Revolut's Morocco entry delayed by three regulatory priorities
Sinziana Albu
25 Jun 2026 / 5 Min Read
Revolut has held exploratory talks with Morocco's central bank, which cited three regulatory priorities blocking a potential market entry.
 Abdellatif Jouahri , governor of Bank Al-Maghrib, confirmed that a Revolut delegation comprising mainly senior UK executives alongside a Moroccan representative visited Rabat earlier in June 2026. The meeting was described as exploratory, with the delegation citing Morocco's macroeconomic indicators and its potential as a gateway for further African expansion.
Jouahri clarified that the delegation did not come to request a banking licence, stating that Bank Al-Maghrib is currently engaged in three major institutional processes that do not allow the central bank to respond favourably to this type of request at present.
The first involves ongoing negotiations with European partners over new EU regulations governing cross-border banking. Remittances represent a critical pillar of Morocco's economy, reaching USD 12.2 billion in 2024, and any regulatory disruption to flows from the Moroccan diaspora concentrated in France and other European countries carries significant consequences for the country's foreign currency reserves and domestic investment.
The second concerns scheduled evaluations by the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund, expected before the end of 2026, which assess the resilience of Morocco's financial system and its alignment with international standards.
The third involves a forthcoming review by the Financial Action Task Force, known by its French acronym GAFI, focusing on Morocco's anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing compliance. Jouahri described this as one of the most sensitive files for financial authorities, given its direct impact on Morocco's standing among international investors and institutions.
In addition, Jouahri noted that Moroccan banks are already active in the digital segment Revolut is targeting, pointing to Attijariwafa Bank's launch of Simple in May 2026, a full-service digital banking application offering online account opening, virtual and physical cards, and instant transfers.
Revolut's position
Revolut's Morocco push began in 2025, when the company appointed Yacine Faqir, a former World Bank and Mastercard executive, as its Morocco-based chief executive. Faqir stated that the meeting was constructive and that Revolut fully understands and respects the current constraints facing the institution, confirming the company remains committed to the Moroccan project.
Revolut reported revenue of approximately USD 5.8 billion for 2025, a 46% increase year on year, with pre-tax profit reaching approximately USD 2.2 billion, up 57%. Its global user base stood at 68.3 million, and the company has announced plans to invest approximately USD 12.6 billion over five years across Latin America, Asia-Pacific, the Middle East, and Africa.
Regulatory experts have estimated that any Moroccan market entry would require between 12 and 24 months from preparation through to deployment, and have suggested that a partnership model with a local bank may prove more realistic than a standalone licence.

# Context-enrichment: Revolut's Morocco entry delayed by three regulatory priorities
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**FRESHNESS: fresh.** This is a NEW development, not a re-run. The corpus already holds [[Revolut names first executive in Morocco]] (2025-07, appointment of a Morocco CEO) and the broader Africa push in [[Revolut hiring in South Africa ahead of banking launch]] and [[Revolut applies for South African banking licence]]. What is new here (25 Jun 2026): a Revolut delegation of senior UK execs physically met Bank Al-Maghrib in Rabat in June 2026, and BAM governor Abdellatif Jouahri PUBLICLY declined to entertain entry now, naming three specific regulatory workstreams. That is a "talks → regulator says not yet" event, materially beyond the 2025 leadership appointment. Not a duplicate.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- A Revolut delegation (mostly senior UK executives + one Moroccan representative) held an **exploratory** meeting with Bank Al-Maghrib (BAM) in Rabat in June 2026. Governor Abdellatif Jouahri confirmed it publicly ~23-25 Jun 2026.
- Crucial de-PR point: Jouahri said the delegation **did NOT request a banking licence** — "They did not come to say: we want a banking licence." So the headline framing of a "delayed entry" overstates a still-pre-application, exploratory contact. There is no application on the table to "delay." (analysis)
- BAM is declining to engage now, citing three institutional priorities: (1) negotiations with European partners over new EU cross-border banking rules (remittances reached ~USD 12.2bn / MAD 122bn in 2024 — a foreign-reserve pillar); (2) World Bank/IMF financial-system assessments due before end-2026; (3) an upcoming FATF/GAFI AML-CFT review. Jouahri reportedly told Revolut to come back "in a few years."
- **Why framed this way:** Revolut/Faqir's PR ("constructive meeting," "remains committed") converts a regulatory brush-off into momentum. The substance is the opposite of momentum: the central bank publicly put the file on ice on a multi-year horizon. The real signal is **regulator gatekeeping**, not Revolut progress.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Local incumbents/digital:** Attijariwafa Bank launched "Simple" in May 2026 — full-service digital app (online onboarding, virtual/physical cards, instant transfers). Cash Plus's M-Wallet reportedly >1M users, positioning as a "digital bank in all but name." Jouahri explicitly pointed to Attijariwafa's Simple as evidence locals already serve Revolut's target segment — a regulator citing domestic champions is itself a protectionist signal. (analysis)
- **Revolut's own MENA/Africa front:** UAE in-principle payments approval ([[Revolut secures in-principle approval for UAE licence]], Sep 2025); South Africa Section 12 bank application ([[Revolut applies for South African banking licence]], Nov 2025), with a waitlist nearing ~100k but launch only ~2028. So Morocco is the **slowest** of Revolut's three MENA/Africa fronts — behind UAE (approval secured) and South Africa (formal application filed).
- **Position:** catching-up / blocked. In Morocco specifically Revolut is behind both local digital launches and its own faster markets.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Morocco push began 2025: appointed Yacine Faqir (ex-World Bank, ex-Mastercard, ex-Quantik Morocco) as Morocco CEO ([[Revolut names first executive in Morocco]]). Earlier reporting named Amine Berrada-linked leadership — confirm exact name vs Faqir (open).
- Fits the declared global plan: ~USD 13bn ("$12.6bn" per The Paypers / ~£11-13bn elsewhere) over five years, 100M users by ~mid-2027, 30+ new markets by 2030, spanning LatAm, APAC, Middle East, Africa ([[India launch central to Revolut's 100 million user target]], [[European fintech deals raise €330M over the week]]).
- 2025 financials cited: revenue ~USD 5.8bn (+46% YoY), pre-tax profit ~USD 2.2bn (+57%), 68.3M users. **Why act this way:** Revolut needs new-market user growth to hit the 100M target and justify its ~$45-75bn valuation; emerging markets (remittance corridors) are the cheap-acquisition engine. Morocco is a logical remittance/gateway play, but the regulator owns the gate.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- Novelty is LOW. No product, no licence, no users in Morocco. This is a regulatory-status update on a still-exploratory file. Traction = zero on the ground.
- Real value-add of the NEWS (not the company): it reveals **how a mid-income African regulator gates a Big Fintech** — BAM is sequencing fintech liberalization behind macro-stability files (EU remittance rules, IMF/WB review, FATF). Second order: the binding constraint on Revolut's emerging-markets thesis is not capital or product but **host-country regulatory readiness + incumbent protection** — the same wall that pushes South Africa to ~2028. (analysis)
- A partnership-with-a-local-bank model is floated by experts as more realistic than a standalone licence — which would dilute Revolut's usual full-stack control. Who captures margin then shifts toward the local partner. (hypothesis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Timeline measured in **years, not months**: experts cite 12-24 months minimum from prep to deployment even if greenlit; BAM signals a multi-year wait. Expect Morocco to lag UAE and South Africa.
- Risks: FATF outcome could tighten, not loosen, the door; EU cross-border rule changes could reshape the remittance economics that make Morocco attractive in the first place. Counter-intuitively, the very remittance flows that draw Revolut are why BAM is most protective. (analysis)
- Sentiment: local press (Moroccan/French outlets) read it as a clear "not now"; "Morocco unlikely to allow entry" (AGBI). Revolut spins commitment. Market-moving? Minimal — no financial impact, signaling value only.

3. **Which of the three priorities is the real binding constraint?** Likely the FATF/GAFI AML-CFT review (Jouahri called it "one of the most sensitive files"); the others are scheduling. Open which one actually moves first.
4. **Why does BAM cite Attijariwafa's "Simple"?** A regulator naming a domestic digital champion signals incumbent protection, not just market maturity. How protectionist is the underlying motive? Open.

7. **Partnership vs standalone licence — who captures the margin?** If Revolut must partner a local bank, the local partner captures distribution/deposit economics and Revolut loses its full-stack model. Open whether Revolut would accept that.
8. **How does Morocco compare to Revolut's faster MENA/Africa fronts?** Behind UAE (in-principle approval, Sep 2025) and South Africa (Section 12 filed, Nov 2025; launch ~2028). Morocco is the laggard.

13. **Is "come back in a few years" a hard no or a soft sequence?** Soft sequence tied to 2026 IMF/WB + FATF cycles; door may open post-review. But no guarantee FATF outcome eases entry.
14. **Any financial-market impact?** None material; pure signaling story. Supports a low importance.
15. **Does the local digital-banking launch (Simple, Cash Plus >1M wallet) close the window?** Incumbents are moving fast; by the time BAM is "ready," local share may be entrenched — Revolut could arrive into a more contested market.

**Importance: 3/5** — A real, fresh, well-sourced regulatory event (central-bank governor on record declining a high-profile Big Fintech, with three named macro/AML files), useful as a clean illustration of EM regulatory gatekeeping and incumbent protection. But it is signaling-only: no licence requested, no product, no users, no financial impact, and Morocco is a minor node in Revolut's 30+ market plan, behind UAE and South Africa. ## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a neobank market-entry / remittance story, not a deal. Morocco's draw is its remittance economy: inflows reached ~USD 12.2bn (note source) / USD 12.9bn = ~8% of GDP in 2024, 3rd-largest in Africa, with a ~3M-strong diaspora concentrated in France/EU (per IFAD, World Bank, via familyremittances.org / ifad.org, 2024-25). Banking penetration is shallow — ~44% of adults banked by 2021 vs 29% in 2017 (World Bank Findex, via The Fintech Times) — the classic emerging-market neobank gap. But the structure is gated, not open: a single regulator (Bank Al-Maghrib) owns market access and is sequencing fintech liberalization behind three macro-stability files — EU cross-border banking rules, IMF/World Bank financial-system review (due end-2026), and the FATF/GAFI AML-CFT review (per the note, Jouahri on record 25 Jun 2026). Why now: this isn't a "Revolut wins" event but a regulator publicly gating a Big Fintech — the binding constraint on the EM thesis is host-country regulatory readiness + incumbent protection, not Revolut capital or product (analysis). Morocco's digital-banks market is small in absolute terms — projected to ~USD 1.07bn volume by 2029, +13.6% CAGR (Statista, 2024) — so the real prize is the remittance corridor, which is precisely what BAM most fears disrupting.

**Competitive landscape.** Neobank KPIs: KYC'd/retail users, MAU & activation (conversion), ARPU, deposits, and for a remittance play the take rate on FX/cross-border flow. Key players in Morocco: domestic incumbents moving first — Attijariwafa Bank launched "Simple" (full-service digital app: online onboarding, virtual/physical cards, instant transfers) in May 2026; Cash Plus M-Wallet reportedly >1M users (note context). Basis of competition is distribution + regulatory licence, not product — and a regulator citing a domestic champion (Simple) as a reason to wait is itself an incumbent-protection signal (analysis). Revolut's own MENA/Africa front: UAE in-principle payments approval (Sep 2025), South Africa Section-12 bank application (Nov 2025, launch ~2028). Recent peer move: Nubank crossed ~120M customers (Q2 2026, businessofapps); Wise continues to compound the cross-border FX flow Revolut covets. Revolut's Morocco position: blocked / laggard — behind both local digital launches and its own faster UAE/South Africa fronts. Moat globally is genuine (network/scale, 68.3M users, full-stack licences) but in Morocco the moat is zero — no licence, no users, no product (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** IR-grounded base case (Revolut Group Holdings Ltd, Consolidated FY2025, audited): Revenue £4,515.8m (2024: £3,090.0m, +46%), of which fee income £3,429.2m and interest income £974.2m; PBT £1.7bn, net profit £1.3bn (5th profitable year); 68.3M retail customers (+30%), 767k business (Annual Report 2025). USD framing: rev ~USD 6.0bn, PBT ~USD 2.3bn (revolut.com news, CNBC 2026-03-24). Note's "$5.8bn rev / $2.2bn PBT" single-source figures are slightly understated vs the audited group accounts — the audited USD-equivalent is ~$6.0bn rev / ~$2.3bn PBT.
- Revolut (private, last round Nov 2025): P/S = USD 75bn / 6.0bn = **12.5x**; price/retail-user = 75bn / 68.3M = **~USD 1,098/user**. Valuation up 67% from $45bn (2024).
- Nubank (NU, public): P/S = ~58bn / ~18bn ≈ **3.1x** (multiples.vc / geo.sig.ai, Jun 2026); price/user ≈ 58bn / ~120M ≈ **~USD 483/user**.
- Wise (WISE.L, public): P/S ≈ 11bn / 3bn ≈ **3.4x** (multiples.vc, Jun 2026).
Read: Revolut's ~12.5x P/S is **rich vs listed neobank peers** (Nubank 3.1x, Wise 3.4x) and ~2x+ the price-per-user, but it is a private last-round mark (not a market clearing price) and Revolut grows revenue ~46% vs the multiple — a hyper-growth premium that is plausible only if the EM/global expansion (Morocco among 30+ markets) actually converts; Morocco's public rebuff is a small data point that the conversion is regulator-gated, not automatic. Internal comps from the base: [[Revolut names first executive in Morocco]] (2025 Morocco CEO appointment), [[Revolut applies for South African banking licence]], [[Revolut Bank launches in Mexico targeting 5.5M customers]], [[Neobanks claim 27% of Spain's retail market as Revolut surges]]. EV/EBITDA — no data (private; clean EV unavailable).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory gatekeeping (binding).** A central-bank governor on record declining to engage, behind FATF + IMF/WB + EU-rules files, means entry is measured in years; experts cite 12–24 months minimum even if greenlit (note). Second-order: the same wall delays South Africa to ~2028 — EM regulatory readiness, not capital, caps the expansion-engine that the 12.5x multiple is priced on.
2. **Remittance-thesis self-cannibalization.** The ~USD 12bn corridor that attracts Revolut is exactly what BAM is most protective of, and pending EU cross-border rules could reshape its economics. Second-order: the very driver of the entry case is the regulator's reason to keep the door shut.
3. **Incumbent entrenchment + forced partnership.** Local digital banks (Simple, Cash Plus >1M) are scaling now; by the time BAM is "ready" Revolut may face an entrenched market, and the floated local-partner model would hand deposit/distribution economics to the partner — diluting Revolut's full-stack margin (hypothesis).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Not a re-rating and not a new entry — a signal that Revolut's emerging-markets growth narrative (the justification for a $75bn / 12.5x mark) is regulator-gated market by market, with Morocco the laggard of three MENA/Africa fronts (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: Morocco stays a non-event for Revolut until after the end-2026 IMF/WB + FATF cycles clear; trigger to revisit = a post-review BAM signal OR a local-bank partnership announcement (which would itself confirm Revolut accepting a diluted, non-full-stack model). What breaks the thesis: a surprise licence/sandbox grant before the 2026 reviews conclude.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
**Note:** the trigger event (Bank Al-Maghrib declining a Revolut market-entry approach) is a regulatory/expansion item, not an earnings print — there is no Morocco-specific results data. But Revolut is IR-covered, so this is a financials read on **Revolut Group Holdings Ltd** from its **own audited accounts** (Annual Report 2025, Consolidated Financial Statements for the year ended 31 December 2025; filed/IR-published 2026-04-29), used as the primary source ahead of the press USD figures in the note.

**Verdict (private co — no consensus; YoY-trend read).** STRONG GROWTH + DEEPENING PROFITABILITY · Group revenue £4,515.8m (+46.1% YoY) · PBT £1.7bn, net profit £1.3bn (5th consecutive profitable year) · 68.3M retail customers (+30%) · drivers: fee/transaction income scaling faster than balance-sheet interest income · expansion read-through: the cash generation that funds the declared ~USD 12.6bn / 5-year emerging-markets build (Africa included) is real and self-financed — the binding constraint on Morocco is the host regulator, not Revolut's balance sheet.

**Key figures (audited, group, with growth).**
- **Group revenue £4,515.8m** (£4,515,770k) vs £3,090.0m FY2024 → **+46.1% YoY**.
- **Fee income £3,429.2m** (£3,429,229k) vs £2,216.8m → **+54.7% YoY** — now ~75.9% of revenue (2024: ~71.7%). Transaction/fee income is the lead engine and is gaining mix share.
- **Interest income £974.2m** (£974,159k) vs £790.2m → **+23.3% YoY** — ~21.6% of revenue (2024: ~25.6%); growing but decelerating relative to fees (rate tailwind fading).
- **Other income £112.4m** (£112,382k) vs £83.0m → **+35.4% YoY**.
- **Cost of sales £(998.1)m** vs £(600.9)m → **+66.1% YoY** — rising faster than revenue (scaling/expansion cost), a margin-watch item.
- **PBT ~£1.7bn**; **net profit ~£1.3bn** — 5th straight profitable year (per the group accounts; cross-checked against the note's USD framing below).
- **Retail customers 68.3M (+30% YoY)**; **business customers 767k**. USD-equivalent framing from the company release / press: revenue ~USD 6.0bn, record PBT ~USD 2.3bn.

**By segment / driver.** Growth is fee-/transaction-led: fee income +54.7% outpaced both interest income (+23.3%) and total revenue (+46.1%), lifting the fee share to ~76% — Revolut is compounding more on usage/cross-border/FX activity than on the rate-sensitive deposit book. The flip side is operating leverage going the wrong way at the cost-of-sales line: cost of sales +66.1% > revenue +46.1%, consistent with the heavy expansion spend (new markets, licences, headcount) it discloses — worth flagging as the figure management is least likely to lead with.

**vs expectations / prior period.** No public consensus (private company) → trend/profitability read only. Revenue +46.1% YoY is broadly in line with the ~46% headline the press carries; the audited group revenue **£4,515.8m / ~USD 6.0bn** is modestly **above** the note's single-source **"~USD 5.8bn"**, and audited PBT **~USD 2.3bn** above the note's **"~USD 2.2bn"** — i.e. the press understated the official figures slightly. Customer growth +30% to 68.3M. Trajectory in the corpus is consistent acceleration: 26M (2022) → 38.0M (2023) → 68.3M (FY2025); revenue +46% on top of a +95%-class 2024 base — still hyper-growth, decelerating off a larger base. Related: [[Revolut names first executive in Morocco]], [[Revolut applies for South African banking licence]], [[India launch central to Revolut's 100 million user target]].

**Guidance / forward (private — no quarterly guidance).** No earnings guidance is given. Disclosed forward intent: ~USD 12.6bn (~£11–13bn) investment over five years across LatAm, APAC, Middle East and Africa, and a ~100M-user target by ~mid-2027. Read-through to the Morocco event: FY2025's ~£1.3bn net profit and ~USD 6bn revenue base means the expansion is **self-fundable** — Morocco's delay is therefore NOT a capital/funding problem; the gate is host-country regulatory readiness (BAM sequencing behind EU cross-border rules, IMF/WB review, FATF). The expansion-funding capacity is intact; the expansion-conversion is regulator-paced.

**Thesis-flags.**
1. **Fee-led mix is the quality signal (positive).** Fee income +54.7% pulling the mix to ~76% → revenue is increasingly usage-driven, not rate-driven → less exposed as interest income decelerates (+23.3%) → second-order: durability of the growth story even in a falling-rate environment.
2. **Cost of sales +66.1% > revenue +46.1% (watch).** Expansion is being paid for at the margin → near-term operating leverage compresses as new markets (Morocco, South Africa ~2028, UAE) are seeded before they earn → second-order: the ~12.5x P/S mark needs the EM markets to convert, and Morocco shows that conversion is regulator-gated, lengthening the payback on the USD 12.6bn programme.
3. **Profitability funds the expansion (de-risks the funding leg, not the regulatory leg).** ~£1.3bn net profit, 5th profitable year → the build is self-financed → second-order: a Morocco "not now" dents the timeline/optionality, not the solvency or the thesis core; it is a signaling data point on EM regulatory friction, immaterial to the FY2025 financials.
