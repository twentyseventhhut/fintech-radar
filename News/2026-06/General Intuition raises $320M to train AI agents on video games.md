General Intuition’s $2.3B bet that video games can train AI agents for the real world
As soon as I entered General Intuition’s R&D floor at its New York office, the company’s 31-year-old co-founder and CEO Pim de Witte directed my attention to a monitor perched on a standing desk. Someone appeared to be playing something like Fortnite. It wasn’t a person. 
“Our agent has been playing for 100 hours straight,” Kent Rollins, the company’s chief product officer, said, beaming. 
Before I could get absorbed in the spectacle of an AI navigating the game’s virtual environment, I heard the electronic footsteps of a large quadrupedal robot approaching. 
“The same brain powering the agent playing the game is powering the robot,” de Witte told me. 
Josh Duplantis, a data analyst carrying a laptop streaming a live feed from the robot’s single camera, piped up to explain that the bot’s default mode was “exploration.” 
Relying on that camera, its singular eye, the giant buglike bot walked up to me, circled around me, and continued into the office. It occasionally clipped the legs of chairs or bumped into an errant trash bin, much like a toddler who hasn’t yet learned how her body relates to the world around it. Duplantis said it took just eight minutes of real-world robotics data to fine-tune an AI model for the quadruped. What’s more, that data was collected on the street, not inside the office where the bot was currently navigating itself.
An agentic model that can generalize from gameplay to simulation to embodiment is General Intuition’s raison d’être. And that model’s ability to figure out its place in the world has secured the backing of some heavy hitters. 
On Thursday, General Intuition said it raised $320 million at a $2.3 billion valuation, confirming TechCrunch’s previous reporting . The round brings General Intuition’s total disclosed funding to $454 million, after the $134 million round it raised at launch last October. 
The startup was spun out of de Witte’s other company, Medal, which allows gamers to upload and share video game clips. The hundreds of millions of hours of uploaded gameplay provided the initial dataset to train General Intuition’s model in spatial-temporal reasoning — or understanding how to move through space and time.
But the key ingredient wasn’t the gameplay footage; it was the action labels embedded in those clips: records of exactly what buttons a player pressed and when. Most competitors, de Witte says, are trying to infer actions from video alone, which he argues is insufficient. 
“We view this as just the next stage of future pre-training,” de Witte said. “We have a single model that can respond to Fortnite information on the screen and take action, but also to real-world dynamics in a way that an LLM could never.”
At one point, de Witte set me up with a laptop running General Intuition’s world model, a simulated environment generated frame-by-frame rather than rendered by a traditional game engine. As I often do when testing world models , I walked straight into a series of walls. In other demos I’ve tried, the agents you control sometimes pass right through, but this one didn’t. From the millions of hours of gameplay, it somehow learned that walls are walls, ladders are for scaling, and shadows lengthen as the sun moves.
For General Intuition, this world model isn’t the product; it’s the training environment (referred to as “the gym” internally). The company ultimately wants to sell the agentic model itself, and de Witte argues that the action data embedded in gameplay helps the model discern the “self” from the “environment” in a way that gives it a richer understanding of causality. 
Impressive though General Intuition’s technology appears in demos, the company isn’t the only one trying to crack this problem. Moreover, getting such a model to hold up in the physical world, at scale, hasn’t yet been done. Most approaches of this kind require enormous amounts of real-world data that’s gathered slowly and expensively. General Intuition’s bet is that gameplay is a scalable shortcut.
Its investors are okay with that bet, too. General Intuition’s latest round was led by Khosla Ventures, with participation from General Catalyst, Jeff Bezos, Eric Schmidt, Nico Rosberg, and researchers at Google DeepMind and MIT.
The vast majority of the round will go toward scaling compute capacity. General Intuition has a deal with CoreWeave and plans to focus on pre-training the next version of the model. A slice has been earmarked for making its API more broadly available by the end of summer. 
Vinod Khosla, whose firm led the round, says he was drawn to de Witte’s vision and the company’s proprietary data position.
“If you look at LLMs, when reasoning emerged, it was a quantum leap,” Khosla told me in a phone interview. “In world models, I think the quantum leap is the emergence of intuition in the AI, a human intuition-like capability. The human action data and reaction data you have in games is the key part to the emergence of intuition.”
The vision is a generational company
General Intuition isn’t the only company to notice that Medal’s human action data is a key piece of the puzzle of building dynamic world models and general agents. Brianna Martin, the startup’s chief of staff, said the company was born, in part, after Medal turned down an acquisition offer from a major lab. There have been other offers since, too. 
De Witte and his co-founders, Eloi Alonso, Adam Jelley, and Vincent Micheli, aren’t interested in being acquired, and neither are the startup’s investors looking for an exit just yet. The amount and quality of proprietary data General Intuition has via Medal is one of the reasons Khosla is convinced the startup is a generational bet, not an M&A target; that it could become the backbone for generalized agents and world models in simulation and the real world. 
“At this point, it would be a data acquisition, which is sort of uninteresting,” Khosla said.
Part of that bet also involves trusting de Witte’s values. 
The entrepreneur spent three years working in the humanitarian space, including with Doctors Without Borders. As such, he has drawn a clear line for how General Intuition’s tech will be used: No agents will be employed to harm humans. 
“We don’t want to be an escalatory part of the system,” de Witte said. “Let’s say I were to come out and say, ‘We’re doing lethal autonomy.’ What do you think would happen in other countries?”
That limit on military use cases comes as Silicon Valley is growing ever more bullish on war, though de Witte says he’s happy for his models to be used for search and rescue missions.
De Witte is Dutch, and much of his team is European, which shapes the company’s identity. He says he brought on Martin in part due to her decision to publicly quit Palantir over its work with the United States Immigration and Customs Enforcement.
“I don’t know why Silicon Valley does what it does,” he said. “There’s a reason I’m not there.”
De Witte’s ethics don’t simply limit what the models won’t do. As a gamer who made $1.5 million by building and hosting a private RuneScape server in his teens, de Witte is also thinking about what happens to the people who get left behind by what AI models can do.
General Intuition recently launched a platform called Nerve, a jobs marketplace that lets gamers earn money using their existing setups. Those who sign up start with data labeling and can eventually move toward robot teleoperation and other tasks. Medal’s user base, de Witte noted, is precisely the generation most exposed to AI-driven displacement, and he wants them to have a stake in what’s coming next.
A data flywheel
De Witte wants General Intuition to be an ecosystem enabler, like Anthropic or OpenAI — a model provider that enables others to build on top of its technology. Today, the startup has a handful of customers in gaming, simulation, and robotics.
“We’re not gonna build a self-driving car company,” de Witte said. “We’re gonna make it 10 times easier for the next person to build a self-driving car company.” 
The company says once it gets its API into more customers’ hands, it would be able to test its mettle with a variety of use cases — like testing a robot in a digital twin of a factory floor, powering a humanlike bot inside a gaming studio, or sending a quadruped to navigate hazardous environments.
While a quadruped is the first physical embodiment that General Intuition has tried in the real world, it has also tried drones and other devices, including testing the model in driving games.
“It works on anything that you can control using a game controller or a keyboard mouse,” de Witte said.
The possibility to build a data flywheel is one of the goals.
“We’ll pick customers where we can diversify the embodiments that this generalized foundation model is serving as the backbone for,” de Witte said. “So we’re going to prioritize picking customers on whether they can offer real-world data that’s going to be interesting and useful to move the needle on research. And if they’d have an agile internal team where we can be real embedded partners and learn from each other.”
Khosla said that General Intuition’s proprietary data is what got it this far, and its ability to continue collecting data that no one else has will be essential. Especially because, despite impressive demos, whether the simulation-to-real-world transfer can hold at scale is an open question that nobody has fully answered yet.

# Context-enrichment: General Intuition raises $320M to train AI agents on video games
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 2026-06-25/26 General Intuition (GI) confirmed a **$320M round at a $2.3B valuation**, led by **Khosla Ventures**, with General Catalyst, Jeff Bezos, Eric Schmidt, Nico Rosberg, and (as individuals) researchers at Google DeepMind & MIT. This brings **total disclosed funding to $454M** after the **$134M ($133.7M) seed in Oct 2025**, also Khosla-led — Khosla's largest seed check since OpenAI (2018). Some outlets label it "Series A"; the size/valuation are consistent across sources. The round "confirms TechCrunch's previous reporting" — i.e., it was pre-leaked (earlier framed as "in talks to raise $300M at $2B"), so the announcement is the *closing* of an already-reported deal, not a surprise.

**The actual asset (de-PR'd):** GI is spun out of **Medal** (de Witte's gameplay-clip sharing app: ~10M MAU, ~2B videos/year across tens of thousands of games). The real moat is **not the video** but the **action labels** embedded in clips — exact button presses + timing. GI's pitch: competitors *infer* actions from pixels (lossy); GI has ground-truth action data. The model learns spatial-temporal reasoning and "self vs environment" causality.
- The "world model" is **internal infrastructure ("the gym")**, NOT the product. The product is the **agentic model**, sold API-first; API "more broadly available by end of summer [2026]" → i.e., **not yet broadly available** = announced, not live.
- **Use of funds:** "vast majority" → **compute** (a CoreWeave deal) for pre-training the next model; a slice for the API.
- **Traction today (de-PR'd):** demos only — a quadruped fine-tuned on "8 minutes" of real-world data; an agent playing a Fortnite-like game 100h. Customers: "a handful" in gaming/simulation/robotics (unnamed, unquantified). **No GMV/ARR/named clients.** Sim-to-real transfer at scale is, per Khosla himself, "an open question nobody has fully answered."

**Why framed this way:** the PR anchors to vision ("generational company," "next stage of pre-training," ethics) precisely because there is no revenue/adoption to anchor to. The honest core is *a data-advantage bet + a compute war-chest*, not a shipped product.

## [1] Competitors / peers
This is a **world-model / embodied-AI funding super-cycle** ("$1.8B in two days," foundation-model funding in Q1'26 ~2x all of 2025):
- **AMI Labs** (Yann LeCun, Paris): **~$1.03B seed** (2026) for world models — largest-ever EU seed.
- **World Labs** (Fei-Fei Li): ~$230M (2024) then **~$1B at unicorn+** (Feb 2026); backers incl. NVIDIA, AMD.
- **Runway**: **$315M**, valuation to **$5.3B**, pivoting to world models (GWM-1).
- **Decart**: **$100M Series B** at **$3.1B** (Sequoia/Benchmark).
- **NVIDIA Cosmos 3** (June 2026): open world-foundation model that *explicitly* supports action-labeled training AND "inferring the actions behind observed demonstrations" — i.e., the incumbent attacks GI's exact "only-we-have-action-labels" moat. **Google DeepMind Genie 3** (research, not an open platform).
- Internal corpus comps (agentic/AI training-data fundings): [[Nof1 raises $15 million for financial market AI models]], [[Kite raises $18M for autonomous agent payments]], [[AgentSmyth raises $8.7M for autonomous AI trading agents]], [[Tether to lead NEURA Robotics Series C financing]], [[Kindred Ventures raises $355 million for AI and robotics bets]].

**Position:** GI's $2.3B is *mid-pack* among world-model labs (well below World Labs/Runway, above Decart's earlier rounds). Its **differentiation is data provenance** (Medal's action-labeled corpus), not model scale or compute. **Why the gap matters (analysis):** if Cosmos/Genie commoditize the "world model" layer, value accrues to whoever has *proprietary, non-replicable data* — which is exactly GI's thesis. But NVIDIA showing action-inference-from-video at scale would *shrink* GI's edge to "cleaner labels," a quality delta, not a categorical moat.

## [2] Company history / fit
de Witte (31, Dutch): teen RuneScape-server entrepreneur (~$1.5M), 3 yrs humanitarian (incl. MSF), founded **Medal** → spun out GI Oct 2025. Medal *turned down an acquisition offer from a major lab* before GI was created; multiple offers since, all declined. **Why GI exists as a separate company (analysis):** Medal is a consumer data app with limited multiple; the *action-data asset* is worth far more as an AI foundation-model play than embedded in a clip-sharing app — hence the spin-out to capture a frontier-lab multiple. Khosla explicitly frames a sale as "just a data acquisition... uninteresting," i.e., the value is the *flywheel* (Medal data → model → Nerve labeling/teleop marketplace → more data), not the static dataset.
- **Nerve** (jobs marketplace: Medal gamers do data labeling → robot teleoperation) is the flywheel's data-supply leg and a deliberate hedge on de Witte's "displaced-gamer" ethics narrative.
- Stated ethics constraints: **no lethal autonomy / no harming humans** (search-and-rescue OK); hired ex-Palantir staffer who quit over ICE. This is identity/recruiting positioning (European, anti-"SV war bullishness") — a differentiator in a market where defense money is flowing.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Genuinely novel:** using a *pre-existing, large, action-labeled* consumer gameplay corpus (Medal) as the substrate for an embodied/agentic foundation model. The action-label provenance is a real, hard-to-replicate input — competitors scraping Twitch/YouTube lack synced button data.
**What's NOT proven (the anti-PR core):**
- **Sim-to-real at scale is unsolved** — admitted by the lead investor. "8 minutes to fine-tune a quadruped" is a demo, not a deployed product; reality-gap literature (contact dynamics, actuator friction, sensor noise) is exactly where game-trained models historically break.
- **No revenue/adoption metrics.** API not yet broadly out. "Handful of customers" is the entire traction story.
- **Moat decay risk:** NVIDIA Cosmos 3 already markets action-conditioned training + action-inference; if "infer actions from video" gets good enough, GI's labels become a *quality* edge, not a *categorical* one.
**Where margin accrues (analysis):** GI wants to be the "Anthropic/OpenAI of agents" — an ecosystem model provider. But the compute layer (CoreWeave/NVIDIA) and the open world-model layer (Cosmos) are owned by others. GI's defensible slice is the **data flywheel** (Medal + Nerve). If that flywheel produces models that *measurably* transfer to real robots, GI captures a frontier multiple; if not, it's a richly-valued dataset with a $454M burn against a compute-heavy roadmap.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Near-term: pre-train next model on CoreWeave compute; open API by end of summer 2026; pick customers that *feed back* real-world embodiment data. Sentiment: world models are the consensus "next frontier" (LeCun, Fei-Fei Li, NVIDIA all in) — capital is abundant and momentum strong, which *supports* GI's ability to keep raising. **Counterintuitive second-order (analysis):** the same super-cycle that funds GI also (a) invites NVIDIA/Google to commoditize the layer and (b) raises the bar — at $2.3B with no revenue, GI must show *sim-to-real transfer in production* within ~12-18 months or face a down-round / acqui-hire fate (the very outcome Khosla dismisses). Capital concentration here is *fragility*, not safety: high valuation + unproven core technical claim + well-funded incumbents = a narrow window to convert demos into deployed, paying embodiment.

## Top challenge / red-team questions (second-order)

1. **Is the round actually new, or a pre-leaked close?** Pre-leaked. TechCrunch had reported "in talks for $300M at $2B"; the 2026-06-25 news is the *closing* at $320M/$2.3B "confirming previous reporting." Marginal news value beyond the final terms. **(answered)**

2. **Is "only we have action labels" a real moat, or already eroding?** Eroding. NVIDIA Cosmos 3 (June 2026) explicitly supports action-labeled training AND "inferring the actions behind observed demonstrations." If video-inferred actions get good enough, GI's edge collapses to *label quality*, not a categorical moat. **(answered — key risk)**

3. **Has sim-to-real transfer at scale ever been demonstrated by GI in production?** No. Lead investor Khosla calls it "an open question nobody has fully answered." Demos only (8-min quadruped fine-tune, 100h game agent). **(answered)**

4. **What is the actual revenue / paying adoption?** Unknown/none disclosed. "A handful of customers" in gaming/sim/robotics, unnamed, unquantified; API not broadly available until end of summer 2026. **(open — likely ~zero meaningful revenue)**

5. **At $2.3B with no revenue, what multiple is being paid — for what?** For a proprietary data flywheel + a technical bet, not cash flows. Comparable world-model labs (World Labs ~$1B+, Runway $5.3B, AMI ~$1B seed) show the *category* commands frontier multiples; GI is mid-pack. The multiple rests entirely on the data-moat-plus-transfer thesis. **(answered)**

6. **Who captures margin in the stack?** Compute = CoreWeave/NVIDIA; open world-model layer = NVIDIA Cosmos/Google Genie. GI's only defensible slice is Medal+Nerve proprietary data. If the model layer commoditizes, GI must own the *data + applied-transfer* layer or become a feature. **(answered)**

7. **Is the Medal data legally/contractually clean for AI training?** Open. Note doesn't address rights to user-uploaded gameplay clips + action labels for commercial model training; potential consent/IP exposure not disclosed. **(open)**

8. **Does the "action label" advantage survive the shift to teleoperation/real-robot data?** Partial. Real embodiment needs real-world action data (hence Nerve teleop). Game button-presses ≠ continuous robot control signals; the transfer from discrete game inputs to continuous actuator control is itself the unproven step. **(open — core technical gap)**

9. **Is Nerve a genuine flywheel or a PR/ethics fig-leaf?** Plausibly real but unproven. It is the logical data-supply leg (labeling → teleop) and an ethics narrative for "displaced gamers." No scale numbers. **(open)**

10. **What does the "no lethal autonomy" stance cost GI competitively?** It forgoes the fastest-growing buyer (defense), where rivals are leaning in. It's a recruiting/identity asset (European, ex-Palantir hire) but a revenue constraint. Net effect unclear. **(open/analysis)**

11. **Why decline acquisition by a "major lab"?** Founders + Khosla want a generational standalone; Khosla says a sale would be "just a data acquisition, uninteresting." Signals confidence but also that *the data alone* is the obvious near-term value — i.e., the product/transfer is the unrealized part. **(answered)**

12. **Is $320M enough for a frontier-compute roadmap?** Likely not for long. "Vast majority to compute" + CoreWeave deal + pre-training next model = high burn. $454M total against rivals raising $1B+ implies more raises needed; valuation must keep climbing on milestones. **(answered)**

13. **What is the single downside trigger?** A public, measurable failure (or non-result) of sim-to-real transfer on the upcoming model — or a NVIDIA/Google free/open model matching action-conditioned performance. Either flips the thesis from "generational" to "expensive dataset." **(answered)**

14. **Who needs whom more — GI or its investors?** Investors (Khosla doubling down across both rounds) are deeply committed and narratively bought-in; GI has leverage now amid a hot category. That can reverse instantly on a missed transfer milestone. **(analysis)**

15. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the world-model super-cycle that funds GI simultaneously (a) commoditizes its layer via NVIDIA/Google and (b) raises the proof bar. High valuation + unproven core claim = fragility, not safety; ~12-18 month window to convert demos into deployed paying embodiment. **(analysis)**

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** GI sits in **world-models / embodied-AI foundation models** — the layer that learns physics, spatial-temporal dynamics and "self vs environment" causality to power agents and robots. Sizing varies wildly by definition (these are early-market estimates, treat as directional, not bankable): the broader *embodied AI* market is put at ~$3.8bn in 2026 → ~$23bn by 2030 at ~39% CAGR (per MarketsandMarkets, via marketsandmarkets.com); a narrower *embodied-intelligence large-model* segment at ~$2.52bn (2025) → ~$7.03bn (2032) at ~15.5% CAGR (per Research and Markets). For the funding flow specifically, ~$6bn went into world-model companies in roughly seven months / Q1'26 (per TechTimes, 2026-06-18) — i.e., capital, not revenue, is the live number. **Structure:** a forming oligopoly of well-capitalized labs (World Labs, AMI, Runway, GI, Decart) sitting *above* a commoditizing compute layer (CoreWeave/NVIDIA) and *below* end applications (robotics, gaming, sim). Entry barriers = capital + frontier compute + (GI's thesis) **proprietary action-labeled data**. **Why now (analysis):** post-LLM, "intuition / physical reasoning" is the consensus next frontier (LeCun, Fei-Fei Li, NVIDIA all in), and a hot capital cycle lets pre-revenue labs raise nine-figure rounds — which is itself the driver *and* the fragility (see risks).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Moat decay / disintermediation by NVIDIA Cosmos 3.** Cosmos (open, 2026-06-01) does action-conditioned training *and* action-inference-from-video across humanoid/arm embodiments — directly attacking GI's "only-we-have-action-labels" edge. *Why it matters:* if video-inferred actions get "good enough," GI's advantage collapses from categorical moat to label-quality delta, and an *open* model from the compute vendor pressures any premium GI could charge.

3. **Margin captured by adjacent stack layers + capital-intensity squeeze.** "Vast majority" of the round → compute (CoreWeave deal); the compute layer (CoreWeave/NVIDIA) and the open world-model layer (Cosmos/Genie) are owned by others. *Why:* $454M total vs rivals raising $1bn+ implies repeated raises on a high-burn pre-training roadmap; GI's only defensible slice is the Medal+Nerve data flywheel — if that doesn't measurably transfer, it's a richly-valued dataset funding someone else's compute.

**What this changes (idea-lens) (analysis).** This is a **new entry** that re-frames the world-model race around *data provenance* rather than model scale — a potentially differentiating thesis in a category NVIDIA is actively commoditizing. **Falsifiable thesis:** GI's action-label edge is real only if its next model shows *measurable, third-party-verifiable* sim-to-real transfer on real robots that pure video-trained models (incl. open Cosmos) cannot match. **What breaks it:** a Cosmos/Genie open model matching action-conditioned transfer performance, or GI's next model failing to demonstrate production transfer within ~12–18 months → thesis flips from "generational lab" to "expensive proprietary dataset." **Trigger to watch:** the end-of-summer-2026 API release + first *named, quantified* embodiment customers.
