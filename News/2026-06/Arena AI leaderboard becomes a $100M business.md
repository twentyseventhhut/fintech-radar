Arena, the AI leaderboard everyone uses, is now a $100M business
Just eight months after launching its commercial service, AI leaderboard provider Arena , which originated as a research project at UC Berkeley in 2023, has reached $100 million in annualized run-rate revenue.
Arena is best known for its popular crowdsourced AI model performance leaderboard, generated from over 10 million user evaluations. Its consumer website lets a user type a prompt it sends to two models; afterward, the user chooses which model did a better job.
While Arena’s popular AI model leaderboard is free for public use, the company began generating revenue from its platform in September when it introduced AI Evaluations , a service that provides model labs and enterprises with deep-dive performance analytics gathered from its community.
Arena’s rapid revenue growth shows that its commercial offerings are as popular with customers as they are with its community of evaluators, who are frequently drawn to the platform for early access to the latest, often unreleased, AI models.
“A lot of people don’t even understand that our business is making any money at all; people still see us as an open source project,” Anastasios Angelopoulos, Arena’s co-founder and CEO, told TechCrunch.
While Arena calls its revenue milestone ARR, a term that traditionally stood for annualized recurring revenue , Angelopoulos clarified that the company charges customers for “consumption,” which means that its revenue is not recurring.
While Arena doesn’t have direct competitors — Yupp, another crowdsourced AI model-picking startup, shut down in March— Angelopoulos said the company competes “for the same dollar” with human labeling startups like Mercor, Surge, and Scale AI, all of which assist model makers in refining their AI during post-training.
As AI providers strive to maximize model performance, their appetite for post-training optimization services continues to surge. When Arena announced in January that it raised a $150 million Series A at a post-money valuation of $1.7 billion, its annualized revenue was $30 million .
Elsewhere, Handshake’s gross annualized revenue from AI training has nearly doubled since January, climbing from $550 million to nearly $1 billion, The Information reported in April. Mercor’s annualized revenue also topped $1 billion earlier this year, up from $500 million last September, according to The Information.
Arena ranks models on a variety of tasks such as text, coding, vision, and image generation, as well as complex, long-running workflows through its recently introduced Agent Mode.
Along with Angelopoulos (pictured left), Arena was co-founded by fellow UC Berkeley postdoctoral student Wei-Lin Chiang (pictured center), who serves as the startup’s CTO. The startup was also co-founded by Ion Stoica (pictured right), the renowned UC Berkeley professor and Databricks co-founder who advised the project before it incorporated as a company in April 2025.
Arena has raised a total of $250 million from investors, including Felicis, Andreessen Horowitz, The House Fund, LDVP, Kleiner Perkins, Lightspeed Venture Partners, Laude Ventures, and UC Investments.

# Context-enrichment: Arena AI leaderboard becomes a $100M business
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Arena (formerly LMArena / Chatbot Arena — the crowdsourced LLM-vs-LLM voting leaderboard spun out of UC Berkeley's Sky Computing Lab in 2023, incorporated April 2025) says it has hit **$100M in "annualized run-rate revenue"**, ~8-9 months after launching its paid product "AI Evaluations" in **September 2025**. The paid product sells labs/enterprises deep-dive performance analytics distilled from the community's 10M+ pairwise votes; the public leaderboard stays free.

**The load-bearing caveat, buried in the PR:** CEO Anastasios Angelopoulos explicitly told TechCrunch the "ARR" is **not recurring** — customers are billed for **consumption**, so the "R" is fiction. This is a run-rate snapshot (last-period × annualizer), not a contracted book of business. It can fall as fast as it rose if labs pause spend after a model release cycle. The trajectory quoted: **$30M ARR in Jan 2026 → $100M by Jun 2026** (~3.3x in ~5 months). Impressive, but consumption run-rates are the most inflatable revenue metric in AI, and a leaderboard vendor's demand is inherently lumpy around lab release calendars.
- **Why framed this way:** anchoring "$100M ARR" to a free open-source halo ("people still see us as an open source project") maximizes the surprise/legitimacy narrative and papers over the recurring-vs-consumption distinction that a sophisticated buyer of the equity would discount heavily.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Arena claims **no direct competitor** — Yupp, the one crowdsourced model-picking rival, **shut down in March 2026**. But it concedes it fights "for the same dollar" as **human-labeling / post-training data shops**: **Mercor** (ARR topped **$1B**, up from ~$500M in Sept 2025 per The Information), **Surge**, and **Scale AI**. Also adjacent: Handshake's AI-training arm (~$550M→~$1B ARR since Jan per The Information). 
- **Second-order read (analysis):** on absolute scale Arena is an order of magnitude smaller than Mercor/Scale. Its moat is *distribution and neutrality* (the leaderboard everyone already checks), not labor supply. That's a different, thinner business: it sells signal/analytics, not a scaled human workforce. The risk is that labs treat the paid evals as a nice-to-have layered on top of their real post-training spend at Mercor/Surge — i.e. Arena captures the *garnish*, not the *meal*, of the post-training budget.

## [2] Company history / fit
Founders: **Anastasios Angelopoulos** (CEO), **Wei-Lin Chiang** (CTO) — both Berkeley postdocs — plus **Ion Stoica** (Berkeley prof, Databricks/Anyscale co-founder) who advised pre-incorporation. Funding ladder: **$100M seed** (led by **a16z** + UC Investments, ~$600M val, mid-2025) → **$150M Series A** at **$1.7B post-money** (Jan 6, 2026), with Felicis, UC Investments, a16z, The House Fund, LDVP, Kleiner Perkins, Lightspeed, Laude. **~$250M raised total.** Rebranded LMArena → Arena around Jan 2026; added Agent Mode for long-running workflows.
- **Why this path:** a free research leaderboard with massive mindshare but zero revenue is a classic "great distribution, no business model" trap. Monetizing labs directly (who most want the eval data) is the obvious conversion — but see [3] on the conflict this creates.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Genuinely new: turning a *neutral public benchmark* into a *paid B2B analytics product* for the same labs it ranks. Traction is real dollars, not just announcements ($30M→$100M consumption). **But the central question isn't "is there revenue" — it's "can a benchmark sell to the entities it judges and stay trusted?"**
- **The margin/trust conflict (the real story):** Arena's asset is *perceived neutrality*. Its customers are the labs on the leaderboard. This is the exact fault line the **"Leaderboard Illusion"** paper (Cohere Labs + AI2/Princeton/Stanford/Waterloo/UW, Apr 2025) exposed: big labs (Meta tested 27 Llama-4 variants Jan–Mar 2025; also OpenAI/Google/Amazon) could privately test many variants and publish only the best — a structural bias toward well-resourced incumbents. Stoica called the paper "full of inaccuracies." Whether or not the paper is fully right, **selling paid evals to those same labs monetizes exactly the relationship critics say is already too cozy** (Goodhart's law — when the metric becomes the target, it stops being a good metric). The unit economics look great precisely *because* the buyer and the judged are the same party — which is also what could break the franchise if trust erodes.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Sentiment is buoyant (a16z-backed, $1.7B val, fast run-rate). Structural drivers: labs' insatiable appetite for post-training/eval signal as pre-training gains plateau, and Arena's unique real-user preference data (hard to replicate). 
- **Counterintuitive second-order effect (analysis):** the more Arena monetizes lab relationships, the more its *free leaderboard's* credibility — the very thing that drives the community that generates the data that makes the paid product valuable — is at risk. Its flywheel and its business model pull against each other. Regulatory/reputational risk is reputational, not legal: a second credible "gaming" exposé could hit the brand harder than any competitor. Watch whether consumption revenue holds through a slow model-release quarter (the true test of durability vs. a release-cycle sugar high).

## Челлендж / ред-тим

**Importance: 3/5.** Real money and a marquee AI-infra story, but consumption-based "ARR", small absolute scale vs Mercor/Scale, a structural neutrality conflict, and only tangential fintech relevance cap it below flagship.

## Top challenge / red-team questions (second-order)

1. **"ARR" that isn't recurring — what's the durable share?** CEO admits billing is consumption-based. What fraction of the $100M is committed/contracted vs. one-off eval bursts around model launches? Without this, the multiple on $1.7B is unknowable. *(open — company disclosed only the headline)*

2. **Can a benchmark sell to the labs it ranks and stay trusted?** This is the whole ballgame. The paying customers are the ranked entities. Is there a Chinese wall between AI Evaluations (paid) and the public leaderboard? *(open — not disclosed)*

3. **Does the "Leaderboard Illusion" critique invalidate the asset?** If big labs already get private-variant advantages (Meta's 27 Llama-4 variants), and now also pay Arena, is the leaderboard's neutrality — its only moat — already compromised? *(partially answered: Arena disputes the paper; conflict is structural regardless)*

4. **$30M→$100M in ~5 months — real or annualization artifact?** A consumption run-rate can 3x if one quarter had heavy lab spend (e.g., a wave of frontier releases). Is this a durable curve or a release-cycle spike annualized? *(open)*

5. **Who captures the post-training margin — Arena or Mercor/Surge/Scale?** Mercor is at ~$1B ARR; Arena at $100M. Is Arena a thin analytics garnish on top of budgets that mostly flow to labor-supply shops? *(analysis: likely garnish, not meal)*

6. **What happens to consumption in a slow-release quarter?** The true durability test. If frontier releases slow, does revenue crater? *(open)*

7. **Is the free-leaderboard flywheel at war with the paid model?** Community trust generates the data that makes evals valuable; monetizing labs risks that trust. Does the business model cannibalize its own input? *(analysis: yes, structural tension)*

8. **Yupp shut down — is "no direct competitor" a moat or a warning?** Did Yupp fail on demand or on the same trust/monetization problem Arena now faces? *(open — Yupp shut Mar 2026, reason not detailed)*

9. **How defensible is the data moat?** 10M+ pairwise votes is large, but could a well-funded incumbent (Scale, an lab consortium, HuggingFace) stand up a rival crowdsourced eval? What's the switching cost? *(open)*

10. **Why does a16z back both labs and their judge?** a16z portfolio includes model labs *and* Arena. Does that create pressure on the leaderboard's independence? *(open — worth flagging)*

11. **Agent Mode traction — real or roadmap?** Long-running workflow evals are pitched as the next frontier. Any paying customers, or announcement-only? *(open)*

12. **Is $1.7B / ~$100M run-rate (~17x) defensible if revenue is non-recurring?** SaaS multiples assume recurring; consumption should trade lower. Is the valuation pricing durability it hasn't proven? *(analysis: aggressive)*

13. **Fintech relevance?** This is AI-infra, not fintech. Does it belong in the radar beyond as a comp for AI-eval/data-labeling economics? *(analysis: tangential — inclusion is thematic)*

14. **What do model labs actually buy — signal or PR?** Are labs paying for genuine diagnostic value, or for privileged access/relationship with the benchmark that ranks them? The distinction determines whether revenue is a product or a toll. *(open)*

15. **Second exposé risk:** if a follow-up paper (cf. arXiv 2605.15761 perturbation-framework work) shows post-monetization ranking bias, how fast does the brand — and thus the data flywheel — unwind? *(open — key downside trigger)*

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Arena sits at the intersection of AI model evaluation/benchmarking and the broader post-training "human data" complex (RLHF, expert labeling, preference data). Adjacent market sizing: AI data-labeling market ~$2.32bn in 2026, projected $6.53bn by 2031 at ~22.95% CAGR (per Mordor Intelligence, as of 2026); RLHF-platform market forecast $2.8bn (2025) → $18.6bn (2034) (per Lemon.io citing forecast, unverified methodology — treat as directional, `[UNSOURCED]` on rigor). Pure "model evaluation-as-a-business" has no clean TAM figure — no data; it is an emerging carve-out from labeling. Structure: fragmented and pre-consolidation — a handful of scaled human-data players (Scale AI, Surge, Mercor, Handshake) plus a thin evaluation-native layer (Arena, and Yupp, which shut down March 2026). Value in this chain is created at the labor/expert-sourcing and methodology layers; barriers are network effects (evaluator community + prompt volume) and trust/neutrality of the benchmark, not capital. Why now: as frontier labs saturate public benchmarks, appetite for post-training optimization and independent evaluation is surging (per TechCrunch, 2026-06-29) — the driver is model commoditization pushing labs to pay for differentiated eval signal.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: annualized run-rate revenue (note: gross vs net matters — see comps), revenue growth rate, and for Arena specifically evaluation volume (>10M user evaluations) + evaluator community depth. Basis of competition = distribution/network (who owns the evaluator pool and prompt flow) and neutrality, not price. Key players & recent moves: Mercor raised a $350M Series C at a $10bn valuation, ~$1.5bn annualized (gross) revenue by May 2026, up from ~$760M end-2025 (per Sacra, 2026); Scale AI took a $14.3bn Meta investment for ~49% at >$29bn valuation in June 2025, ~$2bn 2025 revenue (per Forbes/TechCrunch, 2025-06); Surge AI bootstrapped, profitable, >$1bn annual revenue (per Lemon.io, 2026); Handshake AI-training revenue ~$550M→~$1bn since Jan 2026 (per The Information via TechCrunch, 2026-04). Arena's position: niche but structurally advantaged — it is the evaluation-native entrant with the widely-used free leaderboard as a top-of-funnel moat (community + brand neutrality), converted into paid AI Evaluations (launched Sep 2025). Moat = intangibles/network effects (evaluator community, brand as "the leaderboard everyone uses") `(analysis)`. But it competes "for the same dollar" as Mercor/Surge/Scale (per CEO, TechCrunch 2026-06) — i.e. it is a small share-of-wallet challenger against $1bn+ incumbents, not a category owner.

**Comps & multiples.** All figures blend private round valuations (not market caps) and often GROSS revenue (contractors take 60–70% at Mercor) — apples-to-apples is weak, so this is a qualitative comparison, distribution not computed.
- Arena: $1.7bn post-money (Jan 2026 Series A) / $30M ARR then = 56.7x; on current $100M ARR = 17.0x. Note ARR here is consumption, NOT recurring (per CEO) — the "R" is misleading.
- Mercor: $10bn / ~$1.5bn gross ARR = ~6.7x (gross; net multiple materially higher). (per Sacra)
- Scale AI: >$29bn / ~$2bn revenue = ~14.5x. (per Forbes)
- Internal comps: [[Nof1 raises $15 million for financial market AI models]] (Alpha Arena live benchmark, 2026-05); [[Evident AI Index benchmarks payments networks' AI maturity]] (benchmarking-as-product, 2026-02); [[Baseten reportedly raising at $400 million valuation for AI model infrastructure]] (adjacent AI-infra round, 2026-06).
- Flag: Arena's Jan valuation multiple (~57x on trailing ARR) is rich vs labeling peers (7–15x), but was struck at a much smaller revenue base pre-3.3x growth; on today's $100M run-rate the 17x is roughly in-line-to-slightly-rich vs Scale, and the growth (3.3x in ~5 months) partially justifies it (growth-multiple link). Not an automatic over-valuation flag, but consumption-not-recurring revenue makes the multiple less durable than a subscription comp at the same headline number.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Revenue quality / durability** — "ARR" is consumption-based and explicitly non-recurring (per CEO). Consumption from a small number of frontier labs can drop sharply if a lab cuts post-training spend or in-sources eval; the $100M run-rate is more volatile than the label implies. Second-order: valuation struck on a run-rate that may not hold.
2. **Customer concentration / "same dollar" competition** — buyers are a handful of AI labs, and Arena competes directly with far larger, better-capitalized incumbents (Scale $29bn, Mercor $10bn, Surge >$1bn rev) for the same post-training budget. Second-order: pricing power and share-of-wallet capped; a single lab churning is material.
3. **Neutrality/conflict of interest** — monetizing evaluation of the same models it ranks on a "neutral" public leaderboard is a structural conflict (labs pay to be evaluated). If perceived as pay-to-rank, the free-leaderboard moat (trust) erodes — and the moat IS the business. Second-order: reputational risk directly undermines the top-of-funnel asset.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Arena is the first evidence that neutral evaluation, not just labeling, is a standalone monetizable layer of the post-training stack — a potential new entry point rather than a re-rating of existing labelers. Falsifiable thesis: if evaluation is a durable category, expect Arena to sign multi-year committed contracts (converting consumption → true recurring) and incumbents (Scale/Mercor) to bolt on eval products within ~12 months. What breaks the thesis: if labs in-source evaluation or Arena's community/neutrality is compromised (pay-to-rank perception), the run-rate compresses and eval collapses back into a labeling feature. Watch: contract structure disclosure and any incumbent eval launch as the trigger.
