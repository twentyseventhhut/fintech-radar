## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- On **2026-06-25** Wise Group plc (Nasdaq:WSE, LSE:WISE) reported **audited** FY2026 (year ended 31 Mar 2026) results, its first under **USD / US GAAP** (vs the historical GBP / IFRS), following its 11 May 2026 Nasdaq primary listing.
- Headlines: net revenue **$2,502.8m (+19%)**; income before tax (PBT) **~$660m, ~26% margin**; cross-border volume **$243.5bn (+31%)**; active customers **~19m (+21%)**; customer holdings **$39bn (+40%)**; card spend **$43.6bn (+37%)**; cross-border take rate **~0.52% (52bps)**, down from 58bps. (Sources: GlobeNewswire FY26 release; SEC Form 6-K.)
- New: a share-purchase program **>$500m** (~40% / ~$200m earmarked for the Employee Share Trust to offset SBC dilution; the remainder a genuine buyback) and **FY2027 guidance**.
- **Why this framing.** The digest leads with "$2.5bn, +19%" — but that revenue/volume/customer set is NOT new information: it was disclosed as preliminary USD estimates on **11 May** and in GBP on **13 April**. The audited release's actual news is the **margin beat** (PBT ~26% vs the 20-25% guided band) and the **buyback**. The note's title anchors to the already-known top line, which is exactly the "re-report of an already-published period" pattern the anti-PR gate flags.

- **Western Union** (NYSE:WU), FY2025: structural decline — Consumer Money Transfer adj. revenue (ex-Iraq) **−6%**; the incumbent Wise is disrupting.
- **PayPal/Xoom**: Xoom not broken out separately (open).

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Cross-border payments is a large, structurally growing pool: per Mordor Intelligence (via web, as of 2026) the market runs ~$237bn in 2026 revenue at ~7% CAGR; consumer remittance flows reached ~$905bn in 2024 (per World Bank, via Visa Direct commentary, 2025-09). The chain has two distinct economics: legacy retail/agent rails (Western Union, MoneyGram, Ria) priced at 2-4% markups, versus digital-native rails (Wise, Remitly) compressing toward sub-100bps. Wise's reported FY2026 (year-end 31 Mar 2026) average cross-border take rate of just ~52bps (per Wise FY26 release, via StockTitan/Investegate, 2026-06-25; corroborated by [[Wise reports 24% income jump and 27% cross-border volume growth]]) sits an order of magnitude below incumbent retail pricing — that price gap, not marketing, is the secular driver. **Why now:** the take rate is being *deliberately* cut (H1 FY26 cross-border take rate fell 10bps YoY to 52bps, "a strategic investment into reduced prices" — Wise H1 FY26 RNS, 2025-11-06), trading near-term cross-border revenue (H1 FY26 cross-border revenue +5% vs volume +24%) for volume and customer-holding growth, then monetizing the balance via interest income and the Wise Account (card/other revenue +29% YoY in Q4 FY26).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: cross-border volume, take rate (bps), active customers, and increasingly customer holdings / float (interest income). Wise FY2026 reported: net revenue $2.5bn (+19% YoY, top of its 15-20% medium-term band), cross-border volume +31% to $243bn, active customers +21% to 19m, income before tax $660.4m at a 26% margin (Wise FY26 release, 2026-06-25). Players split: incumbents Western Union (FY2025 adjusted revenue -1% to -4%, a business "in transition" — WU Q2/Q3 2025 8-Ks, via web) and MoneyGram; digital peers Remitly (FY2025 revenue $1.62bn, +28% YoY; ~15% adj-EBITDA margin — Remitly 8-Ks, via web) and PayPal/Xoom; plus stablecoin entrants (MoneyGram+Crossmint, Thunes). Basis of competition is price + speed + distribution/network. **Position:** Wise leads on unit economics (take rate ~52bps vs Remitly's 1-3.7% / WU's 2-4% markup) and is roughly 1.5x Remitly's revenue while growing slower (+19% vs +28%) — the trade-off of a low-take-rate, scale model. Moat = network/infrastructure (own licensing + local clearing across corridors) and switching costs of the Wise Account as a multi-currency balance, not just a transfer rail `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** Wise: market cap ~$11.9bn (companiesmarketcap, as of ~Jun 2026); EV/Revenue ~3.4x on FY2026 net revenue ($11.9bn-ish / $2.5bn ≈ 3.4-4.0x; note large net-cash position depresses EV — some sources show negative EV from customer-holding cash, so the "market-cap/revenue" P/S ~4.7x is the cleaner read). Internal/sector comps from base: [[Wise reports 24% income jump and 27% cross-border volume growth]] (Q4 FY26 trading update, same protagonist), [[Remitly launches Remitly One membership tier]], [[Ria and Xe partner with Google on cross-border money transfers]], [[Thunes partners with WeChat Pay Hong Kong]]. External peer Remitly trades far smaller; Western Union trades at a low single-digit P/E on declining revenue. **Flag: in-line-to-rich vs growth.** At ~3.4-4.7x revenue on +19% growth and a 26% PBT margin, Wise is not cheap but the multiple is broadly justified by the growth+margin combination (not a stretched hyper-growth multiple). Clean EV/EBITDA not computed — net-cash distortion and no free, reconciled EBITDA figure → `[UNSOURCED]`.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Self-inflicted take-rate compression** — cross-border take rate at ~52bps and falling by design; if volume growth slows before interest/Account monetization scales, net revenue growth decelerates faster than headline volumes suggest (H1 FY26 already showed cross-border revenue +5% vs volume +24%).
2. **Interest-income dependence** — a rising share of income before tax leans on yield earned on the £29bn+ customer holdings; a rate-cut cycle compresses this float income directly, and Wise's own guidance carves out interest above the first 1% yield — a margin lever outside its control.
3. **Disintermediation / margin migration** — stablecoin rails (MoneyGram, Thunes, WeChat Pay corridors) target the same sub-1% corridors; if settlement value migrates to stablecoin networks, the low-take-rate model that is Wise's moat is also its exposure.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is a scale-and-monetize-the-balance story, not a re-rating event: Wise is converting a transfers business into a multi-currency account/float business, with the $500m+ buyback signaling capital-return discipline over reinvestment hunger `(analysis)`. Falsifiable thesis: if FY2027 net revenue lands at the *middle* of the 15-20% band (guidance) while Account/card and interest income keep diversifying revenue mix, the low-take-rate model is validated; **trigger to watch** — Q1/Q2 FY27 take rate and the cross-border-revenue-vs-volume gap. Thesis breaks if volume growth dips below ~15% while take rate keeps falling — that would mean price cuts stopped buying growth.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
**Verdict (headline read).** IN-LINE→modest BEAT on the print, but the market read it cautiously. FY2026 (year ended 31 March 2026) net revenue $2,502.8M (+19% YoY), at the top of the 15–20% medium-term range; income before tax $660.4M at a 26% margin, above the company's own 20–25% guided range. Driver = customer growth (active customers +21% to 19.0M) and volume (+31%), partly offset by a deliberate take-rate cut to 0.52% (-6bps). New share buyback "exceeding $500M" introduced. Despite the beat, the stock dipped ~1% on the day on take-rate erosion and opex (+39%) outgrowing revenue (+19%).

**Key figures (with growth, FY2026 vs FY2025).**
- Net revenue: $2,502.8M vs $2,098.9M, +19% YoY.
- Income before tax (IBT): $660.4M, margin 26% — above the firm's guided 20–25% margin band (FY2025 IBT not restated in USD in this release; no directly comparable prior-year IBT in the press tables, so PBT YoY is qualitative).
- Active customers: 19.0M vs 15.6M, +21% YoY.
- Cross-border volume: $243.5B vs $185.2B, +31% YoY.
- Card spend: $43.6B vs $31.9B, +37% YoY.
- Customer holdings: $39.0B vs $27.8B, +40% YoY.
- Cross-border take rate: 0.52% vs 0.58%, -6bps (Personal 0.56% vs 0.58%; Business 0.39% vs 0.40%).
- Interest income on customer holdings: £806M (+6%), of which only ~£197M distributed to customers (regulatory restrictions, UK >50% of unpaid interest) — this retained interest is what lifts IBT margin above the underlying 15–20% target.

**By segment / driver.** Growth is volume-and-customer-led, not price-led: take rate fell 6bps as management cut prices on purpose ("price is the number one argument for customers"). Card and account adoption is the diversification engine — customer holdings +40% and card spend +37% both outran net revenue (+19%). Settlement infrastructure (75% of transfers <20s) and new direct connections (Japan, Brazil) plus Wise Platform are the forward levers. Opex grew ~+39% YoY, roughly double revenue growth — the deliberate reinvestment year.

**vs expectations / prior period.** No formal public Street consensus was captured in the pipeline (no paid feed); press framed the print as a beat — net revenue +19% landed at the top of the 15–20% range and the 26% IBT margin came in above the company's own 20–25% guidance. vs prior quarters the trajectory is accelerating/steady: Q3 FY26 cross-border volume +25–26% YoY, Q4 FY26 +26–27% YoY (£49.4bn, 11.3M active in Q4) — see [[Wise reports 24% income jump and 27% cross-border volume growth]]. Net revenue +19% in FY2026 vs underlying income growth of 16% in FY2025 implies acceleration. [UNSOURCED] for an exact Street net-revenue/IBT consensus number.

**Guidance / forward (FY2027, introduced).**
- Net revenue growth: "around the middle" of the 15–20% medium-term range (i.e. ~17–18% implied; analysis).
- IBT margin: "around the top" of the 20–25% range.
- Profitability front-half weighted due to pricing phasing; opex to grow at a "slightly lower pace" than FY2026's +39% but continued marketing/tech spend.
- Medium-term targets reaffirmed: 15–20% net revenue growth, 15–20% IBT margin (above-target 20–25% expected until interest can be passed back to customers).
- Capital return: new buyback/EST program "exceeding $500M" (~40% recurring EST purchases); FY2026 allocated $470M to the Employee Share Trust (35.9M shares).
- Management tone: confident on scale, framing price cuts as reinvestment not competitive pressure. Dodged/deflected on: a hard floor for take-rate cuts ("a long-term game," no floor stated) and on how fast opex growth would moderate.

**Thesis-flags.**
1. Take-rate erosion is structural, not cyclical (0.58%→0.52%, guided -1–2bps/quarter into FY27). Why it matters: revenue per unit of volume keeps falling, so the bull case depends entirely on volume/customer compounding outrunning price — a thesis on elasticity, not pricing power. Second-order: if volume growth ever decelerates toward the take-rate decline, net revenue growth compresses fast.
2. Opex +39% vs revenue +19% — reinvestment year. Why it matters: the 26% IBT margin is flattered by retained interest income (UK regulatory float), not by operating leverage; strip rate-driven interest and the underlying margin sits in the 15–20% band. Second-order: IBT margin is rate-sensitive and will compress if/when interest is passed to customers or rates fall.
3. Interest-income dependency: ~£806M generated, only ~£197M paid out. Why it matters: a large slice of profitability is a regulatory-timing artifact; the "above-target" 20–25% margin is explicitly framed as temporary. Second-order: re-rating risk if regulators force pass-through.
4. Stock dipped ~1% on a beat — valuation sensitivity. Why it matters: with elevated multiples, the market is pricing perfection; take-rate + opex headwinds dented sentiment despite top-of-range growth and a $500M+ buyback. Mixed reaction = signal that the print, while strong, didn't clear the high bar.
