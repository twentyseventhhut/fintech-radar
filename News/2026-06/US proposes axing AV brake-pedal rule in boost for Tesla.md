Trump administration proposes axing brake-pedal requirement for AVs in a boost for Tesla
The Trump administration’s Department of Transportation (DOT) has proposed new changes to federal vehicle regulations that would allow companies to skip including brake pedals in “vehicles designed to be driven exclusively   by automated driving systems.” 
The proposal, if adopted, would remove a major regulatory barrier for companies like Tesla and Zoox, which are developing vehicles intended to be fully autonomous, without a steering wheel or pedals. The public will now have 30 days to comment on the proposal before the DOT decides whether to approve the changes.
This is the latest of a series of proposed changes to vehicle laws from the Trump DOT. Late last year, the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) proposed removing a number of Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards (FMVSS) requirements around windshield wiping and defogging systems, and tire placards.
President Biden was also working in this direction while in office. During his administration, the NHTSA proposed and ultimately finalized a rule that allowed autonomous vehicles to operate without steering wheels .
Currently, any company developing an autonomous vehicle that is missing parts required by the FMVSS has to request an exemption from the federal government. Even if the exemption is granted, regulations restrict how many such exempted vehicles can be on the road.
Removing requirements for parts like brake pedals will theoretically allow companies to get autonomous vehicles on the road quicker, according to the NHTSA.
“We are at the cusp of the greatest technological revolution in vehicle technology since the innovation of the Model T,” NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison said in a statement. “If we want America to lead the way, we have to reimagine our regulatory framework. That’s why under Secretary Sean Duffy’s AV Framework, NHTSA is tearing down pointless barriers to innovative designs while strengthening the fundamental safety requirements that matter and holding AV developers accountable for safe performance.” 
Tesla has spent the last few years developing a two-seater car it calls the Cybercab that is intended to operate without a steering wheel or pedals. The company has never applied for an exemption to the FMVSS standards requiring those controls. Instead, CEO Elon Musk has repeatedly said that his company would deploy the vehicles nationwide once regulatory approval was granted.
In the meantime, Tesla has spent the last year operating a small robotaxi service in Austin, Texas. The company began the service with safety drivers in the front seats, but has steadily removed those drivers, leaving the cars to operate “unsupervised.” The company has admitted to the NHTSA that it is using teleoperators to monitor and, in some rare cases, move the vehicles remotely at low speeds after crashes or to avoid obstacles.
Zoox, which is owned by Amazon, applied for and was granted an exemption from FMVSS standards last year so it could demonstrate its purpose-built robotaxi. The company has since applied for, and is waiting on, another exemption to operate that robotaxi commercially.
Companies like Waymo, which use retrofitted or modified versions of regular vehicles (such as the Jaguar I-Pace), have been able to deploy as many robotaxis as they want since they already have manual controls.

# Context-enrichment: US proposes axing AV brake-pedal rule in boost for Tesla
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- NHTSA published a **Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM)** — _"Modernization of FMVSS No. 135 to Accommodate ADS-Equipped Vehicles"_ (Docket NHTSA-2026-0728, RIN 2127-AN00, FR doc 2026-12981, published 2026-06-26). **30-day comment window; comments due ~2026-07-27.** Status = **proposed**, not finalized.
- **Two de-PR corrections to the TechCrunch/general framing:**
  1. The NPRM amends **only FMVSS No. 135 (light-vehicle brake systems)** — it removes the requirement for a hand-/foot-operated **brake control** for vehicles "designed never to be operated by a human driver." It does **not** strip mirrors, steering controls, or other manual-control FMVSS. Coverage that lumps in "mirrors/manual controls" conflates this docket with the broader AV Framework's separate, earlier rulemakings.
  2. Braking **performance / stopping-distance** standards are **retained** (via alternative test procedures). Only the physical brake-pedal mandate goes, and only for ADS-only vehicles.
- **Why structured this way:** the Trump DOT is attacking the **mandate itself** for driverless-only vehicles rather than granting per-company exemptions. This matters because the exemption regime carries a **statutory 2,500-vehicle/manufacturer/year cap** (Part 555). Removing the underlying FMVSS requirement lets a compliant ADS-only car **bypass the cap entirely** — that is the real mechanism, not the trivial bill-of-materials saving of a pedal.
- Part of Sec. Sean Duffy's **AV Framework** (announced 2025-04-24) under NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison (Senate-confirmed 2025-09-18). Prior framework FMVSS proposals: shift-sequence/defrost/wiper (FMVSS 102/103/104, published 2026-03-16) and tire placards (FMVSS 110, published 2026-04-01). This is a real sequenced deregulation campaign, not a one-off.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Tesla Cybercab (no wheel/pedals):** Prime beneficiary. Tesla **never filed a Part 555 exemption** — it intends to **self-certify**. Removing the FMVSS-135 mandate removes a live legal risk to that posture and to scaling past the 2,500 cap. First wheel-less unit reportedly off the line ~Feb 2026.
- **Zoox (Amazon):** The company that **did** take the exemption route — demonstration exemption granted ~Aug 2025; a separate **commercial** exemption (filed 2025-08-22) still pending mid-2026. The NPRM would eventually let it avoid the brake-control piece too.
- **Waymo:** **Unaffected.** Uses retrofitted Jaguar I-Pace etc. that **retain** wheel + pedals → already FMVSS-compliant, no exemption needed, deploys at scale today. The rule neither helps nor hurts its current fleet.
- **Cruise:** GM wound it down (2024) — effectively out of the robotaxi race.
- **Why the lay of the land is this way (analysis):** the rule advantages the **purpose-built, controls-free** architectures (Tesla, Zoox) over the **retrofit** architecture (Waymo). Waymo's lead is operational (live paid rides at scale); Tesla's bet is regulatory + manufacturing leverage. This NPRM narrows Tesla's regulatory disadvantage but does **nothing** for its operational gap (Waymo has the live miles).

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Biden-era precedent:** NHTSA's _"Occupant Protection for Vehicles With ADS"_ final rule (issued 2022-03-10, effective 2022-09-26) already allowed wheel-less ADS vehicles on the **crashworthiness** side. The 2026 NPRM addresses the remaining **crash-avoidance / controls** side (the brake pedal). So the wheel-less car was never wholly illegal — this closes one specific gap.
- Tesla's distinctive choice — **self-certify rather than seek an exemption** — is exactly why removing the mandate (vs a capped exemption) is uniquely valuable to its scale story. **Why (analysis):** Tesla's valuation requires _millions_ of robotaxis, not 2,500/yr; the exemption path is structurally incompatible with that, so deregulating the standard itself is the only path that fits Tesla's narrative.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Genuinely new** as a regulatory instrument: it removes a mandate for an entire vehicle class rather than granting case-by-case waivers — the first FMVSS-135 modernization of its kind. Confirmed real (Federal Register + NHTSA release + CNBC/Reuters/AutoNews), not vaporware.
- **Traction = zero deployment impact today.** It is a _proposal_ in a 30-day comment window; no vehicle gets deployed because of it yet. Tesla's Austin robotaxi (launched 2025-06-22) already runs with teleoperators and removed in-car monitors — that traction is independent of this rule.
- **Who captures the value (analysis):** the rule is permissive infrastructure for the autonomy narrative. The margin still depends on whether Tesla's FSD actually achieves unsupervised L4 safety at scale — which regulation cannot grant. Removing the pedal is necessary-not-sufficient.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Next:** comment period closes ~2026-07-27; safety groups (e.g. Advocates for Highway and Auto Safety) expected to oppose; final-rule timing open. Zoox's pending commercial exemption is a parallel catalyst.
- **Capital-markets / fintech angle (the reason this is in the digest):** Tesla (~$375/sh, ~$1.42T cap mid-2026) is heavily levered to the **robotaxi/Cybercab autonomy narrative**. ARK ascribes ~**90% of Tesla's 2029 enterprise value** to robotaxi ($2,600 base target); Wedbush/Ives calls autonomy "could be worth nearly $1T"; Morgan Stanley/Jonas ~$160/sh to Network Services. So a permissive AV rule is, in theory, bullish for the autonomy option value embedded in the stock.
- **But — the counterintuitive second-order point:** **TSLA did not visibly react** to the proposal (~flat, ~-0.1% on 2026-06-25). No source attributes any move to it. This is consistent with an **anticipated, incremental** regulatory step that was already priced in — i.e. the news confirms a known trajectory rather than delivering a surprise catalyst. The real valuation risk is not regulation (which is going Tesla's way) but **execution + safety-data** (NHTSA FSD probes EA26-002 ~3.2M vehicles, PE25-012 traffic-violations probe; 17 Austin robotaxi crashes Jul-2025–Mar-2026, low-speed, no fatalities). The central question shifts from "will regulators allow it" (increasingly yes) to "can the software earn the trust the multiple already assumes."

**Sector & drivers.** The robotaxi/AV-mobility market is small-but-hyper-growth: third-party trackers peg the 2026 market at only ~$1.3–5.8bn (estimates diverge wildly by firm) scaling to a 2033–2035 TAM in the ~$150–260bn range at 65–90% CAGR (per Coherent Market Insights / DataM Intelligence / SkyQuest, via web, as of 2026; treat as analyst-estimate, ranges too wide to be precise — "size = no hard number"). Structure is **consolidated at the frontier, fragmented at the edge**: a handful of well-capitalized operators (Waymo, Tesla, Pony.ai, WeRide, Zoox) hold the deployment lead, with capital intensity (Tesla guided 2026 capex up to ~$25bn vs ~$10bn prior, per web) and **regulation as the dominant entry barrier** — exactly the barrier this note is about. "Why now": the Trump DOT's sequenced FMVSS deregulation (brake-pedal NPRM, FMVSS-135, Docket NHTSA-2026-0728) is removing the *mandate itself* for ADS-only vehicles rather than granting capped per-company exemptions — the second-order effect is bypassing the **statutory 2,500-vehicle/manufacturer/year Part 555 cap**, which is the real mechanism for scaling a purpose-built fleet into the millions (the bill-of-materials saving of a pedal is trivial).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs the operators run on: **fleet size**, **paid rides/week**, **deadhead-mile share** (utilization → unit economics), and **revenue growth %**. Players & basis of competition:
- **Waymo (Alphabet)** — operational leader: ~3,000 vehicles, targeting ~1M rides/week in 2026, expanding to ~20 cities (per web). Competes on *live miles + safety record*. **Unaffected by the rule** (retrofitted Jaguar I-Pace retains wheel/pedals → already compliant).
- **Tesla (the protagonist)** — competes on *manufacturing scale + regulatory leverage*, not live miles: only ~44 robotaxis in Austin (per web), with teleoperators, vs Waymo's ~3,000. Position = **regulatory winner, operational laggard**. The NPRM narrows Tesla's regulatory disadvantage but does *nothing* for the operational gap. Moat (analysis): vertically-integrated manufacturing + FSD data flywheel + self-certification posture — intangibles/scale, not network effects today.
- **Pony.ai (PONY)** / **WeRide (WRD)** — Chinese listed pure-plays scaling fast (Pony Q1-2026 rev +145% YoY; WeRide +58% YoY, per web). Compete on China/overseas city coverage.
- **Zoox (Amazon)** — purpose-built, took the exemption route; secondary beneficiary of the rule.
- **Cruise (GM)** — wound down 2024, effectively out.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Multiple rests on an option, not cashflow.** Tesla's autonomy premium (~90% of ARK's 2029 EV) is priced ahead of any material robotaxi revenue (Musk guided "material in 2027," per web). A permissive rule de-risks the *legal* path but not the *software/safety* path — and that is the binding constraint now (NHTSA FSD probes EA26-002 ~3.2M vehicles, PE25-012; 17 Austin crashes). Second-order: if FSD can't earn unsupervised-L4 trust, no regulation rescues the multiple.
2. **"Priced in" / no-catalyst risk.** TSLA did **not** visibly move on the NPRM (~flat, -0.1% on 2026-06-25, per note). The market treated it as an anticipated incremental step — so traders extrapolating a re-rate from this headline are positioned for a catalyst that already passed.
3. **Reversal/litigation risk.** It is a *proposal* in a 30-day window (comments due ~2026-07-27), short for a safety FMVSS; safety advocates (Advocates for Highway and Auto Safety) historically oppose; final-rule timing and litigation are open. Second-order: a deregulatory rule pushed fast is more vulnerable to court challenge.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is **permissive infrastructure for the purpose-built, controls-free architecture** (Tesla Cybercab, Zoox) and a relative negative-of-omission for the retrofit architecture's regulatory edge (Waymo gains nothing, but its operational moat is untouched). It does **not** re-rate Tesla by itself — the falsifiable thesis is: *the binding constraint on Tesla robotaxi value has shifted from "will regulators allow it" (increasingly yes) to "can FSD earn the trust the multiple assumes."* Trigger/what to watch: the ~2026-07-27 comment close + final-rule signal; Zoox's pending commercial exemption; and — the real catalyst — NHTSA FSD-probe outcomes and Austin safety data. What breaks the thesis: a clean FSD-probe resolution + a step-change in unsupervised paid miles would convert the option into revenue and justify the premium.
