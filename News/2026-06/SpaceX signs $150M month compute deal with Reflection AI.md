SpaceX inks compute deal with Reflection AI, an open source AI lab
First came Anthropic , then Google . Now, open source AI startup Reflection is tapping SpaceX for its abundant source of AI chips.
Reflection AI will pay $150 million a month beginning July 1, 2026 through 2029 for immediate access to Nvidia’s latest GB300 AI chips and supporting hardware across SpaceX’s Colossus 2 data center near Memphis, Tennessee, the company told TechCrunch. The deal is worth up to $6.3 billion and either company has the option to end the contract with 90 days’ notice after the first three months.
The deal is smaller than SpaceX’s deals with Anthropic and Google, which cost the companies $1.25 billion per month and $920 million per month, respectively. Those contracts also run through July 2029, although Elon Musk has publicly downplayed the three-year term, emphasizing that the contracts can be canceled at any time.
Reflection used the compute deal — its first — to tout the value of its open-weight AI strategy , which it has pitched as an open source alternative to closed frontier labs like Anthropic and OpenAI. Open-weight AI models, which publicly release their trained parameters, have received more attention following the U.S. government’s ban of Anthropic’s closed models, Fable and Mythos.
The startup , which was founded in 2024 by two former Google DeepMind researchers, said the compute deal is one of the largest announced open AI infrastructure commitments to date.
“Recent events highlight how important open source is to the AI ecosystem, with more nations and enterprises recognizing the risks and costs associated with exclusively depending on closed models,” a spokesperson said in an emailed statement. “Our deal with SpaceXAI signals Reflection’s strategic importance within the frontier AI ecosystem, and more compute means more runway to build the world’s best open models at scale.”
The Colossus data center was originally built by xAI, a company founded by Elon Musk that is now part pf SpaceX, for its own AI efforts. As its internal pursuits have faltered, SpaceX leveraged its valuable AI chip holdings and began renting them out to some of the world’s top AI labs.

# Context-enrichment: SpaceX signs $150M/month compute deal with Reflection AI
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 2026-06-22, open-weight AI lab **Reflection AI** signed a compute-rental contract to pay **SpaceX $150M/month** starting **July 1, 2026 through 2029** for "immediate access" to **Nvidia GB300** chips + supporting hardware at SpaceX's **Colossus 2** data center near **Memphis, TN**. Headline total **up to $6.3B** — but that is the *if-it-runs-full-term* number. The real structure: **either party can cancel with 90 days' notice after the first three months**. So the firm commitment is ~3 months (~$450M); the rest is optional.
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The "$6.3B" is a marketing ceiling, not a commitment. The 90-day out converts a "multibillion-dollar deal" (Bloomberg's framing) into something closer to a rolling quarterly lease. This matches the template SpaceX used with Anthropic and Google — Musk himself has publicly **downplayed the 3-year terms**, stressing cancelability. The PR anchor ("one of the largest open AI infrastructure commitments") flatters Reflection; the cancel clause protects SpaceX's optionality on scarce GB300 supply.
- **Counterparty note:** "SpaceX" here = the merged **SpaceX-xAI** entity. Colossus was **built by xAI** for Grok; as xAI's own efforts "faltered," SpaceX pivoted to **renting out** its chip holdings. So this is a landlord monetizing stranded capacity, not a strategic AI-to-AI alliance.

## [1] Competitors / peers
**On the supply side (who rents Colossus):** Reflection is the **third** named tenant after **Anthropic ($1.25B/month, all of Colossus 1, >300MW, through 2029, signed late May 2026)** and **Google ($920M/month from Oct 2026, ~110,000 GPUs, through June 2029, signed June 5 2026)**. Reflection's $150M/month is **~8x smaller than Anthropic's and ~6x smaller than Google's** — it is a junior tenant. Notably **Nvidia is on both sides**: it supplies the GB300s and is a Reflection investor.
**On the demand side (who Reflection competes with):** open-weight/"sovereign" labs — **DeepSeek, Mistral, Meta (Llama)**; and as a customer-facing alternative, the closed frontier labs **OpenAI / Anthropic**.
- **Why the landscape looks this way / second-order:** SpaceX is becoming a **neutral compute landlord** renting to direct rivals (Anthropic, Google, Reflection simultaneously) — the chips, not the models, are the scarce asset. Reflection's small ticket signals it is **compute-constrained relative to its $25B valuation**, buying runway rather than dominance. The deal's true competitor isn't another lab — it's **Reflection's own unshipped product** (see [3]).

## [2] Company history / fit
**Reflection AI:** founded **March 2024** by ex-DeepMind researchers **Misha Laskin** (Gemini reward modeling) and **Ioannis Antonoglou** (AlphaGo co-creator). Funding trajectory: emerged from stealth ~$130M / $545M valuation → **$2B at $8B valuation (Oct 2025)** → raising toward **$20B–$25B valuation (March 2026)** — a ~15x re-rate in months. Originally built **autonomous coding agents** (agent "Asimov"); repositioned as the **"American open frontier lab" / Western answer to DeepSeek**.
- **Why it acts this way:** A $25B valuation on a lab with **no publicly released frontier model** (as of early March 2026 the flagship open-weight model was unreleased; Asimov on waitlist; no research papers) creates intense pressure to **show it can train at frontier scale**. Compute is the gating input. This deal buys the **GB300 capacity needed to attempt the 2026 model release** that underwrites the valuation. Logical fit: yes — but it is a **survival/runway buy, not an expansion from strength**.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**What's genuinely new:** very little technically — it's a GPU lease. The *novelty* is **strategic timing**: open-weight momentum surged after the **U.S. government banned/cut off Anthropic's closed models (Fable, Mythos)**, handing open-model vendors the argument that enterprises/governments shouldn't depend on closed providers that can be switched off. Reflection is monetizing that narrative.
**Traction — the honest read:** thin on product. The flagship model is **not yet shipped**; revenue is *projected* ("large enterprises building on our models" + "sovereign AI for governments," per Laskin), not booked. Real demand signals: a **DOE / Genesis Mission** partnership (AI provider for U.S. National Labs) and reported Pentagon interest — i.e. **government/sovereign demand is the actual traction**, not enterprise GMV.
- **Why value-add is fragile / who captures the margin:** In this stack the **margin sits with Nvidia (chips) and SpaceX (the landlord)** — both paid cash up front — while **Reflection takes 100% of the model-delivery risk**. Reflection must convert $150M/month of burn into a competitive open model *and* a paying customer base before the runway (or investor patience) ends. If the 2026 model underwhelms, the 90-day cancel clause lets it stop the bleeding — but also means the "$6.3B vote of confidence" can evaporate quietly. The durable question shifts from *"is there an open-source AI story?"* to *"can Reflection ship a frontier model good enough to justify $150M/month before its capital concentration becomes a fragility?"*

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Watch:** the **first open-weight frontier model release (targeted 2026)** — the single event that validates or breaks the $25B valuation; whether the SpaceX contract is **renewed past the 3-month firm window**; expansion of DOE/Pentagon/sovereign deals.
- **Sentiment:** mixed. Bulls frame it as the open-source ecosystem getting serious compute and a national-security tailwind. Skeptics (Turing Post, AInvest) flag **"$20–25B bet collides with unreleased product"** and **repricing risk**.
- **Why the market goes this way / counterintuitive second-order:** The U.S. ban on Anthropic's closed models is the structural driver lifting *all* open-weight labs — but it is **policy-contingent** (note corpus: Trump administration walked back calling Anthropic a national-security threat; U.S. later cleared Mythos to 100+ orgs). If the closed-model squeeze eases, the open-weight tailwind weakens. And the **capital/compute concentration cuts both ways**: $150M/month makes Reflection look frontier-grade, but it also makes it **fragile** — a single missed model release turns committed burn into a cancel-clause exit and a down-round. Concentration here is a risk amplifier, not a moat.

**Sector & drivers.** This sits in the **"neocloud" / GPU-capacity-leasing** layer — selling raw Nvidia compute by the megawatt/month rather than models. Sizing: neocloud revenue is projected ~**$20bn in 2026** rising toward ~**$180bn by 2030** (per Signisys/industry estimates, via web, as of 2026) — fast-growing but still small vs hyperscaler capex. Structure: **consolidating into a few scaled players** (CoreWeave, Nebius, Crusoe, plus stranded-capacity landlords like SpaceX-xAI) anchored by a handful of mega-contracts; the scarce input is **powered GB300/Blackwell capacity**, not the cloud software. Entry barrier = capital + power + chip allocation, not technology. Unit economics benchmark: CoreWeave reportedly earns ~**$10–12M annual revenue per energized MW** (via DCK/web). Drivers / "why now": (1) acute **GB300 supply scarcity** — Blackwell hourly rental prices reportedly *doubled* since Feb 2026 (Semafor, via [[This Week in Fintech Semafor on the rise of an AI futures market]]); (2) **stranded-capacity monetization** — xAI's Colossus 1 ran at ~11% utilization with a mixed H100/H200/GB200 fleet, so SpaceX pivoted from training Grok to renting the chips out (per Tom's Hardware/wccftech, via web). SpaceX's external committed compute revenue now reportedly exceeds **$80bn through 2029** (via web).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: **$/MW/month** and **revenue per MW**, **contract backlog**, **GPU count**, and **utilization**. On the **supply side**, SpaceX-xAI is now a **neutral compute landlord** renting to direct rivals at once — Anthropic (**$1.25bn/mo**, all of Colossus 1, ~220k GPUs / ~300MW, signed May 2026), Google (**$920M/mo** from Oct 2026, signed 5 Jun 2026), and Reflection (**$150M/mo** from Jul 2026). Competing supply: CoreWeave (~$12–13bn 2026 revenue guide, ~$99bn backlog Mar 2026), Nebius ($7–9bn ARR target end-2026), Crusoe, plus hyperscalers themselves ([[AWS in talks to sell Trainium AI chip racks to outside companies]]). On the **demand side** Reflection competes as an open-weight lab vs DeepSeek/Mistral/Meta and, commercially, vs closed labs OpenAI/Anthropic. Recent move: 22 Jun 2026 deal at **$150M/mo through 2029**, GB300s at Colossus 2 (Memphis), headline up to **$6.3bn** but with a **90-day cancel after 3 months** — firm floor ~$450M. Protagonist's position: **junior tenant** — Reflection's ticket is ~**8x smaller than Anthropic's** ($150M vs $1.25bn) and ~**6x smaller than Google's** ($150M vs $920M), signaling it is compute-constrained relative to its ~$25bn valuation. Moat: **none on the buyer side** — Reflection rents the same chips its rivals can, holds 100% model-execution risk; the durable moat sits with **Nvidia (chips, also a Reflection investor) and SpaceX (landlord)**, both paid cash up front `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** No EV/EBITDA or P/E computable for Reflection (private, pre-revenue, no disclosed financials) → **no data**. Reference comps for the neocloud layer (public, web-sourced, as of ~Apr–Jun 2026):
- **CoreWeave** — ~**7x P/S** on ~$12.5bn 2026 revenue guide (~112% YoY growth latest quarter).
- **Nebius** — ~**62x P/S** (figure unverified/possibly conflated with ARR-vs-revenue across sources — treat as `[UNSOURCED]` directionally "richer than CoreWeave"; growth ~600%+ YoY off a tiny base).
- Spread shows the **growth-multiple correlation**: faster grower trades richer; neither is an outlier given growth, so no over-valuation flag *for the comps themselves*.
Reflection's own valuation: ~**$25bn (Mar 2026 raise)** on **no shipped frontier model** — a revenue multiple cannot be computed (denominator ~0), so this is **not** a clean comp; it is a venture bet, flagged below. Internal comps: [[Rumble plans all-stock acquisition of Northern Data]] (cloud-compute consolidation), [[Seven European fintech deals raise EUR 95 million in a week]] (OpenAI–Broadcom $350–500bn chip order — vendor-financing scale), [[AWS in talks to sell Trainium AI chip racks to outside companies]] (custom-silicon supply alternative pressuring Nvidia/neocloud pricing).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Cancelability masquerading as commitment** — firm spend is ~$450M (3 months) vs the $6.3bn headline; the 90-day out means the "vote of confidence" can evaporate quietly. Second-order: any reported "multibillion-dollar" neocloud backlog (including SpaceX's $80bn) is **optionality, not contracted revenue** — backlog quality is overstated sector-wide.
2. **Counterparty/credit concentration** — a **pre-revenue** lab as SpaceX's #3 tenant paying $1.8bn/yr it has not demonstrably earned; the cancel clause is SpaceX pricing in tenant default risk. If Reflection's 2026 model underwhelms, a 90-day exit + unreleased product likely cascades into a **down-round from $25bn** (AInvest/Turing Post already flag repricing risk).

4. **Policy contingency** — the open-weight tailwind rests on the U.S. ban of Anthropic's closed models; the corpus shows that squeeze already easing ([[US clears Anthropic to release Mythos to 100+ companies and agencies]], [[Trump walks back calling Anthropic a national security threat]]). If it reverses, Reflection's core pitch weakens.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** SpaceX-xAI completing the Anthropic→Google→Reflection trifecta confirms the pivot from "build a frontier lab" to **"be the landlord"** — stranded GPU capacity is more bankable than an in-house model `(analysis)`. Falsifiable thesis: Reflection's lease is a **runway buy, not a strength signal**; trigger that confirms it = **non-renewal past the 3-month firm window** or a delayed/underwhelming 2026 open model; trigger that breaks it = a shipped frontier-grade open model + booked enterprise/sovereign (DOE/Pentagon) revenue before the burn outruns its raise.
