# Context-enrichment: Linas Newsletter — Why a $1.75 trillion SpaceX IPO may be uninvestable
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**Item type:** opinion/commentary (Linas's Newsletter, 2026-06-21). NOT a new event — it is a red-team write-up of the already-completed SpaceX IPO, anchored on the S-1 and the governance/valuation thesis. Source link in note is a Substack redirect; the underlying piece is "SpaceX IPO: Uninvestable at $1.75 Trillion?" (linas.substack.com).

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
SpaceX IPO'd on Nasdaq (ticker SPCX) in mid-June 2026 — priced at **$135/share, ~$1.75–1.77T market cap, ~$75bn raised** (largest IPO in history, ~3x Saudi Aramco's 2019 offering). It opened ~$150, hit ~$176.52 intraday, closed first day at **$161.11 (+19.3%)**, then jumped a further ~20% on the first full trading day before a late-June pullback (shares dipped below debut amid a ~$600bn tech sell-off). The Linas piece is the **bear/governance thesis**, not the event. Its de-PR'd core claims:
- **Valuation:** ~94x trailing revenue, ~266x EBITDA. Morningstar fair value **$780bn — ~48–57% below the IPO target** (analyst Nicolas Owens: ~$611bn EV for core launch+Starlink, +~$170bn probability-weighted for AI).
- **Financials muddied by xAI:** xAI (merged into SpaceX in Feb-2026, all-stock ~$1.25T combined deal) burns **~$6.4bn operating loss**, ~$30bn annualized capex; the AI segment drags a profitable rockets+Starlink business into a **~$4.9bn annual net loss**. A "$7bn Starlink profit machine subsidizing $30bn AI cash burn."
- **Governance ("uninvestable" crux):** Dual-class. Musk holds ~42% equity but **~82–85% of votes** (Class B = 10 votes/share, Class A = 1). **No sunset, no independent board, no path to remove him.** Charter lets Musk take corporate opportunities for himself and arrange related-party deals. Shareholders **waive jury trial + class actions**; **mandatory binding arbitration** (first major US IPO to adopt the SEC's new arbitration policy). Reincorporated Delaware→Texas (2024) to raise hurdles for activists; derivative suits require **≥3% ownership (~$37bn stake)**. Related-party flags include Valor Equity Partners (director Antonio Gracias).
- **+ Why framed this way:** the thesis reframes the central question from "is SpaceX a great business" (largely yes — Starlink ~$11.4bn rev, +50% YoY, ~63% margin) to "are public shareholders buying economic exposure with zero recourse against a controller who can self-deal." That is a governance discount, not a business critique.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Governance comps (dual-class / founder control):** Meta, Google/Alphabet, Snap (no-vote Class A) — but SpaceX is more extreme (arbitration + class-action waiver + Texas incorporation). Internal corpus comp: [[Navan raises ~$920M in IPO at $6.2B valuation]] disclosed Class B = **30 votes/share** — even more concentrated per-share, but Navan lacks the arbitration/opportunity-waiver stack.
- **Mega-IPO comp:** Saudi Aramco (2019, ~$1.7T initial cap, ~$25.6bn raised) — also a low-float, controller-dominated listing; SpaceX raise (~$75bn) is far larger.
- **Sat-broadband peers:** Amazon Kuiper, Eutelsat/OneWeb, AST SpaceMobile — none near Starlink's scale (10M+ active customers, 160 markets by Feb-2026).
- **+ Why:** the market tolerates founder control when execution is elite (Starlink/Starship); the new wrinkle is bundling control with **litigation foreclosure** — a structural escalation, not just super-voting shares.

## [2] Company history / fit
SpaceX valuation arc: ~$350bn (early-2025 tender) → ~$400bn (Jul-2025, $212/sh) → ~$800bn (Dec-2025 insider sale, $421/sh) → **xAI merger Feb-2026 (~$1T + ~$250bn)** → confidential S-1 → ~$1.75T IPO Jun-2026. FY2025 revenue **$18.7bn (+33%)**, adj. EBITDA ~$6.6bn. The xAI bolt-on is what converts a profitable company into a reported net loss and injects the related-party/valuation murk the bear thesis attacks.
**+ Why act this way:** folding xAI in pre-IPO lets a cash-rich Starlink underwrite AI capex without a separate raise, and lets Musk consolidate voting control across his empire under one super-voting structure ahead of the listing.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
The IPO is the most-covered fintech/capital-markets event in the corpus right now. **Novelty of THIS note is low** — the valuation (Morningstar) and governance critiques were already public pre-listing. Its only add-on is packaging the dual-class + arbitration + xAI-drag thesis into one "uninvestable" frame. Traction of the thesis: real institutional pushback — **CalPERS, NYC Comptroller, NYS Comptroller (~$1T combined AUM) demanded reforms; SpaceX rejected all.** That gives the governance angle weight; the "uninvestable" label is opinion, contradicted by the +19% debut (market bought it anyway).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Watch: 180-day lock-up expiry (selling pressure), post-IPO drift toward Morningstar's $780bn (Owens explicitly told investors to wait for "more attractive levels"), Starship regulatory milestones, and whether index inclusion is constrained by the no-vote structure (S&P/FTSE have prior dual-class screens). Sentiment split: retail/momentum bullish (Robinhood retail allocation — [[Robinhood to vie for retail role in SpaceX IPO]]), institutional-governance bearish.
**+ Second-order:** if major indices exclude or underweight SPCX over governance, passive demand is capped → valuation rests on active conviction in Musk → a more fragile, sentiment-driven float despite the trillion-dollar headline.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Two overlapping sectors: (1) **mega-cap capital markets / new listings** and (2) **satellite broadband + space launch**. SpaceX's ~$75bn raise at ~$1.75T is the **largest IPO in history**, ~3x Saudi Aramco's 2019 offering (~$25.6bn raised). Satellite broadband is in a structural up-cycle: Starlink reached **10M+ active customers across 160 markets by Feb-2026**, ~$11.4bn 2025 revenue (+50% YoY), ~63% adj. margin (per company figures via press, mid-2026). Why now: reusable-launch cost collapse + LEO constellation density create a near-monopoly moat that legacy GEO (Viasat/SES) and new entrants (Kuiper, Eutelsat/OneWeb, AST SpaceMobile) cannot yet match. Structure: **consolidated/controller-dominated** at the top of the value chain (launch + constellation), with capital and regulatory (spectrum/orbital slots) as the dominant entry barriers. (analysis)

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: launch cadence/$/kg-to-orbit; for broadband — active subscribers, ARPU, constellation count, EBITDA margin. Players: SpaceX (dominant launch + Starlink), Amazon **Kuiper** (capital-heavy challenger, behind on scale), **Eutelsat/OneWeb**, **AST SpaceMobile** (direct-to-cell), legacy Viasat/SES. Recent moves: SpaceX post-IPO M&A — [[SpaceX to acquire Cursor for $60 billion in stock]] (Jun-2026, all-stock) and Starlink mobile push ([[SpaceX weighs launching Starlink mobile service for US consumers]]). Protagonist position: **ahead/dominant** on both launch and LEO broadband; moat = scale + network/launch cost advantage + spectrum. The xAI bolt-on (Feb-2026) is the wildcard — Grok trails frontier AI peers, so it adds capex drag, not a moat. (analysis)

**Comps & multiples.**
- **SpaceX (IPO):** ~$1.75T cap / FY2025 rev $18.7bn = **~94x revenue**; ~266x EBITDA (per note/S-1). Morningstar DCF fair value **$780bn → implies ~42x rev** — i.e. the IPO is priced ~2.2x Morningstar's fair value.
  - Arithmetic: $780bn / $18.7bn ≈ **41.7x**; $1.75T / $18.7bn ≈ **93.6x**.
- **Saudi Aramco (2019, external comp):** ~$1.7T initial cap on far larger, profitable, cash-generative base — a low-float controller listing precedent, but a profit-machine vs SpaceX's growth/optionality story. Not a multiple comp (different model).
- **Internal dual-class comp:** [[Navan raises ~$920M in IPO at $6.2B valuation]] — Class B = 30 votes/share (more concentrated per-share than SpaceX's 10x), but no arbitration/opportunity-waiver stack.
- **Flag:** **rich vs any cash-flow comp** (94x rev, 266x EBITDA are far above sat/launch peers and even hyper-growth software). The premium is **not justified by current growth alone** (+33% total / +50% Starlink) — it prices Starship + AI optionality + Musk-execution faith. Morningstar/ProfG call it ~half-to-one-third overvalued.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Governance / no-recourse (primary).** ~82–85% Musk voting control, no sunset, class-action + jury waivers, mandatory arbitration, Texas reincorporation, 3% (~$37bn) derivative threshold → public holders own economic exposure with near-zero ability to check self-dealing. Second-order: potential **index exclusion/underweight** caps passive demand → more fragile, sentiment-driven float.
2. **Valuation / xAI drag.** 94x rev on a consolidated entity pushed into a ~$4.9bn net loss by xAI's ~$30bn capex; the "$7bn Starlink subsidizes $30bn AI burn" structure means a clean cash machine is funding an unproven AI bet. Down-trigger: lock-up expiry + drift toward $780bn.
3. **Concentration / single-key-man.** Valuation rests on Musk + Starlink; regulatory (Starship licensing, spectrum) and key-man risk are undiversifiable given the control structure.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** SpaceX normalizes a **new template for mega-IPOs: extreme founder control bundled with litigation foreclosure** — if it trades well, expect copycats among AI/founder-led names, shifting the market's question from "is the business good" to "what governance discount do public holders accept." Falsifiable thesis: SPCX drifts toward Morningstar's ~$780bn fair value within ~12 months post-lock-up unless Starship/AI milestones re-rate it. Trigger to watch: 180-day lock-up expiry, index-inclusion decisions, and any disclosed related-party transaction with Valor/xAI/Tesla. (analysis)
