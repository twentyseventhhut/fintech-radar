Tesla settles FSD crash lawsuit as federal investigations continue
Tesla has settled a lawsuit connected to a fatal 2023 crash involving a vehicle using the company’s advanced driver assistance system known as Full Self-Driving.
Bloomberg was first to report on the settlement. Terms were not disclosed.
The lawsuit was filed against Tesla and the driver by the daughter of Johna Story, a 71-year-old woman who was struck by a Tesla Model Y. Story was hit after she stepped out of her own vehicle to direct traffic around a crash that had occurred earlier due to sun glare.
The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) opened an investigation into Tesla’s FSD (Supervised) automated driving software in 2024 after four reported crashes in low visibility conditions — including the one involving Story. The NHTSA said, at the time, it was investigating the driver assistance system to find out whether it could “detect and respond appropriately to reduced roadway visibility conditions,” such as “sun glare, fog, or airborne dust.” 
That investigation was upgraded in March 2026 to an engineering analysis . In that report, the agency wrote “Available incident data raise concerns that Tesla’s degradation detection system, both as originally deployed and later updated, fails to detect and/or warn the driver appropriately under degraded visibility conditions such as glare and airborne obscurants.”
While the settlement ends the family’s lawsuit, this upgraded NHTSA investigation has not yet been closed. At stake for Tesla for the federal investigation is a host of possible outcomes, including a recall.
The federal agency also opened an investigation into FSD in October 2025 after receiving reports the software caused the vehicles to run red lights or cross into the wrong lane.

# Context-enrichment: Tesla settles FSD crash lawsuit as federal probes continue

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Tesla **settled** the wrongful-death suit brought by the daughter of **Johna Story**, 71, killed **2023-11-28** when a Tesla Model Y on **FSD (Supervised)** struck her at speed in Arizona after she stepped out to direct traffic around a prior sun-glare crash. Widely described as the **first known pedestrian fatality tied to FSD**. **Bloomberg first reported** the settlement (2026-06-26); **terms NOT disclosed**. Plaintiff counsel Dustin Birch (Clear Counsel Law Group).
- **What the de-PR'd reality is:** the headline ("settles") conflates two very different things. The settlement closes ONE private tort claim and — critically — keeps Tesla's FSD crash/telemetry data out of another public jury trial. It does **nothing** to the federal exposure. → Why this matters: the live risk is regulatory (a possible recall of ~3.2M vehicles), not this single payout. A confidential settlement is the cheap way to avoid discovery and a second adverse verdict (analysis).
- **Two NHTSA probes remain open and are the real story:** (1) the low-visibility probe **PE24031** (opened 2024-10-17, ~2.41M vehicles) — **upgraded to Engineering Analysis EA26002 on 2026-03-18/19**, now ~3.20M vehicles, 9 incidents (1 fatality, 1 injury), NHTSA faulting Tesla's "degradation-detection system"; an EA is the final tier before NHTSA forces a recall or closes. (2) a separate traffic-violation probe **PE25012** (opened 2025-10-07, ~2.88M vehicles, red-light running + wrong-lane; grew from 58 to ~80 incidents by 2025-12). No recall as of 2026-06-28.

## [1] Competitors / peers (the AV / FSD-liability landscape)
- **Waymo (Alphabet):** the only at-scale US driverless operator; operates **without** a safety driver in SF/Phoenix/LA/Austin; treats its sensor stack (lidar) as a safety/liability moat vs Tesla's vision-only. Tesla is catching up on robotaxi deployment (Austin invite-only since 2025-06-22) but trails on regulatory standing.
- **Cruise (GM):** the cautionary peer — a 2023 pedestrian-dragging incident triggered a permit suspension and effective wind-down; shows how ONE incident + a perceived cover-up can end an AV program. → Tesla's settle-quietly strategy is partly designed to avoid a Cruise-style narrative spiral.
- **Chinese AV (Momenta):** [[Chinese autonomous-driving firm Momenta prepares US IPO]] — a vision+map stack racing to public markets, a reminder the autonomy race is global and capital is flowing in.
- **Position:** Tesla is **ahead on fleet scale/data and consumer FSD rollout**, but **behind Waymo on driverless legal/regulatory standing** and uniquely exposed on **product-liability tort** because FSD is sold to millions of retail drivers (not a closed operator fleet). The litigation pattern (see [3]) is name-specific to Tesla, not sectoral.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Tesla's legal trajectory on Autopilot/FSD has shifted from **wins to losses**: it **won** the first US Autopilot death trial (Riverside/Molander, 2023-11-01, jury found no defect) and **settled** the Walter Huang case confidentially one day before trial (2024-04-08). Then the dam broke: a **Miami federal jury (Benavides/Key Largo) returned a ~$243M judgment on 2025-08-01** ($43M compensatory + $200M punitive; Tesla 33% at fault) — the first US verdict holding Tesla liable in an Autopilot death — **upheld by the judge 2026-02-20** (Tesla appealing, arguing a punitive cap). Press (Electrek, Feb 2026) reports Tesla has since quietly settled "at least four" more Autopilot death suits.
- **Why Tesla acts this way:** post-Benavides, the downside of letting an FSD-pedestrian-fatality case reach a jury (sympathetic victim, punitive exposure, public discovery of crash data) vastly exceeds a confidential check. The Story settlement fits the post-verdict **avoid-the-jury** playbook (analysis). Structural driver: a large slice of Tesla's ~$1.4T market cap rests on robotaxi/FSD option value, so an adverse verdict or recall is reputationally and narratively expensive beyond the dollar amount.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- The settlement itself is **not novel** — it's the latest instance of a clear pattern. What it confirms (traction of the pattern, not announcements): Tesla now **routinely pays to keep FSD liability cases out of court**. The genuinely new datapoint is the **EA26002 upgrade (2026-03)**: NHTSA moving to engineering analysis on ~3.2M vehicles is the step that can produce a mandated recall — that, not the settlement, is the live value-destroyer.
- **Where the margin/risk sits in the stack:** Tesla's vision-only approach is the cost/scale advantage AND the liability concentration. → Because FSD is a $/feature sold to ~3M+ retail owners (vs Waymo's owned fleet), every degraded-visibility edge case is both a regulatory data point (feeding EA26002) and a potential plaintiff. The "value-add" of vision-only FSD is real on cost, but the second-order effect is that Tesla internalizes a tail of tort + recall risk that closed-fleet operators don't (analysis).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Open federal items:** EA26002 (~3.2M vehicles) can close, demand a software fix, or force a recall — no resolution as of 2026-06-28; EAs typically run ~18 months. PE25012 (~2.9M) unresolved. The **DOJ/SEC criminal-fraud probe** into Autopilot/FSD marketing remains **disclosed as ongoing** in Tesla's Q1 2026 10-Q ("no government agency has concluded wrongdoing") — no charges, no confirmed closure; treat "Trump DOJ dropped it" as unverified speculation.
- **Counterintuitive cross-current:** the SAME administration is **deregulating to clear Tesla's robotaxi path** ([[US proposes axing AV brake-pedal rule in boost for Tesla]], NPRM 2026-06-25/26, comments to 2026-07-27) while NHTSA — under Tesla-skeptic Administrator Jonathan Morrison (confirmed 51–47, Sept 2025) — runs two active FSD probes. → Deregulation lowers the federal *compliance* barrier but does NOT shield Tesla from active enforcement or tort. The headline risk and the headline tailwind are running simultaneously.
- Q1 2026 deliveries 358,023 (+6.3% YoY, missed consensus); Q2 not yet released. TSLA ~$380 / ~$1.4T cap (2026-06-26).
