# Context-enrichment: Russian central bank proposes Gosuslugi fraud-victim support service
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**FRESHNESS / DUPLICATE VERDICT: fresh.** This is a NEW proposal voiced 2026-06-24/25 by Vadim Uvarov (director of the CBR information-security department) at the St. Petersburg International Legal Forum (PMЮФ/SPILF-2026); the CBR has reportedly sent the idea to Mintsifry (Ministry of Digital Development). No prior note in the corpus covers this specific proposal — internal retrieval surfaces only adjacent anti-fraud items from other jurisdictions (Brazil Pix MED, Australia Confirmation of Payee, FCA Firm Checker, JPMorgan, Indonesia OJK), not this Russian one. It is a fresh policy signal — but note it is a *proposal/intention*, not a launched service.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- The CBR **proposed** (not launched) a dedicated section on the Gosuslugi portal to walk a fraud victim through what to do after a theft: where to turn (bank, police, financial ombudsman), what documents are needed, and — emphasised by the regulator — in which cases the bank is *obliged* to return the money. Idea pitched at a forum and forwarded to Mintsifry. There is no timeline, budget, owner, or commitment from Mintsifry in the reporting.
- **Why framed this way / what it reveals.** The de-PR'd substance is thin: it is an informational/navigation layer (a checklist + links), not a new legal right, money, or recovery mechanism. The genuinely interesting admission buried in it is the regulator's own diagnosis — *victims often don't know they can demand reimbursement from the bank, so they never ask*. That is a tacit acknowledgement that Russia's existing mandatory-reimbursement regime (369-FZ, see [2]/[3]) is under-utilised: the law exists, the payouts don't. So the proposal treats a **demand-side awareness gap**, not the supply-side problem of banks actually paying.
- Numbers that anchor the weight (CBR, Q1 2026): fraudsters stole ~₽7.4bn from citizens; the share returned to clients was only **5.7% (~₽420m)**. For full-year 2024: a record **₽27.5bn** stolen (+74.4% YoY), of which reimbursements were only ~9.9% (₽2,713.58m), and reimbursements paid *pursuant to the 369-FZ liability rule* were a trivial **₽1.23m** — i.e. banks almost never trigger statutory liability. (analysis) A web portal that tells victims "you can ask the bank" does little if the statutory trigger almost never fires.

## [1] Competitors / peers (other jurisdictions' victim-help / anti-scam tooling)
- **Brazil — Pix Special Return Mechanism (MED)** [[Brazil's central bank amends Pix special return rules]] (2025-09): an actual *operational* recovery tool letting victims recover funds within ~11 days, plus "follow-the-money" rules. Far ahead of a Russian info-portal — it moves money, not pamphlets.
- **Australia — Confirmation of Payee** [[Australian banks launch nationwide Confirmation of Payee scheme]] (2025-07): prevention at the point of transfer (name-matching) — stops the theft rather than guiding the victim afterwards.
- **UK FCA — Firm Checker** [[FCA launches Firm Checker tool to fight financial scams]] (2025-12): a consumer-facing lookup tool — closest analogue in being informational, but pre-emptive (verify before you pay) vs Russia's post-hoc (what to do after you're robbed).
- **JPMorgan** [[JPMorgan launches biggest-ever fraud and scam initiative]] (2025-11) and **Revolut call-ID** [[Revolut adds call identification feature against impersonation scams]] (2026-01): private-sector prevention/intervention.
- **Indonesia OJK** [[Indonesia's OJK sanctions digital fraud perpetrators]] (2025-08): supply-side punishment of perpetrators.
- **Position: catching up / lagging on substance.** The global frontier is *prevention* (CoP, call-ID) and *automated recovery* (Pix MED). Russia's proposal sits at the weakest tier — *post-hoc navigation* — and is only a proposal. (analysis) Russia already has strong *back-end* anti-fraud (mandatory cooling-off, fraud DB, self-ban), so this fills a real UX gap, but it is downstream of where peers are competing.

## [2] Company / country fit
- Fits the CBR's multi-year anti-fraud build-out: 369-FZ (effective 25 July 2024) — banks must pause suspicious transfers for 2 days if the recipient is in the CBR fraud database, and are liable to reimburse within 30 days (60 cross-border) if they ignored the DB; **self-ban on loans** via Gosuslugi (effective 1 March 2025, >22.1m self-bans by Feb 2026); **loan cooling-off** (effective 1 Sept 2025, 4h for ₽50–200k, 48h for >₽200k); fraud-DB persons barred from new payment means, ₽100k/month transfer caps, tokenised-card limits.
- **Why act this way.** Gosuslugi is already the CBR/state delivery rail for citizen anti-fraud tools (self-ban already lives there), so bolting a victim-help section onto it is low-cost and on-brand. The choice of an *awareness* tool rather than a stronger liability rule is telling: it is the cheapest lever, requires no new bank obligations, and shifts effort onto the citizen and Mintsifry. (analysis) It also lets the regulator be seen "doing something" about a metric (5.7% recovery) that is politically embarrassing without forcing banks to pay more.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty: low.** No new right, money, or mechanism — a navigation/checklist layer over existing rights. Value-add is real *only if* it converts the 369-FZ paper right into actual claims (the regulator's own thesis). The binding constraint, though, is not victim ignorance but the statutory liability trigger almost never firing (₽1.23m of in-law reimbursements in all of 2024). (analysis) Better-informed victims may file more claims, but if banks can show the recipient wasn't in the fraud DB / they followed procedure, the claims fail — so the portal could raise expectations it can't meet.
- **Traction: none.** Proposal stage; sent to Mintsifry; no commitment, date, or build. Contrast with self-ban (22.1m uses) — a CBR tool that *did* fly because it gave citizens a concrete, free, one-click action.

## [4] What's next / sentiment
- Next steps depend on Mintsifry agreeing to build it on Gosuslugi; no signal yet. Likely framed as part of the broader 2025–2026 anti-fraud package and the planned escalation against "droppers" and social-engineering scams.
- **Risks / second-order.** (1) Expectation gap: a "how to get your money back" portal against a 5.7% recovery rate risks public cynicism. (2) It addresses symptoms; the real leverage is tightening bank liability or attacking the SBP/card cash-out chain. (3) Counterintuitive: better victim navigation could increase *measured* fraud-claim volumes and make recovery rates look worse before they improve. The central question is not "will the portal help victims navigate" but **"will the 369-FZ reimbursement obligation ever actually bind banks?"** — and this proposal does not touch that.

## Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Proposal vs build?** Has Mintsifry actually agreed to build it, or is this only an idea pitched at a forum and "sent"? — Open; reporting shows only a proposal forwarded to Mintsifry, no commitment/date/budget.
2. **Is anything legally new?** Does the service create any new right or just signpost existing 369-FZ rights? — Just signposting; no new right, money, or mechanism (analysis from CBR framing).
3. **What's the real binding constraint?** Victim ignorance, or the statutory liability trigger almost never firing? — The latter: in-law reimbursements were ₽1.23m of all 2024 payouts; awareness alone won't move that.
4. **Recovery rate reality?** What share of stolen funds is actually returned? — Q1 2026: 5.7% (~₽420m of ₽7.4bn); FY2024: ~9.9% (₽2.71bn of ₽27.5bn). A "get your money back" portal against 5.7% risks an expectation gap.
5. **Does informing victims increase successful recovery, or just claim volume?** — Open; plausibly raises filed claims more than paid claims, possibly worsening measured recovery short-term.
6. **Why an awareness tool and not stronger liability?** — Cheapest lever; no new bank obligation; shifts effort to citizen/Mintsifry (analysis). Politically lets CBR act on an embarrassing metric without forcing payouts.
7. **Prior art on Gosuslugi?** — Yes: self-ban on loans already lives there (>22.1m bans by Feb 2026), so the rail and pattern exist; this is incremental.
8. **Peer benchmark — is Russia ahead or behind?** — Behind on substance: Brazil's Pix MED actually recovers funds (~11 days); Australia CoP / Revolut call-ID prevent at source. Russia proposes post-hoc navigation only.
9. **Who is silent on what?** — No word on funding, ownership, who maintains legal accuracy of the checklist, or how it interacts with the financial ombudsman's caseload.
10. **Does it touch the cash-out chain?** — No; ignores droppers/SBP/card mule networks where money actually leaves the system, which is where recovery is won or lost.
11. **Could it backfire?** — Yes: raising "you can demand reimbursement" expectations while the 369-FZ trigger rarely binds could deepen public cynicism toward banks and the CBR.
12. **Is the "victims don't know their rights" claim evidenced?** — It is the regulator's assertion (Uvarov); no cited survey/data behind it. Mark as regulator claim, not verified fact.
13. **Timeline/measurability?** — Open; no KPI, launch date, or target recovery-rate uplift stated.
14. **Duplicate check?** — No prior corpus note covers this specific Gosuslugi victim-help proposal; adjacent items are other jurisdictions. Fresh.
15. **Second-order question that matters most?** — Not "will the portal help navigation" but "will the 369-FZ reimbursement obligation ever actually bind banks?" — which this proposal does not address.

Importance: 2/5 — A genuinely fresh, low-stakes regulatory *proposal* (not a launch): an awareness/navigation layer on Gosuslugi for fraud victims. Low novelty, zero traction, no new right or money, and it does not touch the real constraint (banks rarely pay under 369-FZ; 5.7% recovery). Notable mainly as a signal that the CBR itself admits its mandatory-reimbursement regime is under-utilised, and for the stark Q1 2026 fraud stats it surfaces. Adjacent peers (Brazil Pix MED, Australia CoP) are materially further ahead.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Russian retail anti-fraud is a *regulator-driven* sub-vertical, not a market: the spend and rules originate from the CBR, banks are obligated parties, and there is no third-party revenue pool to size — TAM "no data" (and the relevant "market" here is the loss-and-recovery balance, not a product market). The loss base is large and still growing: ₽27.5bn stolen from citizens in 2024 (+74.4% YoY), ₽29.3bn in 2025 (+6.4%, per CBR via Cryptopolitan/iz.ru), ~₽7.4bn in Q1 2026 (CBR). The binding structural feature is on the *recovery* side: only 5.7% (~₽420m) returned in Q1 2026 and ~9.9% in FY2024, of which payments specifically under the 369-FZ statutory-liability rule were a trivial ₽1.23m in all of 2024 (CBR, Review of Authorised Frauds). Entry barriers are 100% regulatory — only the state/CBR can create rights, the fraud DB, or Gosuslugi rails. "Why now": this is the cheapest next lever in a multi-year package (369-FZ liability + 2-day cooling-off, >22.1m loan self-bans, loan cooling-off, dropper account-blocking) where the embarrassing 5.7% recovery metric is now politically visible (Carnegie flags cyberfraud as a political issue) — second-order, an awareness portal lets the regulator act on the metric without imposing new bank payout obligations.

**Competitive landscape.** The KPIs that matter for this sub-vertical: (1) reimbursement / recovery rate (% of stolen funds returned), (2) statutory-trigger hit rate (does the liability rule actually bind), (3) speed-to-claim. Against international "competitors" (other jurisdictions' victim regimes), Russia is at the weakest tier on KPI (1) and (2):
- **UK PSR mandatory APP reimbursement** — 88% of stolen funds returned (Oct-2024–Jun-2025), 89% over 15 months to Dec-2025, 82% of claims closed within 5 business days (PSR dashboard). A *binding liability* model: PSPs must pay, split 50/50 sending/receiving.
- **Brazil Pix MED** [[Brazil's central bank amends Pix special return rules]] — operational money-movement recovery (~11-day window), yet only ~7% of 2025 losses (R$6.5bn) actually recovered and 89% of refund requests denied (insufficient funds / closed mule account, per ClearingPost); MED 2.0 multi-hop tracking made mandatory 2026-02-02.
- **UK/AU prevention layer** — Confirmation of Payee [[Australian banks launch nationwide Confirmation of Payee scheme]], [[PayPoint partners SmarterPay on Confirmation of Payee fraud checks]] stops the transfer at source.
Basis of competition is *substance* (does money come back), and Russia's proposal competes only on *navigation* (a checklist). Protagonist position: **lagging on substance / niche UX fix** (analysis). Moat is purely the state distribution rail (Gosuslugi already hosts self-ban) — a distribution moat, not a recovery one.

**Comps & multiples.** Not a valuation event — no company, round, ticker or deal; trading multiples = N/A. The comparable metric is recovery rate, computed from the corpus + web:
- Russia FY2024: ₽2,713.58m / ₽27,500m ≈ **9.9%** returned; in-law 369-FZ portion ₽1.23m / ₽27,500m ≈ **0.004%**.
- Russia Q1 2026: ~₽420m / ₽7,400m ≈ **5.7%** (CBR).
- UK PSR: ~**88–89%** returned (binding liability). Brazil Pix MED: ~**7%** (operational but mule-constrained).
Distribution (4 regimes): min 5.7% (RU Q1-26) · max 89% (UK) · the two operational/awareness-only systems (RU, BR Pix MED) cluster at 5–10%, the binding-liability system (UK) at ~88%. Internal comps for the loss/recovery framing: [[PSR reports APP fraud reimbursement covers 88% of in-scope claims]], [[UK Finance reports 629 million pounds in fraud losses in H1 2025]], [[JPMorgan launches biggest-ever fraud and scam initiative]]. Read-across: recovery scales with the *liability rule biting*, not with information provision — the very lever this proposal does not touch.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Proposal, not a launch — no mechanism.** Idea pitched at SPILF-2026 and "sent" to Mintsifry; no timeline, budget, owner, or new legal right. Second-order: high deliverability risk and zero traction baseline; easy to over-read as policy.
2. **Awareness layer mis-targets the binding constraint.** The cap on recovery is the 369-FZ liability trigger almost never firing (₽1.23m in 2024), not victim ignorance. More informed victims raise *filed* claims, not *paid* claims — could make measured recovery look worse short-term, deepening cynicism against a 5.7% rate.
3. **Cross-jurisdiction read-across says info alone won't move recovery.** Brazil shows that even an *operational* recovery tool only returns ~7% when mules drain funds; only the UK's binding liability gets to ~88%. A checklist sits below even Brazil's tier — structurally unlikely to lift Russia's recovery rate.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The signal value exceeds the product value: the CBR is implicitly admitting its own mandatory-reimbursement regime is under-utilised (the regulator's stated rationale is "victims don't know they can demand money back"). Falsifiable thesis: this portal will NOT materially move the recovery rate; watch the CBR's quarterly recovery % (5.7% in Q1 2026) and the 369-FZ in-law reimbursement line over 2026-2027 — if those stay flat after launch, the diagnosis (awareness gap) is wrong and the real fix is tightening bank liability or the SBP/card cash-out chain. The thesis breaks if a future revision attaches a hard liability or auto-recovery rule (a Pix-MED-style mechanism) to the portal.
