## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)

The 2026-06-26 digest item ("ZEN.COM strengthens its presence in the UK through a strategy based on local licences") is a **re-packaging / strategy-narrative item, not a new event**. The substantive fact it rests on — ZEN.COM securing a UK Authorised EMI licence — already happened in **2024**: Zen UK Limited was authorised by the FCA as an EMI in **August 2024**, and the ClearBank partnership that enabled it was announced **3 October 2024** (CEO Europe Michał Bogusławski: "ClearBank's role... is pivotal"). So the June 2026 blurb anchors to a ~18-24-month-old approval and dresses it as forward strategy.

- **What was actually "done":** essentially nothing new on 2026-06-26 — this is a thought-leadership/PR framing of an existing licence stack, distributed via the "Connecting the Dots in Fintech" digest. No new licence grant, number, product, or go-live date is attached in the source.
- **The real strategy (de-PR'd):** ZEN historically ran the cheap path — a single Bank of Lithuania EMI (granted 2018-05-24) passported across ~30 EEA states. Post-Brexit, EEA passporting no longer reaches the UK, so a UK presence *requires* a local FCA licence. The "local licences strategy" is therefore partly **necessity dressed as choice**: Brexit killed UK passporting; Singapore/Hong Kong/Ukraine were never EEA. The genuine strategic element is choosing to build a portfolio of *direct* local approvals (UK, SG, HK pending, UA pending) rather than serving those markets via partners/agents — to own the regulatory rails as "global financial infrastructure."
- **Why this framing now:** ZEN is a profitable, fast-growing private fintech (see [3]) using earned-media to signal maturity/credibility ahead of further market entries and a board reshuffle (ex-Polish President Andrzej Duda joined in 2025). The digest item is low-information; the weight lives in the underlying business, not the announcement.
- **Caveat / open:** UAB ZEN.COM's Lithuanian EMI authorisation was reported **cancelled as of 31/12/2025** in one search result (analysis — unverified primary). If true, this could reflect a re-domicile/restructure of the EU entity (the group is HQ'd in London), which would materially change the "local licences" story from *additive* to *substitutive*. Marked **open** — needs FCA/Bank of Lithuania register confirmation.

## [1] Competitors / peers

The "local-licence-as-infrastructure" play is a crowded 2025-26 pattern; ZEN is **parity, not pioneer**:
- [[Finmo secures UK EMI licence]] (2025-07, FCA AEMI, direct UK clearing access) — same UK-EMI move, B2B treasury focus.
- [[YeePay selects ClearBank for UK and European expansion]] (2026-04) — **same enabler (ClearBank)** applying for UK FCA + Dutch DNB EMIs; ZEN used ClearBank for the identical purpose in 2024.
- [[OpenFX acquires Embed for EU and UK payments licences]] (2026-06) — buys a licence stack rather than applying; "first regulated EEA + UK presence."
- [[PayDo secures MFSA licence to expand in EU]] (2025-07) — mirror-image: a UK-adjacent player adding an *EU* EMI to passport into the EEA.
- [[Privalgo gains Dutch approval for EU payment operations]] (2025-08), Ripple's CSSF EMI (2026-02, see [[Connecting the Dots: Twelve European fintech deals raise 165.5 million euros]]) — same multi-jurisdiction licence accumulation.

**Why the landscape looks like this (second order):** Brexit fractured the single EMI-passport model — every cross-border paytech now needs a *paired* EU + UK licence, so the market splits into (a) EU-native firms adding a UK licence (ZEN, PayDo→reverse, Ripple) and (b) acquirers buying licences (OpenFX). The differentiator is no longer *having* a licence (commoditised, ClearBank-as-a-service makes UK EMI fast) but **distribution + unit economics on top of it**. ZEN's edge vs these peers is that it is *already profitable at ~€115m revenue* — most licence-stackers are pre-profit. That, not the licence, is the real story.

## [2] Company history / fit

- Founded **2018** by Dawid Rożek (G2A.COM co-founder); Bank of Lithuania EMI **2018-05-24**; public launch **Nov 2020** (Mastercard partnership). Offices: London, Vilnius, Warsaw, Arnhem, Singapore, Hong Kong.
- Revenue trajectory: **€8.7m (2021) → €93.9m (2024) → ~€115m (2025)**; net income **-€3.2m (2021) → +€24.7m profit (2024)**; EU entity profitable since 2022. ~**1.5m consumers + 10k businesses**; 32 markets; fastest growth in LT, Romania, Spain, France.
- **2nd-largest EMI in Lithuania (14.17% share, 2025)** / **No.1 independent EMI by revenue (16.94% of segment, Q3 2025)** per Bank of Lithuania.
- **Why it acts this way:** ZEN is a genuinely cash-generative, founder-led fintech that explicitly *rejects* the super-app race (per XYZ.pl interview) and instead pursues "global financial infrastructure via local approvals." With the EEA base saturated and the EU EMI fully exploited, the next growth leg has to come from *new jurisdictions* (UK, SG, HK, UA) — each of which is outside the EEA passport, structurally forcing the local-licence approach. The strategy is internally consistent: profitable core funds slow, licence-by-licence geographic expansion (new markets targeted to breakeven in 18-24 months).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction

- **Novelty of the 2026-06-26 item: ~none.** The licence is from 2024; the "strategy" framing is the only new packaging. As an *enrichment-worthy event* it is thin.
- **Real value-add lives in the company, not the announcement:** profitable scale (~€115m rev, ~€25m+ net), 1.5m users, top-2 LT EMI. That is rare among the licence-stacking cohort and is the defensible part — the licence itself is increasingly a commodity (ClearBank productised UK-EMI enablement for ZEN *and* YeePay).
- **Who captures the margin (second order):** owning local licences lets ZEN keep more of the take-rate (direct clearing access, no agent/BIN-sponsor toll) vs. operating as someone's agent — that is the genuine economic logic. But the flip side: each local licence carries fixed compliance/capital cost, and UK e-commerce payments is brutally competitive (Stripe, Checkout.com, Adyen, Revolut). The Q2-2026 Singapore "first e-commerce clients" goal shows the new markets are still **pre-traction (announced, not live)** — so the value-add outside the EU base is unproven.
- **Open:** no disclosed UK-specific user/volume numbers — UK traction since the 2024 licence is unquantified in available sources.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment

- **Pipeline:** Hong Kong (advanced, Asian hub), Ukraine (targeting end-2026), Singapore first e-com clients Q2 2026; deeper push in Germany, Italy, Nordics. Breakeven target 18-24 months per new market.
- **Sentiment:** positive but earned-media-driven; no external analyst coverage (private company). The Duda board appointment reads as a credibility/sovereign-relations signal more than an operational one.
- **Risks / why the market goes this way:** (1) licence commoditisation — if FCA-EMI-via-ClearBank is a buyable service, ZEN's "infrastructure moat" is shallow vs incumbents with scale; (2) the **possible 2025-12-31 cancellation of the LT EMI** (open) could mean restructure risk, not pure expansion; (3) UK e-commerce payments margin compression. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the more ZEN frames licences as the moat, the more it competes in the *commoditised* layer — its actual edge (profitability + e-commerce/gaming heritage from G2A roots) is under-told in this PR.

**Net:** a low-novelty re-announcement of a 2024 licence, but attached to a genuinely strong, profitable underlying company. The *item* is a 2; the *company* would rate higher in a profile.

**Red-team — 13 questions**

1. **Is the 2026-06-26 item actually a new event?** No. It re-frames a UK FCA EMI licence secured in **August 2024** (ClearBank partnership announced 2024-10-03). The "strategy" is the only new wrapper.

2. **Is "local licences" a strategic choice or a necessity?** Mostly necessity dressed as choice — post-Brexit the EEA EMI passport does not reach the UK, and SG/HK/UA are non-EEA, so direct local licences are *required* to operate there. The genuine choice is going direct vs. via agents.

3. **What is the precise mechanism delta vs incumbents?** Owning the local licence gives direct clearing access and keeps more of the take-rate vs. operating as an agent/BIN-sponsored party — but it is the same playbook as Finmo, YeePay (also ClearBank), OpenFX. No unique mechanism.

4. **Did the prior version fly — is there UK traction?** **Open.** No UK-specific user/volume numbers disclosed since the 2024 licence. UK traction is unquantified.

5. **What's the underlying company quality?** Strong: ~€115m revenue (2025), profitable (~€24.7m net 2024), 1.5m consumers, 10k businesses, 32 markets, top-2 EMI in Lithuania (14.17%).

6. **Is the licence a moat?** Weak moat — ClearBank productises UK-EMI enablement (used by both ZEN and YeePay), so the licence layer is commoditising. Edge is profitability + e-commerce/gaming heritage, not the licence.

7. **Was the LT EMI cancelled on 2025-12-31?** **Open / red flag.** One search result reports UAB ZEN.COM's EMI authorisation cancelled as of 31/12/2025. If true, "local licences" may be *substitutive* (restructure) not purely *additive*. Needs FCA + Bank of Lithuania register confirmation.

8. **Are the new markets live or announced?** Mostly announced. Singapore "first e-commerce clients" only planned for **Q2 2026**; HK and Ukraine licences "advanced"/pending. Pre-traction outside the EU base.

9. **Who is silent about what?** The digest is silent on any new number, date, UK volumes, capital cost of the multi-licence stack, and the possible LT EMI cancellation. Pure narrative.

10. **Who are the closest comparables and is ZEN ahead?** Finmo, YeePay (same ClearBank route), OpenFX (buys licences), PayDo/Ripple (reverse: EU licences). ZEN is at parity on the licence move but ahead on profitability.

11. **What changes the central question?** Shifts from "is the local-licence strategy novel?" (it isn't) to "can a profitable EU-base fintech extend that profitability into commoditised, hyper-competitive UK/Asia e-commerce payments?" — that, not the licence, is the real test.

12. **What are the downside triggers?** (a) confirmed LT EMI cancellation/restructure; (b) UK e-commerce margin compression vs Stripe/Checkout/Adyen/Revolut; (c) new markets missing the 18-24-month breakeven target; (d) licence commoditisation eroding the "infrastructure" thesis.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** ZEN sits at the intersection of two markets: European EMI / e-money issuance and UK consumer + B2B payments. UK digital-payments revenue ~$11.7bn (2025), projected to ~$43.7bn by 2034, CAGR ~15.2% (per Straits Research, via search, as of 2025) — large and growing, but ZEN is a sub-scale new entrant in it. The structurally important driver is **regulatory, not market growth**: post-Brexit the EEA EMI passport no longer reaches the UK, so any EU-based EMI wanting UK operations needs a separate FCA authorisation — the precise mechanism that forces ZEN's "local-licences" approach (already de-PR'd in [0]). "Why now": the UK EMI pipeline is *narrowing*, not widening — FCA e-money approvals fell from 171 (2020) to 35 (2025), per Pathlight Associates ([[UK FCA e-money licence approvals fall from 171 to 35 since 2020]]). Second-order: scarcer approvals make an *already-held* FCA EMI (ZEN's, since Aug 2024) a modestly more valuable asset than the 2024 framing implies — the moat is "got in before the door narrowed," not the licence type itself.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: TPV/volume, take-rate, per-transaction margin, and (for an EMI) safeguarded customer funds as a scale proxy. Basis of competition is **distribution + unit economics on the licence, not the licence itself** — ClearBank has productised UK-EMI enablement (used by both ZEN and YeePay), commoditising the licence layer. UK merchant acquiring/e-commerce is moderately concentrated and brutally competitive: top-5 acquirers (Worldpay, Barclays, Adyen, Checkout.com, Lloyds Cardnet) hold most processing value, with Stripe/Adyen/Checkout.com dominating PSP e-commerce; merchants multi-home, capping pricing power (per Mordor/Straits, via search, 2025). Recent peer moves: Revolut won a UK banking licence (Nov 2025, deposit insurance + direct BoE settlement) — a structurally deeper position than ZEN's EMI; Adyen took a minority stake in Banked (Oct 2025). ZEN's position: **niche / catching-up** in UK e-commerce vs these incumbents, but **ahead of the licence-stacking cohort on profitability** (~€115m group rev, ~€25m net 2024). Moat (analysis): intangibles/scale from a profitable EU core + e-commerce/gaming heritage (G2A roots) — under-told vs the licence narrative.

**Comps & multiples.** No round, valuation or deal attaches to this item, and ZEN is private with no disclosed UK volumes — so **EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA, P/E = no data** (no public market cap or EV). Internal comps (same EU↔UK EMI-stacking pattern, all from the base):
- [[Finmo secures UK EMI licence]] (2025-07) — identical UK AEMI move, B2B treasury.
- [[Wallester UK secures FCA electronic money institution authorisation]] (2026-06) — UK FCA EMI, embedded-finance/cards.
- [[Ripple receives FCA EMI license in UK]] (2026-01) + [[Connecting the Dots Twelve European fintech deals raise 165.5 million euros]] (Ripple CSSF EMI 2026-02) — multi-jurisdiction licence accumulation.
- [[Capi Money secures FCA EMI authorization for UK-Africa payments]] (2026-01); [[dtcpay gets green light for Luxembourg EMI licence]] (2025-07, reverse: SG→EU).
Distribution not computed — none of these comps disclose comparable multiples; qualitative comparison only. **Data discrepancy worth flagging (analysis):** the ~€115m figure is *group* revenue; Bank-of-Lithuania-entity UAB ZEN.COM 2025 revenue was reported at **€80.32m** with €333.03m safeguarded funds (per thebanks.eu, via search) — i.e. the headline ~€115m blends the LT EMI with other entities. Not an outlier flag, but the "profitable at €115m" story should not be read as a single regulated entity.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Possible LT EMI cancellation (2025-12-31, open).** If the Lithuanian EMI was truly cancelled/restructured (flagged open in [0]), "local licences" shifts from *additive* to *substitutive* — the EU passport base (~30 EEA states) that funds expansion could be re-domiciled, materially changing the thesis. Needs Bank of Lithuania register confirmation.
2. **Licence commoditisation + scarcity cuts both ways.** ClearBank-as-a-service makes UK EMI a buyable capability, so the "infrastructure moat" is shallow; yet collapsing FCA approvals (171→35) mean ZEN competes as a sub-scale licensee against incumbents (Revolut now a *bank*) with far deeper rails — disintermediation risk where the deposit/settlement layer captures the margin.
3. **UK/Asia execution + margin compression.** New markets are pre-traction (SG "first e-com clients" only Q2 2026), and UK e-commerce take-rates are pressured by Stripe/Adyen/Checkout.com multi-homing — the 18-24-month breakeven-per-market target is unproven outside the EU core.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Not a re-rating or new entry — it is a low-signal narrative item on a 2024 licence (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: *ZEN's edge is profitability, not licences; the real test is whether a cash-generative EU EMI can extend margins into commoditised, incumbent-dominated UK/Asia e-commerce.* Watch for: (trigger that confirms) disclosed UK volume/user numbers or SG go-live with retained take-rate; (trigger that breaks it) confirmed LT EMI cancellation, or new-market breakeven slipping past 24 months.
