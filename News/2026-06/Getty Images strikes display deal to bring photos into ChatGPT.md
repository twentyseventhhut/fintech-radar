Getty Images Enters Deal With OpenAI, Raising Questions On Training
By Gabriel Alin Zainescu , 
Contributor.
Summary
Getty Images' new "display agreement" with OpenAI integrates its licensed photos into ChatGPT, causing Getty's stock to surge. However, the deal's press release conspicuously omits any mention of whether these images can be used for AI model training. This silence is critical, especially after Getty's recent legal defeat against Stability AI over unauthorized data scraping for training. While Getty aims to be a licensed content provider within AI, the lack of clarity leaves its 600,000 contributors questioning if their work remains valuable assets or becomes raw material for AI models, a key right Getty previously defended. The company celebrates a truce, but the true cost for creators remains unstated. 
A few of my photographs live inside the Getty ecosystem, through Unsplash, one of its brands. They've been viewed several million times by people who will never know my name. So when Getty opened its libraries to OpenAI this week, I read the announcement the way any contributor does — not for the word that was there, but for the one that wasn't.
The headline facts are easy. On June 21, Getty Images announced a multi-year "display agreement" with OpenAI that will bring its licensed content into ChatGPT’s search and discovery experiences. In plain terms: When you ask ChatGPT something that calls for an image, the answer may now arrive as a licensed Getty photograph instead of a synthetic approximation. 
The market liked it enormously. Getty shares spiked as much as 200% in premarket trading before settling, after a year in which the stock had fallen more than half.
Read past the surge, though, and the press release is conspicuously thin. It doesn’t say what "multi-year" means, what the financial terms are, or how much money changes hands. It discloses no revenue split and says nothing about whether Getty's images may be used to train future OpenAI models.
That omission is important.
Training is not a footnote for Getty. It is the thing the company went to war over. Getty sued Stability AI on both sides of the Atlantic, alleging that roughly 12 million of its images had been scraped without permission to train an image generator. The war did not go well. In November 2025, the U.K. High Court largely sided with Stability, rejecting Getty's central copyright claim and finding only narrow liability on a trademark point.
So the contrast worth sitting with is this: When Getty cut its earlier deal with Perplexity, the arrangement explicitly did not permit training. The OpenAI release draws no such line in public. It licenses the display of pictures while staying silent on the one right Getty spent years and considerable legal fees trying to defend — and just lost in court.
A word on the stock, because the number is doing a lot of emotional work. Getty trades below a dollar, which makes triple-digit percentage moves less impressive than they sound. Around 17% of its float is sold short, with days-to-cover near 4.6 — the precise conditions under which a single piece of good news becomes a squeeze. The stock opened near $1.40, touched $2.66 intraday, and closed around $1.19, on a business still carrying heavy debt and a negative net margin against roughly $981 million in trailing revenue. What re-rated this week was the story, not yet the company.
And the story is a good one, to be fair to Getty. A deal that turns the largest AI chatbot into a distribution channel for licensed pictures — rather than a competitor that renders them free and obsolete — is a way of being inside the disruption instead of underneath it. 
It fits a pattern: Getty has spent recent weeks locking in AI, sports and entertainment at once, renewing a multi-year U.S. Soccer deal into the 2026 World Cup and re-upping with the Tribeca Festival. The repositioning is coherent. Getty can become the licensed-provenance layer that AI platforms reach for when a synthetic image won't do.
Which brings me back to the word that wasn't there, and to the 600,000 of us whose work is the actual inventory in this deal.
For a contributor, "display" is the comforting half of the sentence. It implies your photograph gets surfaced, perhaps credited, perhaps even routed back to a sale. "Train" is the half left blank — and it is the half that determines whether your images remain assets you license or become raw material a model has already digested. Getty has, for now, sold the visible right and said nothing about the invisible one.
That may be shrewd negotiation. It may be a placeholder while lawyers argue. But the company is celebrating a truce on the battlefield where it just lost, and declining to say what it conceded to get here.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- On **2026-06-21**, Getty Images announced a **multi-year "display agreement"** with OpenAI: Getty's licensed creative/editorial imagery surfaces inside **ChatGPT search & discovery**. When a query calls for a real-world image, ChatGPT may return a Getty photo (attributed, identifiable) instead of a synthetic DALL·E approximation or an unattributed scrape.
- **Display, not training.** This is the load-bearing distinction. Two readings exist and they conflict:
  - Getty's press release + secondary coverage (Engadget, digitalapplied, PPC Land) state OpenAI is **not** permitted to use the images to **train** models / DALL·E — i.e. training is *excluded at signing*.
  - The StrictlyVC/Forbes contributor piece (our primary in-note source) reads the release as **silent** on training — "it licenses the display of pictures while staying silent on the one right Getty spent years trying to defend." It treats the absence of an explicit "no-train" clause as the tell.
  - **Reconciliation (analysis):** Most likely the release frames it as a display deal and Getty stated separately that DALL·E training is *not* included — but the binding contract terms (and whether training could be *added later*) are undisclosed. The skeptical Forbes framing is about durability, not a factual contradiction: training is excluded *now*, but the door isn't publicly bolted shut.
- **Undisclosed:** dollar value, what "multi-year" means, **revenue split**, indemnification scope, how much (if any) reaches Getty's ~**600,000 contributors**, and rollout timing. **Treat as announced, not confirmed-live.**
- **Why structured this way:** A display-only license lets Getty monetize AI *distribution* without conceding the training right it just litigated over — and lets OpenAI buy provenance/legitimacy cheaply (display rights are far cheaper than a training corpus license). The vagueness on contributor pay and revenue share is the PR tell: the visible right is sold; the invisible (training) and the economics are left blank.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Getty–Perplexity (2025-10-31):** the direct precedent — a multi-year, display-only image license, *explicitly* no-training (because Perplexity doesn't train its own models). Not a lump-sum deal; centered on attribution. The OpenAI deal is the second, larger node in the same playbook — but with OpenAI, training is a *live* question because OpenAI *does* train (DALL·E), which is exactly why the omission/silence matters more here than with Perplexity.
- **OpenAI's publisher-licensing track (web):** News Corp (~$250M / 5yr), Axel Springer (tens of M€ / 3yr), Financial Times ($5–10M/yr), AP, The Atlantic, Vox, Le Monde, Dotdash Meredith. Those are mostly **text + training** licenses with attribution. The Getty deal is a *visual, display-only* variant — a different SKU.
- **OpenAI's commerce/integration track (internal corpus):** [[OpenAI to take cut of ChatGPT shopping sales]], [[Stripe helps OpenAI launch Instant Checkout in ChatGPT]], [[Intuit signs $100M OpenAI deal for ChatGPT integration]], [[CommBank and OpenAI form Australia-first AI partnership]], [[PhonePe partners with OpenAI for AI features]]. Pattern: ChatGPT is being built into a **distribution + transaction layer**; Getty is the imagery plug-in to that same surface.
- **Position:** Getty is *catching up to its own Perplexity template* but landing the marquee name (OpenAI). Ahead of rival stock libraries (Shutterstock, Adobe Stock) in striking an OpenAI *display* tie-up; parity-to-behind on the training-economics question, which remains unresolved industry-wide.
- **Why the lay of the land (analysis):** After the UK loss (below), provenance-licensing is the only defensible business model left for stock libraries vs. generative AI — so the race is to become the "licensed-provenance layer" AI platforms reach for. First-mover on the *name* (OpenAI) matters more than the *terms* for the re-rating.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **The war Getty lost:** Getty sued Stability AI (US + UK), alleging ~12M images scraped to train Stable Diffusion. On **2025-11-04** the UK High Court (*Getty Images v Stability AI* [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch)) **largely sided with Stability** — Getty dropped its primary copyright/database claims (no UK training evidence), lost the secondary-copyright claim (model weights ≠ a "copy"), and won only narrow **trademark** points (watermark generation). A first-of-its-kind ruling: if training happens outside the UK, UK courts may not reach it.
- **Pivot:** Having lost the "stop them training" fight, Getty pivoted to "**license the display**." Recent weeks: renewed multi-year **U.S. Soccer** deal through the 2026 World Cup, re-upped **Tribeca Festival**, plus Perplexity then OpenAI. Coherent repositioning as the **licensed-provenance layer**.
- **Why (analysis):** Structural pressure — a stock-photo business is being commoditized by free synthetic images and has heavy debt + negative net margin on ~$981M trailing revenue. It *cannot* win on "make AI stop"; so it monetizes being *inside* the disruption (a distribution channel) rather than *under* it. Display licensing is the only multiple-bearing story it has left.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new:** OpenAI putting *licensed, attributed* stock photos into ChatGPT answers — provenance-as-a-feature inside the largest chatbot. For Getty, the marquee counterparty validates the post-litigation strategy.
- **What's NOT new / not yet proven:** The *mechanism* is a copy of the Perplexity deal (Oct 2025). **Zero disclosed traction:** no revenue figure, no per-use rate, no contributor-pay mechanism, no live-rollout confirmation, no usage metrics. The 90–200% stock pop is **story, not company** — Getty trades sub-$1, ~17% of float short, days-to-cover ~4.6: textbook **short-squeeze** conditions (opened ~$1.40, touched $2.66, closed ~$1.19, then back to ~$0.97 by 2026-06-24). The move re-rated the narrative, not the P&L.
- **Who captures the margin (analysis):** OpenAI captures the *user surface* and pays a (likely modest) display fee; Getty captures a *distribution channel* but on undisclosed and possibly thin economics; **contributors** capture the residual — and the deal says nothing about their cut. The durable value to Getty depends on (a) whether display licensing is a recurring, scalable line vs. a one-off, and (b) whether the *training* right (the bigger prize) ever gets licensed. As structured, this is the *small* right monetized while the *big* one sits unresolved.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Watch:** any disclosure of financial terms / revenue share; whether training rights get added in a later phase (the real value); rollout confirmation inside ChatGPT (announced ≠ live); contributor compensation policy; whether Shutterstock/Adobe Stock follow with their own OpenAI deals.
- **Sentiment:** Euphoric-then-skeptical. The squeeze unwound within days (−13% by 2026-06-24, back to ~$0.97). Analysts/commentators frame it as a credible *strategic* pivot but warn the economics are unproven and the balance sheet (debt ~3.7x equity, negative net margin) is unchanged.
- **Why the market goes this way (analysis):** The bull case is "licensed-provenance layer for AI" — a recurring software-like story that could re-rate a commodity content business. The counterintuitive second-order risk: by *legitimizing* AI display channels without securing training economics, Getty may be training the market to expect *cheap* display licenses while the more valuable training right erodes via court losses (UK precedent) — i.e. the deal that lifts the stock could cap the long-run pricing power. The central question is not "does Getty have an AI story" (it does) but **"can it monetize the training right, or only the cheap display right — and does it capture enough to outrun its debt and the commoditization of stock imagery?"**

**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: AI content-licensing / stock-media at the edge of agentic-content economics. Two distinct markets meet here.
