Meta left potentially sensitive information collected from employee laptops accessible to anyone inside the company, according to an internal security notice seen by WIRED and three current employees familiar with the issue.
The data, which was collected as part of a divisive initiative to train artificial intelligence models , is believed to include keystrokes, mouseclicks, and content displayed on the computer screens of Meta’s US employees.
Meta spokesperson Tracy Clayton initially confirmed to WIRED that the company is investigating the security issue. As this story was being published, he added that Meta is pausing the data collection program indefinitely. "We have carefully designed this program with privacy safeguards and while we have no indication at this time that any data was improperly accessed by Meta employees, we're pausing it while we investigate," Clayton says.
The security notice sent out Monday indicated that “employee data across 45,000 hive tables,” had been exposed. Those tables included employee activity such as “full prompts and transcriptions, private conversations, people and performance data,” according to documents viewed by WIRED.
Some employees at Meta quickly seized on the security failure, saying in internal forums that it validated concerns they had raised when the company began tracking workers’ corporate laptops in April as part of a program known as the Model Capability Initiative.
Comments about the incident posted on internal forums Monday included questions about how Meta’s privacy reviews failed to prevent the breach, and whether everyone whose data was potentially exposed will be allowed to attend a meeting going over what went wrong, according to posts seen by WIRED.
In one internal forum where staffers are known to trade jokes, an employee posted a meme from The Office of the character Jim Halpert holding a sign that reads, “0 days since our last nonsense.”
Sources at Meta, who were not authorized to speak publicly, tell WIRED the incident has now been marked as closed, meaning it was likely resolved.
In an internal post responding to employees’ questions on Monday seen by WIRED, Andrew Bosworth, Meta’s chief technology officer, said that the tracking program’s implementation had fallen short of the standards outlined in its privacy review and that findings from the incident would be shared. “Here we had misconfigured ACLs [access control lists] and we need to understand how that happened, track down every data access and understand it,” Bosworth wrote.
A couple of months ago, Bosworth told employees concerned about potential data leaks that the tracking program is “tightly controlled” and uses the same protection standards, storage systems, and access controls as other sensitive datasets, according to internal posts seen by WIRED.
Last month, more than 1,600 employees at the tech giant signed an internal petition protesting the laptop surveillance effort, warning that “collecting this data introduces both security and regulatory risks for Meta, including the potential for breaches and unauthorized disclosure.” The petitioners also expressed concerns with what they viewed as a lack of safeguards that Meta had put in place. One engineer also wrote a widely shared internal note saying having their laptop screen scraped for training data without their consent felt like an invasion of privacy and amounted to exploitation.
Meta executives have previously defended the data-gathering project, saying it was necessary to train AI systems to use computer software the way humans do. In audio of a company meeting leaked last month, Mark Zuckerberg, Meta’s CEO, told employees that “AI models learn from watching really smart people do things,” and the “average intelligence of the people who are at this company is significantly higher” than the average contractor who could be hired specifically to produce this kind of data.
But after widespread protest from employees, Meta this month began offering more exemptions to the monitoring, including letting staffers briefly turn off the surveillance so they could complete sensitive tasks, such as scheduling a personal appointment, according to two people familiar with the matter. Some employees are still demanding that the tracking be stopped altogether.
Meta faces more regulatory scrutiny about data security than most companies. It is subject to a US Federal Trade Commission consent decree that expires in 2040 requiring it maintain processes to avoid breaches. But current and former employees have told WIRED that the requirements are inadequate and outdated. Meta also has also begun offloading some work reviewing programs and features for potential privacy and security risks to artificial intelligence. It wasn't immediately clear whether AI played a role in the access control issue with the MCI data.
The security incident will likely contribute to the ongoing morale crisis at Meta , where employees have been frustrated by the past few years of mass layoffs, a turbulent reorganization, and an all-out push to develop AI models and features. In March, Meta created a new Applied AI team and moved some 6,500 employees into new roles focused on improving AI models. Some Meta staffers have described the projects they have been assigned as menial and “ soul-crushing .”
Bosworth sent out a memo to employees last week apologizing for the company’s “atrocious” communication about the AI reorganization and promising improvements, including clearer communication and the return of some office perks.

Klue hack results in data breach at several cybersecurity firms
A hacking group has taken credit for a breach at market intelligence provider Klue that allowed hackers to steal reams of data from the company’s corporate customers, which include some of the biggest names in cybersecurity.
Vancouver-based Klue, which lets companies conduct market research by connecting their data to its systems, said on Friday that hackers had stolen data from an unspecified number of its customers during a cyberattack a week earlier. (The blog contains the “noindex” code , which tells search engines to not list the page in search results.)
Cybercrime group Icarus took credit for the breach, saying on its leak site that it will publish the stolen data on Monday if the company does not pay the hackers’ ransom.
Klue has not said how many of its hundreds of customers are affected. Several companies have come forward to confirm they had data stolen during the attack, including Gong , Jamf , HackerOne , Insurity , OneTrust , Recorded Future , Snyk , Sprout Social , and Tanium .
This is the latest of a slew of broad-scale hacks in which hackers target companies that hold the keys to other companies’ cloud databases. By breaching firms like Klue, hackers are betting that compromising a single point-of-failure will let them steal data from a large number of organizations at once. Over the past year alone, hackers have increasingly targeted similar middleware providers, including Gainsight and Salesloft , to gain access to hundreds of companies’ data.
Klue said hackers had gained access to the company’s systems on June 12 using a “compromised legacy credential,” such as a password or a token, associated with an integration tool that allows customers to link their company’s cloud data to their Klue accounts. 
The hackers were able to steal data from Klue’s customer clouds, such as Salesforce databases. Companies often store their customers’ personal information in Salesforce databases, making these a prime target .
Much of the stolen data includes business contact information, like names, email addresses, phone numbers, job titles, and some account information of their customers, according to the various affected companies.
It’s not clear how the hackers acquired the compromised credentials, or why Klue did not detect the theft sooner. Similar recent mass-hacks involving the compromise and misuse of credentials, such as at Snowflake and TanStack , have been linked to employees inadvertently installing password-stealing malware on the devices that they use for work. 
Klue said it has called in incident response firm CrowdStrike, and has disconnected its integrations to prevent further access to customers’ data.
When contacted by TechCrunch on Monday, Klue CEO Jason Smith did not immediately respond to a request for comment, or answer questions about the incident, including if the company has received any communication from the hackers, such as a ransom demand.
Huntress, one of the security companies that had its data stolen in the hack, said in its write-up of the incident that the hackers had contacted it with a ransom note using an Australian company’s email address, whose servers were likely misused for the campaign.
Last June, Klue said it was preparing to lay off around half of its staff , around 100 people, as it doubled down on its AI investments. It’s not clear if the reduction in staff led to lapses in security at the company. It’s not clear who, beyond Smith, is responsible for cybersecurity at the company.
Klue does not currently list a person overseeing cybersecurity on its executive leadership page .

# Context-enrichment: Klue breach exposes data at multiple cybersecurity firms
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

> NOTE ON SCOPE: This note aggregates two TechCrunch items. The de-PR'd story is the **Klue supply-chain breach** (June 2026). The StrictlyVC "Meta laptop-tracking" blurb captured in the aggregated text is a *different, unrelated* incident that the digest mis-bundled — IGNORE it for this enrichment. The ranker merged a LastPass-specific candidate ([[LastPass says hackers stole support data in Klue breach]], story seb1c42fd) into this one, so LastPass exposure is treated here as part of the same event.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **The event:** Hackers breached **Klue** (Vancouver-based competitive-intelligence / market-research SaaS), then used that access to steal data belonging to Klue's *corporate customers* — many of them large cybersecurity firms. This is a **third-party / supply-chain breach**, not a breach of the victims' own infrastructure.
- **Mechanism (concrete, corroborated by multiple vendors):** Attackers got in on **June 11–12, 2026** using a **compromised legacy credential** — reportedly a credential dating to **2022** (per TechCrunch follow-up) tied to an integration service account. They then pushed a **malicious code update inside Klue to harvest OAuth tokens**, and used those long-lived tokens to authenticate to customers' connected **Salesforce** (and Gong) instances, running **automated Python scripts against the Salesforce REST API for ~24 hours** to bulk-exfiltrate data. OAuth tokens **bypass MFA** because they are pre-authorized non-human identities — this is the crux of why the blast radius is so wide.
- **Data stolen:** Primarily **business contact data** in customer CRM/support records — names, emails, phone numbers, physical addresses, job titles, some account info, and (for LastPass specifically) **customer support case contents**. No vendor has reported theft of product secrets, source code, or — critically for LastPass — **password vaults** (LastPass states vaults, products and infrastructure were untouched).
- **Affected (confirmed, as of late June 2026):** Gong, Jamf, HackerOne, Insurity, OneTrust, Recorded Future, Snyk, Sprout Social, Tanium, Huntress, **LastPass**, and **BeyondTrust** (per SecurityWeek). Klue says it has "hundreds" of customers and has not disclosed the full count affected.
- **Attribution & extortion:** Self-styled group **"Icarus"** claimed the breach on its leak site (~June 19), threatening to publish unless paid. Initially mis-attributed to **ShinyHunters / UNC6395** (a Telegram account claimed it June 21). Klue called in **CrowdStrike** for IR and **disconnected its integrations**; **Salesforce disabled the Klue app integration** on its side.
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The victims are mostly security vendors precisely because Klue is a competitive-intelligence tool that security vendors use to track rivals — i.e., the customer base self-selects for high-value, embarrassing targets. The use of a **2022 credential** that was never rotated, plus the absence of a named CISO on Klue's leadership page, points to **degraded security posture** — plausibly (hypothesis) linked to Klue's **June 2025 layoff of ~40–50% of staff** (~85–100 people) in an "AI-first" restructuring. The press framing ("several cybersecurity firms breached") is technically accurate but the *de-PR'd* point is that **none of them were breached directly** — the single point of failure was a small SaaS vendor with broad Salesforce read access.

## [1] Competitors / peers (the breach pattern, not Klue's product rivals)
This is the latest in a **2025–26 wave of OAuth-token / SaaS-integration supply-chain heists** against Salesforce ecosystems:
- **Salesloft / Drift (Aug 2025, UNC6395):** Stole OAuth refresh tokens from the Drift–Salesforce integration, queried Salesforce across **hundreds** of orgs. This is the **closest analog** and the template Icarus copied.
- **Gainsight (2025):** Same class — integration compromise → Salesforce data theft (cited by TechCrunch as a parallel).
- **Snowflake (2024):** Mass data theft via stolen/credential-stuffed credentials (no MFA), 100+ orgs.
- **Within the corpus:** [[UBS data breach exposes employee details via vendor Chain IQ]] (June 2025, supplier Chain IQ → UBS/Pictet), [[Wealthsimple discloses third-party-linked customer data breach]] (Sept 2025, compromised software package), [[Figure data breach exposes nearly 1 million accounts]] (Feb 2026, ShinyHunters Salesforce/SaaS theft) — all the same structural story: **the perimeter is now your vendors' vendors.**
- **Position of this item:** Not novel in *technique* (it is a near-replay of Salesloft/Drift), but notable for the **caliber of victims** (HackerOne, Recorded Future, Tanium, LastPass, BeyondTrust are themselves security leaders) and for the **"hackers get hacked" twist** (see [4]).
- **Why the landscape is this way (2nd order):** SaaS integrations are **non-human identities with persistent, broad, MFA-exempt access** that orgs monitor far less than employee logins. As CRM/CI tooling proliferates, every integration becomes a lateral pathway. The margin/leverage for attackers is enormous: breach one mid-size vendor, harvest hundreds of Salesforce orgs. The defensive gap is **token lifecycle + least-privilege scoping**, which most vendors under-invest in.

## [2] Company history / fit (Klue)
- **Klue:** Founded **2015**, Vancouver; CEO/co-founder **Jason Smith**; raised **~US$80M** (incl. Tiger Global); customers include Autodesk and Salesforce. Sells **AI-powered competitive-intelligence "battlecards"** to sales teams, connecting to customers' data (Salesforce, Gong).
- **June 2025:** Laid off **~40–50% of staff (~85–100 people)** in an "AI-first" restructuring, targeting Q4-2025 profitability; CEO framed it as efficiency, *not* financial distress.
- **Fit / why this happened here:** A CI vendor's value prop *requires* deep read access to customers' CRM/revenue data — so it inherently aggregates sensitive data while being a far smaller, less-hardened target than its blue-chip customers. The **unrotated 2022 credential + no public CISO + a major headcount cut a year prior** is a coherent (if circumstantial) picture of **security debt under cost pressure**. (Hypothesis — Klue has not confirmed a link between layoffs and the lapse; TechCrunch explicitly flags this as unproven.)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new:** Little in *mechanism* — OAuth-token harvesting against Salesforce is a 2025 playbook. The **new/notable elements** are (a) a fresh threat brand, **Icarus**, and (b) the **meta-twist**: Icarus later told Klue it is **deleting** the stolen data, but a **second hacker group obtained the dataset** (reportedly by exploiting an Icarus operator mistake) and is now running its own extortion against victims. "Hackers get hacked."
- **Traction / reality check (this is a breach, so "traction" = confirmed impact, not adoption):** Impact is **confirmed by the victims themselves** (LastPass, Huntress, HackerOne, etc. published write-ups) — this is *real*, not claimed. But the **damage is bounded**: stolen data is overwhelmingly **business contact info + support-case text**, not credentials/secrets/vaults. The headline risk for LastPass (a password manager) is reputational, not vault compromise — and LastPass explicitly says vaults are safe.
- **Where the margin/value is (for attackers, 2nd order):** The durable value of this data is **phishing/social-engineering fuel** — verified contacts at security orgs plus support-case context enable convincing follow-on attacks. That is the real second-order risk, more than the raw PII.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Immediate:** Affected vendors notifying customers; Salesforce has disabled the Klue integration; CrowdStrike running Klue's IR. Watch for (a) the **second group's leak/extortion** outcome, (b) whether **more victims** disclose (SecurityWeek/Register both note "hundreds" potentially affected), and (c) any **regulatory** angle for the security vendors holding EU/customer PII.
- **Sentiment / structural read:** Reinforces the dominant 2025–26 security narrative — **identity & non-human credentials / SaaS supply chain are the breach frontier** (cf. corpus [[Verizon DBIR Stolen credentials no longer top breach vector]], May 2026: vuln-exploitation overtook stolen creds, but token/identity abuse remains central). Expect renewed pressure on **OAuth token expiry, integration least-privilege, and third-party SaaS monitoring**, and likely Salesforce/vendor moves to constrain long-lived tokens.
- **Counterintuitive 2nd order:** The most "secure" companies (security vendors) were hit not through their own defenses but through a **shared, weakly-governed SaaS dependency** — concentration of CRM data in Salesforce + a long tail of integration apps makes the *ecosystem* fragile even when each principal is well-defended. The fragility is structural, not a failing of any one victim.

The narrower **SaaS Security Posture Management (SSPM)** segment is small but the fastest-growing layer — ~$0.48bn (2025) → ~$3.5bn by 2030 at ~48.7% CAGR per Frost (other estimates ~$2.0–2.6bn 2025, ~16–25% CAGR — figures diverge widely, treat as directional, not precise). *Structure:* the breach surface is **fragmented and ungoverned** — non-human identities (service accounts, API keys, OAuth tokens) outnumber humans ~45:1 in large enterprises (per industry estimates cited via grandviewresearch/marketsandmarkets), yet are monitored far less than human logins; the *value chain* concentrates in **Salesforce as the shared CRM datastore** + a long tail of integration apps with persistent, broad, MFA-exempt read scopes. *Why now:* AI-era integration sprawl (CI tools like Klue, revenue-intelligence like Gong) multiplies pre-authorized tokens; attackers have industrialized the playbook (Salesloft/Drift, Gainsight) into a repeatable "breach one vendor, harvest hundreds of orgs" model. Second-order: the perimeter has migrated from your network to **your vendors' OAuth scopes** — a place few buyers have tooling or budget for.

**Competitive landscape.** This is a breach, not a financing — sector KPIs are **blast radius** (orgs affected), **dwell/exfil time**, and **data class stolen** (contact PII vs credentials/secrets). *The breach-pattern peers* (not Klue's product rivals): **Salesloft/Drift (Aug 2025, UNC6395)** — the direct template: stolen Drift OAuth tokens → **700+ Salesforce orgs** queried over ~10 days (Cloudflare, Google, PagerDuty, Palo Alto, Zscaler among victims; Salesforce removed Drift from AppExchange, tokens revoked 2025-08-20; Mandiant IR closed 2025-09-30, per Google Cloud/Unit42). **Gainsight (late 2025)** — same class. **Snowflake (2024)** — credential-stuffing, 100+ orgs. *Recent moves:* the defensive market is responding — Obsidian Security launched an end-to-end SaaS supply-chain protection platform (Jan 2026); in-corpus, [[Socket raises $60 million Series C at $1 billion valuation]] (2026-05) and [[RapidFort raises $42 million Series A for software supply chain security]] (2026-02) show capital flowing to supply-chain security. *Klue's position:* a **niche** CI vendor (battlecards for sales teams), *not* a security player — its relevance here is purely as the **weak link / single point of failure**, the small under-hardened vendor with broad Salesforce read access. No moat protected it; the absence of a named CISO + an unrotated 2022 credential is the opposite of a moat. The *attackers'* moat is leverage: one mid-size SaaS breach yields hundreds of Salesforce orgs.

No valuation comp is computable here. *Internal comps (corpus, same supply-chain-breach structure):* [[UBS data breach exposes employee details via vendor Chain IQ]] (2025-06, supplier→bank), [[Wealthsimple discloses third-party-linked customer data breach]] (2025-09, compromised package), [[Figure data breach exposes nearly 1 million accounts]] (2026-02, ShinyHunters/UNC6661 SaaS theft), [[Verizon DBIR Stolen credentials no longer top breach vector]] (2026-05, vuln-exploitation now #1 entry vector). *Cost benchmark (not a valuation multiple):* global avg breach cost fell ~9% to **$4.44M** (2025), but **US avg hit a record $10.22M** (per IBM Cost of a Data Breach 2025, via ibm.com) — relevant because most victims here are US security firms holding customer PII. *Insurance signal:* cyber premiums fell ~6% in 2025 (first decrease since 2020), **but claim denials rose to ~21% (2025) from ~15% (2023)** (per medhacloud.com cyber-insurance stats 2026) — softening prices, tightening payouts. **Risk flags.**
1. **Systemic SaaS-token risk (the core flag).** OAuth tokens are pre-authorized non-human identities that **bypass MFA**; with integration sprawl, every connected app is a latent lateral path. *Why it matters:* this is the **fourth+ replay** (Snowflake→Drift→Gainsight→Klue) of the same pattern — it is structural and recurring, not a one-off, so more disclosures (Klue has "hundreds" of customers; current ~12 victims is a floor) and more copycat breaches are the base case.
2. **Reputational asymmetry for security-brand victims.** HackerOne, Snyk, LastPass, BeyondTrust, Tanium are sold *on* security competence; being named in a breach headline — even via a third party they didn't control — erodes the exact trust they monetize. *Second-order:* the durable damage is **targeted phishing/social-engineering fuel** (verified contacts + support-case context at security orgs), which can seed follow-on, higher-severity intrusions — a larger long-tail risk than the raw contact PII.
3. **Regulatory disclosure / notification exposure (open).** Victims holding EU/consumer PII may face breach-notification duties; with US breach economics now averaging **$10.22M** and **~21% of cyber claims denied**, the cost is real even when data is "only" contact info — and insurance recovery is no longer assured. No enforcement reported as of late June 2026.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is a **re-rating catalyst for the SaaS-security / non-human-identity layer**, not a fintech repricing — it hardens the 2025–26 thesis that **identity & token governance is the breach frontier**, favoring SSPM/NHI vendors (cf. Socket, RapidFort, Obsidian) and pressuring Salesforce + the integration ecosystem to constrain long-lived tokens (expect token-expiry/least-privilege moves). *Falsifiable thesis:* if no enforced OAuth-token lifetime limits or default least-privilege scoping emerge from Salesforce/major SaaS platforms within ~6–12 months, expect at least one more headline replay of this exact pattern. *What breaks it:* a platform-level fix (mandatory short-lived tokens + scoped grants) would blunt the attack class and the SSPM tailwind. Watch: the second hacker group's extortion outcome, additional victim disclosures, and any Salesforce token-policy change.
