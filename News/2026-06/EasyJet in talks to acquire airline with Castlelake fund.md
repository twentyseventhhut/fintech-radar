[Mergers.ru] ✈️ EasyJet ведет переговоры о покупке авиакомпании инвестфондом Castlelake. Авиакомпания сообщила, что отклонила очередное предложение фонда, но решила начать с ним переговоры. В свою очередь, руководство EasyJet постоянно говорит, что предложения Castlelake оценивают ее гораздо ниже «реальной стоимости».

# Context-enrichment: Castlelake's takeover bid for EasyJet (talks reopened)
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

> NOTE ON THE HEADLINE: The note's title — "EasyJet in talks to acquire airline with Castlelake fund" — is a **mistranslation of the Russian Mergers.ru source and is factually backwards**. EasyJet is NOT the acquirer. The reality (confirmed by Bloomberg, RTE, US News, Aerotime, Simple Flying): **Castlelake, a Minneapolis aviation-focused alternative-investment / asset-based private-credit firm (~$38bn AUM), is trying to BUY EasyJet.** EasyJet is the *target*. The note's own aggregated Russian text is actually correct ("Castlelake ведет переговоры о покупке авиакомпании" = Castlelake in talks to buy the airline); only the English title got inverted. The enrichment below uses the corrected reality.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- On **23 Jun 2026** Castlelake submitted its **fourth** proposal: **650p/share in cash**, valuing EasyJet at **£4.93bn (~$6.4–6.5bn)**. EasyJet's board **unanimously rejected** it (announced 25 Jun 2026) as continuing to "fundamentally undervalue" the company.
- BUT — the genuinely new development vs prior bids — EasyJet agreed to **grant Castlelake limited access to confidential commercial/diligence data**, explicitly to try to elicit a higher ("more attractive") firm offer. This is the "talks" the headline refers to: a rejection-with-an-open-door, not a friendly deal.
- The UK Takeover Code "**put-up-or-shut-up**" deadline was **extended to 5pm BST, Sunday 5 July 2026** for Castlelake to make a firm offer or walk away (and be barred ~6 months).
- Bid escalation ladder: 560p (16 Jun) → 600p (20 Jun) → **625p public bid, ~£4.7bn (22 Jun)** → 650p (23 Jun). Four bids in ~one week. (analysis) The public disclosure of the 625p bid was a classic **bear-hug**: going over the board's head to pressure shareholders.
- New structural feature in the 4th bid: a **partial alternative** letting EasyJet shareholders elect **unlisted, non-transferable, non-voting shares** in the bid vehicle instead of cash — i.e. roll over rather than fully cash out.

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The 4th bid's headline novelty is the **ownership structure**, dictated by EU airline-ownership law (an airline must be majority owned/controlled by EU nationals). The vehicle is **~49% Castlelake + co-investors (including Brookfield Asset Management)** and **~51% held by EU nationals — ex-EasyJet/Ryanair executive Peter Bellew and aviation investor Mark Breen**. So the financial sponsors deliberately hold a *minority* economic-control position to clear the regulatory bar — which is exactly the weak point EasyJet's board attacks: "concern about the ownership structure and **deliverability**" and the time to satisfy conditions, which has a "meaningful impact on the **present value** of the offer price." Translation: even at 650p, if the deal takes 12-18 months and might fail on regulatory grounds, the risk-adjusted value to a shareholder is lower than the headline.

## [1] Competitors / peers (deal context)
- **Castlelake's core competency is aviation finance** (aircraft leasing/ABS, asset-based private credit) — so buying a whole carrier is an adjacency, not a random LBO. This is a **financial-sponsor take-private of a listed airline**, a relatively rare move post-COVID.
- Peer M&A in the corpus: [[Volaris and Viva Aerobus announce merger of Mexican airlines]] (Dec 2025) — but that is a *strategic* airline-airline merger of equals, not a PE take-private; different logic. [[Advent International completes tbi bank acquisition]] (Mar 2026) is a comparable PE take-private of a financial asset.
- Other take-private precedents (external/analysis): the partial cash-or-rollover-shares alternative echoes how PE structures listed-company buyouts to retain anchor holders. The 49/51 EU-national front structure mirrors how non-EU money historically enters EU airlines (e.g. ownership work-arounds at Norwegian, Air Europa, etc.) — a known but fragile template.
- Position: Castlelake is **persistent but rebuffed**; EasyJet board has parity-or-better leverage because the share price (the bidder's lever) is depressed by exogenous factors (Middle East conflict, jet-fuel prices), which the board argues is temporary and "opportunistic."

## [2] Company / fit
- **EasyJet:** FTSE-listed UK low-cost carrier; board's whole defence is that the bid exploits a cyclically depressed share price. Founder/easyGroup (Stelios Haji-Ioannou) is a large, historically activist shareholder — his stance is pivotal and not yet (in this note) confirmed. Corpus note [[EasyJet founder adds crypto trading with EasyBitcoin launch]] (Sep 2025) is the **easyGroup** brand-licensing entity, *not* the airline — relevant only as a reminder the founder runs a separate empire and retains a brand-licence relationship with the airline (a complication for any acquirer).
- **Castlelake:** Founded 2005, Minneapolis; aviation + specialty/asset-based credit; ~$38bn AUM. Corpus shows Castlelake active in UK credit: [[LendInvest forms £250m funding partnership with Castlelake]] / [[LendInvest secures GBP250M from Castlelake for bridging loans]] (Jan-Feb 2026) — i.e. Castlelake is **actively deploying capital into UK asset-based situations**, consistent with a large UK aviation bet.

**Why Castlelake acts this way:** (analysis) A firm whose edge is *valuing aviation assets and ABS cash flows* sees a listed LCC trading below the replacement/break-up value of its fleet, slots, and brand as an asset-based arbitrage — buy the cash-flowing asset cheap while equity markets penalise short-term fuel/geopolitical noise. The repeated rapid bid bumps suggest conviction that EasyJet is mispriced, not a fishing expedition.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What is genuinely new in THIS note** (vs the 4 prior bids): (a) EasyJet **opening its books** despite rejecting — a "talks → diligence" escalation; (b) the **July 5 deadline extension**; (c) the **non-voting-share rollover alternative**; (d) the **explicit 49/51 EU-control structure with Brookfield + Bellew/Breen** named. These are real new terms, not a reprint.
- **Traction = none yet on the deal**: no firm offer, no agreement, no shareholder vote. It is "rejected + diligence access," firmly in the *announced/negotiating* phase, not "live."
- **Where the margin/value sits:** (analysis) The value-add for Castlelake is asset-based (fleet/slots/brand) + financing structuring; for EasyJet shareholders the open question is whether the board extracts a fifth, higher bid before July 5, or whether the regulatory-deliverability risk makes a nominally higher price worth *less* in present-value terms. The board has reframed the central question from "is 650p enough?" to "can this structure even close, and when?" — a deliberate value-discount argument.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Catalyst date: 5 July 2026** (firm offer or walk-away). A **fifth bid, likely >650p, is widely expected** before then.
- Market reaction: EasyJet shares **rose ~5%** on the news (board rejection + diligence access read as "higher bid coming"). So the market prices in **deal-likely / price-up**, siding partly with the board's "worth more" thesis.
- Risks/second-order: (1) **Regulatory deliverability** of the 49/51 EU-control structure is the real binary risk — a higher cash price doesn't fix a structure that may not clear EU/UK ownership-and-control rules. (2) The bid leans on a **temporarily depressed price** (Middle East conflict, fuel) — if those reverse, the board's hand strengthens and Castlelake may walk. (3) Founder/easyGroup brand-licence and Stelios's stake are an **unmodelled wildcard**. (4) Counterintuitive: opening the books can *raise* the bid but also hands an aviation specialist EasyJet's commercial data even if the deal collapses — an information cost.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a financial-sponsor take-private of a listed European LCC, sitting at the intersection of **aviation finance / asset-based private credit** and public-airline M&A. Castlelake is an aviation-focused alternative-investment firm with **~$38bn AUM** (per Castlelake corporate site / Bloomberg, as of 2026) whose core business is aircraft leasing, secured aviation lending and aircraft ABS — it issued three CLAS aircraft ABS deals in 2025 totalling ~$2.3bn (CLAS 2025-1 $819.75m, 2025-2 $719.6m, 2025-3 $758.56m; per Ishka/PRNewswire, 2025) and launched a $1.8bn aviation lending platform in Aug 2025 (Bloomberg). Structure: airline ownership is **regulation-gated** — EU Reg 1008/2008 requires >50% ownership *and* effective control by EU nationals (≥50%+1 share; per LSE/Lexology), the single hardest entry barrier here. "Why now" (analysis): a specialist that prices fleets/slots/ABS cash flows sees a UK LCC trading below break-up/replacement value while equity markets discount short-term fuel + Middle-East geopolitical noise — asset-based arbitrage on a mispriced equity, financed off Castlelake's own credit machinery rather than a syndicated LBO loan.

**Competitive landscape.** Airline KPIs the comp runs on: **EV/EBITDA, P/E, RASK/CASK (unit revenue vs unit cost), net cash/debt, load factor**. Listed European LCC peer set: **Ryanair, Wizz Air, Jet2, Lufthansa/Eurowings**. Basis of competition is unit cost: in recent quarters **Ryanair was the only one of the three (Ryanair/easyJet/Wizz) to post a profitable period and improve unit revenue; easyJet's RASK was flat and it ran a loss**, with easyJet positioned as a hybrid (primary airports, more business mix) vs the ultra-low-cost Ryanair/Wizz (per enginecowl/revman comparisons, Q4-2025/1H-2026). EasyJet 1H-2026: revenue **£3.95bn (+12% y/y)**, net loss **£377m** (seasonal H1 loss is normal for the model) (per Simply Wall St / Yahoo Finance). On the bid side Castlelake is **persistent but rebuffed** (4 bids in ~1 week, 16-23 Jun) with no strategic white-knight visible; AirInsight flags MSC as a name in the rumour mix but nothing firm. Protagonist (EasyJet) position: **catching up** operationally vs Ryanair; its negotiating leverage is the board's "cyclically depressed price / opportunistic" framing, which the ~5% share pop on rejection partly validated.

**Comps & multiples.** EasyJet (per stockanalysis.com / Yahoo Finance, Jun 2026): market cap ~**£4.36bn**, EV ~**£3.92bn**, trailing **P/E ~10.8x**, **EV/EBITDA ~2.75-2.88x**, net **cash** ~£434m (£3.45bn cash vs £3.01bn debt), revenue TTM ~£10.53bn.
- **Bid premium arithmetic:** 650p/share = **£4.93bn** equity (per Bloomberg/RTE) vs ~£4.36bn undisturbed market cap → **≈13% premium to undisturbed equity** (£4.93/£4.36 − 1). On EV the gap is wider because the target carries net cash, so the bid effectively pays for the cash too.
- **EV/EBITDA sanity:** ~2.8x is at the *low end* of the airline sanity band — airlines are structurally low-multiple, capital-intensive, cyclical names, so 2.8x is **in-line/cheap, not an outlier**; it is the cheapness, not richness, that motivates an asset-based buyer.
- **Internal comps (corpus):** [[Advent International completes tbi bank acquisition]] (Mar 2026) — closest precedent as a PE take-private of a listed financial asset; [[Volaris and Viva Aerobus announce merger of Mexican airlines]] (Dec 2025) — airline consolidation but *strategic merger*, different logic, excluded from multiple comparison; [[LendInvest forms £250m funding partnership with Castlelake]] / [[LendInvest secures GBP250M from Castlelake for bridging loans]] (Jan-Feb 2026) — confirm Castlelake is actively deploying UK asset-based credit, consistent with a large UK aviation bet; [[JPMorgan explores new financing structures for leveraged buyouts]] (Mar 2026) — sector backdrop on novel buyout financing. No clean public PE-airline-take-private multiple in the base → peer-buyout multiple distribution **not computed; qualitative only**.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory deliverability (binary).** The 49% Castlelake+Brookfield / 51% EU-national (Bellew/Breen) front structure must clear EU Reg 1008/2008's ownership-*and*-effective-control test (per LSE/Lexology). "Effective control" looks through nominee/front structures, so a sponsor minority that still calls the shots can be challenged — a higher cash price does not cure a structure regulators may reject. Second-order: if it cannot close cleanly, the present-value of even a 650p+ bid is haircut by 12-18 months of conditionality, exactly the board's stated defence.
2. **Bid leans on a cyclically depressed price.** The thesis depends on fuel + Middle-East geopolitics being temporary; if macro reverses before 5 Jul, the board's "worth more" hand strengthens and Castlelake may walk (6-month lockout under the Takeover Code). The bidder's main lever (a weak share price) is exogenous and reversible.
3. **Information leakage from opening the books.** Granting an *aviation specialist* diligence access to commercial data is a real, rarely-priced cost if talks fail — a direct competitor-adjacent financier gains EasyJet's fleet/route/cost intelligence regardless of deal outcome.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) If it closes, this is the template for **aviation private-credit firms moving up the stack from financing aircraft to owning whole carriers** via EU-compliant front structures — a re-rating signal for cheap, net-cash, low-multiple listed airlines as asset-based buyout targets. Falsifiable thesis + trigger: **a fifth, higher firm offer (>650p) lands before the 5pm BST 5 Jul 2026 put-up-or-shut-up deadline.** What breaks it: Castlelake walks at the deadline (6-month bar), or a regulator/the easyGroup-Stelios brand-licence + founder stake blocks the control structure — either flips the read from "consolidation/take-private precedent" to "structurally undeliverable, airlines stay un-buyable by sponsors."
