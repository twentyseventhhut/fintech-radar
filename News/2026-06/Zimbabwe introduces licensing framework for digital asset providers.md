 Under new rules announced by Finance Minister Mthuli Ncube, businesses involved in buying, selling, transferring or safeguarding virtual assets must register with the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU), Zimbabwe’s anti-money laundering agency.
 

 Operators will pay an annual registration fee of $500, while unregistered activity could attract criminal penalties.
 

 The move signals a dramatic change in approach from a government that previously treated cryptocurrencies with caution.
 

 In 2018, Zimbabwe’s central bank directed financial institutions to stop facilitating cryptocurrency transactions, effectively cutting off exchanges from the banking system and forcing much of the sector into informal channels.
 

 The latest decision reflects a broader reality confronting policymakers across Africa, digital assets have continued to grow despite regulatory restrictions.
 

 Zimbabwe’s relationship with alternative financial assets is deeply rooted in its economic history.
 

 Hyperinflation wiped out savings and pensions during the late 2000s, while multiple currency reforms over the following years eroded confidence in traditional financial instruments.
 

 The country abandoned its local currency in 2009, reintroduced it a decade later, and in 2024 launched the Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) currency as part of efforts to stabilise the economy.
 

 Against that backdrop, cryptocurrencies emerged as an alternative means of preserving value, moving money across borders and accessing financial services outside conventional banking channels.
 

 Rather than attempting to suppress the sector, authorities are now seeking to bring it under formal oversight.
 

 The policy change places Zimbabwe alongside a growing list of African countries that have shifted from restrictive approaches towards regulation.
 

 Nigeria has introduced licensing frameworks for digital asset service providers, South Africa requires crypto firms to obtain financial services licences, while Mauritius has established one of the continent’s most developed virtual asset regulatory regimes.
 

 The trend reflects the rapid growth of digital assets across Africa. According to blockchain analytics firm Chainalysis , Sub-Saharan Africa remains one of the world’s fastest-growing cryptocurrency markets, driven by demand for remittances, cross-border payments, inflation hedging and access to dollar-denominated assets.
 

 For Zimbabwe, regulation could offer several benefits. Bringing cryptocurrency businesses into the formal economy may improve oversight, strengthen anti-money laundering controls and provide greater protection for consumers.
 

 It could also encourage fintech investment by reducing regulatory uncertainty for businesses operating in the sector.
 

 The move is particularly notable because Zimbabwe has spent years attempting to restore confidence in its financial system through a combination of monetary reforms, gold-backed instruments and tighter controls on foreign exchange markets.
 

 By regulating rather than resisting cryptocurrencies, authorities appear to be acknowledging that digital assets are becoming an increasingly permanent feature of modern finance.
 

 The decision also highlights a wider shift taking place across the continent. African governments are increasingly moving away from debates over whether cryptocurrencies should exist and focusing instead on how they should be regulated.
 

 For investors, fintech companies and digital asset operators, Zimbabwe’s latest policy change suggests that regulatory acceptance is gradually replacing outright resistance.
 

 For policymakers, it represents another attempt to balance financial innovation with oversight in an economy where alternative stores of value have long played an outsized role.
