Nomerra raises $2 million to tackle private markets’ looming paperwork crisis
Pre-seed round led by 14Peaks Capital to make private market operations AI-native.
Berlin, Germany – June 30, 2026: Nomerra has raised $2 million to make private market operations AI-native for the asset servicers and asset managers behind the world’s fastest-growing capital market. The round was led by 14Peaks Capital, with participation from Redstone Fintech and individuals from firms including KKR and Intapp.
The company was founded by Johannes Gebendorfer and Jakob Zacherl, who were both first employees at bunch, a tech-enabled fund administrator with over $50 million in backing. They helped scale the team to more than 100 people and expand across Europe. Both saw firsthand how AI transforms private market operations and founded Nomerra to bring that shift to the industry at large.
In their previous roles, the founders realized that everything that makes public markets efficient simply does not exist for private markets: there is no standardization, no interconnectedness, no efficient record-keeping. The same data gets manually retyped between isolated systems and spreadsheets, often multiple times for a single transaction. Meanwhile, private markets have become much more complex to operate in recent years: new investor channels, more frequent reporting, tighter regulation, semi-liquid structures, evergreens, and expansion into novel asset classes all add more operational load. The industry’s default response has been to hire more people, but the right people are getting harder to find. As private markets triple in size over the next five years, the number of qualified accountants has decreased by a third over the last decade.
Nomerra tackles this by making private market operations AI-native, starting with the scattered, high-volume work enterprise asset servicers and managers still run by hand: fund accounting, treasury and transfer agency. The work that runs in the background, but holds the entire industry together.
Nomerra connects to the systems firms are already using, including ERPs, banking platforms, email and document storage. It pulls information into a single context layer so agents can see everything a human operator would see. From there, Nomerra agents follow the firm’s own operating procedures: reading documents, extracting the right data, cross-checking it across sources, and delivering outcomes the same way a trained team member would. Users hand off work to Nomerra agents through tools they’re already in or by setting up continuously running background agents.
“Think of how telephone operators used to connect one caller to another by plugging cables into a switchboard,” said Johannes Gebendorfer, Co-Founder and CEO of Nomerra . “Today, the fact that humans once routed every phone call manually is hard to imagine. Private market operations are at the same turning point. In a few years, people will look back and wonder how any of this was ever done by hand.”
The goal is to shift people from preparing deliverables to reviewing them. Nomerra agents handle the end-to-end execution and present the output in purpose-built review interfaces with a full audit trail: what was done, why, and where the data came from. Over time, even the review layer becomes supervisory, and teams orchestrate fleets of Nomerra agents that ship entire deliverables on their own.
“Generic AI tools can only go so far in an environment this complex,” said Edoardo Ermotti, Founding General Partner of 14Peaks Capital . “Nomerra was built from the ground up for the depth private markets require. Johannes and Jakob lived this problem before they set out to solve it, and that vertical focus is already proving to be a distribution edge. We’re thrilled to put our network and experience behind them, to keep building a product this specialized and to get it into the market at scale.”
More capital than ever is expected to flow into private markets, and every manager and servicer needs to be ready to capture their share. Nomerra gives them the bandwidth to do it, letting firms scale without being bottlenecked by operations. The company will use the funding to grow its engineering team and meet surging demand for AI solutions across enterprise asset servicers and managers in Europe and the United States.

External reporting puts the product at **~a dozen unnamed design partners** (Forbes) — i.e. early pilots, **no named paying customers, no revenue, no AUA figure**. - **Fit / structural logic:** the founders lived the "manual retyping between isolated systems" problem at a fund administrator, then spun out to sell the automation layer to the same buyers (servicers/managers). Logical adjacency; the risk is that their former employer (bunch) is now a competitor with 15x+ the capital chasing the same buyers.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new:** little at the category level — "vertical AI agents over messy back-office documents with an audit trail" is the same pattern as F2, Hypercore, Canoe. The claimed twist is agents that follow a firm's own SOPs end-to-end for fund accounting/treasury/TA.
- **Traction (the anti-PR gate):** **none proven.** ~a dozen unnamed design partners; no named customer, no revenue, no AUA. Announced ≠ adopted.
- **Who captures the margin (analysis):** the durable moat in this stack is **integration depth + trust for regulated NAV/register work**, which incumbents (FundGuard, Allvue, bunch) already hold. An agent layer that merely reads/extracts risks being commoditized by whichever incumbent bolts on the same LLM tooling first. Nomerra's edge, if any, is founder access to bunch-era relationships + a clean agent-first stack — **(hypothesis, unproven)**.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Use of funds: grow engineering, meet "surging demand" in EU + US. Watch for the **first named enterprise customer** and a **follow-on seed** — $2M is a tight runway for an integration-heavy enterprise product (implies a bridge within ~12–18 months, hypothesis).
- **PR stats — sourced?** "Private markets triple in ~5 years" is directionally supported ($13T→~$30–32T by 2030; borrowed from Morgan Stanley's Ted Pick / Bain / PwC) — an industry talking point, at the optimistic end. **"Qualified accountants decreased by a third over the last decade" is NOT supported** by any source found (documented declines are ~17–20%, differently framed) → **overstated PR**.
- **Second-order:** the real question is not "is there an AI-in-private-markets story" (there clearly is, and it's crowded) but **"can a $2M pre-seed with no named customers win a trust-and-integration game already occupied by $35M–$130M-funded incumbents including its own alma mater?"** On current evidence, a watch item, not a bet.

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** Nomerra sits in private-markets operations software — the back-office plumbing (fund accounting, treasury, transfer agency) beneath GPs, LPs and asset servicers, now being re-platformed "AI-native." Sizing the demand side: global alternatives AUM is forecast to reach ~$32tn by 2030 (per Preqin's *Private Markets in 2030* report, Oct 2025, via BlackRock/Preqin); PwC's 2025 Global AWM report puts private-markets AUM at ~$26.6tn by 2030 and expects private markets to generate >half of global asset-management *revenues* by 2030. The operating layer is structurally fragmented and manual — no standardization or interconnection across ERPs, banks, email and spreadsheets, with the same data retyped between isolated systems (per Nomerra PR). **Why now:** a labor-supply squeeze meeting a demand surge — the US CPA candidate pipeline is down ~27% over the past decade and >90% of finance leaders report they cannot find enough qualified accounting staff (per Ramp/Robert Half industry data, 2026), while private-markets complexity (evergreens, semi-liquid structures, retail channels, tighter reporting) keeps rising. The industry's default fix — hire more people — is failing, which is the exact wedge for agentic automation (analysis).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs the winners run on: ARR growth, net revenue retention (NRR), and fund-entities/LPs served (proxy for platform stickiness). Basis of competition is **depth/vertical fit + system-of-record integration**, not price — generic AI "can only go so far in an environment this complex" (14Peaks, Nomerra PR). Key players by posture:
- *Incumbents / consolidators:* Carta (agentic ERP for private capital; acquired Avantia for Carta Law, May 2026 [[Carta acquires Avantia to launch AI-native Carta Law for private markets]]); iCapital (bought Passthrough onboarding, Feb 2026 [[iCapital to acquire Passthrough investor onboarding platform]]).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Incumbent disintermediation / land-grab.** Carta, iCapital, Juniper Square and bunch already own the system-of-record and LP relationships; Nomerra "connects to systems firms already use," i.e. rides someone else's rails — value could be captured by the platform layer it plugs into (second-order: thin, replaceable middleware if incumbents ship comparable agents).
2. **Capital & runway gap.** $2M pre-seed vs bunch's $58M and Juniper Square's $1.1bn — in an enterprise-sales, integration-heavy, trust-gated segment, undercapitalization can stall before proving reference customers (second-order: forced down-round or acqui-hire).
3. **Adoption/liability in a regulated back office.** Fund accounting, treasury and transfer agency are error-intolerant and audited; agentic automation must clear controls and fiduciary/regulatory scrutiny before firms hand off "end-to-end execution" — PR is silent on live vs pilot adoption and on who owns errors (second-order: slow, review-heavy rollouts cap the efficiency claim).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The private-markets ops stack is entering a consolidation-plus-new-entry phase simultaneously: incumbents buy in AI (Carta/Avantia, iCapital/Passthrough) while a wave of AI-native seeds (Nomerra, F2, Hypercore, Caruso) attacks specific workflows. Falsifiable thesis: agentic fund-ops belongs to whoever owns the system-of-record, so pure-play automation wedges like Nomerra get acquired or absorbed within ~24 months. **Trigger to watch:** a Series A led by a fund-admin incumbent/strategic (or a named enterprise servicer reference) would signal the wedge is holding; continued silence on paying customers past 12 months would confirm the thesis.
