# Context-enrichment: Russia tightens counter-sanction limits on retail non-resident clients
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 1 June 2026 President Putin signed Decree No. 377, which extends the regime of Decree No. 95 (5 March 2022, "On the temporary procedure for fulfilling obligations to certain foreign creditors") from the existing perimeter — payments on loans, securities and other monetary obligations to "unfriendly"-state creditors — to **bank deposits and accounts**. Practically: when a Russian bank owes money to (or must pay deposit income to) a person linked to an "unfriendly" country, it may be required not to release the funds freely but to credit them to a special **type-C account**. Reporting (RBC, relayed via Финтехно; corroborated by Forbes.ru, The Insider, Novaya Gazeta Europe, Expert, RFE/RL) names clients of T-Bank, Sber, VTB and Alfa-Bank.

The one concrete numeric threshold surfaced in coverage: from 1 June 2026 banks may pay deposit **income** to "unfriendly" persons only up to **10 million rubles/month**, with the excess routed to a type-C account (Forbes/vbr.ru). This is NOT confiscation — funds legally remain the client's — but they cannot be freely disposed of (type-C is effectively frozen, with only a narrow list of permitted operations).

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The change is procedural-looking (a deposit added to a 2022 decree's list of "obligations") but is a category jump. Until now type-C touched creditors, shareholders and securities holders — i.e. counterparties of Russian issuers/borrowers. Decree 377 reaches **the ordinary retail bank relationship**: a foreign individual's own savings. The decree text reportedly does NOT specify HOW the regime applies to retail deposits — whether to all deposits of "unfriendly" nationals, whether the 10M cap is on income or principal, or whether physical residence in Russia exempts you. That vagueness is the substance: it pushes discretion onto banks' compliance desks, producing inconsistent, case-by-case freezes (some access restored after submitting documents, some not). The "PR"/official framing ("temporary procedure", "not confiscation") understates that for the affected client the money is functionally inaccessible.

## [1] Competitors / peers (regime comparison, not corporate)
This is a sovereign measure, so "competitors" = the broader Russian counter-sanction capital-control toolkit and Western mirror-image freezes:
- **Decree 95 (Mar 2022)** — ruble payments to type-C for "unfriendly" creditors.
- **Decree 81/737 / "government commission" approval regime (2022+)** — controls on asset sales/exits by unfriendly owners.
- **Decree 840 (Oct 2024)** — moved type-C-held Russian shares to registrars; froze depositor instructions during transition.
- **CBR amendments (31 Jul 2025)** — narrow easings (asset-exchange transfers, dividend use to settle resident obligations).
- **CBR (8 Dec 2025)** — eased cross-border transfers for residents/friendly countries and for working unfriendly-nationals' wages; kept the ban for non-working unfriendly individuals.

**+ Why the lay of the land / second-order:** Through 2025 the visible trend was selective *liberalization* (Moscow signalling it wants foreign capital back via type-I "investment" accounts and easing transfers). Decree 377 cuts the other way — *tightening* on the retail layer. The two are consistent if read as: ease for capital Russia wants (new investment, friendly flows), tighten the trap on legacy "unfriendly" money already inside. The mirror is the EU/G7 freeze of ~€200bn+ Russian CBR assets — type-C is Moscow's symmetric hostage pool, now extended down to retail balances.

## [2] "Company" history / fit (the regime's trajectory)
The counter-sanction architecture has run continuously since Feb–Mar 2022: list of "unfriendly" states, ruble-only forced settlement, type-C as the holding pen, then years of incremental scope changes (securities 2024, transfer rules 2025). Decree 377 fits this logic of **ratcheting scope while keeping the legal form "temporary."** 

**+ Why now / structural pressure:** No official rationale was given, but plausible drivers (analysis): (a) closing a perceived loophole — unfriendly nationals were keeping/earning on ordinary deposits while the creditor-side was frozen; (b) building negotiating leverage / reciprocity ahead of any settlement on frozen Western-held Russian assets; (c) fiscal/liquidity pressure favouring trapping ruble balances domestically. These are hypotheses; the decree itself states none.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**What's genuinely new:** the *retail individual deposit* now falls inside type-C reach — previously the regime was about obligations to creditors/securities holders, not a foreigner's personal savings account. The 10M-rubles/month income cap (if confirmed as drafted) is a new concrete parameter.

**Traction (real, not announced):** Already biting — multiple outlets report actual frozen deposits at T-Bank, Sber, VTB, Alfa in June 2026, support-line confirmations citing the counter-sanction amendments, and complaints in Banki.ru/Telegram. So this is *live*, not a draft. **+ Why it matters, deeper:** because the rule is under-specified, the binding mechanism is bank discretion, not a clear statutory line — meaning the real "value" (to the state) is optionality and chilling effect: banks over-comply to avoid CBR risk, catching even foreigners who relocated to Russia. Second-order: erodes the deposit base's trust premium for any foreign-passport holder and accelerates de-banking of that segment.

## [4] What's next / sentiment
Expect CBR/Minfin clarifications (the gap between decree and operational rules is the open question), uneven bank-by-bank application, and likely a permitted-operations carve-out list. Risk vectors: scope creep to principal (not just income), to "friendly" dual-nationals, and reputational damage to Russia's parallel effort to attract foreign investment via type-I accounts. **+ Counterintuitive second-order:** tightening the trap on legacy unfriendly money undercuts the credibility of the "come invest via type-I" pitch — capital watches how the *last* cohort was treated. Severity for global fintech/markets is modest (isolated, RU-domestic, no new Western-facing rail); severity for affected individuals is high.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a Russian counter-sanctions / capital-controls regulatory story, not a product or deal. Mechanism: Presidential Decree No. 377 (1 June 2026) extended the regime of Decree No. 95 (5 March 2022, "On the temporary procedure for fulfilling obligations to certain foreign creditors") to ordinary bank deposits and savings accounts; previously No. 95 covered mainly loan repayments, securities coupons/dividends and other monetary obligations to "unfriendly"-country creditors (per RBC via Финтехно; corroborated by reporting on the June restrictions). The economic device is the **Type "C" account**: when a Russian bank must return a deposit or pay deposit income to a client tied to an "unfriendly" jurisdiction, it can be required to credit the sum to a blocked Type C account rather than release it freely — funds remain the client's property in law but are not freely disposable (FX conversion / withdrawal need government-commission approval). Size of the affected pool: aggregate Type C balances were reported above ~RUB 1tn (~$11–12bn) at the start of 2024 (per The Bell / Free Russia Forum, dated 2024 — older figure, no fresh retail-deposit breakdown). The political rationale (asymmetric response to ~$300bn of frozen Russian reserves abroad) is the secular driver; "why now" is the formal trigger of Decree No. 377 pushing the regime down from the wholesale/securities layer to the retail-deposit layer. Note: the news names the same regime that, in parallel, Russia is trying to soften for *new* money via guaranteed "In/Ин" accounts (Putin decree, July 2025) — so the policy runs in two opposite directions (block legacy non-resident money, court new inflows). (analysis)

**Competitive landscape.** Not a competitive market — the relevant actors are the banks that must operate the regime and the regulator (Bank of Russia, which sets the Type C operating rules). Named as affected by retail restrictions in June 2026: **T-Bank (Т-Банк), Sberbank, VTB** (per RBC), with similar client reports for **Alfa-Bank** on thematic chats and Banki.ru — i.e. concentrated among the largest deposit-takers, exactly where non-resident retail balances cluster. There is no "basis of competition" here; the operational burden (KYC re-screening of client nationality/residency, account-type routing, complaint handling) is a compliance cost spread across the systemically important banks, with the largest balance-sheets carrying the most exposure. De-PR caveat: this is live/applied (clients report frozen access since early June), not merely "announced" — but the *scope* (which client categories, what thresholds, dual citizens) is unclear from the source and the banks are silent on numbers.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/multiple content — regulation story, no deal, no metrics disclosed. Trading multiples: not applicable. Internal comps from the base (related counter-sanction / frozen-asset / capital-control precedents, English corpus): [[Telegram's $500m Russian bonds frozen under sanctions]] (frozen Russian-issued bonds, Jan 2026); [[Citigroup to sell remaining Russian operations at $1.2bn loss]] (forced foreign-bank exit, Jan 2026); [[Russia's A7 cross-border payments firm plans global expansion]] and [[Sanctioned Russian crypto network expands across Africa]] (the parallel build-out of sanction-evading rails). These corroborate the two-sided pattern: Russia blocks "unfriendly" capital while building alternative outbound channels. No dividend/deposit-balance figures per bank are publicly verifiable → per-bank exposure = no data.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Capital-flow / trust risk (systemic).** Extending the Type C freeze to ordinary retail deposits signals to *all* foreign and dual-national depositors that "your money is property but not yours to move," which undercuts the parallel "In-account" effort to attract new inflows — the second-order effect is that the credibility of any Russian no-freeze guarantee falls, suppressing exactly the inflows the regime wants.
2. **Reputational / operational risk for the named banks (T-Bank, Sber, VTB, Alfa).** They become the visible enforcers of freezes on individual clients (complaints, litigation, dual-citizen edge cases), and bear KYC re-screening costs; concentration in the top banks means the burden is not diversified away.
3. **Scope-creep / arbitrariness risk.** Source does not define thresholds, client-category boundaries or dual-citizenship treatment; a discretionary, government-commission-gated regime that just moved from wholesale to retail can move further, so any non-resident or dual-national retail balance at a Russian bank now carries open-ended freeze optionality — hard to price, encourages pre-emptive withdrawal by those still able.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** The freeze regime has crossed from the wholesale/securities layer into the retail-deposit layer, formalizing that Russian bank deposits held by "unfriendly"-linked individuals are no longer freely fungible. Falsifiable thesis: this accelerates exit of foreign/dual-national retail money from Russian banks (where still permitted) and further marginalizes the "In-account" inflow scheme. Trigger/catalyst to watch: official Bank of Russia clarification on retail Type C scope/thresholds, fresh aggregate Type C balance data, or any "In-account" uptake figure (prior reporting flagged near-zero uptake) — near-zero uptake alongside the retail freeze would confirm the thesis; a published carve-out protecting retail depositors would weaken it. (analysis)
