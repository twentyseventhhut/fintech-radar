Africa: The big deal: 33 start-ups raised $93mn in funding via $100K+ deals in August
33 African startups raised $93 million in August, primarily through equity.
August's funding was widely distributed across sectors and regions, unlike July, dominated by two large deals.
The 'big four' regions—South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, and Egypt—continue to dominate in terms of funding.
'Tier two' markets in West Africa, such as Ghana, are steadily gaining traction.
Fintech and energy account for two-thirds of 2025’s startup funding.
The rise in debt financing indicates maturity in Africa's financial instrument utilization.
Despite a recent funding 'winter,' there's optimism about reaching the $3 billion mark this year.
In a revealing glimpse into the burgeoning ecosystem of African entrepreneurship, 33 startups on the continent successfully raised $93 million in funding through deals exceeding $100,000 in August. This development highlights the dynamic pulse of Africa's startup arena, which has seen fluctuating fortunes over the past two years. <br> <br> According to the latest report by Africa: The Big Deal, about 75% of this funding was achieved through equity while the remainder was funded by debt instruments. Max Giacomelli, co-founder of Africa: The Big Deal, revealed during a recent interview that he remains optimistic about the potential for positive growth by the end of 2025, predicting a close with over $3 billion raised. <br> <br> Jacomelli elucidated the nuances of the current investment landscape by contrasting August's figures against the previous month. While July's funding total was a substantial $150 million, it was notably buoyed by two massive debt transactions conducted by Sun King and D.light. In contrast, August's more modest sum was a reflection of a wider distribution across various sectors without a dominating singular deal. <br> <br> August's standout deals were led by CoolBox from Nigeria, which specializes in solar-powered refrigeration, and Tele from Kenya, a company at the forefront of oxygen supply innovation. A more balanced geographical spread, as observed this August, marks a departure from July’s figures, which were bolstered primarily by two outsized transactions. <br> <br> Geographically, the concentration of startup funding remains heavily skewed towards the so-called 'big four': South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, and Egypt. Combined, these nations accounted for 80% of all startup funding, despite representing only a third of Africa’s population and half its GDP. This dominance is attributed to a critical mass of entrepreneurs and investors, supplemented by a sufficient pipeline of prospective deals that lure significant capital. <br> <br> Nevertheless, West African markets outside the big four are making headway. Ghana exemplifies this ascent with its startups raising $50 million recently. The emergence of substantial funding in 'tier two' markets underpins this narrative, developing potential hubs of innovation and economic advancement. <br> <br> From a sectoral standpoint, fintech continues to command the lion's share of investment, accompanied by energy startups. Together, these sectors comprise two-thirds of the funding thus far in 2025. Logistics and transportation follow closely as growing areas of interest due to their foundational role in aiding other sectors. <br> <br> The conversation naturally turned towards the sustainability of growth amidst global economic pressures. According to Giacomelli, after a pronounced 'funding winter' during 2022, where yearly funding fell markedly from highs above $6 billion, 2025 is hinting at a resurgence. This year, as of August, African startups have raised $2 billion, paralleling the achievements by the same time last year, yet promising surpassing feats anticipated by December. <br> <br> Giacomelli highlights debt financing as a marker of market maturity, indicating a refinement in leveraging financial instruments beyond equity. Noteworthy debt raises from firms like wave and major players in the energy tech sector reflect this trend. <br> <br> As the year draws to a close, the expectation is set high for potentially groundbreaking deals, including Ethiopian fintech firm PomPei’s rumored $100 million raise and a transformational $300 million equity and over $1 billion debt raise by Nigerian firm Moove. <br> <br> While significant challenges lie ahead, Africa's startup ecosystem demonstrates resilience bolstered by a diversified funding base, an activated pipeline of future deals, and strategic financial structuring. As the continent races towards a potential $3 billion milestone, the movement of funds, both in equity and debt, underscores a maturing sector rich with opportunity.

 After a vibrant first half of the year, Africa’s startup ecosystem has already secured over $2 billion in funding in 2025 , even as August proved relatively quiet on the deal front, according to a report by Africa: The Big Deal. 
 This month was one of the softest months of the year, with deal volume and value dropping compared to the surge in July. This cooling reflects a broader seasonal trend . August often brings fewer announcements as global investors pause during the summer, resulting in reduced activity across venture markets. 
 While August recorded fewer announcements, July buzzed with significant funding, with 61 startups announcing at least $100k in funding, which saw the total amount raised to $550 million. Last month, funding according to reports is the most that start-ups in Africa have raised in a month in more than two years.

 Interestingly, 83% of this amount was claimed by just two companies, d.light and Sun King, both operating in the energy sector with roots in Kenya. Despite the slowdown, the fact that African startups surpassed $2 billion by August underscores the resilience and growing maturity of the ecosystem . 
 Hitting the $2 billion threshold with four months left in 2025 puts the African continent firmly on track to outperform last year’s totals . This suggests strong investor confidence , particularly in fintech, climate-tech, health-tech, and mobility startups, which continue to attract large rounds. 
 What makes the current momentum even more encouraging is that African startups are demonstrating resilience amid global economic headwinds. While many regions are experiencing contractions in venture activity due to high interest rates, geopolitical uncertainties, and investor caution, Africa continues to show that its long-term growth story is intact. 
 The diversity of sectors attracting capital from fintech to climate-tech, Healthtech, and logistics is another promising sign that funding is not only growing but also spreading more evenly across industries. While fintech accounted for 35-60% of investments in recent years, sectors like AgriTech, Healthtech, and climate-tech are gaining ground, particularly in emerging markets like Ghana, Uganda, and Tanzania. Agritech, for instance, raised $88.6 million across 30 deals in 2024. This diversification is a positive sign of a maturing ecosystem. 
 Notably, the $2 billion milestone is especially impressive given the backdrop of global VC slowdown and rising caution in emerging markets. African startups are increasingly drawing funds from a mix of international VCs, development finance institutions, and corporate investors , ensuring a more diversified capital base. 
 The African venture capital landscape is also seeing a shift toward more localized funding sources. The share of capital deployed by local investors has increased for the third consecutive year, reducing reliance on international capital and reflecting growing confidence in the continent’s potential. This trend, combined with strategic partnerships and government-backed initiatives like the Timbuktoo FinTech Hub in Lagos, fosters a more sustainable ecosystem for startups to thrive. 
 The milestone underscores the continent’s resilience and growing investor confidence, putting it on track to surpass last year’s totals well before the year’s end. If funding pace holds till the end of the year, 2025 may go down as a defining year for Africa’s venture landscape, reaffirming the continent’s place as one of the most exciting frontiers for innovation and investment globally.
