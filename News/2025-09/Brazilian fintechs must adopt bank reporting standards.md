The Federal Revenue Service of Brazil is set to publish a new normative instruction (IN) this Friday (29) requiring fintech companies to provide the same financial information currently demanded from traditional banks. Under the new rule, fintechs will be obligated to submit the e-Financeira, a set of data files that includes detailed information on customer registration, transactions, balances, investments, and other financial records, used for monitoring and risk assessment purposes.

 Cautious not to repeat past mistakes, the tax authority will avoid replicating the exact wording of a similar measure issued in September 2024. That earlier regulation was revoked after becoming the target of a wave of disinformation in January 2025, when false claims spread alleging that the government intended to tax Pix, Brazil’s instant payment system. In a note released ahead of the new regulation, the Receita adopted an unusually direct tone, stating it does not want to “give rise to another wave of lies.” 
 The statement emphasized that the forthcoming regulation “will make clear its intent to fight crime.” It further establishes that payment institutions and payment schemes—including fintechs—“are subject to exactly the same obligations as traditional financial institutions.” 
 The drafting of the rule seeks to avoid misinterpretations. To that end, it explicitly references the Brazilian Payment System Law, adopting its legal definitions of payment institutions, payment arrangements, and payment accounts. “We are making it clear that nothing new is being created—only that existing legal definitions are being applied,” the note clarified. 
 “Fintechs have been used for money laundering in major operations against organized crime because there is a regulatory gap: they do not currently face the same transparency and reporting obligations that all financial institutions in Brazil have been subject to for more than 20 years,” said the Receita. “Organized crime knows this and exploits the loophole to move, conceal, and launder illicit funds.” 
 Fintechs first came under scrutiny in 2023, when Finance Minister Fernando Haddad directed the agency to focus on the financial operations of organized crime. Investigations found evidence that criminal organizations were using fintech platforms to launder money. 
 This was the rationale behind IN 2219/24, issued in September 2024, which obligated fintechs to provide the e-Financeira. However, the measure later became the centerpiece of the so-called “Pix crisis,” fueled by disinformation campaigns. 
 The announcement of the new regulation came Thursday (28) morning, shortly after a press conference in which Mr. Haddad and Justice Minister Ricardo Lewandowski detailed three major federal operations against organized crime in the fuel sector, which had used fintechs to disguise illicit proceeds. 
 “With this measure, the Federal Revenue will enhance its monitoring capacity and strengthen its partnership with the Federal Police, enabling authorities to reach the sophisticated money-laundering schemes employed by organized crime,” Mr. Haddad said. 
 The minister also underlined the importance of Congressional approval of the proposed bill that defines and punishes persistent tax evaders. This initiative targets criminal organizations that systematically evade taxes by repeatedly opening and closing corporate registrations under Brazil’s National Registry of Legal Entities (CNPJ) to avoid oversight. According to Haddad, once enacted, the law would allow authorities to prevent such companies from continuing to operate.
