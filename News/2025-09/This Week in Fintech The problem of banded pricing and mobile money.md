The Problem of Banded Pricing and Mobile Money 
A Structural Impediment to Value Decision Making
Why do mobile money fees remain stubbornly high in a country like Tanzania where there is a strong policy push for digital payments, disposable income is limited and most transactions (90%+) still happen in cash?
 While there are a number of emerging value service providers in the market including AzamPesa and Selcom Tanzania , most consumers continue to use existing services with widespread SIM-card based distribution at overall higher costs. 
Lets put distribution aside for a moment. I will address in another post. 
One aspect of mobile money that contributes to the structural nature of higher costs are banded fee tariffs. 
A banded fee, or slab-based pricing, establishes charges based on transaction bands that vary across services. Banded fee tariffs originated with Mpesa in Kenya back in 2007 to create transparency around pricing for services. Instead of a percentage-based system which requires consumers to calculate fees on their own, the banded method allows the exact price of a service via a table lookup. 
Customers Get Confused 
So for example, if you withdraw 8,000 TZS of cash from a mobile money agent, you will fall in the band of 7,000 to 9,999. The charge is the same whether you withdraw 7,000 or 9,999.
This table above includes only 8 bands of one service (withdrawal) for four different providers in Tanzania (the three largest services + Azampesa) . There are more than 24 possible bands and 5 or more services, including withdrawal, person to person transfer, transfer to bank or to other mobile money providers, that falls within the banded framework. That is more than 120 pricing intervals a consumer has to be aware of for a single service provider. Now consider there are more than five mobile money providers in Tanzania, so it means 120 x 5 = 600 different prices that a consumer needs to sift through to figure out what service provider may offer them the best value for a particular service in a specific band. 

 Banded pricing is confusing to understand and almost impossible to keep track of over time for most consumers. According to a study by Innovations or Poverty Action : (page 48) 
 We see a stark disparity in shoppers’ knowledge of transaction fees across countries. Only 6 percent of participants in Tanzania could correctly name the official withdrawal fee of a common transaction value. 
The original intent of the model, to create clear transparency in pricing, is undermined by increasing number of services, taxes and service providers. Banded pricing also makes it difficult for consumers to understand and compare value for services. Even when lower cost service providers reduce prices for specific services or segments, its difficult to communicate that value in concrete terms to consumers. How much am I saving? For what kind of transaction? If I change the band how do the savings change? 
No one has time for it. 
Banded pricing increasingly places the burden of research, analysis and complex table lookups on a consumer population that regularly opts to pay for goods in cash to avoid digital payment costs or at least the perception of high costs. You can imagine why 90%+ payments in Tanzania remain in cash.
Regressive Pricing 
Furthermore, banded pricing is fundamentally regressive and extracts higher economic rents from the poorest users. It is significantly less expensive to send one transaction of 100,000 TZS than 10 transactions of 10,000 TZS for most services. Most Tanzanians cannot afford to transact at higher bands, so the burden of higher cost pricing is placed on the least advantaged consumer. In other words, the banded pricing method, originally meant to create transparency and clarity in pricing, contributes to making mobile money less affordable for less affluent consumers. Banded pricing not only makes smaller transactions more expensive, it also makes the lower end of each banded even more regressive, so for example:
 A consumer is charged 1,450 TZS to withdraw 10,000 TZS from an agent which is a 14.5% fee on the value transacted. A consumer is also charged 1,450 TZS to withdraw 14,999 TZS from an agent which is a 9.5% fee on the value transacted. 
Thats quite a difference. One could argue you could narrow the bands, but this creates an even larger and more overwhelming table of transaction fees that consumers will likely ignore anyways.

Lower band transactions sometimes attract charges up to 40% of the value transacted. I would argue slab-based pricing is purposefully confusing and obfuscates the percentage value charged, allowing service providers to charge higher percentage rates for lower value bands, where most transactions happen - pushing profits while confusing customers and making it difficult to understand value.
The Agent Caveat 
Having criticized banded transactions, I will acknowledge a key caveat. Agent networks in many ways are a key cost driver in mobile money. In order to properly incentivize an agent to cash in or cash out a specific transaction they must be properly compensated. At lower band transactions, that cost of compensation can be loss-making if the customer charges are not high enough. In other words, Agents will refuse to service low value transactions if they are not properly compensated and as a result mobile money providers pass that cost to consumers to ensure services are provided and they do not make losses. So in other words, while banded pricing is regressive in terms of pricing, it is also the case that Agents must be properly incentivied or motivated to offer services in those bands. 

Having said all that, percentage based pricing also can be confusing to a consumer base unaccustomed to managing numerical values accordingly. If you asked an aeverage consumer to approximate a 5% charge on a 27,000 TZS transaction, most would be confused. Ultimately the issue of whether one uses a percentage of banded fee model becomes less and less important when the overall cost of transactions are low. In other words, people worry about the cost of mobile money because they inuitively know its too high. If the perception of mobile money was that it is affordable and not something you need to worry too much about, you will be less likely to avoid it regardless of the model.
A Way Forward 
 Recently SelcomPesa in Tanzania innovated a bundling approach to mobile money that mirrors the way voice, data and text messages are typically sold to consumers on a value basis. For example, a customer can buy X value of transactions across a number of services for one fixed price. Bundling is one way to offer both value and greater price transparency and give consumers a clear understanding of what they will pay for what service. At the same time, bundling for mobile money is more attractive to high-value users who regularly manage money movement multiple times a week and can predict how much they will pay for certain services. For many consumers in Tanzania, mobile money purchases can be somewhat unpredictable and as a result a bundling approach may not always address their needs. 
 At Azampesa we currently follow the banded pricing model for various reasons including regulatory requirements and customer familiarity, but I believe its something we need to move away from to make pricing and value determinations for consumers more clear and easy to understand. 
Azampesa will bring new pricing models to the market soon to address some of the confusion and make decision making on pricing easier, more clear and more affordable. We hope this effort will build greater confidence for consumers that mobile money can present new value propositions to encourage digital transcations especially for transactions where their revealed preference is overwhelmingly for cash. 
Consumers in Tanzania know one thing: fees are expensive, but they do not know how expensive and they are entirely confused about how to determine the actual price. Payments via mobile money are made when absolutely necessary, not as a matter of course within the day. Everyone will think twice about making a mobile money payment because they are not quite sure how expensive it will be, but they for sure know it is expensive. 
Ultimately the pricing models are a problem because services are expensive. The more affordable services become, the less people will worry about cost and make a larger percentage of their every day spending digital, benefitting from convenience, security and the opportunity to access additional value added services.
