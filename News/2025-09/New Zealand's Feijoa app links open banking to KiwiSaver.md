Open banking has met KiwiSaver for the first time through the launch of an app that lets people round up their spending to top up their KiwiSaver.

The Feijoa app is a KiwiSaver version of ASB’s Save the Change banking feature that lets users round up transactions, with the difference between the sum spent and the rounded amount being added to their savings accounts.

In 2021, ASB chief executive Vittoria Shortt said more than $4 million was added to savings accounts each month through the service.

But while Save the Change is only available to ASB customers, Feijoa can be used by anyone from any bank with any KiwiSaver provider.

The founders Ben Davin and Mark White-Robinson hope the app will find favour with people who currently aren’t getting much out of KiwiSaver, and that it will result in more people eventually reaching retirement with adequate nest eggs to supplement NZ Super.

Data released earlier this month by the Financial Markets Authority Te Mana Tātai Hokohoko revealed about 30% of KiwiSaver members between the ages of 18 and 65 are not contributing.

However, using the app comes with a cost of up to 15% of the transaction amount, so when a user who has set Feijoa to round up to the nearest $1 buys a coffee for $6.55, 4 cents of the rounded 45c will go in fees to Feijoa, while 41c will go into their nominated KiwiSaver scheme.

Davin and White-Robinson say over time, as user numbers build, they hope to be able to reduce fees.

Feijoa piggybacks off the systems of open banking company Akahu Technologies, which is partially-owned by Westpac.

Rounded payments are directed to app-users’ chosen KiwiSaver accounts through Inland Revenue Te Taari Take.

Feijoa’s official public launch is on Monday but just over 100 people have been testing it, including the founders and their friends and family, and not all are sending round-ups to their own KiwiSaver schemes.

Davin said: “My father-in-law is not in KiwiSaver, but he's got his five grandchildren on it. He has set them at 20% each.

“Every time he transacts, it's 20c there to that kid, 3c there, or whatever.”

Davin has set his own round-ups to go to his children’s KiwiSaver accounts.

Co-Founders Ben Davin and Mark White-Robinson have developed Feijoa to help Kiwis grow their KiwiSaver balances to set them up for the future.Supplied

KiwiSaver is now seen as the biggest opportunity for young people to learn about investment, KiwiSaver experts said at the recent Financial Services Council conference in Auckland earlier this month.

Davin and White-Robinson do not believe that most people are saving enough into KiwiSaver, and they hope their app will help reduce the savings gap.

“The scary thing is no one’s doing enough for retirement as well, so that's, yeah, you look at the average balance in New Zealand compared to the average super balance in Australia. It's like an order of magnitude difference,” Davin said.

The average KiwiSaver balance is about $37,000.

The two countries super systems are different, with Australia having moved over several decades to require compulsory tax-advantaged superannuation saving to replace the country’s state pension, while New Zealand continues to rely heavily on the taxpayer-funded NZ Super universal state pension, with KiwiSaver effectively topping that up.

Davin and White-Robinson especially hope Feijoa could be used by the likes of the self-employed, or those who have fallen foul of “total remuneration” contracts where employers do not pay contributions to KiwiSaver.

About a third of people with KiwiSaver accounts miss out on Crown contributions, which have now been cut by the Government in a bid to save money to help fund tax breaks to business-owners.

It used to be 50c for every dollar saved into KiwiSaver, up to a maximum of $521 each year. The Crown contribution is now just 25c for every $1 saved up to a maximum of $260 each year.

But Davin said: “It's an instant 25% return. If you're not a big spender and you get $200 through round-ups a year, you're getting $50 free. It can make a massive difference for that group of people.”

The pair chose the name Feijoa for their app because the fruit, which Davin does not even like, is something of a Kiwi symbol of abundance and growth. However, it was also not trade-marked by anyone else in financial services.
