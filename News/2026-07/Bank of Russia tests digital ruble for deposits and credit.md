# Context-enrichment: Bank of Russia tests digital ruble for deposits and credit
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
The Bank of Russia (CBR) is floating an **experiment** to let commercial banks open digital-ruble wallets/accounts **on their own balance sheets** — the opposite of today's design, where all digital-ruble balances sit on the CBR platform and the bank is a pass-through providing app access only. If banks carry the balances, they could (a) **pay interest** on them, (b) **issue credit** in digital rubles, and (c) plug the CBDC into their normal deposit/loan product economics. Per CBR Deputy Governor **Zulfiya Kakhrumanova (Кахруманова)**, such an experiment is realistic **"closer to 2029,"** and the two account models (CBR-platform wallets vs bank-balance-sheet accounts) would **coexist for roughly two years** before a decision on which survives. Source: Финтехно channel (max.ru/fintexno); no primary CBR press release for this specific proposal was retrieved — treat exact wording as channel-reported.
- **De-PR reality check:** this is an **idea/consultation stage**, not a live pilot and not a decision. There is no draft law, no participant bank list, no dates beyond a soft "~2029." The current mass rollout (mandatory from **1 Sep 2026**) still uses the **CBR-platform, no-interest, no-bank-credit** model. So the "test" is a signal of intent to *redesign the CBDC's very form*, not an operational step. (analysis)
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** the CBR is **conceding the core defect of its own CBDC design**. A non-interest-bearing balance parked on the central bank's platform is inferior to a bank deposit for the holder and lethal for the bank's funding model — it drains cheap retail float without compensation (the exact tension the CBR already tried to paper over with tiny payroll subsidies, see [[Финтехно Russian central bank sets digital ruble payroll tariffs]]). Letting banks hold the balances and pay interest would make the digital ruble **behave like a tokenised deposit** rather than pure central-bank money — restoring the bank's role, its float, and its incentive to promote the instrument. The subtext: to get *adoption* the CBR may have to give up part of the *disintermediation/control* that was the point of a CBDC in the first place. That is the real story, and it **changes the central question** from "how fast will the CBDC roll out" to "will the CBDC survive as genuine central-bank money, or quietly converge back into a bank deposit with a CBDC label?" (analysis/hypothesis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
Peer set = other CBDC programmes wrestling with the deposit/disintermediation problem, and the tokenised-deposit alternative that solves it differently.
- **Digital euro (ECB / Italy):** the EU's answer to disintermediation is a **holding cap** (~€3,000) plus a "reverse waterfall" to bank accounts — it protects deposits by *limiting* CBDC balances rather than moving them onto bank balance sheets. Italian banks (ABI) back it but want costs phased ([[Italy's banks back digital euro but want costs phased]]); most euro-area banks are hostile precisely on funding-cost grounds. Russia, uniquely, runs **no holding cap** — hence its disintermediation problem is worse, and this bank-balance-sheet idea is its distinct fix. (sourced + analysis)
- **India (RBI):** RBI ran a **certificate-of-deposit / deposit-tokenisation pilot** on its wholesale CBDC platform (Oct 2025, [[RBI launches certificate of deposit tokenisation pilot]]) and e-rupee welfare/programmable pilots ([[RBI pilots e-rupee welfare payments to boost CBDC adoption]]). Deposit *tokenisation* — a bank liability represented on a ledger — is conceptually close to what Russia now floats, but India kept retail CBDC and tokenised deposits as **separate instruments**; Russia is contemplating **merging** them.
- **China (e-CNY / PBOC):** massive wallet base but low organic retail use; PBOC keeps e-CNY as a strict central-bank liability, no interest, and manages bank impact by keeping usage transactional and adding lenders gradually ([[China adds 12 lenders to digital yuan network]]). China has *not* moved to let banks hold e-CNY as an interest-bearing deposit — so Russia's proposal, if pursued, would be **more radical** than the largest CBDC.
- **Position:** Russia is **mid-pack on retail CBDC adoption but a policy outlier** — the only major programme openly considering putting CBDC balances on commercial-bank balance sheets to enable interest and credit. **Second-order:** this is less "leadership" than **course-correction under pressure** — the design that maximised state control (no cap, CBR platform) is the design banks and users have least reason to embrace, forcing a walk-back. (analysis)

## [2] Company history / fit
- Digital-ruble mass rollout was **repeatedly delayed** (from 1 July 2025) and is now mandatory from **1 Sep 2026** for systemically important banks + large retailers (>120m rub revenue), **1 Sep 2027** for universal-licence banks + retailers >30m rub, **1 Sep 2028** for the rest; outlets <5m rub exempt (CBR, Interfax). Government/budget payments live since **1 Jan 2026**.
- The CBR has consistently designed the digital ruble for **state control and sanctions-resilience** post-2022 (Carnegie), while **banks (Association of Russian Banks) have pushed back on deposit-outflow risk and integration cost** (up to ~100m rub/bank). The payroll tariff (Jun 2026, [[Финтехно Russian central bank sets digital ruble payroll tariffs]]) was the CBR *subsidising* banks — 67 kopecks per payslip — to swallow the threat.
- **Why the CBR acts this way now:** the tiny payroll subsidy clearly did not resolve bank resistance; the deeper fix is to change the instrument's *form* so banks profit from it instead of being disintermediated. This proposal is the **logical escalation** of the same appeasement pattern: mandate first, then progressively redesign to reduce bank friction. It fits a regulator that must hit rollout deadlines yet cannot afford a funding-base shock to systemically important banks it also relies on for sanctions-era stability. (analysis)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new:** openly proposing that a **retail CBDC balance be held on a commercial bank's balance sheet, bear interest, and back credit** — turning central-bank money into something functionally close to a **tokenised deposit**. Few CBDC programmes have floated this for *retail*; most protect banks via holding caps instead. That framing is the novelty.
- **Traction = zero.** This is a **statement of intent with a ~2029 horizon and a ~2-year dual-model coexistence** afterwards — i.e. a decision not before ~2031. No pilot, no participants, no rules. De-PR: "tests"/"считает возможным" ≠ live; this is the earliest possible policy stage.
- **Who captures the margin / where value moves — and the key tension:** under today's design the **CBR captures the rail, the data, and the float**; banks lose deposits. Under the proposed design, **banks recover the float and the interest spread**, and the digital ruble becomes a deposit competitor to *other banks' deposits* rather than to the banking system as a whole. **But** this partly **defeats the purpose of a CBDC** — a balance on a bank balance sheet is a bank liability exposed to bank credit risk, not risk-free central-bank money. So the value-add depends entirely on which model wins: keep it central-bank money and banks stay disintermediated; move it to bank balance sheets and it stops being a "true" CBDC. (analysis)
- **What breaks the value-add:** if the two-model experiment concludes the bank-balance-sheet version is just a rebadged deposit, the CBDC rationale collapses; if it concludes the CBR-platform version starves banks, adoption stalls. Either way, the instrument may end up **structurally redundant** with existing bank deposits + fast-payment rails (the reason Norway declined a CBDC entirely — efficient rails already exist). (hypothesis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Timeline:** mandatory rollout of the *current* model 1 Sep 2026 → 2027 → 2028; the **bank-balance-sheet experiment only "closer to 2029,"** dual models ~2 years, decision ~2031. This is a long-horizon design question layered on top of a near-term rollout.
- **Sentiment:** banks have wanted exactly this — a way to keep deposit float and earn on CBDC balances — so bank reception is likely **favourable**; the CBR is the reluctant party, since interest-bearing bank-held CBDC dilutes its control and blurs the CBDC/deposit line. The broader Russian backdrop (private-savings mobilisation law [[Финтехно Russia approves new private-savings mobilization law]], CBR caution on private payment systems [[Russia's central bank rejects Sber, Alfa, T-Bank payment system project]]) shows a regulator actively re-architecting deposit/payment flows.
- **Why the market goes this way / counterintuitive second-order effect:** the CBR set out to build central-bank money that **bypasses banks**; adoption pressure is now pushing it toward a design where **banks are back in the middle**, earning the spread. If that path wins, the digital ruble ends up as a **state-mandated tokenised-deposit standard** — real economic effect (cheaper programmable rails, forced interoperability) but far less of the disintermediation/control the project was sold on. The counterintuitive outcome: the "revolutionary" CBDC may **survive only by becoming a bank deposit again**, and the deposit-disintermediation fear that drove bank resistance may prove self-limiting because the regulator blinks first. (hypothesis)

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
_Red-team / second-order questions. Each answered from sources or marked "open"._

1. **Is this actually new, or a re-run of the payroll-tariff story?** New development, same underlying conflict. [[Финтехно Russian central bank sets digital ruble payroll tariffs]] (Jun 2026) addressed the deposit-disintermediation tension via a tiny *routing subsidy* while keeping the CBR-platform, no-interest model. This item proposes a **structurally different fix** — moving balances onto bank balance sheets so they can bear interest and back credit. Distinct policy, so **fresh**, not a duplicate.

2. **Is it live?** No. It is a **proposal/consultation** with a "~2029" horizon and a ~2-year dual-model coexistence afterwards (decision ~2031). No pilot, participants, rules, or draft law retrieved. "Tests"/"считает возможным" ≠ live.

3. **Is the current mandatory rollout affected?** No. The 1 Sep 2026 mandatory rollout still uses the **CBR-platform, no-interest, no-bank-credit** model. This is a future design question layered on top.

4. **Does this defeat the purpose of a CBDC?** Partly — the core tension. A balance on a **commercial-bank balance sheet is a bank liability with bank credit risk**, not risk-free central-bank money. Making the digital ruble interest-bearing and bank-held pushes it toward a **tokenised deposit** rather than a "true" retail CBDC. (analysis)

5. **Why is the CBR conceding this now?** Because the payroll subsidy didn't neutralise bank resistance and the no-cap/no-interest design maximises disintermediation. The deeper fix is to change the instrument's *form* so banks profit rather than lose float. (analysis, consistent with ABR pushback reporting)

6. **How do peers handle the same problem?** Differently: the **digital euro caps holdings** (~€3,000) to protect deposits ([[Italy's banks back digital euro but want costs phased]]); **India** kept retail CBDC and **tokenised deposits** separate ([[RBI launches certificate of deposit tokenisation pilot]]); **China** keeps e-CNY a strict non-interest CB liability. Russia contemplating merging CBDC with a bank-held interest-bearing account is the **outlier**. (sourced + corpus)

7. **Does Russia cap digital-ruble holdings?** No — unusually, no holding limit, which makes its disintermediation risk structurally higher than cap-based designs and is exactly why a form-change fix is on the table. (sourced)

8. **Who is silent about what?** No CBR primary release for this specific proposal was retrieved; the channel is the source. Silent on: legal form of a bank-held CBDC, deposit-insurance treatment, credit-risk/capital rules, fraud liability, and whether interest would be CBR-set or bank-set. All **open**.

9. **Who benefits — banks or the CBR?** Banks (recover float + interest spread + credit product); the CBR gives up control and blurs the CBDC/deposit line. So bank sentiment is likely favourable, CBR is the reluctant party — inverting the usual CBDC dynamic. (analysis)

10. **What does the two-model coexistence really test?** Whether a bank-balance-sheet CBDC is anything more than a rebadged deposit. If the answer is "no," the CBDC rationale weakens; if the CBR-platform model starves banks, adoption stalls. Either resolution is awkward. (analysis)

11. **Could the whole instrument prove redundant?** Plausibly. If it converges to a tokenised deposit, it overlaps existing bank deposits + fast-payment rails — the efficiency argument Norway used to *decline* a CBDC. Redundancy is a real downside trigger. (hypothesis)

12. **Does this change the central question of the assessment?** Yes. Press frame: "CBR tests CBDC for deposits/credit." One level deeper: the CBR is **walking back the disintermediating design that was the point of its CBDC** to buy adoption — so the real question is not "new feature" but **"will the digital ruble survive as central-bank money or quietly become a bank deposit with a CBDC label?"** (analysis)

13. **Is there a sanctions/geopolitical angle?** The digital ruble is also a sanctions-resilience play (Carnegie), but this specific deposit/credit-form question is a **domestic banking-structure** issue, not an external-settlement one. Low geopolitical load here. (sourced)

14. **What would falsify the "it stays symbolic" read?** A CBR draft law or named-bank pilot for balance-sheet-held, interest-bearing digital rubles **before 2029**, or a concrete interest-accrual mechanism — none exists today. Absent that, this is a long-horizon signal. (analysis)

15. **Near-term market impact?** Minimal — ~2029+ horizon, no operational change, no numbers. High *analytical* interest (it reframes the CBDC's endgame), low near-term effect. (analysis)

Importance: 3/5 — A genuinely new and analytically important policy signal: the CBR openly reconsidering the *form* of the digital ruble (bank-balance-sheet, interest-bearing, credit-backing) is a tacit admission that its disintermediating design threatens bank funding, and it reframes the CBDC's endgame toward a tokenised deposit. But it is an early-stage proposal with a ~2029 horizon, no pilot/participants/rules, and no near-term market impact — high interest, low immediacy.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The digital ruble is Russia's retail CBDC, now in a mandated rollout: systemically important banks must offer digital-ruble transactions from 2026-09-01, universal-licence banks and larger merchants from 2027-09-01, the rest from 2028-09-01 (per Bank of Russia, cbr.ru, as of 2026). The pilot to date is thin — deputy governor Kakhrumanova cites >20 banks, ~2,500 users and >90,000 transactions (via web coverage, 2026), i.e. still a lab, not adoption. The relevant sector to protect is the Russian deposit base: household deposits stood at ~₽62–68tn (~$770bn–$790bn) through 2025, with ~₽46.9tn of that in fixed-term accounts as of 2025-04 (per Central Bank data, via The Moscow Times, 2025). Deposit growth was already slowing to a 3-year low and saw outflows in mid-2025/2026 as rates fell — so the sector is funding-sensitive *before* any CBDC drain. Why now: the current design deliberately neutered the CBDC to protect banks — balances sit on the CBR platform, earn no interest, cannot be lent, and face a holding limit; the bank is a pass-through UI (per note; corroborated by cbr.ru). This step tests reversing that: letting banks hold digital-ruble wallets **on their own balance sheet** so they can pay interest and lend — the classic knob for driving CBDC adoption without bleeding the sector.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs here: deposit base / funding cost (share of cheap current-account funding), CBDC holding limit, and interest-bearing vs non-bearing status. The "players" are central-bank design choices, not firms. Global precedent on the same knob:
- **Digital euro (ECB):** explicitly "should not bear interest" and holding-limit-capped precisely to avoid disintermediation; Italian banks (ABI) back it but demand costs be phased (per ledgerinsights, 2025-11). See `[[Italy's banks back digital euro but want costs phased]]`.
- **e-rupee (RBI, India):** non-interest-bearing, yet NBER research (Di Maggio et al., w32457, 2024) finds even a non-interest CBDC lowered bank deposits — direct evidence the "no interest" guardrail is *not* fully protective.
- **US / Fed note:** stablecoin/CBDC deposit-substitution modelled explicitly as a threat to credit intermediation (federalreserve.gov feds-notes, 2025). See `[[Circle backs Fed plan to integrate stablecoins into payment systems]]`.
- **Norway (Norges Bank):** declined to recommend a retail CBDC at all (2025-12) — the opposite tack.
Russia's position: an outlier moving *toward* an interest-bearing, on-balance-sheet CBDC while the ECB/RBI hold the line on non-interest + limits. That is more adoption-friendly but structurally more disintermediating.

**Comps & multiples.** No company, no valuation — trading multiples N/A. Internal comparables are CBDC/tokenised-deposit precedents in the base:
- `[[RBI launches certificate of deposit tokenisation pilot]]` (2025-10) — tokenised *deposits* on a wholesale CBDC rail, an alternative to putting retail CBDC on bank balance sheets.
- `[[RBI introduces offline CBDC payments]]` (2025-10) — adoption-driver playbook.
- `[[Italy's banks back digital euro but want costs phased]]` (2025-11), `[[Norway's central bank declines to recommend a CBDC]]` (2025-12) — the interest/limit and go/no-go debates.
- `[[Circle backs Fed plan to integrate stablecoins into payment systems]]` (2026-02) — deposit-disintermediation framework.
Multiple = "no data" (no financial subject).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Disintermediation / funding drain.** An interest-bearing, on-balance-sheet digital ruble competes directly with the ~₽62–68tn deposit base. NBER (India) shows even *non*-interest CBDC pulled deposits; an interest-bearing one is worse. Second-order: higher funding cost → tighter credit supply, in a sector already seeing deposit outflows.
2. **Timing / execution — vapourware risk.** The note attributes an on-balance-sheet experiment to "closer to 2029," with two account types coexisting ~2 years before consolidation (per Финтехно, Kakhrumanova). That is a multi-year "maybe," not a decision — and the mandated *payments* rollout (2026–2028) is a separate, nearer track. Treat the deposit/credit variant as announced intent `(analysis)`, not policy.
3. **Reversal of the core guardrail.** The entire "won't hurt banks" promise rested on no-interest + no-lending + holding limit. Testing the removal of those signals the CBR itself doubts adoption under the safe design — a live regulatory-uncertainty flag for bank net-interest margins.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` If Russia proceeds, it becomes a real-world test of the one lever ECB/RBI have refused to pull — an interest-bearing retail CBDC on commercial balance sheets — making it a global reference case for whether CBDCs *must* stay non-interest to spare banks. Falsifiable thesis: the deposit/credit variant will *not* materially advance before ~2028–2029 and stays a design study, not a product; trigger that breaks it = CBR publishing concrete on-balance-sheet pilot rules or a named participating bank. Watch: mandated-rollout adoption in Sep-2026 as the real near-term signal, since weak uptake is exactly what would push CBR toward the disintermediating interest-bearing design.
