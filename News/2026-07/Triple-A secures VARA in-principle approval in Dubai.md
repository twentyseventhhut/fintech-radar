Triple-A secures VARA in-principle approval in Dubai
Claudia Pincovski
15 Jul 2026 / 4 Min Read
Singapore-based payment institution Triple-A has been granted In-Principle Approval (IPA) for broker-dealer services from Dubai's Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority (VARA). The approval marks a step toward a full licence and follows an ongoing regulatory assessment of the company's operations.
A selective regulatory framework
VARA is described as an independent regulator overseeing the provision, use, and exchange of virtual assets in and from the emirate of Dubai. According to the source material, the VARA-licensed market remains one of the more restrictive virtual asset frameworks globally, with just over 50 entities currently holding full licences.
Receiving an IPA represents a defined regulatory threshold within VARA's licensing structure, positioned ahead of the final stage before a full licence is issued. Triple-A's licensing assessment is still in progress. The company has now entered a specified period during which it must complete operational readiness requirements and address any remaining post-IPA conditions before a full licence can be granted.
Existing compliance footprint
Triple-A holds licences in the EU and Singapore under the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) and the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) framework, respectively. In the US, the company holds Money Transmitter Licenses across more than 20 jurisdictions and is registered as a Money Services Business in both the US and Canada.
Per the source material, compliance is the company's second-largest department after engineering, and has been part of its operational structure since its founding. The IPA from VARA is presented as an extension of this existing regulatory footprint into the Dubai market.
Company statement
A company official said the milestone builds on the payments infrastructure the company has already developed for the direction the market is heading, adding that it reflects work carried out by the compliance, legal, and cross-functional teams involved, alongside the company's stated commitment to operating within regulated frameworks.
Context
Dubai's virtual asset regulatory regime has developed as a distinct licensing structure for firms operating in digital assets, separate from broader UAE federal frameworks. For payment and crypto-asset firms already operating under EU, US, and Singapore licensing regimes, an IPA from VARA adds a further jurisdiction to an existing compliance base, ahead of the completion of a full licensing process.

# Context-enrichment: Triple-A secures VARA in-principle approval in Dubai
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 2026-07-15, Singapore-based payment institution Triple-A received an **In-Principle Approval (IPA)** from Dubai's Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority (VARA) for **broker-dealer services**. De-PR'd, this is a mid-process regulatory milestone, **not a live licence**: an IPA under VARA's two-stage flow confirms core eligibility but **does not permit any commercial transactions**. Triple-A now enters a defined window (guides cite ~12 months) to complete operational-readiness / post-IPA conditions and pay the remaining (~50%) fees before a full VASP licence is issued. So nothing is authorised to go live in Dubai yet.

**Why structured this way / what it reveals.** Two tells. (1) The scope is **broker-dealer**, one of VARA's seven categories — VARA's *trading/dealing* bucket (order arrangement, dealing on own account, market-making, placement). This does **not** map to Triple-A's actual core business, which is stablecoin *payment acceptance and payouts* — that would sit under VARA's separate **"Payments & Remittances"** category. So either Triple-A is opening a new activity line, or broker-dealer is a first foothold; the PR doesn't explain the mismatch. (2) The note leans hard on Triple-A's *existing* licence stack (MiCA/France, MAS, US MTLs 20+ states, US/Canada MSB) — a familiar "most-regulated payments institution" framing used since its Oct-2023 Series A. The UAE line is being appended to that footprint as breadth, not as UAE depth. The source itself concedes the assessment "is still in progress."

## [1] Competitors / peers
This is a well-trodden VARA path, not a first mover. The closest internal parallel is **[[Kraken secures VARA authorization to expand in UAE]]** / **[[Dubai's VARA grants Kraken's Payward preliminary approval]]** — Kraken's Payward took the *identical* "preliminary approval for a broker-dealer, investment and management licence" route (May–Jun 2026). Same regulator, same category, same "milestone-not-launch" structure. Others on the same broker-dealer IPA conveyor per external research: **Rain** (MENA FZE — VARA IPA 2025, now holding CBB + ADGM FSRA + VARA, the fullest GCC footprint), plus Shipfinex, ARP Digital, XBase, Relm.

For Triple-A's *actual* line (stablecoin payments/settlement in UAE), the leaders are already live and further along: **Circle** (ADGM/FSRA Money-Services permission, Dec 2025, USDC settlement operational), and AED-stablecoin issuers **[[Zand issues regulated dirham-backed AED stablecoin]]** (CBUAE, Nov 2025) and **AE Coin** (CBUAE final approval Dec 2024, live with e&). VARA itself has grown restrictive/selective — see **[[Dubai's VARA penalizes Fuze over anti-money laundering gaps]]** (enforcement, Sep 2025) — reinforcing that a licence *carries* weight, but an IPA is not yet that.

**Why the lay of the land is this way / second-order.** The ~50-entity full-licence count (external: VARA's 50th VASP licence issued 2026-06-29; reportedly only ~39 actively operating end-2025) is the real "scarcity" signal, and Triple-A isn't in it yet. Position: **catching up**, not ahead. The differentiator Triple-A can credibly claim is *global* multi-jurisdiction breadth, not UAE lead — and on the day, Revolut also announced VARA approval, diluting signal.

## [2] Company history / fit
Triple-A: founded ~2017 (operational Singapore launch ~2020) by **Eric Barbier** (ex-Thunes, ex-TransferTo — serial cross-border-payments founder). MAS Major Payment Institution (first DPT-licensed firm, 2021); ~$10M Series A led by Peak XV + Shorooq + 1982 Ventures (2023-10-25, ~$14M total raised). Claims 1,000+ enterprise customers (Razer, Farfetch, Grab) reaching 700M+ digital-currency owners. Recent activity in the corpus is partnership-led: **[[Wego partners with Triple-A for stablecoin travel payments]]** (May 2026, Dubai/MENA travel) and **[[Yuno and Triple-A partner to enable stablecoin acceptance]]** (May 2026).

**Why the company acts this way.** Triple-A's moat is regulatory breadth ("most-regulated stablecoin payments institution") — a commodity payment-gateway function differentiated by compliance surface. Adding VARA is consistent with that strategy: each new licence is a marketing asset and a market-access key. The Wego tie-up (MENA travel, Dubai-datelined) gives a plausible commercial reason to want a Dubai perimeter. Note the Abu Dhabi thread: Series A backer **Shorooq is Abu-Dhabi-based** — a regional relationship predating this, though no prior UAE office/VARA news surfaced.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Low novelty, no incremental traction. What's genuinely new: Triple-A's *first* VARA/UAE regulatory step (no prior Triple-A VARA note exists in-corpus → this is a **fresh** event, not a reprint). What is NOT new: the mechanism (IPA→full licence) is identical to Kraken's a month earlier, and IPA authorises **zero** live activity. No new clients, GMV, or UAE go-live — an IPA is by definition pre-commercial.

**Why value-add is thin (1–2 levels deeper).** Value in stablecoin acceptance accrues to whoever captures the merchant relationship + the FX/settlement spread. In the UAE specifically, AED-referenced stablecoin acceptance falls to **CBUAE**, not VARA — so a VARA broker-dealer licence doesn't even unlock the most obvious UAE stablecoin-payments use case. The margin-capturers already onshore (Circle for USDC settlement; Zand/AE Coin for AED issuance) are ahead of where a pre-licence Triple-A sits. The broker-dealer scope, if taken literally, points Triple-A at *trading/dealing* economics it doesn't currently run — raising the question of whether this is strategic pivot signalling or just the category that was fastest to clear.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
The real event to watch is **conversion of the IPA to a full licence** (post-IPA conditions closed within the window) — and, more tellingly, whether Triple-A pairs it with a **Payments & Remittances** licence (the actual fit) and/or moves toward AED-stablecoin acceptance (which needs CBUAE). Broad UAE sentiment stays strongly positive: 50 VASP licences by mid-2026, a maturing CBUAE PTSR (July 2024) / VARA / ADGM stack, blue-chip issuers live. MENA crypto market ~$110B (2024), projected ~$234B by 2033 (external).

**Why / counterintuitive second-order.** The proliferation of near-identical VARA "approval" headlines (Kraken, Rain, Revolut, now Triple-A) means each individual one carries *less* signal — the moat is no longer "being in Dubai" but converting to a full licence and shipping live volume. For Triple-A, a broker-dealer licence that doesn't map to its payments core could end up an under-utilised permission unless it either expands activity or adds the payments category — i.e., the licence's strategic value is contingent, not automatic.

Fresh (first Triple-A VARA step, distinct from the Kraken notes) but low-weight. Would upgrade only on full-licence conversion or a Payments & Remittances add.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Triple-A sits in regulated stablecoin cross-border payments — the "real-economy payments" slice, not trading. Genuine (non-trading) stablecoin payment activity reached ~$390bn in 2025, more than double 2024 (per McKinsey/Artemis, via getlatka/BCG citations, as of 2025); B2B stablecoin cross-border is a fast-growing subset (Juniper projects it toward multi-$tn by 2035 — figure paywalled, treat as directional). Asia originated ~60% of global stablecoin payment volume (per McKinsey/Artemis), which fits Triple-A's Singapore base. **Structure:** the value chain is fragmenting into (a) issuers (Circle/Tether), (b) rails/orchestration (Triple-A, BVNK, Bridge, Fipto), (c) acquirers/networks (Mastercard Move/Thunes). Barriers here are almost entirely *regulatory + compliance* — capital and network effects are secondary; Triple-A explicitly makes compliance its 2nd-largest department after engineering, i.e. licensing IS the moat. **Why now:** the MiCA→CASP transition (June 2026) plus a MENA licensing land-grab is turning "which jurisdictions you're licensed in" into the primary competitive axis. Second-order: as licences proliferate, the differentiator shifts from *having* a licence to *breadth* of a multi-jurisdiction stack under one counterparty.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: TPV/settlement volume, take rate on payment/payout, number of live licences/jurisdictions, and time-to-full-licence (IPA → Full). **Basis of competition = regulatory perimeter + distribution**, not price. Key players in regulated crypto-payments/VARA perimeter: Kraken/Payward (VARA preliminary approval, 2026-06-04, broker-dealer/investment [[Dubai's VARA grants Kraken's Payward preliminary approval]]), Revolut (VARA approval for UAE crypto services, 2026-07-15 — same-day peer move, per The National), ARP Digital and Relm (VARA IPA broker-dealer, 2026), plus payment-rail peers BVNK, Bridge, Fipto. **Recent Triple-A moves:** MiCA CASP from AMF France (2026-06-01, one of only two AMF holders with both PI + CASP [[Triple-A secures MiCA CASP licence from France's AMF]]); EU multicurrency stablecoin accounts (2026-06 [[Triple-A launches stablecoin multicurrency accounts in Europe]]); Mastercard Move integration (2026-02/03); Circle Payments Network settlement (2026-03). **Position:** breadth leader among *payment-institution* (not exchange) stablecoin players — EU (MiCA), Singapore (MAS), US MTLs in 20+ states, US/Canada MSB, now Dubai VARA IPA. It is *catching up* to Kraken/Revolut inside the VARA perimeter specifically (both are further along than an IPA), but *ahead* on multi-jurisdiction payment-rail licensing as a category. Moat = intangible/regulatory (accumulated multi-jurisdiction licences + compliance stack) `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** Triple-A is private; getlatka lists 2025 ~$39.7M ARR at a ~$119.1M valuation → **P/S = $119.1M / $39.7M = 3.0x** (getlatka figures are self-reported/estimated — treat as `[UNSOURCED]`-grade, indicative only). Prior raise: ~$10M Series A (Peak XV/1982 Ventures/Shorooq) — round size, not post-money. If the $119.1M/$39.7M holds, 3.0x sales is *cheap* for a payments-infra name growing off a doubling market — but the input is unverified, so no rich/cheap call is defensible. Internal comps (regulatory/VARA precedent, not valuation): [[Kraken secures VARA authorization to expand in UAE]], [[Dubai's VARA grants Kraken's Payward preliminary approval]], [[SmartCrowd's Nawy Shares wins VARA approval for tokenized real estate (2)]]. Public trading multiples: **no data** — no comparable listed pure-play regulated stablecoin-payments issuer with a free EV/Revenue. Distribution not computed (only one, unverified, valuation figure).

**Risk flags.**
1. **IPA ≠ Full Licence — no revenue yet in Dubai.** VARA's two-stage model bars customer-facing activity until Full Licence; Triple-A is still in the operational-readiness window and "may fail post-IPA conditions." The headline overstates market access; monetisation in Dubai is a future, conditional event.
2. **Licensing arms race / commoditisation.** VARA hit ~49–50 licensed entities by mid-2026 (per valuethemarkets/EdaFace), and same-day Revolut + Kraken/Payward + ARP + Relm are all inside or entering the perimeter. As multi-jurisdiction licensing becomes table stakes, the "compliance-as-moat" thesis weakens — the moat erodes exactly as more firms replicate it. Only ~39 of licensees were *actively operating* end-2025, i.e. a licence does not equal live volume.
3. **Disintermediation up/down the stack.** Triple-A is orchestration in the middle; issuers (Circle/Tether) and networks (Mastercard Move/Thunes, on which Triple-A depends) can capture take-rate or route around the middle layer — margin risk from dependence on someone else's rails.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is *breadth accumulation*, not a re-rating catalyst — one more jurisdiction on an already-broad licence map. The falsifiable thesis: if regulatory breadth is a durable moat, Triple-A converts IPA→Full Licence and turns Dubai into live MENA payment volume within ~2–4 quarters, differentiating on stack-breadth vs single-market rivals. What breaks it: same-day Revolut/Kraken approvals show the VARA perimeter filling fast — if breadth becomes commoditised faster than Triple-A monetises it, the licence map stops being a moat. Watch: Full-Licence issuance date and first disclosed MENA TPV.
