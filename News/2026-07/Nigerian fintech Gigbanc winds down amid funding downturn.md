# Nigerian fintech Gigbanc winds down amid funding downturn

> [!info] 2026-07-13 · 1 упоминаний · 0 источника(ов) с текстом

## Агрегированный текст (из дайджестов)

[Connecting the Dots in Fintech] 🇳🇬 Nigerian FinTech Gigbanc is winding down operations, becoming the latest casualty of the funding downturn affecting early-stage startups across Africa. The company, which set out to build cross-border financial infrastructure for African freelancers, creators, entrepreneurs and businesses, confirmed the decision in a statement signed by its co-founders.

## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
**[0] What happened (one line).** Gigbanc, a Nigerian cross-border neobank for freelancers/creators/remote workers, is winding down operations after ~3 years, citing an inability to raise capital plus high KYC and infrastructure costs; customers must convert balances to naira and withdraw by 2026-07-31. The company says it is in acquisition talks with an undisclosed Nigerian fintech infrastructure provider. (Confirmed by co-founder statement, reported 2026-07-10/11.)

**[1] Company background (confirmed).**
- Founded 2023 by Paul Okundaye (co-founder & CEO; Harvard/Ivey MBA background) and Babatope Oni (co-founder & CTO; engineer, Obafemi Awolowo University).
- Product: multi-currency wallets (NGN/USD/EUR), virtual dollar cards, FX, bill payments, transfers to 200+ Nigerian banks; targeted Africans earning income from abroad (freelancers, creators, remote workers, digital businesses).
- Traction claimed: 150,000+ users across 30+ countries; processed 10bn+ naira (~$7.2M) in lifetime payment volume.
- Community programs: Global Talent Fellowship, GigConnect, GigSocial.
- NOT disclosed / unconfirmed: total external funding raised, specific investors/backers, headcount, layoff numbers, customer-fund balances held. Sources explicitly note Gigbanc did not disclose fundraising details. Treat "funding raised/backers" as UNKNOWN — do not invent.

**[2] Wind-down mechanics (confirmed).** Reasons given: (1) inability to raise fresh capital, (2) high KYC/compliance costs, (3) high infrastructure costs. Customer deadline 2026-07-31 to convert to NGN and withdraw non-fraudulent funds free of charge. Acquisition talks ongoing (counterparty undisclosed) — an asset/infra acquihire is plausible but unconfirmed as of writing.

**[3] Sector context — African/Nigerian funding downturn (confirmed).**
- African startup funding fell ~25% in 2024 to ~$2.2bn (second consecutive down year); H1 2024 African tech funding ~$780M, lowest since late 2020, down ~57% y/y.
- Fintech hit hardest: continental fintech funding down ~70% y/y in H1 2024.
- Nigeria led 2024 shutdowns: 6 of 11 African startups that shut/hibernated/entered administration were Nigerian. Drivers: risk-averse investors amid higher global rates, regulatory shifts, high compliance/infra costs.
- Comparable failures: Thepeer (Nigerian wallet-API, shut 2024 despite $2.1M seed — compliance + slow adoption); Joovlin (Nigerian fintech, shut Jan 2025). Gigbanc is the latest 2026 casualty in the same cross-border-payments niche.

- [[Kenyan remittance startup Bonto shuts down]] — Kenyan remittance/FX startup, shut Sept 2025, collapsing FX margins and unrealistic breakeven scale (same cross-border economics squeeze).

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Sub-vertical: African cross-border neobanking for freelancers/creators (multi-currency wallets, virtual USD cards, FX, payouts) — a thin slice of Africa's fintech stack, itself the continent's largest equity sector. Africa-wide funding is in a hard downturn: African tech startups raised just ~$260m in Q2 2026, down ~40% YoY vs ~$427m; the year is tracking to ~$1.28bn vs $1.6bn in 2025 (per Disrupt Africa / au-startups, as of Jul 2026). Debt is displacing equity ($340m debt vs $364m equity in first 4 months of 2026, vs $138m debt / $652m equity in 2025) — a signal that growth-equity for early-stage, unprofitable neobanks has dried up (analysis). Structure: highly fragmented at the freelancer-payments end (Grey, Geegpay/Raenest, Payday, plus incumbent Payoneer) and increasingly consolidated at the top of Nigerian retail fintech (OPay, PalmPay, Moniepoint dominate volume). Entry barriers are rising: KYC/compliance cost and cross-border FX-infrastructure cost — the two Gigbanc cited as fatal alongside inability to raise (per TechCabal, 10 Jul 2026). Why now: capital-cycle reversal + a shift to profitability/unit-economics scrutiny leaves sub-scale, cash-burning niche players stranded.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: users, TPV/payment volume, take rate, CAC/KYC cost per user, FX spread. Gigbanc's disclosed scale (per TechCabal): 150k+ users across 30+ countries, ~₦10bn (~$7.2m) processed over its *lifetime* — trivial vs peers. Direct freelancer-payments rivals: Grey.co (ex-Aboki), Geegpay (YC-backed Raenest), Payday, and global incumbent Payoneer — all offering named USD/GBP/EUR virtual accounts, the exact product Gigbanc built. Broader Nigerian neobank scale-leaders dwarf it: OPay 60m+ users, PalmPay 35m+, Moniepoint 16m (unicorn, >$1bn valuation, processed ~₦412tn / ~$294bn in 2025), Kuda 7m, plus FairMoney/Carbon in lending (per Innovation Village, Nairametrics-cited data, 2026). Basis of competition: distribution + FX rates + local-payout reliability; the freelancer niche has no network-effect moat and low switching costs (users hold accounts on multiple apps simultaneously). Gigbanc's position: sub-scale niche laggard with no defensible moat (analysis) — it "lost the cross-border payments race" (thecondia framing).

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round disclosed for Gigbanc (founded 2023; wind-down is a distressed exit, in acquisition talks with an unnamed Nigerian infra company) → market multiples "no data." Lifetime TPV ~$7.2m and ~$0 fresh capital make trading multiples meaningless. Internal comps (base): [[Chimoney shuts down after failing to raise capital]] — near-identical African fintech shutdown on failed fundraise (2026-05); [[Yellow Card discontinues retail services to focus on businesses]] — African fintech retreating from unprofitable retail toward B2B; [[Moni rebrands as Rank after acquisitions]] — Nigerian fintech consolidation via M&A; scale-leader contrast [[Billionaire Patrice Motsepe-backed bank TymeBank hits 12 million clients]] and [[MTN Nigeria's fintech arm earns $91.6 million in nine months]] show where capital and users concentrate. Per external data, fintech is the highest-share sector in African shutdowns (~24% of 29 shutdowns 2023–25; 58% of failures driven by financial difficulty, Startup Graveyard Africa). Verdict: not a valuation story — a survivorship/consolidation story.

**Risk flags.**
1. Funding-cliff / unit economics (sector-wide): sub-scale neobanks with high KYC + FX-infra fixed costs and no path to profitability are structurally uninvestable in a 40%-down equity market — expect more freelancer-payments shutdowns, not just Gigbanc. Second-order: capital and users consolidate onto OPay/PalmPay/Moniepoint/Grey/Geegpay, widening the scale gap.
2. Customer-fund / wind-down execution risk: withdrawals free only until 31 Jul 2026, then services end; distressed acquisition talks are unsigned (no timeline/terms). Second-order: any botched payout erodes trust across the whole freelancer-fintech category and invites regulatory scrutiny of e-money custody.
3. Regulatory/compliance cost creep: rising KYC cost was cited as a kill factor; CBN is actively reshaping the market (Jun-2026 circular capping issuing/acquiring share). Second-order: compliance overhead favors incumbents with scale to amortize it, raising the effective entry barrier for the next cohort.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) Not a re-rating — a consolidation/shakeout signal for African cross-border freelancer payments: the niche is collapsing into a handful of funded players (Grey, Geegpay/Raenest) plus incumbent Payoneer, with weaker entrants exiting or being absorbed. Falsifiable thesis: over the next 2–4 quarters, expect ≥1 more freelancer-payments neobank to shut or get acqui-hired, and surviving players to pivot toward B2B/infrastructure revenue (à la Yellow Card) where unit economics work. Watch: whether Gigbanc's unnamed acquirer materializes (validates "absorb, don't fund" dynamic) and Q3 2026 African funding print (a second sub-$300m quarter confirms the cliff). Thesis breaks if a large freelancer-focused equity round lands, reopening the niche.
