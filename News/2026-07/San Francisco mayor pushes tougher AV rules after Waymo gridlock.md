San Francisco mayor pushes for tougher rules after the Waymo traffic fiasco
It turns out that even San Francisco mayor Daniel Lurie, who once declared that the city should be a testbed for emerging tech , has his limits. Especially when that emerging tech creates a massive hours-long traffic jam that leaves thousands at a standstill.
Mayor Lurie has asked state regulators to bolster rules for autonomous vehicles nearly two weeks after Waymo robotaxis became immobile in heavy July 4 traffic, ran out of power, and blocked key streets, further compounding the gridlock. The traffic jam, which trapped municipal shuttles, became a citywide problem that affected thousands of people. 
In his letter to the state Department of Transportation, which was viewed by TechCrunch, Lurie pointed to two events — a widespread power outage in December and the Golden Gate Bridge fireworks show on July 4 that attracted 100,000 spectators — both of which led to dozens of stranded Waymo vehicles and paralyzed traffic. The San Francisco Chronicle first reported on the letter .
The events, he said in the letter, “demonstrated that California’s current regulatory framework does not adequately address how autonomous vehicles operate during major incidents, planned or not. California’s challenge now is not just whether autonomous vehicles can operate safely under normal conditions, but also whether they can perform reliably during extraordinary ones.”
Lurie said autonomous vehicle manufacturers should be able to demonstrate four “core operational capabilities” and asked the California Department of Transportation to establish statewide standards to prevent future problems like the July 4 gridlock incident. Under Lurie’s vision, companies would be required to immediately remove or relocate robotaxis from active travel lanes to keep people moving and be required to be able to adapt in real time, adjusting their routes, service area, and pickup and drop-off locations. Companies would also have to share real-time operations data with local agencies, including service disruptions, the locations of immobile robotaxis, and recovery efforts as well as demonstrate through testing that they can handle large influxes of people and traffic.
In an emailed statement, Waymo spokesperson Katherine Barna noted the company has successfully supported numerous large events in the city.
“We appreciate Mayor Lurie and the City of San Francisco for the ongoing partnership with Waymo, and keeping SF at the forefront of innovation,” the email reads. “Waymo has successfully supported some of the city’s biggest events, including FIFA World Cup games, this year’s Super Bowl and Fan Fast, and NBA All-Star Weekend. The City and Waymo share a mutual goal of providing safe and accessible transportation for visitors and residents alike. We will continue to partner with the City’s agencies, collaborating with them on the learnings based on the millions of rides we have provided in San Francisco.”
Any company that wants to operate a robotaxi service in California has to successfully navigate two testing and deployment permit processes, one administered by the state’s Department of Motor Vehicles and the other by the Public Utilities Commission. California’s existing regulatory framework is stricter than that of other states like Texas and Arizona, but that hasn’t dissuaded companies from trying to operate there.
San Francisco and the wider area that stretches south into Silicon Valley have long been a testbed for autonomous vehicle technology. Six companies, including Nuro, Waymo, and Zoox, hold driverless testing permits, which allow the vehicles to drive without a human safety operator behind the wheel. 
But the area has also become the launch point for commercial services, which requires other permits from the DMV and CPUC.
Waymo is the largest, with an estimated 1,000 robotaxis operating in the Bay Area today. But there are plenty of others either testing or poised to launch commercial operations, including Amazon-owned Zoox as well as a premium robotaxi service that will be operated by Uber. Tesla has a branded robotaxi service but it doesn’t use driverless vehicles, nor does it have the permits to do so. Instead, Tesla has a charter transportation permit, which allows its own drivers to pick up and drop off riders throughout San Francisco in vehicles equipped with its advanced driver-assistance system rather than fully autonomous software.
Waymo’s scale has made it the focal point for regulators in San Francisco and beyond. The company now operates in 11 cities and has said it completes more than 500,000 paid rides every week. In San Francisco, Lurie noted that Waymo had agreed to restrict its service on July 4 near the waterfront and had even assigned a representative to the city’s emergency center. But that wasn’t enough to keep the Waymos out of the heavy traffic that occurred outside of that district.
Lurie said these voluntary actions are no longer enough — a reflection of just how big Waymo’s fleet has become. He said the four proposed requirements “will not undermine autonomous vehicles; they will strengthen them.”

# Context-enrichment: San Francisco mayor pushes tougher AV rules after Waymo gridlock
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **Thursday, July 16, 2026**, SF Mayor Daniel Lurie sent a letter to the **California Department of Transportation (Caltrans) secretary** (first reported by the *SF Chronicle*, covered by TechCrunch the same day) asking the state to set statewide AV safety standards after two Waymo failures during major incidents: the **Dec 20, 2025** citywide power outage (~1,600 Waymo stoppages, 60+ vehicles manually moved, SF service halted) and the **July 4, 2026** Golden Gate Bridge fireworks gridlock (~100k spectators), where **30+ Waymos** froze in travel lanes, some ran out of charge and were towed, trapping riders and municipal shuttles for hours.

Lurie asks AV makers to demonstrate four "core operational capabilities": (a) immediately **remove/relocate** vehicles out of active travel lanes; (b) **adapt in real time** — routes, service area, pickup/dropoff; (c) **share real-time operations data** with local agencies (disruptions, immobile-vehicle locations, recovery); (d) **prove via testing** they can handle large influxes of people/traffic.

**Why structured this way / what it reveals (the anti-PR gate):** This is a **lobbying letter, not a rule** — and that is the whole story. SF cannot regulate robotaxis itself; California preempts local AV authority (CPUC + DMV, see [1]). Lurie writing to *Caltrans* — which doesn't even license robotaxis — underscores how little direct leverage the city has. The framing "voluntary actions are no longer enough" is the real tell: on July 4 Waymo had *already* voluntarily restricted service near the waterfront and staffed the city's emergency center, and it still wasn't enough — so the mayor is trying to convert a scale problem into a mandate he can't issue himself. Waymo's response (Katherine Barna) is textbook PR deflection: it lists FIFA World Cup / Super Bowl / NBA All-Star successes and never addresses the July 4 failure. (analysis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Zoox** (Amazon) — ~105-vehicle fleet; **just issued a software recall (July 17, 2026)** after a robotaxi was confused by heavy smoke at a fire scene (see [[Zoox recalls robotaxi software after heavy-smoke confusion]]). Poised for commercial launch.
- **Uber** — launching a *premium* robotaxi service; separately lobbying in DC in ways that pit it against Waymo (see [[Uber's robotaxi lobbying pits it against Waymo in DC]]); split from Waymo in Phoenix (see [[Waymo and Uber quietly part ways in Phoenix]]).
- **Tesla** — holds only a CPUC **charter-party (TCP)** permit in California; **not** driverless passenger authority. Runs human drivers with ADAS, not autonomy.
- **Nuro** — secured a CPUC testing passenger-service permit (~May 2026). **Cruise** — the ghost at the table: DMV suspended its permits within weeks of the Oct 2023 pedestrian-dragging; GM wound the unit down.

**Why the lay of the land / second-order:** Waymo is the focal point *because* it is the largest (~1,000 Bay Area robotaxis, 11 cities, 500k+ paid rides/week nationally). Scale is exactly what turns "an incident" into "a citywide problem" — 30 stranded cars from a 300-car SF fleet paralyzes traffic in a way 5 cars never would. So Waymo's dominance is also its regulatory liability: every peer's incident (Zoox smoke, first-responder blocking) tightens the net around the category leader. The pressure is **sectoral, not name-specific** — it targets the AV category, but lands hardest on whoever has the most vehicles on the road.

## [2] Company history / fit
Waymo has scaled aggressively into 2026: ~500k paid rides/week nationally (10× vs May 2024), targeting 1M/week by year-end, and announced 4 more cities on July 8, 2026 — days *before* the Lurie letter. It has repeatedly hit the same failure mode: cars freezing en masse during atypical events (Dec 2025 blackout → Waymo blamed "confirmation checks" at dark signals, promised fleet updates; then July 4 again). **Why the company acts this way:** the growth story *requires* rapid geographic and volume expansion to justify its valuation and outrun rivals, but the operational design (remote fleet-response confirmations, charge management) doesn't yet scale to extraordinary-conditions load. Structural tension: expansion pace outruns edge-case robustness, and every high-visibility failure invites exactly the regulation that could cap the fleet. (analysis)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**What's genuinely new here:** a *mayor* of the flagship AV testbed publicly declaring Waymo's voluntary cooperation insufficient and asking the state to mandate operational standards. That is a shift in tone from a city that branded itself a "testbed for emerging tech." **But the value-add of the letter itself is thin:** it is a request to a state agency, not binding, and SF already lost this fight (2023–2025 CPUC challenge, appellate court denied). The substantive threat to Waymo is **not** Lurie's letter but pending state legislation and existing law (see [4]). Traction of the *complaint* is real, though: SF Supervisors launched inquiries, the firefighters' union is vocal, and this piles onto the NHTSA first-responder directive (see [[NHTSA demands autonomous vehicles stop blocking first responders]]) and Zoox's recall — a converging regulatory-attention wave, not a one-off.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Jurisdiction is the crux:** DMV = testing/deployment permits; CPUC = commercial passenger service/fares. Cities are preempted. Real teeth come from **state law**: **AB 1777** (signed Sept 2024, key emergency-geofencing + 2-way-comms provisions **effective July 1, 2026** — days before July 4) lets local officials order fleets out of an area within minutes; **SB 1246** (Cortese, 2025–26, advancing) would mandate clearing obstructing AVs (≤5 min if driveable / ≤30 min if towing), US-based licensed remote operators, and a ≥1:3 remote-assistant ratio. **SB 915** (the prior local-control bill) died in 2024.
- **Does it slow expansion? Marginally.** Waymo is still expanding (4 new cities July 8). No permit-suspension signal as of July 16.
- **Cruise as prior art — but a weaker trigger:** Cruise's collapse followed **injury + a false federal report**; Waymo's incidents are **congestion/immobility**, historically a much lower escalation trigger for DMV suspension. **Why the market likely tolerates this:** regulators distinguish "blocked traffic" from "dragged a pedestrian." The counterintuitive second-order risk is reputational compounding — each visible freeze (blackout, fireworks, smoke) normalizes the "not ready at scale" narrative and hands legislators (SB 1246) the political cover to impose fleet-clearance clocks that raise Waymo's per-vehicle operating cost, even without a suspension. That cost drag — not a ban — is the realistic downside.
