The popular open source AI tool Ollama has raised a $65 million Series B, led by Theory Ventures, founder and CEO Jeff Morgan tells TechCrunch.

This round follows a previous $15 million Series A led by Benchmark’s Peter Fenton. All told, the company has now raised $88 million.

Ollama, which launched in 2023, helps devs run open-weight AI models on their PCs, getting them up and running in minutes. It has been praised by developers across countless training sites, videos, blogs and social media posts. It has amassed 176,000 stars and nearly 17,000 forks on GitHub.

Developers can also use Ollama to find models and access larger, more complex ones that it hosts on its neocloud via several subscription tiers, from free to $100/month. It also tracks usage based on GPU time, not token limits.

If the mission to help developers more easily build on their PCs sounds vaguely familiar, it should. Morgan and his co-founder Michael Chiang previously helped build Docker Desktop.They landed at Docker after it bought their previous startup, Kitematic. Docker makes containers that help cloud apps easy to move from cloud to cloud, or from desktop to cloud, abstracting away all the pesky hardware configuration issues.

So Ollama essentially did for AI what Docker and Docker Desktop did for cloud.

“Open models started coming out in 2023 but they were really hard to use,” Morgan said. They had been geared toward researchers at the time, not programmers. “As a result, it was really hard to get them up and running.”Three years after launching, Ollama is now “used by over 8.9 million developers every month, sitting in 85% of the Fortune 500 and growing like crazy,” he said. All with only 14 employees.

That career experience is what drew Benchmark’s Peter Fenton to lead its earlier round and join the board.

“What Jeff and Michael built with Docker is being used by 10 million-plus developers every day. The creative powers to create a product that goes to ubiquity for developers is extremely rare,” Fenton told TechCrunch.

Morgan and Fenton declined to discuss the startup’s revenues and new valuation. However, Morgan says that the proving point for Ollama as a business happened around January, when OpenClaw became hot. That’s when larger open models “suddenly became able to do these agentic tasks, like coding. Obviously, we saw the explosion of the assistants like OpenClaw, and this idea that open models can get real work done.”

Since then, the industry has been abuzz with the idea that paying users (particularly deep-pocketed enterprises and fast-growing AI application-layer startups) will increasingly turn to more affordable open models, reserving their use of closed models like Anthropic for more of an as-needed basis.

“I still think that this is the part that most of the debate gets wrong. It’s not an either/or,” Fenton says of open versus closed AI models. There will be plenty of business for both, he contends. However, every company with high inference expenses — the costs of using the models — has a “vital existential project” pushing them to move “to open-weight models,” he says.

There’s plenty of evidence that such startups and enterprises are already turning to open models for their daily needs. That, obviously, bodes well for Ollama’s cloud business.

But even more interesting, Ollama is another example of how AI is birthing a large new crop of open source projects that are turning into companies pursued by VCs.There are open source inference providers like Inferact, maker of vLLM,and RadixArk, maker of SGLang. There is OpenClaw and its alternatives like NanoClaw. There are even tiny startups building their own open models from scratch, like Arcee.

To be sure, not every Ollama fan has been happy that the company has been pursuing making a living. About a year ago,a bunch of blog and social media posts complained that its cloud business was drawing attention away from its beloved free project and cited Ollama as an example of the so-called “Enshittification” of dev tools, as the trend is called.

But Morgan sees its cloud service as an evolution of its open source mission to help programmers find and easily use models. Those state-of-the-art, large, open models are often “too big to run on your own computer. So we said, ‘Hey, let’s help find the compute for that,’” he explained.

Board member Fenton adds, “Nothing has changed for the core product that’s free on the desktop. There’s zero change to the premise that this is the place you can discover and run local models.”

# Context-enrichment: Ollama raises $65M Series B, nears 9M developers
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Ollama (Palo Alto; launched 2023) raised a **$65M Series B led by Theory Ventures**; participation from Benchmark (led the prior $15M Series A, Peter Fenton on board), 8VC, Y Combinator, Pace Capital, 49 Palms, GTMFund, angels. **Total raised now $88M** (TechCrunch, 2026-07-09).
- **Valuation NOT disclosed** — Morgan and Fenton "declined to discuss revenues and new valuation." This is the single biggest silence: an $88M cumulative raise is modest, so the withheld valuation is likely the story's soft underbelly (analysis).
- Metrics claimed: **~8.9M monthly active developers**, **176k GitHub stars / ~17k forks**, sits in **85% of Fortune 500**, 67k+ community integrations, **14 employees**. → Why this framing: the "9M users / 85% of F500" headline anchors on the *free* desktop tool's reach, NOT on paying-cloud traction, which is exactly the number they won't give.
- Product: free desktop tool to run open-weight LLMs locally (Mac/Win/Linux) "in minutes" + a paid **neocloud** hosting bigger models, subscription tiers **free → ~$20 (Pro) → ~$100–200 (Pro Max)/month**, billed by **GPU-time, not tokens**. GPU-time billing "squares with agentic workloads" that run long and quiet (SiliconANGLE / pricing pages).
- **Why structured this way:** classic open-core. Free tool = distribution/funnel; neocloud = the monetization layer bolted on top. The pitch to VCs is "9M-dev funnel → cloud conversion," but the conversion rate is undisclosed → the raise is a bet on a funnel, not on proven cloud revenue.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Local-runtime layer (Ollama's actual turf):** LM Studio (GUI + HF model browser, free commercial), **llama.cpp** (the engine Ollama historically wrapped), MLX (Apple Silicon). Ollama = the "default for a solo dev who wants a model in minutes" (one command, OpenAI-compatible API). Position: **category leader by mindshare/adoption at the hobby+dev tier**, but the tier is thin on margin.
- **Production inference / cloud layer (where the money is):** vLLM/**Inferact** ($150M seed at $800M val, Jan 2026; vLLM runs on 400k+ GPUs, ~19x Ollama throughput at peak concurrency per Red Hat), **Baseten** (~$13B val, June 2026; ~$600M ARR), **Together AI** ($8.3B val, $800M Series C July 2026, $1.15B bookings), **Fireworks AI** ($4B→ talks at $15B val; ~$800M ARR est.), SGLang/RadixArk. See [[Baseten reportedly raising at $400 million valuation for AI model infrastructure]], [[DeepSeek raises over $7 billion in debut funding round]].
- **Why the lay of the land is this way:** the value/margin is concentrated in **production serving at concurrency** (vLLM/Baseten/Together), NOT single-user local inference. Ollama dominates a segment (developer laptops) that is beloved but low-ARPU; its cloud push aims it *upstream* into a lane already occupied by $4–13B-valued, $600–800M-ARR incumbents. Second-order: Ollama's moat (frictionless UX, brand) may not travel upmarket where SLA/throughput/cost-per-token win.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Founders **Jeff Morgan + Michael Chiang** previously built **Docker Desktop** (after Docker acquired their startup Kitematic). Ollama is explicitly "Docker for AI models" — abstract away hardware config, get a model running in minutes.
- **Why the company acts this way:** the Docker precedent is also a *warning* — Docker built ubiquity (10M+ devs) but struggled to monetize, forcing painful commercial pivots. Ollama's neocloud is the monetization attempt Docker learned it needed early. The "enshittification" backlash (~2025 blog/social complaints that the cloud push distracts from the free tool) is the same open-core tension Docker lived through → structural, not incidental.
- Fit: logical. A local-first tool bumps into the ceiling that state-of-the-art open models are "too big to run on your own computer," so hosting them is the natural adjacency. But it also means competing on compute economics against players with far more capital.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real novelty is thin** — Ollama is a UX/distribution wrapper over open engines (llama.cpp/MLX), not a new inference technology. Its value-add is *ergonomics and reach*, which is real and durable at the dev-tooling tier (traction: 8.9M MADs, 85% F500 penetration are strong, citable adoption — not just announcements).
- **The cloud value-add is UNPROVEN:** no disclosed cloud revenue, no valuation, no conversion metric. Morgan cites Jan 2026 (open models becoming "agentic," "OpenClaw hot") as the "proving point" — but that's a market-timing narrative, not a revenue disclosure.
- **Who captures the margin:** at the local tier, essentially nobody pays → Ollama captures brand, not dollars. At the cloud tier, the margin sits with whoever owns GPU economics + throughput (Baseten/Together/vLLM) → Ollama risks being a **top-of-funnel brand whose users graduate to cheaper/faster production stacks** rather than converting to its own neocloud (analysis/hypothesis).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Use of funds: product + open-source community, scale cloud compute footprint, key hires (still only 14 employees).
- **Sentiment is hot for the sector, softer for this tier:** inference is "eating the AI industry" (Deloitte: inference ≈ 2/3 of AI compute in 2026; >$50B chip spend). Capital is flooding open-weight inference (Together $800M, Baseten $13B, Fireworks talks at $15B). Against that, Ollama's $65M/$88M-total is a **rounding error** — it is under-capitalized for a compute arms race.
- **Why the market goes this way + counterintuitive second-order:** open-weight adoption genuinely favors tools like Ollama for *access*; but the same trend rewards deep-pocketed serving infra, and a GPU-cost war is won by capital, not UX. Fenton's own thesis ("every high-inference company has a vital existential project to move to open-weight") is bullish for the *category* but doesn't privilege Ollama specifically — those enterprises are the exact customers Baseten/Together are winning at scale. Risk: Ollama stays the beloved on-ramp while the money accrues one layer up.

## Top challenge/extra questions (12, second-order)
1. What's the **undisclosed valuation**? An $88M total raise with no valuation on an "8.9M-user" story usually signals a valuation that isn't as flattering as the user count. **Open.**

3. Is "8.9M monthly developers" **MADs of the free tool** or of anything monetizable? Almost certainly the former → headline overstates commercial reach. **Answered (analysis).**
4. Does "85% of Fortune 500" mean **paid enterprise contracts or ≥1 dev with the free app installed**? Likely the latter. **Open, lean free.**
5. Can Ollama win **production serving** vs vLLM's ~19x throughput edge at concurrency? At single-user parity, but production is multi-user. **Answered: disadvantaged at scale.**
6. Is $65M **enough capital** for a GPU-compute war vs Baseten's $1.5B / Together's $800M rounds? No — undercapitalized by ~20x. **Answered.**
7. Does the neocloud **cannibalize goodwill** ("enshittification") and risk a community fork? Tension is live; a hard-fork of the free core is the tail risk. **Open.**
8. Who **supplies Ollama's GPUs** and at what margin? Undisclosed; if reselling third-party compute, margin is thin/structurally squeezed (cf. Nvidia neocloud backstop). **Open.**
9. Is the moat **switching-cost or just default-choice**? Mostly "easiest first install" — low switching cost once a team standardizes on a production stack. **Answered (analysis): weak moat upmarket.**
10. Why **GPU-time not token billing**? Genuinely better for long agentic jobs, but also harder to benchmark against token-priced rivals → pricing opacity cuts both ways. **Answered.**
11. What stops a **hyperscaler (Docker itself, or Nvidia's stack, or HF)** from bundling equivalent one-command local runtime for free? Little; the UX is replicable. **Open, structural risk.**
12. Fintech relevance? Marginal — this is dev-infra/AI, not fintech; touches finance only via "85% of F500 incl. finance." **Answered: low fintech weight.**

**Sector & drivers.** AI inference / local + open-weight LLM tooling. **Size:** per Deloitte (via TechTimes, 2026-07-03), inference ≈ **two-thirds of all AI compute in 2026** (up from ~1/3 three years ago); inference **chip spend >$50bn** in 2026. Structure: **two distinct sub-layers** — (a) local/desktop runtimes (Ollama, LM Studio, llama.cpp, MLX): low capital barrier, distribution/UX-driven, thin monetization; (b) production cloud serving (vLLM/Inferact, Baseten, Together, Fireworks): capital- and GPU-intensive, where the ARR lives. Value is created upstream in serving-at-concurrency, not single-user local inference. **Why now:** open-weight models turned "agentic" (~Jan 2026), so enterprises with high inference bills are structurally pushed off closed models onto open-weight (Fenton: a "vital existential project") — a genuine tailwind for open-source tooling, but one that rewards capital-heavy serving infra as much as friendly on-ramps.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: monthly active developers / funnel size (top-of-funnel tier); **ARR + GPU-throughput (tokens/sec at concurrency) + cost-per-token** (serving tier). Players & basis of competition: **Ollama** — leader of the local/dev tier on UX & brand (8.9M MADs, 176k GitHub stars, 85% F500 install base); competes on frictionless distribution. **LM Studio** — GUI + HF model browser. **vLLM/Inferact** — PagedAttention, ~19x Ollama throughput at peak concurrency (Red Hat), $150M seed at $800M val (Jan 2026), running on 400k+ GPUs. **Baseten / Together AI / Fireworks AI** — production serving on price/throughput/SLA. Protagonist's position: **category-leading (dev on-ramp), but niche/undercapitalized upmarket** — its cloud push enters a lane owned by far larger balance sheets; moat is default-choice ergonomics, low switching cost (analysis).
