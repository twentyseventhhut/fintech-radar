## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
No event, no numbers — an opinion piece. The one load-bearing, non-obvious claim is regulatory: stablecoin **issuers are barred from paying interest/yield to holders**, so the reserve-float yield must be captured elsewhere in the stack. Real: GENIUS Act §4(a)(11) (signed 2025-07-18) prohibits a permitted issuer from paying "any form of interest or yield… solely in connection with holding" the coin; EU MiCA Art. 22(4) is stricter. The article pivots away from headline-rate competition to "who is paying this return, out of what economic activity, against what protection" — and its worked example is Maple's syrupUSDG (~7% from institutional *lending*, >$22B originated since 2022), quietly substituting **credit risk for rate risk**. → Why it matters: the ban doesn't destroy the ~$300B float's yield (~$10-11B/yr gross at ~3.6% effective fed-funds) — it **relocates** it to whoever owns distribution. That relocation IS the "monetisation model" the piece sells; "ask the right questions" is the tell that it stops before the hard part — who *durably* keeps the margin.

## [1] Competitors / peers
The bundled models are captured by different players: issuer float (proven — Tether Q1'26 net profit ~$1.04B, ~$141B US Treasury exposure; Circle FY2025 reserve income ~$2.64B of ~$2.75B total, ~94% carry); distributor rewards, the GENIUS loophole (PayPal funds ~4% on PYUSD, not issuer Paxos; Coinbase keeps 100% of on-platform USDC reserve income, 50% off — Circle paid Coinbase $907.9M in 2024, ~$1.4B in 2025); embedded rails (Stripe/Bridge $1.1B closed 2025-02-04, [[Cross River launches unified stablecoin payment platform]]). → The issuer earns the yield but cedes most of it to whoever touches the customer; a fintech that merely resells rails captures little — only distribution ownership captures margin.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The stablecoin market is ~$303–321bn in circulating supply as of mid-2026 (per DefiLlama / market trackers, as of Jul 2026), up ~12x since end-2020 ($27bn) but roughly flat-to-down over the trailing 90 days (-1.7% to Jul 12). Structure is highly concentrated: USDT ~59% (~$187bn) and USDC ~24% (~$75bn) = ~83% combined; no other token holds even 3% and PYUSD sits below $3bn despite PayPal's brand (per market-cap trackers, Jul 2026). Value chain has three layers — **issuance** (the issuer holds reserves and earns the float yield on T-bills), **distribution** (exchanges/wallets/fintechs that put the coin in front of users), and **rails/orchestration** (payments, FX, settlement). "Why now": the GENIUS Act (signed Jul 18 2025) is the first US federal stablecoin law; it bans *issuers* from paying yield to holders but leaves *third-party/affiliate* rewards alone — so the economic prize migrates to whoever controls distribution. Yield-bearing constructs drove >half of net supply growth in Q1 2026 (per ProMarket/CRS, 2026). This is precisely the "monetisation model" the FinTech Futures piece flags for fintechs.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: circulating supply (float), reserve yield capture (bps of float retained after distribution), and distribution reach (wallets/MAUs holding the coin). Key players by layer — issuers: Tether (USDT), Circle (USDC), PayPal (PYUSD); distributors: Coinbase, and increasingly fintechs/wallets (Stripe/Bridge, Revolut, KakaoPay, Kraken); rails: Visa Stablecoin Platform [[Visa introduces Stablecoin Platform for banks and fintechs]], Mastercard/Thunes [[Mastercard and Thunes bring stablecoin payouts to mainstream]], Worldline/Fipto. Basis of competition is shifting from **issuance to distribution**: Circle pays Coinbase 50% of *all* USDC reserve interest earned off-Coinbase and 100% of interest on USDC held on Coinbase; Coinbase-linked distribution cost was ~$1.4bn in 2025, ~51% of Circle's $2.7bn revenue+reserve income, leaving Circle a ~39% margin (per Circle filings via Decrypt/CryptoSlate, 2026). The FinTech Futures thesis positions fintechs to occupy that distribution seat — the party that captures the float economics — rather than issue.

**Comps & multiples.** Internal comps (base): [[a16z report stablecoin supply tops $300 billion]] (supply >$300bn, top issuers ~87%); [[Circle explores native token for Arc as Q3 profit surges]] (Circle Q3'25: $740m revenue, $214m net income, USDC growth); [[Stablecoin payments jump 70% since the GENIUS Act]]; [[S&P downgrades Tether's USDT stablecoin to weak]]; [[Coinbase pulls out of $2B BVNK acquisition]] (distribution M&A). Issuer economics arithmetic (analysis): on ~$75bn USDC float at ~4% short rates ≈ ~$3bn gross float yield; Circle's FY revenue+reserve income was $2.7bn with ~51% paid out in distribution — i.e. the distributor captures roughly half the float. Trading multiples for the private fintech protagonist: no data (piece is unnamed commentary, no company/round/metrics). Distribution vs issuance value split is the load-bearing comp here, not an EV/Revenue line.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory disintermediation of the model itself.** The OCC's 2026 NPRM would extend the GENIUS yield ban to affiliates/third parties (the Coinbase "loyalty reward" structure). If finalized, the exact distributor-reward economics the article pitches to fintechs could be curtailed — the monetisation model is regulator-contingent.
2. **Value capture sits with distribution, and distribution is concentrated.** Coinbase already extracts ~half of USDC reserve income; the Circle-Coinbase term ends Aug 2026. Fintechs entering late may face the same fate — the float yield accrues to whoever owns the user, and incumbents (Coinbase, Visa/Mastercard rails) are entrenched. Second-order: latecomer fintechs risk being rails-takers, not rent-takers.
3. **Rate and reserve-quality dependence.** The entire "free money" narrative rests on ~4% short rates; float yield compresses if rates fall. Reserve risk is real — S&P cut USDT to "weak" (Nov 2025) on riskier reserves; a peg/credibility event on a major issuer would hit fintechs distributing it.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** The re-rating is that stablecoins are becoming a distribution business, not a crypto bet — the fintech that owns the customer captures the float, so expect neobanks/PSPs to add native stablecoin balances and yield-rewards (Revolut, KakaoPay, Stripe/Bridge already moving) (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if the OCC finalizes the affiliate/third-party yield ban in 2026, the fintech "monetisation model" narrows sharply and value re-concentrates on issuers + pure payment rails. Trigger to watch: the OCC final rule and the Aug 2026 Circle-Coinbase renewal — both will reveal how much of the float distributors can actually keep. (analysis)
