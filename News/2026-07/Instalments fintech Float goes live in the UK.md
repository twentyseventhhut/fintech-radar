# Context-enrichment: Instalments fintech Float goes live in the UK
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Identity — read first (three different "Floats"):** The UK launcher is **Float, a South African card-linked instalments fintech** (founded 2021, CEO Alex Forsyth-Thompson; Cape Town). It is the SAME company as our prior note [[Float secures $2.6M for South Africa's credit market]] — same US$2.6M round, same Invenfin + SAAD Investment Holdings co-leads, whose stated use of funds was "international expansion." This UK launch IS that expansion. It is NOT the Canadian [[Float Financial raises CAD $85M Series C]] (Toronto corporate cards / expense management), and NOT [[Float raises $100M CAD to scale business banking]] (also the Canadian company). Keep all three separate.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **Fact:** ~7 July 2026, Float announced it "goes live in the UK," offering merchants a way to let shoppers split a purchase into **up to 12 interest-free, fee-free monthly instalments on the credit card they already hold** (Visa/Mastercard). No new credit is originated; it runs within the shopper's existing card limit, so card rewards/protections (incl. Section 75) are retained. Compatible with "55m+ UK credit cards." [finextra, pymnts]
- **De-PR'd reality:** This is a **launch announcement + PR, with no demonstrated UK traction**. No UK merchants are named; no UK merchant count, GMV or transaction data. The named brands (Samsung, Trek, Reebok, The North Face, Diesel) are **South African** partners, not confirmed UK partners. Quoted "£500+ AOV / 134% AOV uplift / 20–30% higher conversion / £250bn unused UK credit" figures appear to be **global/SA vendor stats reused for the UK** — treat as marketing (unverified). (analysis)
- **Why framed this way:** Anchoring to "55m+ cards / £250bn unused credit" reframes Float from "another small BNPL entrant" into a "we unlock credit that already exists" story — deliberately positioning AGAINST classic BNPL right as UK BNPL regulation bites (see [4]). The absence of a single named UK merchant is the tell that this is early. (analysis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Wrong comp set = classic BNPL (they originate NEW credit):** Klarna [[Klarna reports Q1 2026 revenue up 44% to $1 billion]], Clearpay/Afterpay, Zilch [[Zilch raises over $175M ahead of potential IPO]] / [[Visa, Zilch and Thredd launch Flexible Credential in UK]], PayPal Pay in 3 [[PayPal relaunches in the UK with debit and credit cards]], Monzo Flex, and Affirm's new UK entry via [[Affirm and Stripe partner to bring pay-over-time to UK checkouts]]. All of these lend and all fall under the 15 July 2026 FCA regime.
- **Right comp set = card-linked instalments (no new credit):** Amex Plan It, Curve Flex, Citi Flex Pay, U.S. Bank Split [[U.S. Bank launches Split Card for automatic interest-free installments]], plus the Visa/Mastercard network instalment APIs and the "Flexible Credential" rail Visa/Zilch/Thredd launched in the UK [[Visa, Zilch and Thredd launch Flexible Credential in UK]]. Analogous overseas card-linked instalment plays: [[Satispay launches Pay in 3 BNPL for physical shops in Italy]], WiZink/Aplazame [[WiZink launches Aplazame installment payments in Portugal]].
- **Position:** In SA, Float is a category leader; in the **UK it is a small new entrant catching up** against issuer-native features (Amex Plan It, Curve Flex) and the card networks' own instalment rails. Its only real differentiator is **merchant-side POS integration that works across ANY Visa/MC card**, vs issuer features locked to one card brand. (analysis)
- **Second-order:** The strategic risk is that the value ("instalments on your existing card") is a **feature the card networks and issuers can — and are — building themselves** (Flexible Credential, Plan It). Float's edge is distribution/merchant integration, not a defensible mechanism. The central question shifts from "is card-linked instalments a good model?" (yes) to "why does the merchant integrate FLOAT rather than the network rail?" — which the launch does not answer. (analysis)

## [2] Company history / fit
- **2021** — founded, South Africa ("Africa's first card-linked instalment platform").
- **Mar 2024** — US$11M funding facility from Standard Bank to fund merchant settlement/growth.
- **Sep 2025** — US$2.6M equity, co-led Invenfin + SAAD Investment Holdings; use of funds explicitly incl. "international expansion" → our note [[Float secures $2.6M for South Africa's credit market]].
- **~7 Jul 2026** — UK go-live (this note). Fits logically: the UK launch is the international-expansion leg the 2025 round was raised for.
- **Why this way:** Float is a **thinly-capitalised (~US$2.6M last equity round) SA startup** entering the UK — it cannot outspend Klarna/Amex, so it leans on a differentiated "no new credit" narrative and merchant POS integration rather than a balance-sheet war. That thin capital is exactly why there's no UK funding line and no named UK merchants yet. (analysis)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Genuinely new (mechanism, vs BNPL):** originates NO new loan — converts a purchase on the customer's own Visa/MC into fee-/interest-free instalments within their existing limit. That is a real category distinction from Klarna/Clearpay, and it keeps Section 75 protection intact — a meaningful consumer/merchant selling point in the UK right now. (fact + analysis)
- **NOT new (vs card-linked peers):** Amex Plan It, Curve Flex, Citi Flex Pay and the Visa/MC instalment APIs already do card-linked instalments. Float's novelty is narrower than the PR implies: merchant-side, cross-issuer POS integration. (fact)
- **Traction = essentially unproven in the UK:** zero named UK merchants, no UK numbers; all metrics look recycled from SA. "Live" here means "announced + open," not "adopted." (fact)
- **Who captures the margin:** In the card-linked model the underlying credit and the interest/rewards sit with the **card issuer**, not Float — Float earns a **merchant fee** for the instalment UX + integration. Merchant fee (MDR), fraud/chargeback liability split, and how Float funds settlement timing in the UK are all **undisclosed** — so the durability of the take-rate is unknown. If issuers/networks bundle the same feature for free, Float's fee is disintermediated. (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Regulatory timing is the whole story:** UK BNPL ("deferred payment credit") comes under the FCA on **15 July 2026** (FCA PS26/1) — affordability checks, FCA authorisation, FOS access. Our note: [[FCA confirms BNPL protections under Consumer Duty from July 2026]]. Float launched ~one week before.
- **Does Float sidestep it?** Plausibly YES for the new DPC regime: the rules target **newly-originated** unregulated interest-free credit; Float originates none and rides already-regulated credit cards. So Float likely sits OUTSIDE the new BNPL regime — a genuine competitive angle as rivals absorb compliance cost. **BUT** whether Float's own UK entity needs FCA payments/agency permissions is **open/unconfirmed** (no FCA authorisation found). Section 75 treatment under the new regime is contested. (reasoned inference, not a ruling)
- **Trade sentiment:** eMarketer/FinTech Weekly frame card-linked instalments as a rising threat to BNPL fintechs — a tailwind for Float's thesis, but it's a **crowded, well-capitalised field** where Float is small.
- **Counterintuitive second-order:** the FCA clampdown that Float pitches as its opening could equally push Klarna/Clearpay to add card-linked, no-lending instalment options themselves (to escape the same rules) — competing away Float's regulatory arbitrage before it scales. (hypothesis)

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** UK BNPL is a large, fast-growing pool: the UK BNPL market is projected at ~$48.56bn in 2026, growing ~20.7% YoY, and forecast to reach ~$106.45bn by 2031 (per a UK BNPL business report, via Yahoo Finance/ResearchAndMarkets, as of 2026). Structurally it is fragmenting into two models: (a) classic BNPL that *originates a new loan* at checkout (Klarna, Clearpay/Afterpay, PayPal Pay in 3/4, Zilch), and (b) *card-linked instalments* that ride the shopper's existing Visa/Mastercard limit with no new credit (Float, Splitit, and bank "Flex" products). Barriers are shifting from pure distribution to regulatory capital and compliance. **Why now:** from 15 July 2026 the FCA formally regulates BNPL in the UK — mandatory affordability checks, s.75-style protections and disclosure (per FCA/PennyPlan/checkmyfile, 2026). This is the pivotal driver for Float's timing: a model that *does not originate new credit* and reuses an already-underwritten card limit is positioned to argue a lighter regulatory footprint than loan-originating peers, exactly as new-lending rules bite.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: GMV, take rate (merchant fee), active consumers/merchants, and credit-loss rate. Float's model is fee-and-interest-free to the shopper, up to 12 monthly instalments in the UK across 55m+ compatible UK credit cards, with no sign-up and no new credit check (per PYMNTS/Finextra, 2026); in South Africa it advertises up to 24 fee-free instalments across ~7m pre-approved cards (per Disrupt Africa/Daba, 2024–25). Merchant economics (the take rate Float charges) are NOT disclosed — `[UNSOURCED]`; it monetises the merchant side, citing a case-study 134% uplift in average order value (Float PR — data, not verified). Key players & basis of competition: **Klarna, Clearpay, PayPal** compete on scale/checkout distribution; **Zilch** (UK, card-network model) on brand/marketing; and the *closest structural peers* are **Splitit** (card-linked instalments, launched Splitit Go in Mar 2026) and bank card-linked flex — **Monzo Flex** (3 interest-free, then 6/12 at ~29% APR), **Curve Flex** (3/6/12/24 mths, ~19.99% APR), plus Barclays/NatWest instalment plans. Position: Float is a **niche new entrant** in the UK — differentiated by "no new credit," but a late arrival against embedded incumbents and against banks that own the card relationship natively. Moat `(analysis)`: thin so far — the "existing-limit" trick is replicable (Splitit does it), so the moat rests on merchant distribution and Visa/Mastercard integration, not proprietary tech.

**Comps & multiples.** Float is private (backed by a Standard Bank ~$11m / R208m revolving facility, 2024; launched Nov 2021) and discloses no UK GMV/revenue — no valuation, so **no multiple can be computed for Float (no data)**. Benchmarks from named/adjacent peers: **Klarna** (public post-IPO) Q1 2026 revenue ~$1.0bn (+44% YoY), GMV ~$33.7bn, adj. operating profit ~$68m, ~119m active consumers, >1m merchants ([[Klarna reports Q1 2026 revenue up 44% to $1 billion]]) — annualised GMV run-rate ≈ $33.7bn × 4 ≈ $135bn, take rate ≈ $1.0bn / $33.7bn ≈ **3.0% per-quarter revenue/GMV** `(analysis)`. **Zilch** raised >$175m debt+equity ahead of a possible 2026 IPO ([[Zilch raises over $175M ahead of potential IPO]]); **Tabby** marked a $4.5bn valuation on a 2025 secondary ([[Tabby completes secondary share sale at $4.5B valuation]]). Private-peer valuations are round marks, not market caps; forward multiples `[UNSOURCED]`. Distribution not computed (mixed models/geos) — qualitative only: Float is orders of magnitude sub-scale vs these comps.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory ambiguity, not immunity.** Float leans on "no new credit," but the FCA's 15 Jul 2026 regime targets BNPL broadly; whether card-linked instalments fall fully outside affordability/disclosure rules is unconfirmed — a wrong bet on scope is existential to the UK GTM.
2. **Disintermediation by the card issuers themselves.** The whole model sits *on top of* a Visa/Mastercard limit the issuing bank already owns; Monzo Flex, Curve Flex, Barclays and NatWest offer instalments natively, so banks can capture this margin without Float — thin defensibility.
3. **Sub-scale late entrant vs entrenched distribution.** Klarna/Clearpay/PayPal own checkout real estate and merchant relationships; Float must win merchants one by one with undisclosed economics while burning a debt facility sized for SA, not the UK.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Float's launch is a small signal of a broader shift: as UK BNPL regulation raises the cost of *originating* new credit, the "reuse the existing card limit" model becomes the regulatory-arbitrage lane, and expect incumbents (and card issuers) to lean harder into card-linked flex. Falsifiable thesis: card-linked instalments gain share post-15 Jul 2026 as loan-origination BNPL absorbs compliance cost. Trigger to watch: FCA guidance explicitly classifying card-linked instalments, and any Float UK merchant/volume disclosure or a network (Visa/Mastercard) tie-up — absence of both within ~2 quarters would suggest the UK entry is stalling.
