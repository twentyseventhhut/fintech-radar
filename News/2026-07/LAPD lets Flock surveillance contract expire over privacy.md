LAPD lets contract with surveillance giant Flock expire, citing ‘serious concerns’ over civil liberties and privacy
The Los Angeles Police Department (LAPD) is reportedly ending its deal with Flock Safety, a surveillance company that helps law enforcement track vehicles using thousands of its license plate cameras placed across the United States.
A senior LAPD official told news outlets, first reported by ABC7 and the Los Angeles Times , that the police department would allow its three-year contract with Flock to expire when it ends on Saturday. The department cited “serious concerns” around civil liberties and privacy. Flock’s cameras are operated by the Atlanta, Georgia-based company and not the LAPD.
“This contract is not being renewed because of serious concerns around civil liberties and civil rights issues, particularly around privacy and the data that is being collected from these cameras,” LAPD’s chief information officer Dean Gialamas was quoted as saying. “The LAPD had to make a difficult decision, in this case discontinuing using Flock services until we can get those data, privacy, security and sharing concerns ironed out through a contractual relationship.”
A spokesperson for the LAPD did not respond to a request for comment from TechCrunch over the weekend, and it’s unclear if Flock’s cameras will continue recording in absence of an active contract. According to ABC7, the police department is seeking new language in its contract addressing privacy and data storage concerns.
As the third-largest police department in the U.S., the LAPD is one of Flock’s largest government customers to date. Several major U.S. cities have also stopped working with Flock, including Mountain View, California and South Portland, Maine , citing privacy worries and concerns that federal immigration officials used the cameras to track people in violation of their local laws governing their sanctuary city policies.
The contract expiry caught the surveillance company by “surprise,” said Flock spokesperson Holly Beilin in an email to TechCrunch. Flock said it was confident that the company could “clear up the current misconceptions” that led to the contract’s end. Flock would not say which specific misconceptions it was referring to.
Flock has a network of at least 80,000 cameras around the U.S. that scan license plates and allow police and federal agencies to track vehicles. 
The company has faced heavy backlash from local communities that have approved and then reneged on their deals with Flock over concerns with privacy and surveillance. Some locals have taken matters into their own hands by dismantling Flock cameras and covering them with trash bags , even as some communities found that Flock reinstalled cameras without permission from local authorities.
Researchers have identified an uptick in documented cases of motorists being pulled over, detained, and held at gunpoint by police, or jailed, due to false positives and errors with license plate readers. Last week, a journalist with car reviews and news website The Drive detailed how he was tracked for days and later boxed-in by police after a Flock camera mistakenly flagged the license plate of the on-loan review unit he was driving as stolen.
Flock has also faced scrutiny following several security lapses that have exposed cameras and data, which in one case allowed independent news outlet 404 Media to watch themselves live on publicly exposed Flock cameras. Lawmakers have also urged federal consumer authorities to investigate Flock for failing to implement measures that would prevent hackers and spies from gaining access to its security cameras, warning that many of the police user logins are not protected with multi-factor authentication.
 404 Media also reported that the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration used a local police officer’s password without their knowledge to search for a suspect accused of an immigration violation.

# Context-enrichment: LAPD lets Flock surveillance contract expire over privacy
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
The LAPD let its 3-year contract with **Flock Safety** (Atlanta ALPR/automated-license-plate-reader network) **lapse** around 2026-07-11/13 rather than actively "cancelling" it — CIO Dean Gialamas cited "serious concerns around civil liberties and civil rights issues, particularly around privacy and the data being collected." Crucially this is a **pause + renegotiation over contract language on privacy and data storage**, not a hard ban (Fortune, 2026-07-15) — so the headline "cancel" overstates finality.
**Why it matters / de-PR:** two forces converged and the press frame ("privacy concerns") flattens them. (a) An LAPD Inspector General audit found **~32% false stolen-vehicle alert rate** — 161 false "hot-list" hits in two months, each capable of triggering an armed high-risk stop (TechTimes, 2026-07-17; 404 Media). That is an **accuracy/operational failure**, not just an abstract privacy worry. (b) A sanctuary-city fear that Flock's *nationwide* searchable network is a side-door for ICE/DEA despite California **SB 34** (Civil Code §1798.90.55) barring ALPR data-sharing with federal/out-of-state agencies. Flock's cameras keep running with no LAPD contract — the data pool doesn't stop; LA just loses its seat at the table. LAPD is one of Flock's **largest government customers**, so this is the single biggest defection in a broad wave. (analysis) The real story is not "one city got nervous" but that Flock's core value prop — a *shared national* plate database — is exactly what triggers the SB 34 / immigration liability that makes sanctuary jurisdictions bail.

## [1] Competitors / peers
ALPR & police-surveillance stack: **Axon** (ranked #1 by sales reps, winning citywide 50+ camera deals), **Motorola Solutions / Vigilant Solutions** (#2, enterprise integration), **Rekor Systems** (public co, cloud-only, no own hardware — the cleanest public read-through), **Genetec AutoVu**, **Verkada** (closest cloud-dashboard analog), plus ELSAG/Perceptics/Jenoptik. Gunshot detection: Flock's **Raven** (AI-only, ~$25k/sq-mi/yr, no human-review center) vs **SoundThinking's ShotSpotter** (human-validated).
**Why the landscape is this way (analysis):** Flock's moat is *density* — it fuses HOA/business private cameras with police feeds into one searchable national pool (~100k cameras, ~20B scans/month), which rivals with pure per-agency hardware can't match. But that same fusion is the regulatory liability: Axon/Motorola sell agency-siloed systems that don't create a *national* immigration-lookup surface. So Flock's differentiator and its biggest risk are the same asset — competitors may win on "less scary" architecture even at inferior tech. Internal peer: [[Peregrine Technologies raises $250 million for public-safety software]] ($250M Series D at $6.8B, Sequoia) shows investors still pouring into public-safety software — but Peregrine sells *data-integration* to agencies, not a controversial camera network, a telling contrast in risk profile.

## [2] Company history / fit
Founder Garrett Langley; YC-backed; Atlanta. Funding: Series F **$275M at $7.5B** led by a16z (2025-03-13); newer **Series G ~$200M at ~$8.4B** (2026-04-17). 2024 revenue **~$300M, +70% YoY** (Sacra/Contrary); **100,000+ cameras** across 5,000+ communities in 49 states (the note's "80,000" is now stale). 2025 CNBC Disruptor 50.
**Why Flock acts this way (analysis):** valuation kept *climbing through* the scandals ($7.5B→$8.4B, camera count 80k→100k) — so the company has every incentive to dismiss LAPD's exit as "misconceptions" and keep expanding, because growth (not caution) is what the private multiple is priced on. It launched a one-toggle "turn off Federal Sharing" (Jan 2026) and a CJIS immigration/reproductive-law disclaimer (Feb 2026) — reactive mitigations timed to the backlash, not proactive design.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
The *news* is real and material: LAPD, the #3 US police department and a flagship customer, walking away is a concrete traction reversal, not an announcement. It sits in a documented exodus — **80–82 terminations across 28 states** (Aug 2021–May 2026), ~39 in the first 5 months of 2026 (State of Surveillance). Named: Mountain View CA (disabled all 30 cameras 2026-02-03 after finding unauthorized ATF/GSA-IG access), Austin TX (~June 2025), Flagstaff, Cambridge, Eugene, Santa Cruz. (Norfolk VA / South Portland ME cited by TechCrunch but unconfirmed in web pass — **open**.)
**Why the value-add erodes (analysis):** Flock's unit economics rest on network effects — each new camera makes the searchable pool more valuable to every police customer. But the *same* network effect concentrates reputational and legal risk: one 404 Media exposé (60–67 no-password cameras live on the open internet; a reporter watched himself in real time) or one DEA-password-misuse story hits *all* customers at once. So the flywheel that drives the multiple is also a single point of political failure. Who captures the margin: Flock still captures it operationally, but the *durability* of that revenue now depends on surviving an FTC probe request, a House Oversight investigation, and an SB 34 class action (Feb 2026).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
LAPD recovery is **plausible** if Flock accepts new privacy/data-storage terms (open outcome). Flock stays private; IPO positioned "within a few years," no filed timeline (open). Regulatory trajectory worsening: FTC-probe request (Wyden + Krishnamoorthi, Nov 2025), House Oversight investigation (Krishnamoorthi/Garcia, Aug 2025), Illinois SoS referral, CA AG Bonta suing El Cajon (Oct 2025), Feb 2026 class action.
**Why the market may misread this (analysis):** the counterintuitive second-order effect is that Flock's *rising* valuation through scandals looks like resilience but may be fragility — capital and camera concentration make the network a bigger single target for regulators and hackers, and a national moment (an FTC action or a marquee wrongful-arrest lawsuit) could cascade cancellations faster than any competitor could. Backdrop irony: [[StrictlyVC Diamandis says global surveillance is becoming inevitable]] — the VC narrative that ubiquitous surveillance is unstoppable is running headlong into concrete municipal pushback. Regtech frame: this pairs with [[Финтехно EU deems AI credit scoring high-risk]] as the same 2026 theme — automated data systems getting reclassified as high-risk and pushed toward accountability.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: public-safety surveillance / ALPR (automatic license-plate recognition), a govtech niche sitting inside the "public-safety operating system" theme. Market sizing is noisy — third-party estimates diverge widely by scope (ALPR vs ANPR-systems vs LPR-cameras): the ALPR market is put at ~$8.97bn (2025) → ~$10.1bn (2026) at ~12.5% CAGR per The Business Research Company, while narrower "ANPR system" definitions land at only ~$3.3–3.9bn (2025–26) at ~6–10% CAGR (Fortune Business Insights / 360iResearch) — treat any single TAM with caution, no consensus figure. Structure: fragmented on the vendor side (Flock, Motorola/Vigilant, Axon, Rekor, Genetec/AutoVu, Verkada, ELSAG) but concentrating around two "stacks" — Flock in small/mid-size cities, Motorola/Axon in enterprise/citywide. Entry barriers are moderate on hardware but rising on the data-network side (cross-agency search network = network effect) — and, increasingly, on *regulation/trust*, which is the "why now": the news is a demand-side, not tech, shift. Driver reversing into a headwind: municipal procurement is now gated by privacy/civil-liberties politics, not just price/coverage.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: installed camera count, ARR & net-new-city adds, contract renewal/churn rate, and search-network volume. Flock: ~$300M ARR at ~70% YoY growth (Sacra est., Mar-2025), >100,000 cameras (up from ~80,000 cited in the note), last valued ~$8.4bn (Apr-2026 share authorization) after a $275M Sept-2025 round at $7.5bn (a16z-led). Key players & basis of competition: Flock competes on price and fast small-city deployment (~$2,400/camera-yr) and its cross-agency search network; Motorola/Vigilant on enterprise LE integration and legacy fixed-reader networks ($40k–$250k+ deployments); Axon on the bundled "public-safety OS" (Tasers, body-cams, evidence software), a public comp at ~$46bn market cap on ~$2.8bn TTM revenue (~38% YoY). Recent moves: 53 cities cancelled over unauthorized federal/ICE data access (Tech Times, 29-Jun-2026); Dayton bagged all 72 cameras after ~7,000 prohibited immigration searches; LAPD (3rd-largest US PD, a top customer) let its 3-yr contract lapse 13-Jul then moved to *renegotiate* data-governance terms (Fortune, 15-Jul). Position: category leader by growth and camera count but with the *thinnest trust moat* — its network-effect advantage (data shared across agencies) is exactly what now triggers cancellations (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** Internal comps: none — Flock is the only `company/flock` note in-base (2026-06/07); no ALPR/surveillance peer notes to wikilink, so no internal comp set. External (mark: private = round valuation, not market cap): Flock EV/Revenue proxy on the $7.5bn Sept-2025 round vs ~$300M ARR ≈ `$7.5bn / $0.30bn ≈ 25x`; on the ~$8.4bn Apr-2026 mark ≈ `$8.4bn / $0.30bn ≈ 28x` (ARR is trailing; forward would be lower). This is above the ~0.5–20x EV/Rev guide range but is a hyper-growth private round at ~70% ARR growth, so richness is partly growth-justified rather than an automatic flag — the concern is that the multiple was struck *before* the cancellation wave accelerated. Public anchor: Axon trades ~`$46bn / $2.8bn ≈ 16x` trailing revenue at ~38% growth — a slower-but-profitable, diversified comp; Flock's ~25–28x on a single-product line with mounting churn looks full against it. Motorola Solutions FY2026 revenue not sourced here → `[UNSOURCED]`. EV/EBITDA / P/E: not computable (private, no disclosed profitability) → no data.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory/political churn is now the core risk, not a tail.** 53+ documented cancellations and a class action (SF, alleging 1.6M federal/ICE queries in 7 months) mean revenue durability and net-retention are directly exposed to privacy law and sanctuary-city politics — second-order: raises effective CAC and lengthens sales cycles as every deal needs bespoke data-governance terms (LAPD/Dunwoody-style).
2. **Customer concentration in marquee accounts.** Losing/renegotiating a flagship like LAPD is a reference-account risk that can cascade to peer departments — reputational, beyond the direct ARR.
3. **The moat cuts both ways / disintermediation by "reformed contracts."** The cross-agency data network drives both the valuation and the backlash; cities like Dunwoody demanding full data ownership + federal-sharing kill switches erode the network value that justified ~25–28x — margin/pricing pressure if data-sharing becomes an opt-out default.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** For the sector: this is a *governance re-rating* of ALPR, not a demand collapse — ~800 cities still approved Flock in 2026, so the falsifiable thesis is "trust, not coverage, becomes the buying criterion; vendors that hand cities data-sovereignty (kill switches, audit logs, no default federal sharing) take share." Trigger to watch: whether LAPD signs a renegotiated (not renewed) deal and whether Flock's next disclosed ARR/net-adds decelerate — a sustained cancellation rate outpacing gross adds would break the growth-justifies-the-multiple case (analysis).
