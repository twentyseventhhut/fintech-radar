## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
**[0] What happened.** On 30 June 2026 the U.S. House Financial Services Committee (HFSC) advanced **H.R. 9330, the Earned Wage Access (EWA) Consumer Protection Act**, on a bipartisan **29–22** vote (markup 30 Jun; reported out of committee). The bill, introduced 18 Jun 2026 by **Rep. Bryan Steil (R-WI)**, creates the first federal framework for EWA: it mandates a free access option where fees are charged, requires disclosures, prohibits interest and third-party debt collection, and — the crux — declares that compliant EWA products are **not loans or extensions of credit under federal law**. It is a near-rerun of Steil's 2024 **H.R. 7428**, which cleared committee 28–22 but died on the House floor at the end of the 118th Congress. ([Crowdfund Insider](https://www.crowdfundinsider.com/2026/06/288684-house-financial-services-approves-earned-wage-bill/); [HFSC press release](https://financialservices.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=411188); [American Banker](https://www.americanbanker.com/news/house-finance-committee-passes-ewa-bill-among-others))

**[1] Why it matters.** EWA sits in a live definitional fight — "payroll benefit" vs. "high-cost loan." Federalizing the "not credit" designation would exempt qualifying products from the Truth in Lending Act (TILA) and could weaken state consumer protections. It codifies into statute the position the **CFPB adopted in Dec 2025** (advisory opinion, effective 23 Dec 2025, published in the Federal Register): "Covered EWA" — no underwriting, access only to already-accrued wages, no recourse against the worker — is **not "credit"** under TILA/Reg Z, and expedited-delivery fees and tips are not "finance charges." That CFPB opinion withdrew the opposite July-2024 proposed interpretive rule but is non-binding and does not touch state law. H.R. 9330 would harden that stance and, per critics, preempt tougher state regimes. ([Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/23/2025-23735/truth-in-lending-regulation-z-non-application-to-earned-wage-access-products); [Venable](https://www.venable.com/insights/publications/2025/12/earned-wage-access-certain-models-fall-outside); [Goodwin](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/12/alerts-finance-ftec-cfpb-finally-starts-getting-ewa-right))

**[2] The two camps.**
- **Industry / supporters:** The **Financial Technology Association (FTA)** applauded the "bipartisan" markup, framing EWA as low-cost early access with no credit checks, no interest, no recourse, no credit-score impact. ([FTA statement](https://www.ftassociation.org/fta-statement-on-bipartisan-hfsc-markup-of-ewa-legislation/); [FTA applause](https://www.ftassociation.org/fta-applauds-house-financial-services-committee-passage-of-critical-earned-wage-access-bill/))
- **Consumer / labor coalition:** A letter from **226 labor, civil-rights, consumer and community groups** (dated 29 Jun 2026) opposed the bill. The **National Consumer Law Center (NCLC)** calls it a measure that "does not protect consumers… fuels the affordability crisis by protecting payday-loan apps." **Americans for Financial Reform** (Tom Feltner) calls EWA "high-interest payday loans in disguise." The **Center for Responsible Lending** cites cross-party polling favoring bank-equivalent rules; **New Economy Project** launched a national coalition. Rep. **Ayanna Pressley (D-MA)** offered an amendment to preserve states' authority to add protections. ([NCLC opposition letter PDF](https://www.nclc.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/2026-6-29-HR-9330-EWA-opposition-letter-updated.pdf); [NCLC](https://www.nclc.org/congress-ignores-affordability-crisis-with-legislation-to-supercharge-predatory-lending-and-boost-harmful-credit-reporting/); [Pressley](https://pressley.house.gov/2026/07/02/watch-pressley-proposes-amendment-to-allow-states-to-improve-consumer-protections-for-pay-day-loans/); [CRL poll](https://www.responsiblelending.org/media/new-poll-finds-voters-across-parties-agree-earned-wage-access-payday-loan-apps-should-play))

**[3] State-law backdrop & market.** State regimes are a **patchwork**: Nevada (first, 2023), Kansas, Missouri, Wisconsin, South Carolina treat EWA as *non-loan* with licensing/tip rules; **California** (effective 2025) treats it as *lending* under the California Financing Law, requiring DFPI registration and data reporting; ~20 states are considering bills. Market: U.S. EWA ~**$2.5–3.3bn** (2025 est., high-CAGR); employer-partnered EWA advanced **$22.8bn across 214M transactions in 2022 (CFPB)**; **55M+** U.S. workers had EWA access by end-2024. ([hr-brew](https://www.hr-brew.com/stories/2025/05/15/earned-wage-access-laws); [American Banker state guide](https://www.americanbanker.com/payments/list/a-complete-guide-to-earned-wage-access-regulation-by-state); [CFPB Data Spotlight](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/data-research/research-reports/data-spotlight-developments-in-the-paycheck-advance-market/); [market.us](https://market.us/report/earned-wage-access-market/))

**[4] Internal context (prior notes).** EWA is a recurring theme in the DB, though this is the **first note on U.S. federal EWA legislation** specifically:
- [[Rain launches embedded earned wage access within Workday]] — US employer-partnered EWA product (the model H.R. 9330 protects).

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
**Red-team / challenge**

1. **Is the 29–22 vote confirmed?** Answered — yes; Crowdfund Insider, HFSC press release, and American Banker all report a bipartisan 29–22 committee vote at the 30 Jun 2026 markup. Matches the note's source text.
2. **Did the bill pass the full House or just committee?** Answered — committee only. It was *reported out of* HFSC; it has NOT passed the House floor. The note headline ("clears House committee") is accurate; do not overstate.
3. **Did H.R. 7428 really die on the floor?** Answered — H.R. 7428 (118th Cong.) cleared committee 28–22 (17 Apr 2024) but never got a floor vote and lapsed when the 118th Congress ended Jan 2025. The note's "died on the House floor" is loosely worded (it died *without reaching* a floor vote) but directionally correct.
4. **Is 226 (vs. note's "225") groups correct?** Answered — the updated NCLC opposition letter (29 Jun 2026) is signed by 226 groups/academics; the note says "more than 225." Consistent.
5. **What are the odds of enactment?** Open — commentators note few legislative vehicles remain before year-end and no Senate momentum; passage this Congress is uncertain. Symbolically important, practically stalled.
6. **Does the bill federally preempt state EWA laws?** Partly open — it declares EWA "not credit" under *federal* law; opponents (and Pressley's amendment) fear it undermines state protections. The exact preemption text of H.R. 9330 was not verified from primary source; treat "preempts states" as a contested claim, not confirmed.
7. **How does H.R. 9330 differ from H.R. 7428?** Open — described as "largely similar." Specific deltas (e.g., credit-reporting provisions) could not be confirmed from primary bill text; flagged as unresolved.
8. **Is the CFPB Dec-2025 opinion the same as this bill?** Answered — no. The CFPB advisory opinion (non-binding, effective 23 Dec 2025) reaches the same "not credit under TILA" conclusion administratively; H.R. 9330 would codify it in statute. Distinct instruments, aligned direction.
9. **Market-size figures reliable?** Caution — $2.5–3.3bn US 2025 estimates come from vendor market-research firms (market.us) with wide variance; the $22.8bn / 214M-transaction figure is CFPB 2022 data (authoritative but dated). Use CFPB numbers as the anchor.
10. **Is EWA actually high-cost like payday loans?** Open/contested — CFPB's own 2024 data spotlight found effective APRs can be high once expedited fees/tips are annualized; industry disputes framing since products are non-recourse and often free. Genuinely two-sided; do not adjudicate.
11. **Is this US-only relevance?** Answered — yes, US federal policy. Internal DB EWA notes are mostly non-US (Indonesia, Vietnam, South Africa, UAE), so limited direct read-through, but the "is-it-credit" fault line is global.
12. **Source quality of the note itself?** Caution — the note's only sources are Substack redirect links (Fintech Business Weekly digest); no primary source loaded. External corroboration (Congress, CFPB, trade press) is strong, so the underlying facts stand.
13. **Duplicate risk?** Answered — no prior note covers U.S. federal EWA legislation or the CFPB TILA opinion; this is a fresh event, not a restatement.
14. **Any conflict of interest in sources?** Noted — FTA (industry lobby) and NCLC/AFR/CRL (advocacy) are both interested parties; presented as opposing camps, not neutral fact.
15. **Second-order effect for fintechs?** Open — enactment would give US EWA providers (DailyPay, EarnIn, Payactiv, Rain) statutory safe harbor and de-risk expansion; failure keeps the state patchwork (esp. California's lending treatment) as the binding constraint.

**Importance: 3/5** — A meaningful, well-covered US regulatory milestone on a fast-growing ($2.5bn+, 55M users) fintech vertical, defining whether EWA is "a benefit or a loan," and reinforcing the CFPB's Dec-2025 pivot. Docked from higher because it is committee-stage only, likely stalls without Senate momentum, and largely reprises the 2024 H.R. 7428 fight rather than breaking new ground.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** US earned wage access (EWA) is a small-but-fast subvertical of consumer lending/payroll. Sizing is vendor-heavy and inconsistent (treat as directional, not precise): North America EWA ~**$2.99bn in 2025, ~42% of the global market, projected ~$3.76bn in 2026** (per market.us, as of 2026 — vendor estimate); global "employer-sponsored EWA" put at ~$4.53bn (2025) → ~$5.9bn (2026), ~30% CAGR (per Research and Markets, 2026). The one authoritative anchor remains **CFPB 2022 data: $22.8bn advanced across 214M transactions** (employer-partnered), so headline "market size" $bn figures conflate advance volume, revenue and software — do not read them as revenue. Structure: **fragmented**, split between direct-to-consumer apps (EarnIn, Branch) and employer-/payroll-integrated providers (DailyPay, Payactiv, Rain), with payroll/HCM integrations (Workday, SAP SuccessFactors) as the emerging distribution moat. Entry barriers are regulatory (state licensing) + capital (advances are balance-sheet-funded via credit facilities/ABS), not technology. **Why now:** the note's event (H.R. 9330 out of HFSC 29–22, 30 Jun 2026) plus the **CFPB Dec-2025 "not credit under TILA" advisory opinion** together mark a decisive federal tilt toward the "benefit, not loan" framing — the first coordinated attempt to lift EWA out of TILA/Reg Z and the state patchwork.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: **advance volume (GTV/wages disbursed), transaction count, active users, employer/enterprise logos, and effective all-in fee/APR** (the contested one). Key players & basis of competition (distribution + funding cost, not price — most offer a free/slow tier):
- **DailyPay** — employer-integrated leader; **180+ HCM/payroll integrations, 1,000+ enterprise clients** (Target, Hilton, Kroger). Competes on enterprise distribution + funding scale (>$1bn debt financing: $960M secured facility + $200M June-2025 ABS). (per DailyPay, 2025)
- **Payactiv** — category pioneer (2012); **$15bn+ wages moved, 5M+ employees, 40,000+ businesses**; expanding compliance footprint (CA registered Feb-2025, CT licensed Mar-2026) and channels (SAP Store Dec-2025, U.S. Bank partnership). (per Payactiv, 2025–26)
- **EarnIn** — direct-to-consumer app; **~$93.6M revenue (2025, getlatka est.)**; debt-funded (Line of Credit $150M Sep-2025; $75M MUFG facility Sep-2025).
- **Branch / Rain** — payroll-embedded challengers (Rain lives inside Workday per [[Rain launches embedded earned wage access within Workday]]).
Protagonist here is not a company but the **industry lobby (FTA)** vs. a **226-group consumer/labor coalition** — this is a regulatory contest, not a product race. Position of the EWA industry: **on offense at the federal level** (CFPB + HFSC both moving its way) but the win is committee-stage and fragile.

**Comps & multiples.** These are private companies; only round/estimate valuations exist, so multiples are illustrative, not market-cap-based:
- **DailyPay** — last valuation **~$1.75bn (Jan-2024)**, revenue **~$235M (2024)** → implied **P/S ≈ $1.75bn / $235M ≈ 7.4x** (round valuation, not market cap; stale). (PitchBook/CB Insights est.)
- **EarnIn** — one vendor puts valuation **~$280.8M** on revenue **~$93.6M (2025)** → **P/S ≈ 3.0x** — but these figures are low-confidence single-source (getlatka) and conflict with reported total funding of $350–518M; treat as `[UNSOURCED]`-adjacent.
- **Payactiv, Branch, Rain** — no reliable valuation → **no data**.
Distribution not computed (only ~1 semi-credible P/S). Qualitative read: DailyPay's ~7x P/S is in-line-to-rich for a capital-intensive, lending-adjacent model whose growth is real but whose margins depend on cheap funding — **not a pure-SaaS multiple**, so 7x should be read against balance-sheet risk, not compared to software peers.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory / "is-it-a-loan" reclassification.** The whole business model's economics hinge on EWA *not* being credit. CFPB Dec-2025 + H.R. 9330 tilt that way, but the CFPB opinion is non-binding and reversible with an administration change, and its own 2024 data spotlight showed high annualized effective APRs once expedited fees/tips are counted. A future re-classification under TILA would force disclosures/APR caps and compress unit economics. (second-order: repricing of the entire vertical)
2. **State preemption is now explicit and contested.** Unlike the 2024 H.R. 7428, the current H.R. 9330 **explicitly preempts state EWA laws** (per Fintech Business Weekly) — resolving note red-team Q6 in the affirmative for the *bill text*, though enactment is unlikely. This makes California (EWA-as-lending under the CFL) the live battleground; if the federal bill stalls (likely), the binding constraint stays the state patchwork, not the friendlier federal frame. (second-order: legal uncertainty caps enterprise adoption)
3. **Funding-cost / balance-sheet dependence.** Advances are funded via credit facilities and ABS (DailyPay >$1bn debt; EarnIn MUFG/LoC). Margins are rate-sensitive; a funding-cost or credit-quality shock hits the whole model — this is a lending business wearing a "benefit" label. (second-order: EWA is not a capital-light SaaS despite the framing)

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` The event confirms EWA is being *institutionalized as a payroll benefit rather than a loan* at the federal level, following the CFPB's Dec-2025 pivot — favorable for incumbents (DailyPay, Payactiv) that need regulatory certainty to sell into large enterprises. But this is a **symbolic, not a done, win**: committee-only, no Senate companion, no must-pass vehicle → enactment this Congress is unlikely, and the state patchwork (esp. California-as-lending) remains the real constraint. **Falsifiable thesis:** if H.R. 9330 stalls (base case), watch for the binding action to shift to *states and the courts* over the "not credit / preemption" question — the trigger to re-rate the sector is a Senate companion bill or a shift in CFPB posture, not this committee vote.
