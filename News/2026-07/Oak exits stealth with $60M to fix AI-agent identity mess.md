Backed by $60M in funding, Oak steps out of stealth to fix the identity mess that AI agents are making worse
Physical badges used to be all you needed for identity management at a company. But with humans now working alongside machines and AI agents in digital environments, even the identity tools built for the cloud era are proving inadequate.
That’s the gap Israeli startup Oak is stepping out of stealth to fill, it says. Co-founded by serial entrepreneur Shai Morag, the company has been quietly building a unified control plane that governs identity across an organization, and is now emerging publicly with its product generally available and already deployed by enterprise clients, backed by $60 million in seed funding that it raised late last year.
The company didn’t disclose client names, but said its solution is already generally available and deployed by enterprise clients.
Outdated credentials and poor identity access management — or IAM, the systems that control who and what can access company data — are a common security vulnerability, one that AI is expected to make even easier for attackers to exploit. Oak also calls itself AI-native, positioning itself as a replacement for legacy tools that were already showing their limits but had no consolidated alternative.
According to Oak’s other co-founder, chief product officer Tal Marom, the startup spent months talking to 100 CISOs and IAM leaders before building its product: an AI connector framework that maps access to actual app usage and removes permissions that are no longer needed in real time, rather than only during periodic reviews.
“Right now, the whole process is too manual, and it’s operations-based, not risk-based — for instance, there’s no trigger when an employee logs in from an unusual location,” said Morag, a former army major who spent more than two decades in cybersecurity. During that time, he had three exits, including selling cyber startup Secdo to Palo Alto Networks in 2018.
This track record helped Oak raise what is a very big round by local standards, one that matches its plans to invest heavily in R&D and growth, Morag said. “Our vision is to be born as a giant,” he told TechCrunch. 
Morag’s résumé already includes a stint at a giant organization. After public cyber company Tenable acquired his cloud identity and security startup Ermetic for $265 million in 2023, he stayed on as CPO. But after CEO Amit Yoran became ill and passed away , Morag left and told his wife he’d retire.
Instead of stepping back, though, Morag co-founded Oak with Marom, a product team lead he’d met at Tenable who’d previously held similar roles at Salesforce and in the Israeli military. While in stealth, the two also built a team of 50 people and are actively hiring, particularly in the U.S., where a majority of Oak’s staff will soon be based, Morag said.
Oak’s $60 million round was co-led by Accel, CRV, and Greylock Partners, with participation from AlphaDrive Ventures, Hetz Ventures, and angel investors. Morag told TechCrunch that VC interest was strong from the outset.
Accel partner Andrei Brasoveanu said Morag’s track record alone was a strong argument. Accel had led Ermetic’s Series A when it was pre-revenue; when Tenable acquired it, Accel gave Morag an informal standing offer to back whatever he built next, Brasoveanu said. “I knew he had it in him to build another company, but this time even bigger and even better.”
With AI as “a democratizing force,” Accel has been backing founders right out of high school, Brasoveanu said. But when it comes to identity management, experience still counts. “There’s complexity in the product, and there’s also complexity in the organizations you have to navigate to figure out how to sell something like this,” he said.
Both Brasoveanu and Morag expect Oak will face plenty of competitors trying to use AI as a catalyst for change in a space where vendor lock-in runs deep. That makes it critical for Oak to scale fast. Morag, who told his wife this will be his last company, says he won’t retire until he’s given it everything he’s got: “I will go big or go home.”

# Context-enrichment: Oak exits stealth with $60M to fix AI-agent identity mess
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5. Freshness: FRESH (no prior note covers this raise; not a duplicate)._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Israeli startup **Oak** (oak.id) exited stealth on 2026-07-15 with a **$60M seed** co-led by **Accel, CRV, Greylock**, plus AlphaDrive Ventures, Hetz Ventures, Startpoint Capital and angels. The round was **raised "late 2025"** and the company was **founded ~Dec 2025** — so the mega-seed and the founding are roughly concurrent (a pre-product raise). ~50 staff, majority relocating to San Francisco. Co-founders: **Shai Morag (CEO)**, **Tal Marom (CPO)**.

What it actually is: an "AI-native identity operating system" / unified control plane for human + machine + AI-agent identities. The one real mechanical claim: an **AI connector framework that maps granted access to *actual app usage* and revokes stale permissions in real time**, versus periodic access reviews — i.e. continuous IGA + CIEM rebuilt around usage-based real-time revocation.

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** This is functionally **Ermetic v2** (Morag's prior cloud-identity co, sold to Tenable for ~$265M) generalised from cloud to *all* identity types including non-human/agent identities. The "$60M seed" framing is the tell: it is a **founder-premium round priced on a ~$265M+ exit track record, not on Oak traction**. The PR anchors to pedigree ("born as a giant," "go big or go home") precisely because the product story is thin. "Generally available and deployed by enterprise clients / dozens of customers" is stated with **zero named logos, no integrations, no metrics** — treat GA-at-7-months as an unverified vendor claim, not proven traction.

## [1] Competitors / peers
The "AI agents break identity" (non-human identity, NHI) category is crowded and **actively consolidating**. Oak enters *behind on shipped product and named logos, ahead only on capital.*

NHI-native startups: **Oasis Security** — $120M Series B (Mar 2026, Craft/Sequoia/**Accel** — same lead as Oak), shipped Agentic Access Mgmt Nov 2025; **Astrix Security** — acquired by Cisco (~$400M, closed Jun 2026, terms unconfirmed) — see [[Menlo Ventures raises record $3 billion fund on Anthropic bet]] which footnotes the Astrix/Cisco exit; **Token Security** ($20M Series A, Jan 2025); **Silverfort** (~$1B unicorn, acquired Fabrix Apr 2026); **Aembit** (IAM for Agentic AI + MCP Identity Gateway, GA Apr 2026). Internal peer: [[Teleport gives every human, machine, and agent a cryptographic identity]] (2026-06) — nearly identical "unified identity layer for humans, machines and agents" thesis; and [[Vouched launches Agent Checkpoint to verify AI agent identity]] (2026-02) — the clearest prior articulation of the same "agents break the rules of cybersecurity" argument.

Incumbents (the real threat, given deep vendor lock-in): **Okta** ("Okta for AI Agents" GA Apr 2026; cf. [[Okta to purchase Axiom Security to bolster privileged access]] — Israeli PAM startup with the *same* AI-connector/JIT/no-standing-privilege mechanics Oak describes); **Microsoft Entra Agent ID**; **SailPoint** (Agentic Fabric); **CyberArk** (being acquired by Palo Alto, ~$25B).

**Why the landscape is this way / second-order:** Standards are emerging that threaten a proprietary control plane — Anthropic's **MCP authorization** spec (2025-11-25, OAuth 2.1 resource-server model), IETF/OIDC-A agent-auth work, SPIFFE/SPIRE for workloads. If the connective/authz layer commoditises into a standard, Oak's differentiation collapses to the usage-graph. The central question shifts from "is the thesis real" (it is — funded everywhere) to **"can a greenfield control plane beat incumbents who already ship agent identity AND own the directory, before the category standardises or consolidates around them."**

## [2] Company history / fit
Morag = serial cyber founder, three-to-four exits: Integrity-Project → Mellanox (2014, amount undisclosed); **Secdo → Palo Alto Networks (2018)** — bios say $100M but **PANW's FY2018 10-K reports $82.7M cash** (cite the SEC figure); **Ermetic → Tenable (2023, ~$265M)** confirmed via SEC 8-K. Became Tenable CPO (Aug 2024); left after CEO **Amit Yoran died Jan 2025**, said he'd retire, then founded Oak instead. Former army major (specific unit unconfirmed — do not attribute 8200). CPO Tal Marom: product roles at Tenable and Salesforce.

**Why the company acts this way:** Oak is a logical fourth act — Morag returns to the *exact* adjacency of his last (Ermetic cloud identity), riding the agent wave, and monetises the pedigree via an outsized seed rather than bootstrapping traction. The "un-retirement" narrative and "this is my last company" are GTM theatre that de-risk the round for VCs.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Thesis: real and heavily funded** — Gartner projects **40% of enterprise apps will feature task-specific AI agents by 2026 (up from <5%)**; NHI:human ratios cited 45:1–144:1 (high end unconfirmed); every incumbent shipped an agent-identity product in H1 2026; Oasis raised $120M, Astrix was acquired. **Novelty of Oak specifically: thin/unproven.** "Usage-based real-time deprovisioning + one graph across all identity types" is credible but not clearly differentiated from Oasis, Token, Silverfort/Fabrix, or Okta/CyberArk's shipped agent products — and **heavily overlaps Morag's own Ermetic**.

**Why value-add is unconfirmed:** No named customers, no architecture detail, no benchmarks, only GDPR/CCPA (no SOC 2/ISO stated). $60M seed is genuinely outsized (2026 cyber seed median ~$5.7M; Calcalist: "one of the largest ever by an Israeli cyber company") — but that measures **investor conviction in the founder**, not product validation. In the stack, the margin risk is that incumbents own the directory/identity source-of-truth and standards (MCP/OAuth-for-agents) commoditise the connective layer, leaving Oak a governance overlay that must be *bought into* against entrenched lock-in.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Expect fast US GTM build-out, a marked-up Series A within ~12 months, and a likely **M&A endgame** — Morag has never IPO'd (all exits were acquisitions), and Cisco/PANW/CyberArk/Snowflake are all actively buying NHI. Sentiment is hype-forward but pedigree-justified: coverage (TechCrunch, Globes, Calcalist) is an orchestrated PR wave whose real story is Morag's fourth act, not proven differentiation. **Counterintuitive second-order:** capital and hype concentration make Oak *fragile*, not safe — it must out-execute a consolidating field where incumbents already ship, and its own lead investor (Accel) is simultaneously funding a direct rival (Oasis), signalling category conviction rather than Oak exclusivity.

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** Oak sits in non-human / agent identity & access management (NHI-IAM) — governing machine + AI-agent credentials alongside human ones. Sizing is contested and vendor-driven: Grand View put the NHI-AM market at ~$11.1bn (2025) → ~$27.3bn (2033) at ~11.9% CAGR; MarketIntelo pegs a narrower NHI-management slice at ~$3.6bn (2025) → ~$22bn (2034, ~22% CAGR) — treat the absolute figures as promotional, but the direction (double-digit CAGR, AI-agent sub-segment fastest) is consistent (per Grand View / MarketIntelo, as of 2026). "Why now": machine identities now outnumber humans ~109:1, up from ~82:1 a year earlier, and ~79 of those 109 are AI agents (per Palo Alto Networks 2026 Identity Security Landscape); a SANS 2026 survey found 76% of orgs report machine-identity growth and agentic AI opening governance gaps, yet only ~15% feel confident preventing NHI-based attacks. Structure: still fragmented across legacy IGA/PAM incumbents (Okta, CyberArk/[[Ping Identity to acquire Keyless for biometric authentication|Ping]], SailPoint, Microsoft Entra) plus a cluster of NHI startups; barriers are enterprise distribution and deep vendor lock-in, not tech — Oak's own investor concedes lock-in "runs deep."

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: enterprise logos / seats, ARR & NRR (land-and-expand into an existing IAM footprint), and coverage breadth (human + machine + agent on one control plane). Key players & basis of competition:
- **NHI-native startups** — Astrix, Oasis, Entro, Clutch, GitGuardian, Token Security. Compete on agent/machine-identity depth and AI-native discovery.
- **Incumbents** — Okta, CyberArk, Microsoft Entra, SailPoint. Compete on installed base + suite bundling.
- **Recent moves:** Cisco moved to acquire **Astrix** (~$250–350M, closed 2026-05); **Oasis** raised ~$120M from Craft Ventures (mid-2026), ~$195M total; **GitGuardian** +$50M (Insight, Feb 2026); CrowdStrike bought **SGNL** for ~$628M (Jan 2026); Cyera acquired Otterize (Jun 2025). **Risk flags.**
1. **Category already consolidating around Oak** — Astrix (Cisco), SGNL (CrowdStrike) and GitGuardian moves mean the best exits (acquihire into a platform) may be taken before Oak scales; a seed-stage entrant risks arriving to a market where incumbents have bought the differentiation.
