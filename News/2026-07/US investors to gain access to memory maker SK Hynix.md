US investors will soon get access to SK Hynix, another memory maker riding the AI boom
South Korean memory chipmaker SK Hynix, rival to Samsung and U.S.-based Micron , is planning to sell nearly 17.8 million shares in a U.S. IPO, the company said on Monday . Should its shares sell well (and there’s indication that they will), the company could raise around $28 billion, based on SK Hynix’s closing share price last Friday in Seoul, Bloomberg reports .
SK Hynix will be offering American depositary receipts (ADRs), a type of certificate that lets U.S. investors buy a foreign stock without trading directly on an overseas exchange. Each ADR will represent a tenth of a common share. It is expected to price those securities on Thursday and begin trading on Friday.
Like Micron, SK Hynix is riding an AI-fueled boom credited to AI in both sales and stock price. Its first-quarter revenues were up nearly 200% over the same quarter last year, it said, and its stock is up about 260% so far this year. This is because systems that run AI are very memory intensive. As hyperscalers like Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Oracle race to build out so-called AI factories, and as new AI data centers multiply nationwide, demand has outpaced supply, creating a shortage of memory chips — including high-bandwidth memory (HBM), DRAM, and NAND (the different types of chips that store and move data inside AI systems). The situation has been called “ RAMageddon .” Apple executives said the shortage is forcing it to raise prices on Mac computers and iPads .
South Korean tech companies, led by SK Hynix and Samsung, have vowed to spend over $550 billion on building out new manufacturing capacity to keep up. That’s actually a risky venture. By the time those facilities are built, memory needs for AI may change, leaving them with more supply than the market wants and, potentially, crashing prices. But for now, Wall Street is looking for another Nvidia, and memory chipmakers are among the closest options that they have.
Micron, the closest U.S. comparison, has shot up nearly 700% over the past year to a more than $1 trillion valuation, fueled by record AI-driven memory demand and revenue.

# Context-enrichment: US investors to gain access to memory maker SK Hynix
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **Monday 2026-07-06 SK Hynix launched** a US securities offering: **~17.79M newly issued common shares** sold to US investors as **~177.9M American Depositary Receipts (ADRs), each ADR = 1/10 of a common share**, on the **Nasdaq Global Select Market under ticker SKHY**. Reference/expected pricing ~$165/ADR; target raise **~$28B** (trimmed from an earlier ~$29–29.65B / ₩45.45T plan after the stock pulled back). Pricing set for Thursday, first trade **~Friday July 10**. Global coordinators: **BofA Securities, Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, J.P. Morgan** (+ ~9 others). Proceeds fund **Korean fab expansion (Yongin cluster, Cheongju packaging) and ASML EUV scanners** — not US operations.

**+ Why structured this way / what it reveals — the core de-PR:** TechCrunch and much of the press call this "a US IPO" and "the largest IPO by a foreign company in US history" (ahead of Alibaba's $21.8B 2014 NYSE debut). **This is a misnomer.** SK Hynix is **already public on Korea's KOSPI (000660.KS)** with a >$1T market cap. This is a **primary follow-on capital raise packaged as a Nasdaq ADR cross-listing** — new shares whose proceeds flow to the company (not insiders cashing out), diluting existing holders by ~2.5% of market cap. The "IPO" label is press shorthand for the US *debut*, not a first-ever equity sale. The **1/10 ADR ratio is a deliberate retail-accessibility design** — it lowers the per-unit dollar price so US retail can buy round lots. Framing it as an "IPO" flatters the deal; the reality is a mega-cap secondary raise into a hot AI-memory tape.

**Timeline note (freshness-critical):** the listing plan was **first public ~2026-06-24 (CNBC)**, F-1 amended **~06-30**; July 6 is the **launch milestone**, not the first announcement. Within *our corpus*, however, the US-listing event itself was never covered (June notes treat SK Hynix only as a memory supplier), so it is a new event for the pipeline.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Micron (MU, US):** the closest comp and the pipeline's reference frame ([[Wall Street eyes memory maker Micron as next Nvidia]]). FQ3 2026 revenue ~$42B (~4x YoY), **net income $28.24B vs $1.89B ≈ 15-fold jump** ([[Micron quarterly profit jumps 15-fold]]), gross margin ~85%. TechCrunch's "Micron up ~700%, >$1T valuation" claim is directionally plausible but **unverified** in this research.
- **Samsung (KOSPI):** #2 HBM (~21%), also Korea-listed, no comparable US listing; separately in talks with Anthropic on custom AI chips ([[Anthropic in talks with Samsung on custom AI chip]]).
- **HBM share (Q1 2026):** SK Hynix **~58–60%** vs Samsung ~21%, Micron ~21% — SK Hynix is the **leading HBM supplier to Nvidia** (~$5.2B HBM to Nvidia in Q1, +62.6% YoY).
- Capital-markets peer context: Asian AI/chip listings are the IPO story of 2026 ([[Chinese AI and chip firms drive mainland IPO rebound]]).

**+ Why the lay of the land / second-order:** memory is a **3-firm oligopoly controlling >95% of DRAM** ([[AI memory-chip demand pushes Apple to raise iPhone prices]], [[Apple seeks memory chips from sanctioned supplier, FT reports]]). SK Hynix leads specifically in HBM, the highest-margin, most AI-levered slice — so it captures the richest part of the "RAMageddon" shortage. Wall Street wants "another Nvidia"; Micron got the US-listed proxy first, and SKHY now offers a **purer HBM-leader proxy** without the KRW/Korea-brokerage friction. The valuation premium here is **sectoral (AI-memory cycle), not name-specific** — which is also its fragility (see [4]).

## [2] Company history / fit
SK Hynix rode the AI-memory boom hard: **Q1 2026 revenue ₩52.58T (~$35.5B), +198% YoY, +60% QoQ, ~72% operating margin**; stock **+~250–260% YTD** and **~+770% trailing 12 months** to a >$1T cap. HBM sold out well in advance; chairman flagged possible wafer shortage into 2030. Korean chipmakers led by SK Hynix and Samsung have pledged **>$550B** in new capacity.

**+ Why the company acts this way:** SK Hynix needs **enormous capex** (fabs + scarce ASML EUV tools) to defend its HBM lead, and its equity is at an all-time-high multiple. Selling ~2.5% dilution at peak valuation into a US ADR structure is a **cheap-cost-of-capital move**: monetize the AI-memory re-rating to fund the very capacity race that sustains it, while broadening the shareholder base beyond KOSPI and improving index/passive-fund eligibility. Doing it as ADRs (vs a full US primary listing) keeps the KOSPI listing and Korean control intact.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**The mechanism is not new — ADRs are the legacy, regulated wrapper** that lets US investors hold a foreign equity in USD through an ordinary US brokerage (Schwab, Fidelity, Robinhood, Webull all expected to carry SKHY), with a depositary bank holding the underlying Korean shares. What's genuinely notable is **scale**: at ~$28B it is the **largest ADR offering on record** (past Alibaba's $21.8B). Traction is real, not just announced: **~$7B of anchor/cornerstone interest** reportedly indicated (Baillie Gifford, Coatue, "Situational Awareness"-type funds) — though that is *indicated interest*, not committed.

**+ Why the value-add is real / who captures the margin, deeper:** the **fintech value-add is access, not innovation.** ADRs achieve the same "own a foreign stock without touching the foreign exchange" goal that **fractional foreign-share and tokenized-equity** platforms now chase — but through the incumbent depositary-bank rail, so **the fee margin accrues to the underwriters (BofA/Citi/GS/JPM), the depositary bank, and Nasdaq**, not to any fintech disruptor. For US brokerages, SKHY is incremental order flow, not a new capability. The second-order fintech read: **tokenization's pitch is precisely to disintermediate this stack** (24/7 trading, fractional-by-default, cheaper settlement) — a $28B ADR mega-deal is a reminder of how much value still sits in the legacy cross-listing rail that tokenized-equity startups are targeting.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Pricing Thursday, trading ~Friday July 10 on Nasdaq (SKHY). Sentiment euphoric — "another Nvidia," record AI-memory demand, HBM sold out. But: the deal was **downsized ~20% from the June peak** as the stock fell, and the Motley Fool caution — "a lot of good news is already in the price" — is apt.

**+ Why the market goes this way / counterintuitive second-order:** memory is **deeply cyclical**, and the >$550B capacity buildout is the classic setup for a glut — by the time fabs come online, AI-memory demand may have shifted, crashing prices (the note's own primary source flags this). **The AI re-rating that makes SKHY attractive is also what makes it fragile:** buyers get a leveraged bet on the HBM cycle, not a durable moat. A US-listing at cycle-peak valuation transfers cyclical downside to a broader US retail base — the "access" story cuts both ways. Regulatory backdrop is benign (standard SEC F-1/ADR), but the political memory-shortage angle ([[AI memory-chip demand pushes Apple to raise iPhone prices]]) means the underlying commodity is politically charged.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The fintech angle is *foreign-equity access mechanics*, not the chip itself: SK Hynix is doing a **traditional ADR listing on Nasdaq (ticker SKHY), ~$28bn raise, priced ~$158–166/ADR, first trade July 10 2026** (per CNBC 2026-06-24; Yahoo/Reuters; SK Hynix 07-06 filing) — the *second-largest US stock listing ever* by a foreign firm. Structure: **1 common share = 10 ADRs**, a 10:1 split deliberately setting a low retail per-share price so any standard US brokerage account (incl. Robinhood) can buy it like a domestic stock (WEEX/moomoo guides). So the "US investors gain access" story is the **oldest, most regulated cross-border access rail — the depositary receipt** — not tokenization. Sector context: retail platforms are racing to open *global* access, but via two divergent rails — (a) **regulated ADR/listing** (what SK Hynix chose), (b) **tokenized/synthetic exposure** (Robinhood, Gemini/Dinari, Coinbase, Backed). Demand driver: memory shortage ("RAMageddon") from AI data-centre buildout; HBM market ~$62bn 2026 vs ~$30bn 2025 (TrendForce, via marketdigests). "Why now": AI-memory names are the "next Nvidia" trade, and Korea's frozen domestic IPO market pushes issuers to deeper US pools.

**Competitive landscape.** For the *access platforms*, the KPIs are jurisdictional coverage, asset breadth, fractional support, and whether US persons are actually eligible. Two camps: (1) **ADR/listing rail** — universal, works in any US brokerage account, SEC-registered, full economic + voting rights; SK Hynix, Coupang, Alibaba precedent. (2) **Tokenized rail** — **Robinhood Chain** (Arbitrum L2, mainnet live 2026-07-01, ~200 US stocks/ETFs across 120+ jurisdictions) [[Coinbase launches 24 7 US stock trading with Yahoo Finance]], **Gemini + Dinari** [[Gemini launches tokenized stocks in Europe]], **Citi DDR** [[Citi launches tokenized shares of private companies]], LatAm apps [[This Week in Fintech LatAm fintechs open US equities access]], **Bitso** [[Bitso commits 5 million to democratize equity investing in Mexico]]. **Critical de-PR fact:** tokenized equities are **NOT available to US residents** and coverage of *Asian* primary listings is **nil on all four major platforms** (eco.com; cryptobriefing 2026). So for a US retail investor wanting SK Hynix, the tokenized rail does *nothing* — the ADR is the only route. Protagonist position: SK Hynix is a *supply-side* participant here (it picked the incumbent ADR rail); the fintech "winner" is whichever broker captures the flow, and the incumbent rail (any brokerage) wins by default over tokenization for this name.

**Comps & multiples.** Access-platform comps are qualitative (private/pre-scale, no clean disclosed access-revenue). The load-bearing comp is the *underlying* — **Micron (MU)**, the memory peer US retail already owns: ~**$1tn market cap** (reached 05-2026, fastest ever), FY2026 consensus revenue ~**$76bn** → **P/S ≈ $1,000bn / $76bn ≈ 13.2x** *(analysis; both figures sourced)*; forward **P/E ~5.7–7x** (Micron guidance run-rate, per marketdigests/Forbes) — cheap vs 20–40x AI peers because memory is still priced as cyclical. SK Hynix ADR: ~$28bn raise at ~$158/ADR; a full market-cap and clean EV/Rev is **[UNSOURCED]** here (float vs total shares not confirmed in-note). Internal ADR/listing comp: **[[South Korea's Toss postpones US ADR listing plan]]** — same rail, opposite outcome (Toss *slipped* its ADR into a frozen Korean IPO backdrop), underlining that SK Hynix is clearing a window Korean peers can't. Flag: MU's ~5.7x forward P/E is **below** the EV/EBITDA sanity floor for the sector — a *cheap* signal that the market disbelieves the memory earnings are durable; a re-rating risk cuts both ways for SK Hynix's debut multiple.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Cyclicality mispriced as secular** — Korea vowed >$550bn capex; if AI-memory demand shifts before fabs finish, oversupply crashes DRAM/HBM prices. Retail buying SKHY at the AI-euphoria top inherits commodity-cycle risk dressed as a growth story (MU's ~6x P/E is the market saying exactly this).
2. **ADR ≠ tokenization decouples the "access" narratives** — the fintech disruptors (Robinhood/Gemini/Coinbase tokenized rails) are *irrelevant* to US buyers of SK Hynix (no US residents, no Asian coverage). Access here is delivered by the boring incumbent rail; the second-order effect is that for *hard-to-reach Asian names*, tokenization has **not** disintermediated the ADR — a caution against the "tokenization opens global equities" thesis.
3. **Window/valuation risk** — largest-ever foreign US listing into a hot but narrow "next-Nvidia" trade; [[South Korea's Toss postpones US ADR listing plan]] shows Korean ADR windows can slam shut. A soft SKHY debut would chill the ADR pipeline.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** *(analysis)* Thesis: the SK Hynix ADR is a **stress test of "old rail vs new rail" for foreign-equity access** — it proves that for the most-wanted hard-to-reach names (large Asian issuers, US retail buyers), the **regulated ADR/direct-listing still beats tokenization**, because tokenized platforms structurally exclude US persons and Asian primaries. Watch: (a) SKHY's 07-10 debut and first-week volume as the retail-demand proxy; (b) whether any tokenized platform ever lists an *Asian* equity for US persons — the trigger that would falsify flag #2 and make the disruptor thesis real. Falsifier: if SK Hynix had listed via a tokenized wrapper for US retail, the ADR-primacy call breaks — it did not.
