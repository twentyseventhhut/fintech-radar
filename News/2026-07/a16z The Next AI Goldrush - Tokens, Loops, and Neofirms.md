# Context-enrichment: a16z — "The Next AI Goldrush: Tokens, Loops, and Neofirms"
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5. Freshness: fresh._

Two framing corrections that change how to read this before anything else:
1. **Not an a16z partner thesis.** It is a **guest op-ed by George Sivulka, founder/CEO of Hebbia** (an a16z portfolio company), published on a16z's Substack (a16z.news) on **2026-07-14**. The on-page headline is actually "You just hired a million bad employees"; "The Next AI Goldrush: Tokens, Loops, and Neofirms" is the promo/slug title. Treat as founder-talking-his-book amplified by a16z, not a fund position.
2. **The text is more skeptical than the bullish title.** De-PR'd, Sivulka argues most token spend and most agent "loops" are *waste*; the real prize is boring "transformation" work, not the shiny "neofirm" category.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Source: a16z.news, 2026-07-14 (https://www.a16z.news/p/the-next-ai-goldrush-tokens-loops).
- **Core inversion:** "For the first time in history, humans are cheaper than software." Intelligence is now abundant, but orgs waste it the way they waste human labor.
- **Tokens** = LLM API consumption, treated as employees. Claim: "80% of tokens today do nothing" because "1 in 100 employees knows how to give AI context" — "spending tokens on spending tokens."
- **Loops** = agents recursively calling themselves ("meetings about meetings") — framed pejoratively as a bandaid for bad prompting, not a feature ("looping is the new empire building").
- **Neofirms** = AI-native services startups funded to attack "$21 trillion of services spend across the knowledge economy." Sivulka's twist: "transformation companies" (encoding incumbents' processes into agents) will be ~10x larger than any neofirm; cites **Palantir** ("half-trillion-dollar business" selling transformation, not software) and Musk's X (~80% headcount cut) as evidence most employees add no value. Actionable claim: "a firm's eval suite will become its most valuable resource."
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals (analysis):** the piece is a management critique dressed as a goldrush call. The charts ("token spend per employee") are shown *without hard numbers* — rhetorical, not data-backed. And Hebbia sells exactly the "institutional AI / context / eval" layer Sivulka names as the real prize → the essay is a positioning document for his own category. → The central question is not "is the neofirm real" but "who captures the value once agent labor is abundant — the neofirm, the incumbent that encodes its own moat, or the tooling/eval layer in between."

## [1] Competitors / peers (framing lineage)
- **"Service-as-Software"** is the ancestor concept — coined outside a16z by Joanne Chen (Foundation Capital), Forbes 2024-04-29, sizing a **$4.6T** professional-services opportunity. The $21T "knowledge economy" number is a broader top-down restatement, not a bottom-up TAM.
- **a16z's own services thesis** (Joe Schmidt IV: "Trading Margin for Moat: the Forward-Deployed Engineer"; investments in FurtherAI/insurance, Treeline) is the same "encode incumbents' workflows" idea Sivulka calls transformation → he is echoing his backer's house view, not breaking from it.
- Peer VC framings: the "picks-and-shovels / goldrush" trope is recycled (a16z "State of AI," "The Next Token of Progress"). **Why the landscape is this way (analysis):** every large fund needs a labor-TAM narrative to justify AI deployment scale (a16z raised a ~$20B AI fund in 2025); the essays are deployment marketing. Second-order: convergence of VC theses on "$X-trillion services TAM" is a signal of narrative crowding, not of a proven operating model.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Internal corpus shows a16z has been building to exactly this for weeks, from adjacent angles:
  - [[a16z Charts of the Week on cycles and AI-native startups]] (2026-06-26) — cites a **515-startup study** on what it takes to move from "AI improves a task" to "AI improves the firm." This is the empirical scaffold under Sivulka's "neofirm" claim.
  - [[a16z speedrun The rise of the solo founder in the AI era]] (2026-06-30, guest essay by Evan Armstrong) — solo founders = **63% of Stripe Atlas C-corps in Q2 2026**, crossing $1M revenue ~3x faster than 2019 cohort. This is the "small headcount, high output" premise of the neofirm.
- a16z essay lineage: Andreessen "Software Is Eating the World" (2011) → "AI Will Eat Application Software" → "Big Ideas 2026" (2025-12-12) → Sivulka's own prior guest posts ("Institutional AI vs Individual AI," ~Mar 2026). This July piece is a direct sequel. **Why a16z acts this way (analysis):** the fund monetizes a coherent, repeated worldview; guest posts from founders (Sivulka, Armstrong) let it push the thesis while keeping deniability on the harder claims.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction (announced vs adopted)
- **Genuinely new/proven — revenue-per-employee is real:** Cursor/Anysphere ~$2B ARR early 2026, reported ~$3.3M/employee (Dealroom), ~15x the enterprise-software average; SpaceX-announced acquisition at **$60B (2026-06-16)**. Peers: Midjourney ~$2M/emp, OpenAI ~$1.5M/emp. *Caveat: headcount figures vary wildly across sources, so per-employee numbers are soft.*
- **Announced/theorized, NOT proven:** the "neofirm replaces the org chart with agent loops" claim has **no cited company with durable revenue + slashed headcount together**. Cursor proves revenue/employee, not the org-replacement model — and it is an AI-native *product* company, not an agent-loop-run *services* firm (category mismatch, used loosely). Sivulka himself concedes loops mostly fail.
- **Where the margin goes (analysis):** if inference commoditizes (below), the token layer captures little; if evals/context are the durable asset, tooling players (Hebbia et al.) capture it; if incumbents encode their own moats ("transformation"), value stays with the incumbent, not the neofirm. → The neofirm is the *weakest* margin-capture position in the stack — which is precisely why Sivulka steers toward transformation and evals.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Hard token-volume numbers (primary):** OpenRouter/a16z "State of AI" (2025-12-04) — ~10T → 100T+ tokens/yr by mid-2025, >1T tokens/day, OpenAI API ~8.6T tokens/day (Oct 2025). Gartner: worldwide AI spend $2.5T in 2026.
- **Inference cost decline (directional, secondary aggregators — treat as estimates):** frontier-quality cost down ~95% over two years by some blog estimates; Epoch AI (more credible) stresses declines are *uneven across tasks*.
- **Adjacent rail — agentic commerce (where these agents would transact):** OpenAI×Stripe ACP / Instant Checkout is **LIVE** (2025-09-29, Etsy/Shopify) — [[Stripe unveils 288 launches for AI economic infrastructure]]. Visa Intelligent Commerce = pilot→early rollout ([[Visa completes live agentic commerce pilots with Nuvei and BBVA]], 2026-07-03). AI-native banking entrant: [[Circle cofounder raises $30 million for AI-native bank Catena Labs]]. → Agentic *commerce* is further along than agentic *labor* (neofirms).
- **Skeptic bench (stronger than the bull case):** MIT "GenAI Divide" (Aug 2025) — 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots show no P&L impact; Gartner — >40% of agentic-AI projects cancelled by end-2027, ~89% of agent pilots never reach production; compound-reliability math (95%/step → ~36% clean completion over 20 steps). **Why the market may still go this way (analysis, counterintuitive):** cheaper inference *enables* neofirms but simultaneously *compresses their moat* via price war (Forbes 2026-06-11) → capital and narrative concentration around AI-native labor makes the category fragile, not safe; the durable winners are more likely the eval/context layer and incumbents that encode their own processes.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a16z commentary, not a deal — the "sector" is AI-native services / agentic software (the applied-AI layer above foundation models). The piece is a guest essay by Hebbia CEO George Sivulka on a16z's Substack ("The Next AI Goldrush: Tokens, Loops, and Neofirms" / "You just hired a million bad employees"), so treat it as an interested party's argument, not neutral research. Size/drivers of the addressed opportunity: the "neofirm" thesis targets ~$21tn of global services (labor) spend that AI-native providers aim to convert from headcount into software (per a16z framing, via a16z.news). Demand driver = token consumption: per Goldman Sachs Research (via siliconangle/duckittech, mid-2026), agentic AI could drive a ~24x increase in monthly token consumption by 2030 (to ~120 quadrillion tokens/month), while inference cost/token falls ~60-70%/yr — the two forces that make agent "loops" economically viable. Agentic-AI *spend* estimates are wide and unsettled: BofA ~$155bn by 2030 (via Fortune, Jun-2025) vs. narrower AI-agents-software TAMs of ~$50bn by 2030 (MarketsandMarkets, ~46% CAGR) — a >3x spread that itself is a signal the category is pre-consensus. **Why now:** reasoning models crossed the threshold where multi-step agent loops beat single-shot prompting, and cost/token is falling fast enough that "spending tokens to save labor" pencils out.

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs the category runs on: ARR + ARR growth rate, EV/ARR (revenue multiple, since all pre-profit), and — Sivulka's real point — token efficiency ("100x tokens," loops that work vs. "spending tokens on spending tokens"). Two competing plays: (1) **neofirms / AI-native services** — greenfield firms selling outcomes into the $21tn services pool (Harvey in legal, Sierra in customer service, Hebbia itself in knowledge work); (2) **AI transformation** — selling net-new capability *into incumbents* who already own differentiated processes + distribution. Basis of competition = distribution and proprietary process/data, not model access (models are commoditizing). The essay's thesis: transformation companies will be "10x larger than any neofirm" because the durable moats sit inside incumbents, not with new entrants — a self-aware hedge from a founder a16z funded on the *services* side. Position: a16z is talking its book across BOTH sides (it backs Hebbia and the neofirm cohort), so the essay reads as thesis-shaping, not a call. `(analysis)`

**Comps & multiples.** No deal in this note; comps below are the AI-native-services peers the thesis is about (round/post-money valuations, not market caps — all private).
- **Harvey** (legal): ~$11bn Mar-2026, ARR >$200m → ~$11bn / $0.2bn ≈ **~55x ARR** (via CNBC).
- **Sierra** (CX): ~$11bn Feb-2026, ARR ~$150m → ~$11bn / $0.15bn ≈ **~73x ARR** (ARR grew $26m→$150m in ~13 mo).
- **Cursor** (coding): ~$29.3bn, ARR ~$500m → **~59x ARR**.
- **Hebbia** (the author's co.): reported **~54x ARR** (multiple only; absolute figures not verified here → `[UNSOURCED]`).
Distribution across 3 verified names: min ~55x, max ~73x, median ~59x EV/ARR. Sanity range for software is ~0.5-20x EV/Rev; these run 55-73x — a **4-15x breach**. Per the growth-multiple test, triple-digit ARR growth can justify a premium, but 55-73x is extreme even so and only holds if hyper-growth persists. Rich vs. any historical software comp. Internal base comps (grep fallback): [[McKinsey projects agentic commerce could drive $5 trillion by 2030]], [[a16z speedrun The rise of the solo founder in the AI era]] (a16z's own solo-founder/AI-native-firm data point — 63% of Q2-2026 Stripe Atlas C-corps solo-founded), [[Circle cofounder raises $30 million for AI-native bank Catena Labs]], [[Stripe unveils 288 launches for AI economic infrastructure]].

**Risk flags.**
1. **Talking-its-book / circular incentive.** a16z is an investor in Hebbia and the broader neofirm cohort; a "goldrush" framing from a portfolio-adjacent author inflates a category a16z is marked into. Second-order: essays like this become the narrative that supports the next markup — read as promotion, not diligence.
2. **Valuation vs. durability of growth.** 55-73x ARR prices in years of triple-digit growth with zero model-commoditization or churn. If model providers (OpenAI/Anthropic) move up the stack into applications, or ARR growth normalizes, these multiples compress violently — and private marks lag, so the risk is unrealized until a down-round.
3. **Thesis self-refutes for the neofirms.** The essay's own conclusion — moats (process + distribution) sit inside incumbents, and "transformation" beats "neofirm" 10x — is an argument that the greenfield AI-native-services names it's hyping are the *weaker* structural bet. Disintermediation risk runs toward incumbents, not away from them.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Watch whether spend actually shifts from the neofirm land-grab toward "AI-transformation-into-incumbents" — the falsifiable trigger is the next 2-3 large rounds/M&A: if incumbents (or picks-and-shovels transformation vendors) capture the funding and revenue rather than pure AI-native-services startups, Sivulka's thesis is confirmed and the 55-73x neofirm marks are at risk. What breaks the thesis: a neofirm crossing ~$1bn ARR with retained hyper-growth would prove greenfield players *can* hold the $21tn pool. Net: a category-defining essay, but source it as an a16z-interested narrative, not an independent read.
