# Context-enrichment: Saible raises £2.9m to expand construction payments platform
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **Deal:** Saible (Birmingham, UK; founded 2023 by Jarvey Moss and Tim Whitehill) announced 2026-07-15 that total funding now stands at **£2.9m** — but this is **£2.1m previously raised + a fresh ~£800k angel round**, not a single new £2.9m round (fintech.global, Tech.eu, UKTN, 2026-07-15). So the "raises £2.9m" headline overstates the new money: the genuinely new capital is **~£0.8m from angel investors**, plus a **£50k Crowdcube allocation** (15–31 July 2026) — i.e. a small top-up / community-round, not a priced institutional seed.
- **What it actually is (minus PR):** Saible's product is the **Digital Parallel Payment Account (DiPPA)** — project funds sit in a **trust held with FCA-regulated banking partner [[Griffin signs Uber and Marqeta as customers|Griffin]]**, and Saible operates the platform doing approvals, verification and audit. Funds are released **simultaneously to approved firms across every tier** of the supply chain rather than cascading contractor→subcontractor. This is a **digital take on the long-standing Project Bank Account (PBA)** concept — the novelty is the software layer + parallel/simultaneous release, not the trust-account idea itself.
- **Fee / model:** **0.25% of payment value paid by the project owner; the supply chain pays nothing.** A thin, volume-dependent take rate.
- **Traction (verifiable, thin):** DiPPA launched **Jan 2025**; first use was a **proof-of-concept project that completed Oct 2025** (saveconstruction.uk). Public-sector **pilots** with the **Environment Agency** and **BAM Nuttall** — first a **£1.5m–£2m footbridge replacement**, expected to **begin summer 2026, run 12–16 months**. So: one completed PoC + pilots that have **not yet started** — announced, not adopted at scale.
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** A £2.9m *cumulative* figure, an £800k angel top-up and a Crowdcube raise together signal a **pre-scale, capital-light company** still proving the model on live projects — consistent with "one PoC done, first real pilot not yet begun." The 0.25%-to-owner / free-to-supply-chain pricing is designed to remove adoption friction for the party that suffers least from late payment (the owner pays; the squeezed subs get protection free) — clever incentive alignment, but it means revenue rides entirely on **project value flowing through DiPPA**, which is still near zero.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Construction payments is crowded in the corpus, but split by **which problem each solves** — and Saible sits in a distinct sub-lane:
- **Trust / ring-fenced payment (Saible's lane, closest concept):** Project Bank Accounts (mandated on UK public projects >£5m), escrow/vesting providers, and retention-deposit schemes. Saible's edge is a **digital PBA with simultaneous multi-tier release** — few pure-software competitors here; the incumbents are legal/banking constructs, not fintechs.
- **Working-capital / early-pay (adjacent, different problem):** [[Earlytrade raises $25M for construction payments AI]] (subcontractor early-pay marketplace, $25M, US/AU), Billd, Speedchain, [[Renno raises EUR1M to speed European construction payments]] (EU faster-payment for contractors). These **advance money faster**; Saible **protects money already committed** — a different mechanism.
- **Billing / pay-app + ERP workflow:** [[Access Coins and Payapps integrate for construction payments]] (UK, Payapps standardises applications-for-payment into Access ERP), [[Agave raises round to automate construction finance workflows]] (US integration/AP layer). These automate the **approval workflow**; Saible could sit downstream of them (approved → released via DiPPA).
- **Position:** **Ahead on the specific "digital PBA / insolvency-protection" idea** in the UK, but **behind on scale/traction** vs any funded peer (Earlytrade's $25M and 211k subs, Agave's 500 profitable customers dwarf Saible's single completed PoC). **Why the landscape is this way (analysis):** the early-pay players monetise a **spread/fee on capital** (fat, transactional revenue); Saible monetises a **0.25% platform fee on protection** (thin, but recurring if it becomes standard). Whoever the government/large contractors standardise on for ring-fencing could own a regulatory-tailwind position — but that is a policy bet, not a product win yet.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **2023:** Founders Jarvey Moss and Tim Whitehill meet at UKREiiF (May 2023) over Project Bank Accounts; company founded. Linked to the **Save Construction Initiative (SCI)** advocacy body.
- **Jan 2025:** Launches "world's first DiPPA." **Oct 2025:** first PoC project completes.
- **2026-07:** cumulative funding reaches £2.9m (£2.1m prior + £800k angels + £50k Crowdcube); Environment Agency / BAM Nuttall pilots lined up for summer 2026.
- **Why it acts this way (analysis):** Saible is deliberately **policy-adjacent** — it rides the UK's live **retention-reform / late-payment reform** agenda (the government has consulted on banning cash retentions and mandating stronger protections under amendments to the Construction Act 1996). Building an SCI-linked, pilot-with-public-bodies profile is a **regulatory land-grab**: if reform mandates ring-fenced parallel payment, a ready digital PBA is well-placed. Hence the small, community-and-angel funding rather than a big VC round — the bottleneck is **regulatory/adoption proof**, not capital.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Genuinely new?** **Partially.** Trust-based ring-fencing of construction funds (PBAs, escrow, retention trusts) is decades old and government-mandated on large UK public jobs since ~2011. Saible's real novelty is **(a) digitising the PBA into an account product (DiPPA) with a modern approval/audit platform, and (b) simultaneous multi-tier release** so tier-3/4 firms don't depend on those above passing cash down. That last point is the meaningful mechanism delta.
- **Traction is announced, not proven at scale:** **one completed PoC (Oct 2025)** and **pilots that have not started**. No GMV, no live client count, no project-volume-through-DiPPA figure disclosed. This is the anti-PR core: the press frames "expanding a platform" that has processed essentially one project.
- **Who captures the margin (analysis):** At 0.25% to the owner, DiPPA needs **large project value flowing through it** to be a real business — e.g. £1bn of project value = £2.5m revenue. That requires becoming a **standard** on many projects, which depends on either regulation or large-contractor mandate. The value-add to squeezed subcontractors (insolvency protection, no dependence on the tier above) is **real and structurally important** — UK construction insolvencies leave retention money as unsecured debt — but Saible captures value only if it becomes the default rail. Risk: a bank, a large contractor, or the government's own PBA framework offers the same ring-fencing without Saible's platform, commoditising the idea.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Plans:** run the Environment Agency / BAM Nuttall footbridge pilot (summer 2026, 12–16 months), close the Crowdcube allocation, expand the platform.
- **Backdrop:** UK late-payment and **retention reform** is live policy — CMS/Lexology/Weightmans note government intent to curb/ban cash retentions via Construction Act amendments. Media cite an "**£11bn payment crisis**" / "300-year-old problem" framing (Construction News, TechFundingNews). This is a **genuine, large structural pain** — but the framing is advocacy-flavoured.
- **Second-order (analysis):** The tailwind cuts both ways. If reform **mandates** ring-fenced/parallel payment, Saible's window opens — but a mandate also invites **banks and incumbent PBA administrators** to offer the same, and public frameworks may specify a non-proprietary model. Saible's advantage is being **early, digital, and SCI/public-pilot-embedded**; its fragility is that the entire thesis rests on **adoption it has not yet demonstrated** and on **policy it does not control**. A small angel/Crowdcube raise is appropriate for that risk profile — this is an early, un-de-risked bet, not a scaled fintech.

9. Does the reform tailwind help or threaten Saible? — Both. A mandate opens the market but invites **banks and incumbent PBA administrators** to offer the same ring-fencing; public frameworks may specify a non-proprietary model, commoditising DiPPA.

14. What breaks the thesis? — Pilots don't convert to standard use; reform stalls or specifies a non-Saible model; owners resist paying even 0.25%; or a bank/incumbent offers equivalent ring-fencing for free.
15. What would move importance up? — Real project volume through DiPPA, a signed mandate from a Tier-1 contractor or public framework, or reform explicitly favouring digital parallel payment. None present yet.

**Importance: 2/5** — A genuinely useful mechanism (digital PBA with simultaneous multi-tier release) aimed at a real, large structural problem (UK construction late payment / insolvency loss), with a supportive reform backdrop. ## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Vertical construction-fintech / project-payment infrastructure. UK construction has led all sectors in insolvencies for four years running — 4,450 firm failures in 2025 (+9% YoY) and ~1,180 in Q1 2026 alone; ~17% of all UK company insolvencies in the year to May 2026 were construction (per Tech.eu / UK Credit Insurance Brokers, via the funding coverage, as of 2026-07). Late payment is the structural cause: 93% of construction firms report late payment with invoices ~53 days overdue (Menzies research, cited via Construction News, 2026). The sector's payment "TAM" claimed in press is an ~£11bn UK payment-crisis figure (techfundingnews, 2026-07) — press-attributed, not independently verifiable, treat as marketing framing [UNSOURCED]. *Why now:* the Commercial Payments Bill (introduced May 2026, second reading in the Lords) would ban retentions, cap terms at 60 days and impose 8%-over-base interest on late payments — a regulatory tailwind that makes trust/ring-fence payment models commercially relevant. Value chain is deeply tiered (main contractor → subs → sub-subs); cash cascades top-down, so the lowest tiers bear the working-capital and insolvency-contagion risk. Structure: fragmented, low barriers to a software layer but high barriers to the *banking/trust* leg (regulation, safeguarding, agency-bank relationship).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: payment value processed (TPV) on-platform, take rate, number/value of live projects, tier-penetration (how deep down the chain funds reach). Basis of competition = trust/regulatory credibility + distribution to main contractors and public bodies, not price. Saible's model: a Digital Parallel Payment Account (DiPPA) holding project funds in trust with agency-banking partner **Griffin**, releasing to all approved tiers simultaneously on verification; owner pays 0.25% of payment value, supply chain pays nothing (per fintech.global / ffnews, 2026-07-15). Direct UK peer on the same "protect the whole chain" thesis: **ProjectPay** (embedded-finance / cashflow-protection for public projects). Adjacent but distinct models in-base: early-pay / receivables financing (Billd, Earlytrade, Mango), lien/waiver + AP automation (Handle, Agave), construction-ops + finance OS (Pillar, Prolo). Saible's position: **niche / early** (analysis) — differentiated by the *simultaneous multi-tier release + regulated trust* mechanic rather than lending; moat is the Griffin trust wrapper + government pilots (switching cost / regulatory intangible), not scale. Recent traction: public-sector pilots with the Environment Agency and BAM Nuttall; first — a £1.5m–£2m footbridge replacement — expected to start summer 2026, running 12–16 months (fintech.global, 2026-07). All pilot-stage; no processed-volume disclosed [UNSOURCED].

**Comps & multiples.** Private seed/early-stage; no revenue, TPV or post-money disclosed → valuation multiples = **no data**. Round composition: £2.1m prior + £0.8m fresh angel = £2.9m cumulative, plus a £50k Crowdcube allocation (15–31 Jul 2026) — i.e. this is a small top-up/cumulative figure, not a fresh £2.9m institutional round (de-PR flag). No multiple comparison possible; qualitative only (distribution not computed — round sizes span different models/stages).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Rail dependence on Griffin (single BaaS/trust provider).** The regulated-trust leg — Saible's core moat and safeguarding promise — is entirely outsourced to one agency-banking partner; any disruption to Griffin's licence, safeguarding or commercials directly breaks the DiPPA proposition and Saible has no fallback (concentration + disintermediation risk).
2. **Pilot-stage, thin capitalisation, no live volume.** ~£2.9m cumulative and reliance on angel + Crowdcube retail money (rather than an institutional lead) signals a long runway-to-scale; revenue = 0.25% of payment value means economics only work at large processed TPV, which is unproven — execution/adoption risk against entrenched main-contractor behaviour.
3. **Regulatory outcome is a two-way bet.** The Commercial Payments Bill is a tailwind if it passes with retention bans/term caps, but if diluted (as prior "project bank account" mandates were, which in practice protected only top tiers) the compulsion to adopt trust models weakens; Saible's TAM narrative leans on legislation not yet enacted.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is a *new-entry* story, not a re-rating: a wave of construction-payment fintechs (Saible, ProjectPay, Earlytrade, Handle) is forming around the UK late-payment/insolvency crisis + pending legislation. Falsifiable thesis: the winners will be those that convert *government/public-body pilots into repeatable framework mandates* — watch whether Saible's Environment Agency / BAM Nuttall footbridge pilot (summer 2026, 12–16 mo) produces a public reference and a second contract by mid-2027. What breaks it: the Commercial Payments Bill stalls or is watered down, removing the regulatory forcing function and leaving trust-based models as optional overhead main contractors decline to fund.
