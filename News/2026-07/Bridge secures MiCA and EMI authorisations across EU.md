# Context-enrichment: Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

**Freshness verdict: FRESH.** This is a genuinely new regulatory event (EU MiCA CASP + EMI, granted ~2 Jul 2026 by Luxembourg's CSSF), distinct from Bridge's US-side [[Bridge wins initial approval for national bank trust charter]] (OCC national trust charter, Feb 2026) and from [[Stripe closes $1.1 billion Bridge acquisition]] (Aug 2025 close of the $1.1B deal). No prior EU-license note exists in the corpus → not a duplicate.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Bridge (Stripe's stablecoin-infrastructure arm) obtained TWO licenses via Luxembourg's regulator **CSSF**, granted ~**2 Jul 2026**: (1) a **MiCA CASP** (Crypto-Asset Service Provider) authorisation and (2) an **EMI** (Electronic Money Institution) license. Together they **passport across all 27 EU member states** — no country-by-country approval needed.
- Enabled services: businesses can issue **custom EUR-backed stablecoins**, create **virtual named IBANs**, and open **euro accounts** without standing up separate EU banking relationships.
- **Timing is not incidental:** the grant landed days after the EU finished its **final MiCA transition phase on 1 Jul 2026**, after which regulated venues may only support MiCA-compliant stablecoins. → Bridge deliberately anchored a "regulated from day one" narrative to a moment when non-compliant rivals (Tether/USDT) were being delisted and the field was thinning (reports of EU crypto firms shrinking from ~3,000+ to a few hundred). **Why structured this way:** Luxembourg CSSF is the fast MiCA/EMI hub of choice; the dual license (CASP *and* EMI) is the specific combo that lets a stablecoin infra firm both custody/service crypto-assets AND move e-money/IBANs — one without the other would leave gaps.
- **PR to discount:** the note lists *capabilities the license unlocks*, not live EU customers or volumes. "Enable to expand" = authorisation, not adoption. No EU-issued stablecoin AUM, no named EU merchants disclosed.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Circle (EURC)** is the clear incumbent: France's ACPR granted Circle an EMI in Jul 2024, passporting USDC+EURC EU-wide 2 years earlier. EURC holds ~40–50% of the euro-stablecoin market (cap ~$460M as of early 2026). Circle is the only top-10 issuer MiCA-authorised for BOTH a USD and EUR token. → Bridge is **~2 years behind Circle on EU regulatory footing.**
- **Paxos Issuance Europe** (Finnish EMI, Nov 2024) issues USDG/Global Dollar Network (Robinhood, Kraken, Galaxy).
- Other MiCA-authorised euro tokens: EURCV (Societe Generale), EURI, EUROe (Membrane), EURQ/EURR. The regulated euro-stablecoin shelf is already crowded on the *token* side.
- **Position:** Bridge is NOT trying to win the "which euro token has the biggest cap" race. Its wedge is **infrastructure/white-label** — letting Stripe's business customers mint *their own* branded EUR stablecoin + IBANs. That is closer to Circle's Open/CPN + Stripe's Open Issuance than to a direct EURC competitor. **Why the landscape is this way:** MiCA rewards regulated passporting → concentration among a few licensed issuers/infra players; the second-order effect is that distribution (who owns the merchant relationship — here, Stripe) matters more than the token brand.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Trajectory: MetaMask mUSD ([[MetaMask launches mUSD stablecoin with M0 and Bridge]]), Phantom CASH, Visa cards to 100+ countries ([[Visa and Bridge expand stablecoin cards to 100+ countries]]), Payoneer ([[Payoneer to launch stablecoin services powered by Bridge]]), MoneyGram MGUSD ([[MoneyGram launches MGUSD stablecoin on Stellar via Bridge]]), Veem ([[Veem to launch stablecoin accounts powered by Bridge]]), Banking Circle rails ([[Banking Circle helps power Bridge's global money movement]]), US OCC trust charter ([[Bridge wins initial approval for national bank trust charter]]).
- Fits logically: Bridge already ran a US-licensing sweep ([[Fintech Wrap Up Bridge US licenses and registrations]]); the EU license is the mirror move on the euro side. **Why:** Stripe (owner since Aug 2025 close) is building an owned, regulated global money-movement stack (US trust charter + EU MiCA/EMI) so it captures the reserve/float and compliance layer rather than renting it — a take-rate-to-regulated-infrastructure play. Bridge transaction volume reportedly grew >4x in 2025 (Stripe annual letter; Stripe total volume $19T, +34%).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty is modest, not zero:** EU-wide passporting for a stablecoin infra provider is real, but Circle got there in 2024. What Bridge adds is the **white-label EUR-stablecoin + virtual IBAN combo tied to Stripe's distribution** — arguably the largest merchant/enterprise funnel of any MiCA-licensed issuer.
- **Traction caveat (red-team):** everything disclosed is *authorisation*, not live EU issuance. Bridge's >4x volume growth is global/USD-dominated, not EU-euro. No EU stablecoin cap, no named EU clients. → treat as "green light" not "adoption."
- **Who captures the margin:** with the EMI, Bridge/Stripe holds the **reserve float and e-money economics** on EUR balances + IBAN rails — the durable, recurring value vs one-off transaction fees. But the euro-stablecoin pie itself is small (~sub-$1B total cap) vs the USD stablecoin market → near-term revenue is thin; the value is *optionality* on becoming the default EU issuance layer.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Expect Bridge to convert the license into named EU enterprise issuances (loyalty, in-app pay, on/off-ramp) and to plug into the Visa card expansion (100+ countries by end-2026).
- **Regulatory backdrop:** MiCA final phase (1 Jul 2026) is a tailwind — it culls unlicensed rivals and makes a passportable license a moat. Counterintuitive second-order effect: as MiCA concentrates the field into a handful of licensed players, **distribution owners (Stripe) win over token brands** — the euro-stablecoin question shifts from "which coin" to "whose rails and whose float."
- **Risks:** small euro-stablecoin TAM; Circle's 2-year head start and EURC liquidity; MiCA reserve/redemption constraints on e-money tokens; execution gap between license and live volume.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
### Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Is this actually new, or a re-report?** New: EU MiCA CASP + EMI via Luxembourg CSSF (~2 Jul 2026). Distinct from the US OCC trust charter ([[Bridge wins initial approval for national bank trust charter]], Feb 2026) and the acquisition close (Aug 2025). → **fresh**, not a duplicate.
2. **Live or announced?** Only authorisation. No live EU stablecoin issuance, no named EU clients, no EU EUR-token AUM disclosed. → capability granted, adoption unproven. **Open.**
3. **How far behind Circle?** Circle got its French EMI passporting USDC+EURC in Jul 2024 — ~2 years ahead; EURC holds ~40–50% of the euro-stablecoin market. Bridge is a late entrant on EU regulatory footing.
4. **How big is the euro-stablecoin TAM?** Small — total euro-stablecoin cap is sub-$1B (EURC ~$460M). Near-term EU revenue is thin regardless of license. **Why it matters:** value is optionality, not immediate P&L.
5. **Which license does the real work?** The EMI (e-money, IBANs, EUR float) arguably matters more than the CASP for Stripe's payments business; the CASP covers the crypto-asset servicing. Both needed to avoid gaps.
6. **Who captures the margin?** With EMI reserve float + IBAN rails, Bridge/Stripe holds durable e-money economics — but only if EUR balances scale. **Open** on scale.
7. **Why Luxembourg?** CSSF is the fast MiCA/EMI hub; not a substantive signal about business location, more regulatory-shopping efficiency.
8. **Does Stripe's distribution actually convert?** Stripe = largest merchant funnel among MiCA issuers; but no EU-specific merchant issuance named yet. Distribution advantage is theoretical until proven.
9. **What's the volume claim really?** ">4x Bridge volume growth in 2025" is global/USD-dominated (Stripe letter), NOT EU-euro. Do not read it as EU traction.
10. **Is the "27 member states" framing meaningful?** Yes structurally (passporting removes per-country approvals) but it's a standard MiCA passport, same mechanism Circle/Paxos already use — not unique to Bridge.
11. **What's silent?** Reserve composition, redemption terms, fee/float economics, exclusivity, and whether any EU enterprise has committed. All undisclosed.
12. **Regulatory risk?** MiCA reserve/redemption/e-money constraints could limit yield capture on float; final-phase rules (post-1 Jul 2026) still bedding in.
13. **Second-order effect of MiCA cull?** Field shrank from ~3,167 to ~244 licensed firms → concentration favours licensed distributors (Stripe) over token brands — shifts the central question from "which coin" to "whose rails + float."
14. **Cannibalisation vs Circle partnership?** Does Bridge white-label EUR compete with or complement EURC in the Stripe stack? **Open.**
15. **Downside trigger?** If EU EUR issuance stays negligible 12 months out while Circle extends its lead, this becomes a defensive/table-stakes license rather than a growth driver.

**Importance: 4/5** — A structurally significant, genuinely new regulatory milestone: EU-wide passporting completes Stripe/Bridge's dual-continent (US OCC + EU MiCA/EMI) owned, regulated money-movement stack, timed to the MiCA cull that thins rivals. Docked from 5 because Circle is ~2 years ahead, the euro-stablecoin TAM is small, and it's authorisation not adoption (no live EU volume/clients disclosed).

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Global stablecoin market cap crossed ~$310–312bn in early 2026, with USDT ~60% (~$183–187bn) and USDC ~$74–76bn; per Mordor Intelligence the market is put at ~$0.33tn in 2026 growing ~28.8% CAGR (via mordorintelligence.com — secondary/paywalled, treat as directional, not verified TAM). Euro-denominated stablecoins are a thin sliver — <€350m, <1% of global supply as of 2025 (via utila.io) — so the "EU euro-stablecoin" market Bridge is targeting is nascent, not established. Structure: the value chain splits into (a) issuers of the coin (Circle/EURC, Tether — who capture reserve yield), (b) regulated infrastructure/orchestration (Bridge, BVNK, Conduit) and (c) rails/distribution (Visa, Mastercard). Entry barrier is now primarily regulatory: MiCA's final transition ended 1 July 2026, forcing regulated platforms to support only compliant coins, and one estimate has EU-licensed crypto firms shrinking from ~3,000 to 300–400 (via cryptonomist.ch). **Why now:** MiCA's single-passport regime — a licence in one member state (Bridge chose Luxembourg) covers all 27 — plus USDT's delisting for EEA retail created a euro-stablecoin vacuum that Circle's EURC (~41% of euro-stablecoin cap, up from 17% YoY) has been filling; Bridge's dual CASP+EMI stack lets it plug enterprises into that opening (virtual IBANs, custom EUR-backed coins, euro accounts without separate bank relationships).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: stablecoin float/AUC (reserve income = float × yield), on-chain settlement/orchestration volume, and take rate on FX/payment flow. Key players and basis of competition: Circle (issuer, EMI in France pre-MiCA, first fully MiCA-compliant issuer, competes on the coin + reserve yield); Bridge/Stripe (orchestration + issuance-as-a-service, competes on distribution via Stripe's ~$1.9tn platform volume and Visa card reach); pure-play infra peers BVNK and Conduit (competes on B2B corridors). Recent moves: Circle expanded USDC to Europe/India ([[Circle expands USDC payments network to Europe and India]], Jan 2026); Coinbase pursued then abandoned a ~$2bn BVNK deal ([[Coinbase targets $2 billion BVNK deal for stablecoin push]] → [[Coinbase pulls out of $2B BVNK acquisition]], Nov 2025); Crossmint took the same Luxembourg-style MiCA route ([[Crossmint secures MiCA authorization to expand across EU]], Jan 2026); Payoneer and Privy both built on Bridge rails ([[Payoneer to launch stablecoin services powered by Bridge]], [[Privy adds custodial wallets backed by Bridge]], Feb 2026); Visa extended its Bridge card partnership to 100+ countries (via fortune.com, Mar 2026). Bridge's position: ahead on distribution, catching up on issuance vs Circle (which owns the dominant euro coin). Moat `(analysis)`: distribution + switching costs from Stripe integration and the now-scarce MiCA+EMI licence pair; but it does NOT own reserve economics the way an issuer does.

**Comps & multiples.** No private valuation is disclosed for Bridge post-acquisition, so a direct multiple is not computable.
- **Bridge/Stripe:** acquired for $1.1bn (closed Feb 2025, via stripe.com); Bridge volume >4x in 2026 (via decrypt.co); parent Stripe last marked at ~$159bn tender-offer valuation, 2026 (via decrypt.co). Standalone Bridge revenue — **no data**; no clean multiple.
- **Circle (CRCL, public):** best public read-across. FY2025 total revenue + reserve income $2.747bn (up from $1.676bn FY2024, via SEC 8-K). Market cap ~$25.7bn (Apr 2026, via theblock.co) → P/(rev+reserve) ≈ $25.7bn / $2.747bn ≈ **9.4x** `(analysis)`. Sell-side had it at ~39x EV/2026E EBITDA and ~23x EV/2027E EBITDA (via coindesk.com) — rich, but the coin business is reserve-yield levered, so revenue is rate-sensitive `[UNSOURCED clean EV]`.
- **BVNK (private):** last talked-of price ~$2bn in the abandoned Coinbase deal (Nov 2025, via corpus) — a round/deal marker, not a market cap; underlying revenue not disclosed → **no data** on multiple.
With only one truly comparable public figure (Circle), distribution/median not computed — qualitative comparison only. Circle's ~9.4x price/(rev+reserve) is in-line for a high-growth, rate-exposed issuer; Bridge, as unpriced infra inside Stripe, can't be flagged rich or cheap.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Reserve-economics disintermediation.** Bridge orchestrates and can issue "custom" EUR coins, but the reserve yield — the primary stablecoin profit pool — accrues to whoever holds the reserves; if enterprises default to Circle's EURC, Bridge captures fees on flow, not the fat float income. Margin risk sits one layer up the stack.
2. **Rate sensitivity + regulatory tightening.** Stablecoin infra revenue is levered to interest rates (reserve yield ~4.5%); ECB cuts compress the pool. MiCA also caps/constrains non-euro coin usage and imposes reserve/e-money rules under the EMI licence — compliance cost is now a permanent tax and a moving target.
3. **Concentration / dependence on Stripe's rails.** Bridge's edge is Stripe distribution and Visa reach; that same dependence means its addressable growth is gated by Stripe's roadmap and by Visa/Mastercard, who could in-source orchestration. "Announced" 27-state coverage ≠ live adopted volume — de-PR: the note gives licence grant, not euro-coin float or client count.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is a land-grab in a still-tiny euro-stablecoin market (<1% of global supply), not a re-rating — the licence removes a barrier but the revenue is unproven. Falsifiable thesis: if Bridge/Stripe convert distribution into euro-coin float, watch for a disclosed EURC-vs-Bridge-issued euro supply figure over the next 2–3 quarters; the thesis breaks if enterprises keep defaulting to Circle's EURC and Bridge is left earning thin orchestration fees while Circle keeps the reserve yield. Trigger to watch: any Bridge/Stripe disclosure of euro-stablecoin volume or a competing incumbent (bank consortium, e.g. EUR.bank) taking the enterprise slot.
