## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
**[0] TL;DR.** On **13 Jul 2026** Kraken began issuing the **Kraken Card**, a Mastercard-network crypto debit card, to eligible customers in the **UK and the EEA**. It lets users spend from **600+ crypto and fiat balances** held on Kraken (converted in near real-time at point of sale), pays **up to 2% cashback** (tiered 0.5–2% by 30-day average balance, weekly, in BTC/EUR/GBP), and carries **no Kraken transaction or ATM fees**. Physical + virtual cards are issued by **Monavate** (UK: Monavate Ltd, FCA-authorised EMI, FRN 901097; EEA: UAB Monavate, authorised by the Bank of Lithuania). **FRESHNESS: STALE / DUPLICATE** — this is an evolution/relaunch of the card Kraken already launched in **Nov 2025** in the same UK+EEA markets, covered in [[Kraken launches Mastercard for UK and European users]]; the "Kraken launches a Mastercard crypto card in UK/Europe" headline is not new.

**[1] What's actually new vs. already-known.**
- **KNOWN / near-duplicate:** Kraken already launched a Mastercard card for UK + European users in **Nov 2025** ([[Kraken launches Mastercard for UK and European users]], same "Connecting the Dots in Fintech" channel). The Mastercard partnership itself was announced **Apr 2025**. So the announcement framing (Kraken + Mastercard, crypto debit card, UK/Europe) is a repeat.
- **INCREMENTALLY NEW (the only genuinely fresh deltas):** the Nov-2025 card was P2P/"Krak"-tied with a flat **1% cashback**; the Jul-2026 **Kraken Card** is a **full general-purpose spending card** with **tiered 0.5–2% cashback**, **600+ supported assets** (vs ~400 earlier), **Monavate** as the named EMI issuer (FCA + Bank of Lithuania), zero FX/ATM/transaction fees, and a stated pipeline to further markets **including the US** (no timeline).
- **Verdict:** upgrade/relaunch of an existing product, not a first-of-kind event → treated as **stale** per the near-identical-prior-note rule.

**[2] Why now / strategic fit.** The card slots into Kraken's "become-a-bank"/neobank push ahead of its planned US IPO: high-yield Vaults, salary/direct-deposit features, and the [[Kraken to pursue European banking licence via Lithuania|Lithuania banking-licence pursuit]]. Notably the EEA card issuer is a **Bank-of-Lithuania-authorised** entity (UAB Monavate) — same jurisdiction Kraken is targeting for its own charter, consistent with a Lithuania-centred EU strategy. Diversifying toward spend/payments and recurring engagement de-cyclicalises the trading-fee-heavy P&L (Q1 2026 adj. EBITDA collapsed to ~$18m as spot volumes fell ~38%).

**[3] Competitive frame.** Crypto debit cards are a crowded, commoditised category in EU/UK: **Coinbase Card** (~0.5% rewards), **Crypto.com Visa** (1–5%, staking-gated), **Wirex** (up to 8% Cryptoback, staking/spend-gated), **Bitpanda Card**, **Gnosis Pay** (self-custody/on-chain). Kraken's differentiators are the **breadth of assets (600+, more than most peers)** and a **2% headline cashback that doesn't require token-staking**, plus no own FX/ATM fees. But the top cashback tier is balance-gated, and the core "spend crypto via Mastercard" product is table-stakes, not novel. Adjacent internal precedent: [[Satispay partners with Mastercard for debit cards]] (Mastercard fintech-issuing win, same period).

**[4] Watch-items / open questions.** (a) Actual adoption/activation — announced/rolling, **zero usage data**. (b) US launch timing (flagged, no date). (c) How the 0.5–2% tiering and weekly BTC/fiat cashback economics hold up vs staking-based rivals. (d) Whether the Monavate/Bank-of-Lithuania issuing arrangement is transitional pending Kraken's own EU banking/EMI licence. (e) Relationship/overlap with the "Krak" app brand (some 2026 coverage uses "Krak Card").

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Crypto/stablecoin debit cards are among the fastest-growing digital-payments sub-segments. Per Artemis research (via CoinDesk, 2026-01-16), crypto card spend hit ~$18bn annualized by late 2025 — nearly matching P2P stablecoin transfers (~$19bn) — up from ~$100m/month in early 2023, a ~106% CAGR. The structural point: cards remain the dominant "bridge" for spending crypto because they ride existing Visa/Mastercard rails and need no new merchant integration — so the value accrues to the network + issuer/program-manager stack, not to a new acceptance network. Barriers are regulatory (MiCA / e-money licensing, FCA authorization) and distribution (an existing funded user base), not technology. **Why now:** MiCA gives EU stablecoin/issuance a rulebook, and exchanges are racing to convert trading users into everyday-spend/neobank users (the note itself frames it as "neobank race"). Concentration flag on the rails: per Artemis, Visa captures >90% of on-chain card volume — Mastercard is the challenger here, which likely explains why it is aggressively signing exchange programs (Kraken, Binance, OKX).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: card GDV / spend volume, funded/active accounts, take rate (interchange + FX spread), attach rate of card to the exchange's trading base. Basis of competition = rewards economics + fees + which self-custody/exchange balance you can spend. Recent moves (all Mastercard-network crypto cards, dated): OKX Card in Europe (2026-01-28, stablecoin from self-custody, 0.4% FX spread, up to 20% limited-time rewards); Binance Mastercard card across CIS incl. Armenia (2026-02-16); plus Gemini, Lemon (Argentina, BTC-backed), and Spendl (South Africa) card launches through 2026. Kraken's own prior UK/EU Mastercard debit card dates to 2025-11-26 — so this July-2026 launch reads as a relaunch/broadening (600+ currencies, tiered cashback, issued via Monavate, an FCA-authorized issuer), not a first entry. **Protagonist position:** catching up / at-parity in a crowded field — Kraken is well-capitalized ($20bn valuation, 6.1m funded accounts) but its card is one of many near-identical Mastercard crypto cards; the moat is distribution (its existing trading base and $40bn customer assets) and switching costs of an integrated app, not the card product itself `(analysis)`. Cashback is tiered 0.5%–2.0% by 30-day balance (Light €200 → 0.5%; Max €50k → 2%), so the headline "up to 2%" is a top-tier rate, not the typical rate — a de-PR flag.

**Comps & multiples.** *Network (Mastercard, MA):* Q1 2026 net revenue $8.4bn (+16% reported), GDV $2.7tn, net income $3.9bn (earnings release, 2026-04-30). Market cap ~$469.7bn, EV ~$520bn (as of 2026-07-07). EV/Revenue on ~$33.6bn annualized run-rate = $520bn / $33.6bn ≈ **15.5x**; P/E ~30.7x trailing; EV/EBITDA 22.7x (gurufocus, 2026-04). Rich in absolute terms but in-line for a ~55%+ operating-margin network compounder — the card deal is immaterial to MA's own multiple; it is program volume, not a re-rating. *Protagonist (Kraken/Payward, private):* Q1 2026 adjusted revenue $507m (+3% YoY), adjusted EBITDA only $18m (heavy M&A/investment), customer assets $40bn, funded accounts 6.1m (Payward Q1 2026 highlights, 2026-05-18). Last round: ~$20bn valuation on an $800m raise (2025-11-18). Implied P/S on ~$2.0bn annualized revenue = $20bn / $2.0bn ≈ **10x** — a round valuation (not market cap), rich vs the ~3% top-line growth, justified only if IPO/diversification (tokenization, derivatives, payments via Reap) re-accelerates growth `(analysis)`. Internal comps: [[Kraken launches Mastercard for UK and European users]], [[OKX Card launches in Europe]], [[Binance launches Mastercard crypto card across CIS countries]], [[Rain raises $250M Series C at $1.95B valuation]], [[This Week in Fintech Artemis data shows crypto card spending rising]]. Distribution of card-program valuations not computed — mix of private exchanges and one public network; qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Commoditization / no differentiation** — Kraken's card is functionally interchangeable with OKX, Binance, Gemini cards on the same Mastercard rails; competition collapses to cashback economics, which compress margin (0.5–2% cashback funded partly from thin interchange). Second-order: card is a customer-retention/loss-leader, not a P&L driver.
2. **Disintermediation of economics to the rails** — Mastercard (and Visa, >90% of on-chain volume) captures scheme fees on every transaction; the exchange bears rewards + fraud/AML liability while the network takes a rents-like cut. Kraken depends on someone else's rails and an external issuer (Monavate).
3. **Regulatory / spend-conversion risk** — MiCA and FCA e-money rules govern the card; and the underlying thesis (crypto balances become everyday spend) is still nascent — crypto trading revenue fell industry-wide (BTC −22% in Q1), so card volume tied to volatile balances is cyclical.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Not a re-rating for anyone — it is table-stakes feature-matching in the exchange-neobank convergence. Watch whether Kraken discloses card GDV / active-card numbers in future Payward updates (it is silent on card economics today); a falsifiable bull thesis is that the card lifts funded-account retention and non-trading revenue mix — the trigger to confirm would be card-linked spend showing up as a named revenue line ahead of the (slipping, possibly 2027) IPO. If crypto-card spend stalls near the current ~$18bn while cashback wars continue, the thesis breaks and cards stay a cost center.
