# Context-enrichment: Rosatom decides to buy out Shishkarev's stake in Delo Group
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 6 July 2026, at the "Innoprom-2026" forum in Yekaterinburg, Rosatom head Alexei Likhachev told reporters that Rosatom (which owns 49% of Delo Group's management company OOO "UK Delo") has taken a **corporate decision to buy out** Sergei Shishkarev's 51% stake, under the "Russian roulette" (shotgun) clause. Legal execution "may take all of July." Mechanics as stated by Likhachev:
- The Rosatom decision mechanism switched on from **1 July 2026**.
- In the next ~2 weeks, notaries must formally record Shishkarev's **refusal to buy** Rosatom's 49% (i.e. that he paid nothing and does not intend to).
- Another ~2 weeks for legal formalities of payment — where/what account/what wording.
- Likhachev flagged the shotgun procedure as "original, never before applied in Russia," and said it can be **accelerated if Shishkarev agrees to a direct sale** without the full roulette procedure.

**This is the resolution of the OTHER side of the shotgun, not a reprint.** The prior published note [[Shishkarev drops plan to buy 49% of Delo Group from Rosatom]] (26 Jun 2026) covered Shishkarev DECLINING to buy Rosatom's 49% and ceding the ownership decision to Rosatom. This note is the follow-up: Rosatom exercising its buy side to take Shishkarev's controlling 51% — i.e. state consolidation to (effectively) 100%. Different event, different direction, new development. (freshness = fresh)

**+ Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The two-step notarial choreography (record the refusal, then formalize payment) exists because the "Russian roulette" is a first-of-its-kind mechanism in Russian law with no precedent to lean on — hence the deliberate, procedural month. The offer to "accelerate if Shishkarev is not against a direct buyout" reveals the roulette is a fallback enforcement path: Rosatom would prefer a negotiated direct purchase (cleaner, faster) but is willing to grind through the shotgun if Shishkarev resists. The subtext is that Rosatom now holds the initiative unambiguously and is signalling the deal WILL close in 2026, on Rosatom's terms. (analysis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
Delo is the dominant Russian logistics holding, so "peers" = other strategic assets being pulled into state-corporation orbits, not market-share rivals:
- **State-consolidation peers:** Rostec (was the intended strategic-management JV partner for Delo), Transmashholding (Rosatom explicitly ruled out selling the Delo stake to TMH earlier), and Rosatom's own Arctic/Northern Sea Route logistics push. A TMH–Rosatom–Delo configuration had been floated as possibly emerging "within 6 months" (Interfax) — now overtaken by Rosatom taking full control directly.
- **The asset base at stake:** Global Ports (Delo owns >90%; Russia's #1 sea-container-terminal operator, ~23% of national container turnover), TransContainer (Delo consolidated 99.64%; that block alone valued at >120bn rub per portnews/Interfax), DeloPorts stevedoring, plus grain terminals and rail intermodal.

**+ Why the lay of the land is this way / second-order:** Post-2022, Western operators (APM Terminals) exited and strategic gateways were re-domesticated into state holdings. The Delo endgame is the tell: once a state corporation sits at 49% with a shotgun clause, even the sanctioned founder cannot re-privatize — the clause, not operating performance, decides control. Rosatom moving to 100% removes the last private veto over a chokepoint that touches rail, ports and grain export simultaneously. (analysis)

## [2] Company history / fit
- 1993: Delo founded by Shishkarev.
- Dec 2019: Rosatom's Atomenergoprom buys 30% of UK Delo for 29.9bn rub → implied ~100bn rub 100% valuation.
- 2022: Rosatom exercises option → 49%.
- 2018–2024: Delo rolls up Global Ports (30.75% → 61.5% → >90%); consolidates TransContainer to 99.64% (>120bn rub).
- Feb 2026: Rosatom triggers "Russian roulette" over strategy disagreement.
- Apr–May 2026: FAS clears Shishkarev's purchase (10-yr pricing-disclosure provisions); VTB approached, "no decision" (Kostin); Rostec JV in play.
- **26 Jun 2026:** Shishkarev declines to buy Rosatom's 49% (financing/macro); initiative passes to Rosatom → [[Shishkarev drops plan to buy 49% of Delo Group from Rosatom]].
- **1 Jul 2026:** Rosatom's decision mechanism activates.
- **6 Jul 2026 (this note):** Rosatom announces corporate decision to buy out Shishkarev's 51%; execution "all of July."

**+ Why the company acts this way:** Shishkarev took a state co-owner in 2019 for capital/political cover for the Global Ports + TransContainer roll-up; that bargain embedded a state veto and a shotgun. When visions diverged and Shishkarev couldn't fund the ~74bn-rub buyback of the 49% at 2026 rates, the roulette flipped to Rosatom — which, unlike the private founder, has state-corporation balance-sheet depth to fund taking the 51%. The structural asymmetry (state cost-of-capital + no sanctions constraint vs a sanctioned, leverage-dependent founder) is exactly why the shotgun resolves toward Rosatom. (analysis)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
No new operational capability — this is a control/ownership resolution. What is genuinely NEW vs the 26 Jun note: it is now **Rosatom acquiring Shishkarev's controlling 51%**, i.e. state consolidation to full ownership, and the first-ever practical application of a "Russian roulette" shotgun clause in Russia. The real, cash-generating traction (Global Ports #1 terminals, TransContainer 99.64%/>120bn rub, grain terminals) was never in question; what changes is who owns 100% of the upside.

**+ Who captures the value:** By exercising the buy side, Rosatom captures the control premium and the full asset upside; Shishkarev, as the constrained/sanctioned seller who declined to buy, is now the seller and exposed to whatever price the roulette / direct negotiation sets. The margin sits with the party that held the optionality (the shotgun), not the operator. Open question: the strike price and whether Shishkarev extracts a premium by trading a fast "direct sale" for a better number, vs grinding the roulette. (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Watch for: (1) the disclosed **buyout price** for the 51% and whether it beats the ~74bn-rub-for-49% implied mark (~151bn rub for 100%); (2) whether Shishkarev consents to a direct sale (faster) or forces the full roulette; (3) whether Rostec is slotted in as operator/co-owner post-consolidation; (4) closing within July as Likhachev guided; (5) FAS treatment — its earlier 10-yr pricing-disclosure provisions were tied to Shishkarev's purchase; whether equivalents bind Rosatom is open. Risks: legal novelty (untested shotgun mechanism could slip the timeline); Shishkarev's sanctioned status complicates payment/formalities; execution risk on a first-of-its-kind procedure.

**+ Why the market goes this way (second-order):** This is a clean data point in the re-statisation of Russian strategic logistics — a founder losing his own company to a state co-owner not via expropriation but via a contractual shotgun he could not out-finance. Counterintuitively, the "polite" mechanism (a mutual buy-sell clause) delivers the same concentration outcome as a forced sale, with better optics: capital scarcity + state balance-sheet depth = one-way flow of chokepoint control to state holdings. (analysis)

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: Russian ports / transport-and-logistics M&A and state-asset consolidation — not core fintech; the financial angle is the shotgun buyout and its ~77bn rub cash outlay. This event is the resolution (buy side) of the shotgun set up in the June reversal (see [[Shishkarev drops plan to buy 49% of Delo Group from Rosatom]]): Rosatom's "Russian roulette" proposal priced 100% of Delo at ~150–151bn rub — Shishkarev could sell his 51% for 77bn rub OR buy Rosatom's 49% for 74bn rub (Interfax/Investing.com, Mar–Jul 2026). He declined to buy (26 Jun); on 6 Jul, at Innoprom-2026, Rosatom head Alexei Likhachev said the *corporate decision to buy Shishkarev's 51%* is taken, with legal formalisation expected to run "all of July". Structure: highly **consolidated** and re-domesticated post-2022 (APM Terminals and other Western operators exited; strategic gateways pulled into state-corporation orbits — Rosatom/Arctic-NSR). Barriers: capital, FAS, sanctions, and state veto/shotgun rights embedded in ownership. **Why now = the cost of money did the work in June, and now the shotgun mechanics take over.** The Bank of Russia key rate (14.25% at the June cut, from a 21% 2025 peak, per cbr.ru) made a ~74bn rub leveraged buyback unaffordable for Shishkarev; by declining, he handed Rosatom the buy side. Second-order: a sanctioned private founder cannot re-privatise a strategic logistics asset from a state co-owner in a high-rate 2026 — capital scarcity *is* the consolidation mechanism, and the outcome is the state corporation moving to 100%. (analysis)

**Competitive landscape.** Control/ownership story, not a market-share contest — Delo is already the dominant domestic operator. KPIs that matter here: the shotgun strike price (77bn rub for 51% / 74bn rub for 49%), implied 100% valuation (~150bn rub), and Delo's underlying container throughput (Global Ports, 91.26%-owned per Interfax, is the crown jewel; ~23% of national container turnover per 2022 note). Players are co-owners/state holdings: Rosatom (49% → moving to 100%, holds and now exercises the shotgun), Shishkarev (51% founder, the seller), Transmashholding/TMH (earlier floated as a partner but Rosatom explicitly said it is NOT selling the stake to TMH — Interfax). Recent moves with dates: Feb 2026 Rosatom triggers "Russian roulette"; Apr 2026 FAS clears Shishkarev's *purchase* (10-yr pricing-disclosure provisions); ~Apr 2026 VTB approached, "no decision" (Kostin); 26 Jun 2026 Shishkarev declines to buy; **6 Jul 2026 Rosatom's corporate decision to buy his 51% is taken**, formalisation through July, notaries to record Shishkarev's refusal to buy within ~2 weeks then ~2 weeks on payment mechanics. Protagonist's position: founder is the **exiting/constrained party** — sanctioned, out-financed, now the seller. Moat is the physical asset base (Global Ports #1 container terminals, rail/intermodal), but the *control premium* accrued to whoever held the shotgun optionality (Rosatom), not the operator. Note Likhachev's own hedge: the process is "оригинальная, ещё ни разу не применялась в РФ" — first-ever use of a shotgun clause in Russia, so procedural/legal execution risk is real. (analysis)

**Comps & multiples.** No public Delo Group financials (revenue/EBITDA disclosed), so EV/Revenue and EV/EBITDA = **no data**; only implied equity valuation is observable, from disclosed stake prices:
- 2019: Rosatom paid 29.9bn rub for 30% → implied 100% ≈ `29.9 / 0.30 = ~99.7bn rub`.
- 2026 shotgun: 51% at 77bn rub → implied 100% ≈ `77 / 0.51 = ~151bn rub`; 49% at 74bn rub → implied 100% ≈ `74 / 0.49 = ~151bn rub`. The two legs are internally consistent at ~150–151bn rub (a slight control premium sits on the 51% leg: 77 vs 74×(51/49)=~77 — effectively symmetric, i.e. a *pro-rata* shotgun price, no explicit minority discount).
- Implied appreciation `~151 / ~100 = ~1.5x` over ~7 years (`~6%`/yr nominal) — **largely rouble nominal inflation, not real value creation** (analysis); deflating by 2019–26 RUB CPI would compress most of it. Cross-deal EV multiples not computed: distribution not computed, qualitative comparison only.
- Internal comps (corpus, analogous Russia/state-exit/buyout dynamics, NOT direct sector peers): [[Shishkarev drops plan to buy 49% of Delo Group from Rosatom]] (direct prior chapter — the reversal that set up this buy side), [[Citigroup to sell remaining Russian operations at $1.2bn loss]] (Western exit → state-linked buyer, Putin sign-off). No in-base Russian ports/logistics buyout comp surfaced — the base is fintech-weighted, so true sector comps are absent.

**Risk flags.**
1. **First-ever-shotgun execution / legal risk.** Likhachev himself calls the "Russian roulette" procedure original and never before applied in Russia; it needs notarised recording of Shishkarev's refusal plus resolution of payment mechanics ("куда платить, с какой формулировкой"). A "corporate decision to buy" is announced, not a closed deal — timing (all of July) and any contestation could slip or unwind it. Announced ≠ live.
2. **Sanctions / forced-seller and financing risk.** Shishkarev is sanctioned (portseurope); the buyer is a state corporation and the ~77bn rub must be funded and paid at 2026 rates. Whether Rosatom self-funds or leverages (and to whom Shishkarev, sanctioned, can even be paid) is unresolved — a payment/route risk, not just a price risk.
3. **Re-statisation / control-capture concentration.** The shotgun clause, not the operating business, captured the value; a strategic chokepoint (Global Ports, ~23% of national container turnover) moves to 100% state ownership. Concentration of strategic-infrastructure control in a state corporation, with 10-yr FAS pricing-disclosure provisions binding whoever ends up in control.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** The June thesis — Rosatom consolidates to majority/100% rather than Shishkarev regaining the 49% — is now playing out: this is the buy side of the shotgun, taking Rosatom toward full ownership of Russia's #1 logistics holding. Falsifiable thesis: the deal closes in 2026 with Rosatom at (or near) 100% and Shishkarev fully exited from ownership (operational role open).
