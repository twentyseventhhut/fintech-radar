SpaceX falls to $135 IPO price ahead of Starship launch
SpaceX’s shares fell to just above $135 on Wednesday, the price that CEO Elon Musk and his company chose ahead of its blockbuster June 12 IPO that raked in nearly $86 billion.
The company’s stock spent much of the day below that IPO price, at one point dipping beneath $133 per share, before it traded back up to finish at $135.27.
The dip on Wednesday followed a steady decline in the month since the company went public. SpaceX initially saw its stock price rise to more than $200 in the days after it went public, briefly giving it a valuation that rivaled tech giants like Amazon and Microsoft. Its shares have lost value basically every week since reaching that high point.
Some of the volatility is attributable to the fact that just 4% of the company’s total shares are trading on the Nasdaq. That small “float,” as it’s known, combined with an immense amount of constant attention on the company, has created wild swings during the first month of trading.
The markets also appear to be sobering up on CEO Elon Musk’s grand vision for the company, part of a broader deflation in tech stocks over the last month. Not only has SpaceX’s stock traded down, but also bonds the company sold in the wake of the IPO are suffering.
A prolonged downturn for SpaceX could have wider effects because the company’s stock price is a sign of how investors view the (literal) otherworldly promises Musk has made about what his company can accomplish. SpaceX’s IPO has also set the table for other Big Tech companies like Anthropic and OpenAI to go public. Both of those companies have filed confidentially for an IPO. While neither has set a date to go public, SpaceX’s stock is being closely watched to gauge how successful those IPOs could be.
SpaceX is about to face another early test of the durability of its stock price. On Thursday the company will test launch its Starship rocket for the first time since the IPO. Starship is still very much in development, which means it is prone to failures — the result of SpaceX’s “fly, fail, fix” approach.
This will be the first Starship flight since it experienced a booster failure in May. And once again, the company does not plan to try to recover the Starship booster or upper stage on this flight, instead opting to have them simulate a landing in the Gulf of Mexico. That means both parts of the overall Starship rocket system will end in an explosion no matter what, even if they don’t run into any problems during the flight plan.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **Wed 2026-07-15**, newly-public **SpaceX (Nasdaq: SPCX)** traded most of the day *below* its **$135 June-12 IPO price**, dipping under **$133** intraday before closing at **$135.27** — i.e. it "fell to" but ultimately held the IPO line. This is a **single-day price-tick + narrative story**, not a corporate action: no raise, no deal, no guidance. The peg to "$135 IPO price" is the flattering/dramatic frame — the real move is a **~1-month, ~15-40% slide from the post-listing peak (~$225 on ~June 15-16)**, not a one-day crash.

**Why framed this way:** anchoring to the exact IPO price ($135) manufactures a clean "sobering up" headline the day *before* the first Starship launch as a public company (Thu Jul 16). The de-PR'd reality: a ~40% draw-down from an obvious post-IPO gamma/float squeeze back toward the offer price is a **normalization**, not necessarily a verdict on fundamentals — but the accompanying **bond weakness is the more serious tell** (see [3]).

Corroborated across TechCrunch, Yahoo Finance, CNBC — **not satire**, despite the eye-watering scale. (Note: the internal aggregated text says "just above $135"; some outlets quote a $138.29 close on Jul 14 — day-to-day figures differ, treat $135.27 close as the Jul-15 print per the primary source.)

## [1] Competitors / peers — the AI/mega-IPO cohort
SpaceX is the **first mover and bellwether** of the 2026 mega-IPO wave. **Anthropic** and **OpenAI** both **confidentially filed** draft S-1s in early June 2026 (per external reporting; internal [[TechCrunch Anthropic, OpenAI, SpaceX IPOs top 25 years of exits]] frames the trio as "bigger than 25 years of tech exits"). [[Anthropic holds IPO investor meetings, could list by October]] shows Anthropic advancing. **Why it matters (2nd order):** SpaceX's *aftermarket* — not its opening pop — is the live read-through. A slide back to the offer price with junk-trading bonds tells Anthropic/OpenAI bankers that **euphoric mega-cap pricing does not hold**, pressuring them to price more conservatively or delay. Peer space/hardware capital: [[Bezos space firm Blue Origin seeks $10B new financing]] and [[China lands reusable orbital rocket booster for first time]] show the sector is capital-hungry and increasingly contested.

## [2] Company history / fit
SpaceX **IPO'd June 12, 2026** at $135 → **~$86B raised (a record)**, **~$1.75-1.77T valuation**, top-10 global market cap, above Tesla (~$1.6T). Opened ~$161 (+19%), peaked ~$225, then **declined nearly every week**. Only **~4% float** on Nasdaq — a structurally thin, volatile setup that amplifies both the pop and the fade. A **~44% insider unlock is flagged for September**, a known future overhang. **Why:** Musk/SpaceX chose a tiny float to maximize valuation optics and minimize dilution — the same choice that now drives the "wild swings" the article cites. Prior corpus skepticism is on record: [[Linas Newsletter Why a $1.75 trillion SpaceX IPO may be uninvestable]] (2026-06-21) argued the valuation was uninvestable *before* this slide — this note is partial validation of that thesis.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Little genuinely *new* here as an event — it's price color. The **real, under-covered signal is the debt side:** SpaceX's ~$25B 2056 bond (issued ~Jun 24) has traded *below* issue price with the yield pushing ~7.5% and spreads widening ~28-32bp in week one — "investment-grade rating, junk-like trading" (Forbes, 24/7 Wall St, Investing.com). **Why this is the load-bearing fact:** equity in a 4%-float meme-adjacent name is noisy; **credit is where informed money prices default/execution risk**, and it is flashing caution. The equity dip "to $135" is the headline; the bond is the substance. (Analysis) The central question shifts from *"is the stock down?"* to *"is the market re-rating SpaceX's Starship/Starlink cash-flow promises, and does that re-rate the whole AI-IPO cohort's pricing power?"*

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Immediate catalyst: **Starship Flight 13 on Thu Jul 16** (Starbase, TX; ~6:45pm EDT), the **first launch as a public company** and second flight of V3, carrying first V3 Starlink sats. Both booster and upper stage will end in intentional splashdown/"disassembly" in the Gulf of Mexico — [[SpaceX cleared to fly Starship after May booster failure]] confirms FAA clearance. **Why it matters:** a visible failure feeds the "sobering up" narrative into the Sept insider unlock; a clean flight could stabilize sentiment. **Counterintuitive 2nd-order effect:** because SpaceX is the **bellwether for [[Anthropic holds IPO investor meetings, could list by October]] and OpenAI**, a bad Starship + bad tape doesn't just hurt SpaceX — it **compresses the pricing window for the entire 2026-27 AI-IPO pipeline**, which is the fintech/capital-markets-relevant angle here.

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** SpaceX is the reference benchmark for the 2026 mega-cap tech/AI IPO window, not a fintech name itself — but it sets the tone for the AI-adjacent listings (Anthropic, OpenAI, xAI) that the fintech/capital-markets base tracks. Facts: SpaceX priced its IPO at $135 and debuted June 12, 2026 on Nasdaq, raising ~$85.7bn (note text says "nearly $86bn"; Nasdaq confirms $85.7bn) — the largest IPO in history, implied valuation ~$1.77tn at pricing, briefly ~$2.1tn market cap intraday on debut (per Nasdaq / CNN, 2026-06-12). By 2026-07-15 the stock closed at $135.27, back at the IPO price, off a post-IPO high above $200 / 52-week high $225.64 (per Investing.com / CNBC, 2026-07). Structure: the 2026 primary market is barbelled — H1 was dominated by a handful of blockbuster AI/semiconductor/space mega-deals (SpaceX, plus [[Chinese memory giant CXMT to IPO at $85.6B valuation]] $85.6bn and [[South Korea's Toss postpones US ADR listing plan]]'s peer [[SK Hynix raises record $26.5B in largest US foreign IPO]]), and strategists now flag H2 as a "rebalancing" as money rotates to mid-caps (per Fortune, 2026-07-11). Why now: SpaceX's aftermarket is the live gauge for whether the ~$3.8tn combined AI mega-IPO pipeline can be absorbed — its slide is already cited as the reason OpenAI is weighing a delay into 2027 (per CNBC/TechCrunch, 2026-06).

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs for a mega-IPO aftermarket: free float %, first-month drawdown vs offer price, and lock-up/index-flow dynamics. SpaceX's defining feature is an unusually thin float — only ~4–5% of shares trade (note: 4%; Wikipedia: 5%) — which mechanically amplifies volatility and makes the price a sentiment gauge rather than a fundamentals read (analysis). Recent peer moves: [[Anthropic holds IPO investor meetings, could list by October]] — Anthropic filed confidentially in early June at a $965bn last-round mark and is the next mega-test; [[OpenAI IPO delayed to potentially 2027, behind Anthropic]] (last valued ~$852bn post-money); [[Bending Spoons prices Nasdaq IPO, hits $25B market cap]] (2026-07) shows the window is still open for smaller, profitable names; [[Kissht debuts 12% above IPO price at $374 million valuation]] (2026-05) is a contrasting well-received small debut. Positioning: SpaceX is the bellwether — its at/below-offer trading a month post-listing is a negative read-through for the sequenced AI mega-floats that follow it, since the staggered calendar (SpaceX → Anthropic Oct → OpenAI Q4) assumed each could ride sustained enthusiasm.

**Comps & multiples.** Mega-IPO offer-to-current, arithmetic on price (revenue/EBITDA multiples for SpaceX are "no data" — SpaceX runs large losses and does not publicly disclose clean financials; valuation is narrative-driven):

Flag: SpaceX's ~$1.77tn valuation on a pre-profit, thin-float structure is a rich, sentiment-driven outlier — no earnings multiple is computable, so "distribution not computed, qualitative comparison." **Risk flags.**
1. Thin-float distortion — with ~4% floating, price swings reflect flow (Nasdaq-100 index inclusion, retail attention) more than fundamentals; the "IPO-price" signal is noisy and easily overshoots in both directions (second-order: index-flow buying can mask deteriorating sentiment, then reverse sharply).
2. Read-through contagion to the AI IPO pipeline — a bellwether trading at/below offer directly pressures Anthropic (Oct) and OpenAI (Q4→2027) pricing; the window can slam shut on the follow-on mega-floats even if their businesses are unrelated (already visible in OpenAI's mooted delay).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** SpaceX is now the sector's live thermometer for whether 2026's AI/mega-cap primary market has topped (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if SpaceX holds below its $135 offer through the Starship launch and into Anthropic's roadshow, the mega-IPO window compresses and later AI floats price down or slip to 2027 (trigger: Anthropic's Oct pricing / Starship test outcome). What breaks the thesis: a clean Starship flight plus a firm Anthropic debut would reframe the SpaceX dip as thin-float noise, not a window closing.
