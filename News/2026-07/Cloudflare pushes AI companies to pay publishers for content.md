Cloudflare’s new policy pushes AI companies to pay for publishers’ content
Cloudflare has just issued the AI industry a new deadline to separate the web crawlers used for traditional search purposes, like Google Search, from those used for AI agents and training. Starting on September 15, 2026, Cloudflare’s default settings will block “mixed-use” crawlers from any pages that host ads, the company announced on Wednesday.
That means that the crawlers that blend search, agent use, and training will be blocked from crawling these sites by default, unless the site owner adjusts the settings otherwise. These changes to the defaults will apply to new Cloudflare customers, new sites set up by existing customers, and all existing free customers, the company says.
The move could impact how AI model providers are able to access web content for training purposes and to help power their agentic services. 
Cloudflare points out that most website owners want their content to be discoverable via search and often through AI services as well, but they want protections against having their intellectual property given away for free.
Cloudflare specifically calls out the “world’s largest search engine” (clearly a Google reference!) as having access to about “2x more information” than other AI companies because the search giant makes it difficult for customers to remain discoverable without being used for AI. 
Google has pushed back against this generalization in the past, noting that it provides a bot called Google Extended that lets site owners opt out of having their content used for training and AI products and services like Gemini Apps and Vertex API. Its use doesn’t impact a site’s inclusion in Google Search. However, the tech giant’s flagship Googlebot crawls for Search, including AI features like AI Overviews and AI Mode.
“Now that the majority of traffic on the Internet is non-human, we must go further and act faster so that a sustainable ecosystem can emerge,” said Cloudflare co-founder and CEO Matthew Prince in his announcement of the news, referring to the recent milestone where bots surpassed human traffic online for the first time. That shift was not expected to occur until next year.
“Cloudflare’s new tools and partnerships give website owners increased visibility and commercial opportunities and benefit AI companies that have bots with clear and transparent intent. We hope that our proposed default changes encourage mixed-use crawlers to separate out search from agent use and training,” Prince said.
While Cloudflare offers a number of products to help users launch their own AI systems , the company has also released a range of tools to give publishers more control over their content in the AI era. In recent years, Cloudflare launched tools to combat AI bots , including a marketplace that lets websites charge AI bots for scraping , dubbed Pay Per Crawl.
The latter is now also evolving into “Pay Per Use,” the company said, which will allow publishers to charge AI companies when their content creates value, not just when it’s fetched. 
The change could also help conserve publishers’ bandwidth and compute resources for AI model providers, as Cloudflare’s data suggested that over 50% of crawl traffic from AI crawlers is spent re-fetching unchanged pages. 
To put this into action, Cloudflare is initially working with two partners, Ceramic.ai and You.com. When a publisher opts in, they’re paid when their content appears in Ceramic’s AI search results or when You.com accesses a piece of their premium content. 
Other AI companies can customize this model for how they work, Cloudflare says.

# Context-enrichment: Cloudflare pushes AI companies to pay publishers for content
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

Builds directly on [[Cloudflare rolls out pay-per-crawl micropayments for AI crawlers]] (2025-07, Pay Per Crawl beta launch) and on Cloudflare's agentic-payments push [[Cloudflare to launch US dollar stablecoin for agentic web]] (2025-10, NET Dollar). Sits in the same content-monetization arena as [[Stripe and AWS enable AI agent payments for publishers]] (2026-06) and the pay-per-use crypto rail [[Coinbase x402 pay-per-use payments gain traction in DeFi]] (2025-11).

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 2026-07-01 Cloudflare announced that from **September 15, 2026** its **default** settings will block "mixed-use" crawlers — bots that blend Search + AI training + agent use — from any page that hosts ads, unless the site owner opts them back in. Scope: new Cloudflare customers, new sites of existing customers, and **all existing free customers**. Search-only crawling stays allowed by default; Training and Agent categories get blocked on ad-monetized pages.
- **The de-PR'd core:** this is a *default-setting change*, i.e. a policy nudge, not a new technical capability. The block-by-default and Pay Per Crawl machinery already shipped in July 2025. What's new here is (a) a hard **deadline** aimed at forcing crawler separation, and (b) the **"Pay Per Use"** evolution — charging AI companies when content *creates value* (appears in an answer / is accessed as premium), not merely when it is *fetched*.
- **Why structured this way:** the ad-page carve-out is the lever. Cloudflare isn't blocking everything — it targets exactly the pages where publisher economics live (ad-monetized content), maximizing pain for AI crawlers while letting Cloudflare claim it's "pro-discoverability." The Sept 15 deadline manufactures urgency to pressure the one holdout that matters: Google.
- **Named target (de-PR'd):** the "world's largest search engine with ~2x more information" is Google. Under the new default, single-purpose bots that crawl for both Search and Training — **Googlebot, Bingbot, Applebot** — get treated under the most restrictive rule and are blocked on ad pages for any customer enabling Training blocks (per Cloudflare's own blog). That is the real teeth: it can block Googlebot, not just niche AI scrapers.
- **Live vs announced:** Pay Per Use is launching with just **two** partners — **Ceramic.ai** (pay when content appears in its AI search results) and **You.com** (pay when it accesses premium content). Two design partners = early/pilot, not adoption. Cloudflare's supporting stat: >50% of AI-crawler traffic is re-fetching unchanged pages (bandwidth/compute waste framing).

## [1] Competitors / peers
The AI-content-licensing "tollbooth" layer went from a handful of startups to 12+ since 2024. Key models:
- **TollBit:** licensing infra, publishers set price per 1,000 pages scraped, rights holders keep 100% of revenue, TollBit charges the AI company a transaction fee.
- **ProRata:** ad-revenue share on AI answers that cite a publisher; 50/50 split, allocated by each source's contribution. Claims **500+ publishers** (The Atlantic, TIME, Fortune, BuzzFeed, ADWEEK) — the strongest publisher network in the space.
- **Microsoft Publisher Content Marketplace** (announced Feb 2026): pay-per-use, take-rate undisclosed.
- **Stripe + AWS WAF** (2026-06): Stripe provides the rails so content owners charge AI-agent traffic at the firewall — a **direct infrastructure competitor** to Cloudflare's edge-level model.
- **Cloudflare:** estimated **~30% take** on the pay-per-crawl marketplace, plus an Attribution/Business Insights dashboard.
- **Position:** Cloudflare is not first on the *licensing* idea (TollBit/ProRata predate it) but is uniquely positioned on **distribution** — it sits in front of ~20% of the web at the edge, so it can *enforce* blocking by default in a way a SaaS marketplace cannot. **+ Why it matters:** the marketplaces control terms only for publishers who sign up; Cloudflare controls the *choke point*. That is a structurally stronger position — but a 30% take vs TollBit's "keep 100%" is a weaker offer to publishers, so the value is enforcement/reach, not economics.

## [2] Company history / fit
Trajectory: block-AI-bots tools → **Pay Per Crawl beta (July 2025)** with a dozen+ publishers endorsing (Condé Nast, AP, Time, Reddit, UMG) → **NET Dollar stablecoin (Oct 2025)** for agentic payments → **this (July 2026)**: default-block deadline + Pay Per Use. **+ Why Cloudflare acts this way:** it is trying to convert its neutral-infrastructure position into a *toll-collecting* position — moving up the stack from "we protect your site" to "we run the marketplace and settle the payments." The stablecoin, the marketplace, and the crawler-separation policy are three legs of the same bet: own the settlement layer of the agentic web. CEO Matthew Prince frames it as "bots surpassed human traffic → the ecosystem is unsustainable → we must act." That framing is self-serving but factually anchored (the bot-majority milestone did arrive early).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Genuinely new vs July 2025:** (a) the hard Sept 15 deadline + explicit targeting of mixed-use bots including **Googlebot**; (b) **Pay Per Use** (value-based, not fetch-based billing). These are real deltas, not a reprint of the 2025 launch.
- **Traction is thin:** Pay Per Use ships with **2** partners. No disclosed publisher payouts, no GMV, no dollar volume. Contrast ProRata's 500+ publishers. So on the licensing side Cloudflare is *behind* on network; ahead only on enforcement reach.
- **+ Who captures the margin (why the value-add is real-but-capped):** Cloudflare's edge position lets it *enforce* the block — that is the durable asset. But value-based billing ("paid when content creates value") requires **attribution** that neither Cloudflare nor anyone else has solved: proving a given article drove a given AI answer is contested. If attribution is fuzzy, Pay Per Use degrades back to fetch-based pricing, and Cloudflare's edge is just "a nicer Pay Per Crawl." The margin question: does Cloudflare capture a 30% toll, or does it merely become the enforcement muscle while ProRata/TollBit own the publisher relationships and pricing? (analysis)
- **Second-order:** the policy's real power is coercive, not commercial — it's a negotiating stance against Google. The Pay Per Use marketplace is the carrot; the default block is the stick.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **The Google fight is the story.** Cloudflare explicitly wants to force Google to structurally separate its crawlers (Search vs Training). Google's counter: Google-Extended lets publishers opt out of training, but it does *not* stop content appearing in AI Overviews/AI Mode (those draw on the Search index). So publishers face a real double-bind — block AI and lose Search visibility, or allow Search and feed AI for free. Sept 15 forces the issue.
- **Regulatory backdrop:** a May 2026 report (Nieman/Brookings) warns the licensing market puts publishers in a "double bind" — the same gatekeepers (Google, Cloudflare, Microsoft) are erecting the new tollbooths. Antitrust/competition angle is live.
- **OpenAI/Anthropic stance:** per Cloudflare, OpenAI and Anthropic *do* separate crawlers by purpose and thus comply more cleanly — leaving **Google as the primary non-compliant target**. Sam Altman has publicly backed micropayment-style publisher compensation (May 2026), which softens OpenAI's opposition.
- **+ Counterintuitive second-order:** if Google refuses to split Googlebot, Cloudflare customers blocking Training would also lose Google *Search* crawling — a nuclear outcome most publishers won't accept. So the default is unlikely to bite Google at scale; its value is as leverage to extract a licensing deal, not as an actual mass block. The risk for Cloudflare: if Google calls the bluff, publishers quietly opt back in and the "default" becomes symbolic.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Sub-sector: AI-content licensing / crawler-monetization / publisher economics. Market sizing is thin and definition-dependent: the AI training-dataset market was ~$3.59bn (2025) → projected ~$4.44bn (2026), and a broader AI-IP-&-licensing basket is quoted at ~24.8% CAGR 2026–2033 (per HTF Market Intelligence / secondary citations, as of 2026 — treat as directional, single-vendor, unaudited → [UNSOURCED] for a hard TAM). Individual licensing deals give a cleaner read on economics than the TAM: News Corp ~$50m/yr, Amazon–NYT ~$20–25m/yr, Reddit's AI-licensing run-rate near ~$130m/yr (~10% of Reddit revenue) — per Digiday / mediaandthemachine, as of 2025. Structure: fragmented and early — an intermediary "toll layer" has grown from a handful of startups to 12+ players since 2024 (TollBit, ProRata, ScalePost, Created by Humans, Sphere AI) plus infra incumbents (Cloudflare, Akamai/DataDome, AWS WAF+Stripe). Value is being created at the choke-point between publisher origin and AI crawler; the barrier is distribution/reach (who already sits in front of the traffic), not technology. Why now: Cloudflare states bots surpassed human web traffic for the first time (ahead of schedule), and that >50% of AI-crawler traffic re-fetches unchanged pages — a cost/leakage problem that makes metering economically rational, not just ideological (per TechCrunch/Cloudflare, 2026-07-01).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: opt-in publisher coverage / domains under management, take rate on crawl or per-use fees, and the split between per-fetch vs per-use (outcome) pricing. Key players & basis of competition — competition is on **distribution and default position**, not price: Cloudflare sits in front of a large share of web traffic and can flip a *default* (block mixed-use crawlers on ad-bearing pages from 2026-09-15) — a lever no pure-play startup has. Pure-plays compete on marketplace depth/negotiation (TollBit — $24m Series A led by Lightspeed Oct-2024, ~$31m total; integrated with Akamai/WordPress VIP/DataDome). Standards angle: RSL (Really Simple Licensing), backed by Creative Commons/Yahoo/Reddit, embeds machine-readable license terms — a potential commoditizer of the "who-can-crawl" signal. Recent moves: Stripe+AWS WAF publisher agent-payments (2026-06-17); Stripe AI-monetization tools (2026-03, 2025-09); Cloudflare Pay-Per-Crawl launch (2025-07). Cloudflare's position: **ahead on distribution/default power, early/niche on the monetization economics** — Pay-Per-Use launches with only two partners (Ceramic.ai, You.com), i.e. announced not yet at scale (analysis). Moat = network/scale + intangible position as merchant-of-record and default-setter; switching cost for publishers already on Cloudflare is low-friction opt-in.

**Comps & multiples.** Protagonist (public): **Cloudflare (NYSE:NET)** — Q1 2026 revenue $639.8m (+34% YoY), ~$2.56bn annualized run-rate; market cap quoted ~$69–91bn depending on timing (per StockTitan/GuruFocus/TIKR, 2026). NET is net-loss-making (Q1'26 net loss $22.9m), so EV/EBITDA and P/E on a clean basis → [UNSOURCED]/"no data". Private peer: TollBit — last round ~$24m Series A (Lightspeed, Oct-2024); post-money not disclosed → round-stage only, no revenue multiple ("no data").
Internal comps (base): [[Cloudflare rolls out pay-per-crawl micropayments for AI crawlers]] (2025-07) · [[Stripe and AWS enable AI agent payments for publishers]] (2026-06) · [[Stripe unveils tools to help AI companies monetize]] (2025-09) · [[InFlow launches payments platform to monetize AI agents]] (2025-12). Distribution not computed (only one public comparable, NET) → qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Adoption/economics unproven (de-PR):** Pay-Per-Use ships with just 2 partners and pricing on "value created" is undefined; a default-block is coercion of *supply* but does not create *demand* — AI labs can litigate, route around, or refuse to pay, leaving publishers with blocked traffic and no revenue. Second-order: if labs walk, Cloudflare has picked a fight that hurts its own publisher customers' discoverability.
2. **Disintermediation / standards risk:** RSL (Creative Commons/Reddit/Yahoo) and direct publisher↔lab deals (News Corp, NYT, Reddit) can bypass any single toll-collector; the margin may accrue to whoever owns the *standard*, not the *gateway* — Cloudflare's edge is distribution, which a widely-adopted open standard erodes.
3. **Regulatory / antitrust & concentration:** a private infrastructure company unilaterally setting a web-wide default that gates content access invites competition-law and net-neutrality scrutiny; concentration of the "toll booth" in one CDN is itself a systemic risk (Brookings framing). Also directly antagonizes Google, whose crawlers are the target.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is an attempt to **re-rate the toll layer from a startup niche into an infrastructure-default primitive** — Cloudflare converting reach into a metered-access rail (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: *if* by the 2026-09-15 deadline at least one Tier-1 AI lab (OpenAI/Anthropic/Google) agrees to pay under Pay-Per-Use, the metering model is validated and the pure-play tolls (TollBit et al.) get pulled toward Cloudflare's default; catalyst = the deadline + first lab signature. What breaks it: labs collectively refuse and RSL/direct-deal bypass wins, making Cloudflare's default an unenforceable gesture. Watch: partner count beyond Ceramic.ai/You.com, and whether Cloudflare ever discloses Pay-Per-Crawl/Pay-Per-Use GMV.
