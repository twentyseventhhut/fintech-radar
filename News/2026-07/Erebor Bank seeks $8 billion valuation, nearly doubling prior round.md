Erebor Bank, the crypto- and defense-focused startup founded by Palmer Luckey and backed by Peter Thiel, is in talks to raise funding at a valuation of at least $8 billion.

The potential valuation would roughly double the $4.35 billion level Erebor secured in a $350 million funding round in December, Bloomberg reported, citing people familiar with the matter. The talks are preliminary, and the valuation is still under discussion, the people said.

The bank's deposit base has nearly quadrupled since March, reaching $4.05 billion from the $1.1 billion it disclosed to regulators at the end of that month, per the report. Erebor also added nearly 400 customers over the past three months and expects to be profitable by year-end.

Luckey said none of the bank's deposit growth this quarter came from his own companies, adding that hundreds of new customers had chosen Erebor independently.

## Crypto plans

Erebor plans to offer U.S. dollar stablecoin deposits and payments, though demand for crypto-backed lending has been lower than the bank initially expected, one of the sources said.

Erebor received preliminary approval from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) in October 2025, before securing final approval to operate as a national bank in February, the first new national charter granted under the Trump administration.

The bank has also signed a non-binding letter of intent with Banco de Venezuela to provide correspondent banking services. Bloomberg said such a deal would ease foreign exchange flows into the sanctioned country and open a channel for transactions with the U.S.

Erebor’s fast rise has drawn scrutiny from Sen. Elizabeth Warren, who reportedly said she has "serious concerns" about how quickly Erebor secured its charter and questioned whether political connections in the Trump administration helped speed the process.

# Context-enrichment: Erebor Bank seeks $8B valuation, nearly doubling prior round
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Per Bloomberg (2 Jul 2026), Erebor is in **preliminary, non-binding talks** to raise a new round at a valuation of **at least $8B** — roughly double the **$4.35B** it secured in a **$350M round in Dec 2025** (led by Lux Capital). No lead, size, or close date disclosed → this is a *talks*, not a *signed round*. The hard, verifiable delta since the last note is **traction, not the raise**: deposits grew **$1.1B (end-March, disclosed to regulators) → $4.05B (now)**, ~4x in one quarter; ~400 new customers added; profitability guided "by year-end."
- **Why framed this way:** the PR anchors the valuation to explosive deposit growth. But a ~2x valuation on ~4x deposits is the story the *investors* want told; Luckey pre-empting the obvious rebuttal — "none of this quarter's deposit growth came from my own companies (Anduril etc.)" — signals the market already suspects the deposit base is captive/related-party. That denial is itself a tell about the central question (below).
- **What's quietly under-delivering:** management admits **crypto-backed lending demand is lower than expected**. Erebor's entire differentiation is being a stablecoin-native, charter-owning crypto bank — yet the crypto *lending* leg is soft. So growth is coming from plain deposits, not the novel crypto rails.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Charter-owning neobanks:** **Mercury** filed its own OCC national-charter application (Dec 2025) — moving off the partner-bank model onto Erebor's exact playbook. **Brex** was acquired by **Capital One for $5.15B (Jan 2026)**, giving it a Tier-1 balance sheet. So Erebor's "we own the charter/balance sheet" moat is closing fast on both flanks.
- **Digital-asset banks:** **Anchorage Digital** has held an OCC charter since **2021** — Erebor is not first-of-kind in crypto-charter banking, only first *de novo under Trump 2.0*.
- **The gap it fills:** **SVB** (now First Citizens) — the startup/VC-ecosystem banking playbook Erebor copies post-2023 collapse.
- **Position:** ahead on the *regulatory-narrative/political-tailwind* axis (first Trump-era charter, stablecoin-native), but **at parity-to-behind on durable moat**: Anchorage predates it, Brex now has a real bank behind it, Mercury is chartering. The $8B ask is a *scarcity/momentum* premium, not a defensible-moat premium (analysis).

## [2] Company history / fit
Trajectory (all dated, from corpus): [[Erebor raises at $2.25B valuation, backed by Palmer Luckey]] (Jun 2025) → [[Palmer Luckey to launch crypto bank Erebor]] (Jul 2025, charter applied) → [[Trump-backed crypto bank Erebor wins preliminary OCC approval]] (Oct 2025) → [[FDIC approves Thiel-backed Erebor Bank for deposit insurance]] (Dec 2025) → $4.35B/$350M round (Dec 2025) → [[Erebor Bank receives national bank charter under Trump]] (Feb 2026, first Trump 2.0 de novo charter, ~$635M capital) → **$8B talks (Jul 2026)**. Valuation path: $2.25B → $4.35B → $8B in ~12 months.
- **Why it moves this fast:** a charter is a one-time, politically-gated asset; the founders (Luckey/Thiel/Lonsdale, Founders Fund, a16z, 8VC, Lux, Haun) monetize the *scarcity of the charter + the AI/crypto/defense narrative* while the regulatory window is open. Raising now, pre-profitability, locks in a high mark before Mercury/others dilute the scarcity.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real & new:** a genuinely *chartered, FDIC-insured, stablecoin-native* national bank is rare — it can put stablecoins/blockchains on its own balance sheet without a sponsor bank (Mercury/Brex historically couldn't). That is a structural, not cosmetic, advantage.
- **Traction is real but thin & possibly captive:** $4.05B deposits and ~400 customers in a quarter is fast, but (a) off a tiny base, (b) Luckey's own denial flags related-party concern, (c) the *crypto-lending* value-add — where the fat margin would live — is under-delivering. So the multiple rests on deposit *growth* + narrative, and the highest-margin novel product is soft.
- **Who captures the margin (why it matters):** if crypto-lending stays weak, Erebor is a fast-growing but ordinary spread bank paying up on deposits to win startups — a business that gets an ~1-2x-book bank multiple, not an $8B venture multiple. The $8B ask assumes the *stablecoin/crypto rails* eventually capture platform-like margin; that thesis is currently unproven by its own admission.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Plans:** USD-stablecoin deposits & payments; **non-binding LOI with Banco de Venezuela** for correspondent banking — geopolitically loaded (eases FX flows into a sanctioned country) and a reputational/sanctions-risk lightning rod.
- **Regulatory backdrop — the real risk:** **Sen. Elizabeth Warren** has "serious concerns," probing whether **political connections fast-tracked the charter** (~4 months vs the typical 12–18); a 25 Feb 2026 Senate Banking letter to Comptroller Gould exists. A change in administration or an OCC review could impair the charter — the very asset the $8B rests on.
- **Second-order (analysis):** the same political tailwind that produced a first-Trump-era charter makes the valuation *politically fragile*, not safe — concentration of the moat in one regulatory relationship is a single point of failure. Counterintuitively, the faster the mark rises on political scarcity, the more exposed it is to a political reversal.

## Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Talks vs signed?** The $8B is a *preliminary, non-binding* talk — no lead, size, or close. Is there any confirmation of a term sheet? → **Open** (Bloomberg: "talks are preliminary, valuation still under discussion").
2. **Is the deposit growth captive?** Luckey volunteers that "none" of this quarter's growth came from his own companies. Who *did* the ~400 new customers come from, and how many are related to Founders Fund / a16z / 8VC portfolios? → **Open** (self-denial is a tell, not proof).
3. **Why 2x valuation on ~4x deposits?** If deposits quadrupled but valuation only doubled, what does the market think about deposit *quality/stickiness* (near-zero rate, uninsured over $250k, startup runway burn)? → Partly answered: implies deposits are discounted vs a normal bank.
4. **Crypto-lending is under-delivering — so what is the actual business?** If the novel crypto-lending leg is soft, is Erebor just a fast neobank paying up for startup deposits? What multiple does *that* deserve? → **Open** (thesis-breaking).

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** US "bank-for-startups / crypto-native banking" niche — the vacuum left by SVB's March 2023 collapse plus a re-opened chartering path. No credible TAM figure is publicly sourceable → no data; instead, the structural facts: the OCC under Comptroller Gould has "expressly signalled openness to digital-asset banking" and finalised a new national-chartering rule, with ≥18 charter applications filed in the prior year and more in 2026 (per American Banker / Banking Dive, as of 2026). Erebor got preliminary OCC approval Oct-2025 and final national-bank approval Feb-2026 — the first new national charter under Trump 2.0 (note text; Banking Dive). Structure: highly regulated (charter + capital = the binding entry barrier — OCC imposed a 12% tier-1 leverage requirement for Erebor's first three years, well above peers, per Banking Dive), otherwise fragmented at the startup-deposits layer (Mercury, Brex, Ramp, Arc, regional banks). Why now: (1) political/regulatory tailwind — charters flowing again after a decade of near-freeze; (2) stablecoin/crypto-banking legitimisation post-GENIUS-era; (3) a rate environment where deposit-heavy balance sheets earn real NIM.

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs the niche runs on: deposit base and its growth, cost of deposits, net interest margin, deposit-to-valuation, path-to-profit. Players & basis of competition: Mercury (~$20bn deposits, 300k+ customers, ~$650m annualised revenue, profitable, conditional OCC charter Apr-2026 — competes on product breadth/distribution); Brex (acquired by Capital One Apr-2026, ~$5.15bn — exiting independence); Ramp (~$32bn valuation, corporate-card/spend, not a bank); plus regional banks that absorbed the SVB book. Erebor competes on a distinct wedge — defense/hard-tech + crypto/stablecoin deposits + a founder network (Anduril/Thiel/Palantir orbit) — customers "often underserved by traditional lenders" (note). Recent moves: Erebor deposits $1.1bn (Mar) → $4.05bn (nearly 4x in ~3 months), ~400 new customers, targets profitability by year-end; non-binding LOI with Banco de Venezuela for correspondent banking (note, Bloomberg 2026-07-02). Position: fast-growing niche entrant, NOT scale leader — Mercury's deposit base is ~5x larger. Moat `(analysis)`: intangible/network (founder ecosystem) + a regulatory moat (one of few crypto-friendly national charters); switching costs are still shallow this early, and Luckey's claim that "none of the deposit growth came from his own companies" is a de-PR flag — unverifiable, and related-party concentration would materially weaken the moat story.

**Comps & multiples.** Deposit-to-valuation (round valuations, not market caps):
- Erebor now: `$8.0bn / $4.05bn deposits = ~2.0x`; on prior round `$4.35bn / $1.1bn (Mar deposits) = ~4.0x` — so the headline "doubling" of valuation is roughly matched by ~4x deposit growth, i.e. the deposit multiple actually *compressed*.
- Mercury (May-2026, `[[Mercury raises $200 million at $5.2 billion valuation]]`): `$5.2bn / ~$20bn deposits = ~0.26x` deposits; and `$5.2bn / $0.65bn revenue = ~8.0x` revenue (profitable).
- Erebor vs Mercury on deposits: ~2.0x vs ~0.26x = Erebor carries a ~8x richer deposit multiple. Distribution not computed (only 2 clean comparables) → qualitative: Erebor looks **rich vs Mercury on deposits**, but Erebor is pre-scale hyper-growth (deposits nearly 4x/quarter) and Mercury has a fee/software revenue engine Erebor lacks — so the multiples aren't clean like-for-like. Erebor's revenue multiple = no data (revenue not disclosed).
- Internal comps: `[[Erebor Bank receives national bank charter under Trump]]`, `[[Palmer Luckey to launch crypto bank Erebor]]`, `[[Mercury raises $200 million at $5.2 billion valuation]]`, `[[Mercury wins conditional OCC approval for bank charter]]`, `[[Mercury applies for OCC national bank charter]]`. Brex exit (`Capital One, ~$5.15bn, Apr-2026`) is a comparable liquidity data point but a full-acquisition, not a growth round.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Political/regulatory concentration.** The charter was the fastest under Trump 2.0 and has drawn Sen. Warren's "serious concerns" about whether political connections sped it (note). A charter obtained via a favourable OCC is exposed to reversal risk on any administration/regulatory shift — second-order: the core moat (a scarce crypto-friendly national charter) is politically contingent, not durable.
2. **Sanctions / AML exposure.** The non-binding LOI with Banco de Venezuela for correspondent banking (a sanctioned country) is a live compliance landmine — one enforcement action against a one-year-old bank could freeze the franchise and the raise. High severity relative to the bank's age and capital.
3. **Valuation vs unit economics / deposit stickiness.** ~2x deposits is rich vs Mercury's ~0.26x, and demand for crypto-backed lending is "lower than the bank initially expected" (note) — meaning the crypto thesis underpinning the premium is under-delivering on the asset side. Deposits grown ~4x in a quarter are unproven for stickiness/cost; if hot money, NIM and the premium both compress.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is a re-rating of the "SVB-successor / crypto-native bank" category on deposit momentum, not on proven earnings — the readable signal is that a national charter + a crypto/defense wedge can compound deposits faster than incumbents thought. Falsifiable thesis: Erebor sustains the deposit-multiple premium only if it (a) hits year-end profitability and (b) shows the deposit base is non-related-party and low-cost. Trigger/what breaks it: the actual round terms + first regulatory/AML action (esp. the Venezuela channel) or disclosure that growth is founder-ecosystem-driven — either would collapse the premium toward Mercury's ~0.26x deposit multiple.
