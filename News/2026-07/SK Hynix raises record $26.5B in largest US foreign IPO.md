SK Hynix raises $26.5B in the biggest foreign IPO in US history, is urged to build new US fabs
The AI chip boom just produced its biggest Wall Street moment yet. SK Hynix, a South Korean memory chip giant, said Friday it has raised $26.5 billion (KRW 40 trillion) in its U.S. market debut.
SK Hynix sold 177.9 million American depositary shares (ADRs) at $149 each, structured so U.S. investors can buy in at roughly a tenth of what a full share costs in Seoul. This deal, the largest-ever U.S. debut by a non-American company, topped Alibaba’s $25 billion IPO in 2014.
The company begins trading on the Nasdaq today, Friday, July 10, under the temporary ticker SKHYV. Regular trading opens Monday, July 13, when the ticker officially becomes SKHY. So far, U.S. investors are lapping it up. The stock opened at 14% over its IPO price , and the price was still rising in early trading on Friday.
This even as it priced its U.S. shares at a 2.7% premium to its own three-day average back home in Seoul, according to its Korea Stock Exchange filing . Yet, demand for the offering was reportedly more than seven times the available shares, per media reports.
That’s especially amazing considering Korean companies have long traded at a discount to their global peers. That valuation gap is called the Korea Discount. Investors often cite factors such as complex corporate governance structures, low shareholder returns, regulatory uncertainty, and geopolitical risks related to North Korea to justify why companies from that country don’t command higher share prices. 
But SK Hynix clearly isn’t suffering from the Korea Discount and that’s because it makes memory chips, including high-bandwidth memory (HBM). HBM is a key component of AI GPUs processors. And right now, Nvidia relies on SK Hynix as one of its primary suppliers.
Per its filing, the money raised from eager U.S. investors will go to three places: a new fab in South Korea (being built now to address the worldwide shortage of memory cause by AI); a new packaging facility in that country; and EUV scanners, the machines that make next-generation chips possible.
Meanwhile, U.S. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick stopped by a Micron event Thursday with a message for the broader chip industry, not just for U.S. memory maker Micron (who is one of SK Hynix’s biggest competitors). Lutnick reportedly said he’s already in talks with Samsung (the third major memory maker, worldwide) and SK Hynix about building new factories in the U.S. The idea being not to let South Korea continue to be the country that dominates this important tech.
Micron, naturally, is in. It announced it plans to invest $250 billion in new U.S. manufacturing, a commitment the U.S. memory chip company says will create more than 90,000 jobs and keep leading-edge chip production on American soil.
The timing of Lutnick’s request is notable beyond this U.S. IPO for SK Hynix: Both Korean chipmakers just pledged more than $550 billion for new manufacturing investment in South Korea.

# Context-enrichment: SK Hynix raises record $26.5B in largest US foreign IPO
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **Friday 2026-07-10** SK Hynix completed its US debut, having priced on **07-09** at **$149.00 per ADR**, selling **177.9M American Depositary Shares** (each ADS = 1/10 of a Seoul ordinary share, i.e. ~17.79M common shares) to raise **~$26.5B gross (≈KRW 40T)**. It trades on **Nasdaq** under the temporary "when-issued" ticker **SKHYV** from 07-10, converting to **SKHY** for regular trading **Monday 07-13** (one outlet said Tuesday — minor discrepancy). It priced at only a **~2.7% premium to the Seoul 3-day average**, yet the book was **>7x oversubscribed** (reported order interest ~$171B). First day: **opened ~+14% (~$168–170), closed ~+13% at ~$168.01** — i.e. it drifted *down* from the open. (Sources: [Bloomberg pricing](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/sk-hynix-is-said-to-price-us-share-offering-at-149-apiece-mrdz562z), [CNBC debut](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/10/sk-hynix-skhy-stock-nasdaq.html), [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/7/10/south-koreas-sk-hynix-raises-26-5bn-in-record-breaking-us-ipo).)

**+ Why structured this way / the core de-PR — TWO corrections to the headline:**
1. **"Largest US foreign IPO" is true; "record largest US IPO" is NOT.** It genuinely tops **Alibaba's Sept-2014 debut ($21.8B initial → $25.03B with greenshoe)** as the biggest-ever US listing by a *non-American* company. But it is **not the largest US IPO ever** — **SpaceX's June-2026 Nasdaq IPO** was far bigger (~$75B per [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/11/spacex-raises-75-billion-in-record-setting-ipo-ahead-of-nasdaq-debut.html); some trackers ~$85.7B). Among *all* US IPOs, $26.5B ranks ~**2nd–3rd** (behind SpaceX; ahead of Facebook $16B, Visa $17.9B). SpaceX's own mega-IPO is already in the corpus ([[Robinhood to vie for retail role in SpaceX IPO]]).
2. **This is NOT a first-time IPO.** SK Hynix is already **KOSPI-listed (000660.KS)** with a >$1T cap since May 2026. This is a **primary follow-on capital raise packaged as a Nasdaq ADR cross-listing** — new shares, proceeds to the company (Korean fabs + EUV), diluting existing holders ~2.5%. "IPO" is press shorthand for the US *debut*. This is the completed follow-up to the pre-listing access preview [[US investors to gain access to memory maker SK Hynix]] — **fresh** (new: final $149 price vs the earlier ~$165 reference, actual $26.5B vs the ~$28B target, the first-day +13% pop, and the Lutnick US-fab politics), **not a duplicate**.

The **1/10 ADR ratio** is a deliberate retail-affordability design (lower per-unit USD price so US retail buys round lots via any brokerage). The **~2.7% skinny premium + >7x book** together read as **deliberately conservative pricing to guarantee a clean pop** — SK Hynix left money on the table to buy a strong debut narrative, not to maximize proceeds.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Micron (MU, US)** — the pipeline's reference frame and the memory comp US retail already owns ([[Wall Street eyes memory maker Micron as next Nvidia]], [[Micron quarterly profit jumps 15-fold]]). It crossed ~$1T (fastest ever, May 2026) and pledged **$250B** of US manufacturing / 90,000+ jobs — the political stick used against the Koreans (see [4]). Trades ~6.6x forward earnings.
- **Samsung (KOSPI)** — #2 in HBM (~21%), Korea-listed, no comparable US listing; also being pressed on US fabs ([[Anthropic in talks with Samsung on custom AI chip]]).
- **HBM position** — SK Hynix is the clear #1: sources range **~50–62%** by metric; "roughly half, more than Samsung + Micron combined" is safe, and it reportedly took **~70% of Nvidia's HBM4 (Rubin) allocation** ([Semicone](https://www.semicone.com/article-385.html)). It is Nvidia's primary HBM supplier.
- **IPO-comp context** — Asian AI/chip listings are the 2026 IPO story ([[Chinese AI and chip firms drive mainland IPO rebound]]); **Arm (Sept-2023, ~$4.87B)** is an order of magnitude smaller; **Saudi Aramco (~$25.6B, 2019)** listed in Riyadh, not the US, so it doesn't count in a US ranking. Korean-ADR comp with the *opposite* outcome: [[South Korea's Toss postpones US ADR listing plan]] — a frozen Korean IPO backdrop that SK Hynix cleared.

**+ Why the lay of the land / second-order:** memory is a **3-firm oligopoly (>95% of DRAM)** and SK Hynix owns the highest-margin, most AI-levered slice (HBM), so it captures the richest part of "RAMageddon." The valuation premium here is **sectoral (the AI-memory cycle), not name-specific** — which is exactly its fragility. Micron's *cheap* ~6.6x forward multiple is the market saying it disbelieves the memory earnings are durable; the same skepticism will cap how far SKHY's US listing can close the "Korea discount" (see [2]).

## [2] Company history / fit
SK Hynix rode the AI-memory boom hard: Q1-2026 revenue ~₩52.6T (~$35.5B), **+~198% YoY**, ~72% operating margin; the Seoul stock is **up ~230–240% YTD 2026** ("roughly tripled" — confirmed), crossing **$1T** in May 2026. The oft-quoted **"~18x since 2021" and the "$1.03T ≈ Coca-Cola + Netflix + Goldman combined"** comparison are **UNCONFIRMED** (could not source them; the ~$1T-plus magnitude and top-10/11 US-traded rank are confirmed, but present the analogy as color, not fact). At the $170 open the debut-day implied cap was **~$1.2–1.3T**, so the aggregated note's "$1.03T" is the at-offer figure, not the debut-day figure.

**+ Why the company acts this way:** SK Hynix needs **enormous capex** to defend its HBM lead — proceeds go to a **Korean fab (Yongin), a packaging plant (Cheongju P&T7), and ASML EUV scanners**, NOT US operations ([Tom's Hardware](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/sk-hynix-files-to-raise-up-to-29-billion-in-nasdaq-listing)). Doing this as a Nasdaq ADR at an all-time-high multiple is a **cheap cost-of-capital move**: monetize the AI re-rating to fund the very capacity race that sustains it, broaden the shareholder base / index eligibility, and test whether US pools will price it above the Korea discount — all while keeping the KOSPI listing and Korean control intact.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
The **mechanism (ADRs) is not new** — it is the legacy, SEC-regulated wrapper for holding foreign equity in USD via an ordinary US brokerage. What is genuinely notable is **scale + traction**: at ~$26.5B it is the **largest US listing ever by a foreign company**, and demand was **real, not just announced** — >7x oversubscribed, +13% first day. The fintech angle is **access, not innovation.**

**+ Who captures the margin, deeper:** the fee margin accrues to the **underwriters (BofA/Citi/GS/JPM), the depositary bank, and Nasdaq** — not to any fintech disruptor. For US brokerages (Schwab/Fidelity/Robinhood) SKHY is incremental order flow, not a new capability. Second-order read for the corpus: **tokenized-equity platforms pitch precisely to disintermediate this stack**, yet they are **irrelevant to US buyers of SK Hynix** (tokenized equities exclude US persons and carry no Asian primaries). So this $26.5B mega-ADR is a live **stress test of "old rail vs new rail"**: for the most-wanted hard-to-reach Asian names, the regulated ADR still beats tokenization — a caution against the "tokenization opens global equities" thesis.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Regular SKHY trading opens Monday 07-13. Sentiment is euphoric ("another Nvidia"), backed by a real HBM shortage worsening into 2H-2026 with little new supply before **late 2027**, and HBM4 ramping on Nvidia's Rubin platform. Politics moved the same day: **Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick** publicly urged **Samsung and SK Hynix to build US memory fabs** ("Micron is in the lead… others will have no choice but to follow") — pressure/talks, **not a commitment** ([Korea JoongAng Daily](https://www.koreajoongangdaily.com/business/lutnick-urges-samsung-sk-hynix-to-build-memory-chip-plants-in-us/12767060), [UPI](https://www.upi.com/Top_News/World-News/2026/07/10/us-commerce-secretary-howard-lutnick/7961783724473/)). Meanwhile the Koreans have pledged **>$550B for manufacturing in South Korea** (domestic).

**+ Why the market goes this way / counterintuitive second-order:** memory is **deeply cyclical**; the >$550B buildout is the classic setup for a 2027–2028 glut that could crater DRAM/HBM pricing. **The AI re-rating that makes SKHY attractive is what makes it fragile** — buyers get a leveraged bet on the HBM cycle at cycle-peak valuation, not a durable moat, and a US listing transfers that cyclical downside to a broader US retail base. The Lutnick pressure adds a **geopolitical fork**: build US fabs (margin-dilutive, appeases Washington/tariffs) or resist (tariff/export-control risk). The skinny debut premium says even the underwriters wanted a cushion.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** SK Hynix sits in the AI-memory (HBM) subsegment of the semiconductor capital-markets cycle. HBM market size is far smaller than the trillion-dollar headline suggests: 2026 estimates cluster ~$3.7–9bn with a ~26% CAGR into the early 2030s (Mordor / Fortune Business Insights / The Business Research Company, via secondary market-report citations, as of 2026 — figures diverge widely, treat as directional). AI/ML training+inference reportedly drives >55% of HBM demand (secondary analyst citation), and only three firms make HBM at scale (SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron) — a near-oligopoly with extreme entry barriers (EUV capex, packaging IP, multi-year customer qualification). Why now: an AI-driven memory "supercycle" — HBM demand growth cited at ~130% y/y in 2025 and ~70% in 2026 (secondary), with SK Hynix and Micron reportedly sold out of 2026 HBM capacity. Second-order effect: HBM is now the binding bottleneck for AI GPUs, so memory has migrated from commodity DRAM economics toward supplier-priced, pre-contracted allocation — which is what let SK Hynix escape the "Korea Discount" and clear a record foreign IPO.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: HBM revenue share, DRAM/HBM bit-supply growth, ASP/pricing, capex intensity. Players: SK Hynix (leader), Samsung (#2/#3), Micron (#3 but gaining — reportedly overtook Samsung in HBM by some 2026 measures). Basis of competition: technology/qualification (HBM4 allocation to Nvidia) and capacity, not price. Recent moves (dated): both Korean makers pledged >$550bn for domestic manufacturing; US Commerce Secretary Lutnick (Jul 2026) publicly pushing SK Hynix + Samsung toward US fabs; Micron announced ~$250bn US investment / ~90k jobs. Protagonist position: **ahead** — 51% HBM share per the note (secondary sources cite 58–63% by revenue for 2025, ~50% projected 2026E as Micron/Samsung close on HBM4). Moat (analysis): intangibles (HBM process/packaging lead) + switching costs (multi-year Nvidia qualification) + scale — durable but narrowing.

**Comps & multiples.** Public peers, forward P/E (secondary press citations, as of 2026):
- SK Hynix — ~6.2x forward P/E; mkt cap ~$1.03T (note) rising to ~$1.35T (later secondary). IPO: 177.9m ADRs at $149 → 177.9m × $149 ≈ **$26.5bn** (arithmetic checks out vs the note); topped Alibaba's 2014 $25bn; ADR opened +13–14%; ADRs priced so US retail buys at ~1/10 the Seoul share price; book reportedly >7x oversubscribed.
- Micron — ~7.0x forward P/E; mkt cap just above $1T.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Memory-cycle cyclicality.** TechInsights and others flag a possible 2027 downturn; every prior memory supercycle rolled into a glut within ~1–2 years as high margins pull forward capex and bit-supply. A ~6x P/E on peak earnings is the market pre-discounting this — a demand air-pocket would compress EPS and the multiple at once (double hit), and the record IPO is being struck near the cycle peak.
2. **Customer / AI-demand concentration.** Nvidia was ~25–27% of 2025 revenue (secondary); a single AI buyer's roadmap slip or in-housing (custom accelerators) would hit HBM volumes fast. Partial mitigant: a new >10% hyperscaler customer emerged in 2026 — first sign of diversification, but concentration remains the key fragility.
3. **Geopolitics / fab-relocation drag.** US pressure (Lutnick) to build US fabs plus >$550bn Korea pledges means heavy multi-year capex into a potential down-cycle; North-Korea / US-Korea trade risk underpins the persistent Korea Discount. US fabs raise cost-per-bit and could dilute returns if demand normalizes — IPO proceeds themselves fund Korean fab + packaging + EUV, so the raise is a bet the supercycle outlasts the build.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The largest-ever foreign US IPO reframes HBM leaders as core AI-infrastructure equities rather than cyclical memory names — a potential re-rating trigger for the whole memory complex (watch Micron/Samsung multiples pulling up in sympathy) and a proof-point that regulated ADR/direct-listing, not tokenization, is the rail for hard-to-reach large Asian issuers. Falsifiable thesis: "AI-memory = secular, not cyclical" breaks if DRAM/HBM ASPs roll over in 2026-2027 or Nvidia HBM4 orders soften — triggers to watch: Micron's next earnings guide and SK Hynix HBM4 allocation commentary. If supply stays sold-out through 2027, the ~6x P/E is a value signal; if a glut appears, the ADR's +13% debut pop marks the top.
