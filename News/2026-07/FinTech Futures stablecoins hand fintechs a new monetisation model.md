## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
No event, no numbers — an opinion piece. The one load-bearing, non-obvious claim is regulatory: stablecoin **issuers are barred from paying interest/yield to holders**, so the reserve-float yield must be captured elsewhere in the stack. Real: GENIUS Act §4(a)(11) (signed 2025-07-18) prohibits a permitted issuer from paying "any form of interest or yield… solely in connection with holding" the coin; EU MiCA Art. 22(4) is stricter. → Why it matters: the ban doesn't destroy the ~$300B float's yield (~$10-11B/yr gross at ~3.63% effective fed-funds) — it **relocates** it to whoever owns distribution. That relocation IS the "monetisation model" the piece sells; the vagueness ("ask the right questions") is the tell that it stops before the hard part — who *durably* keeps the margin.

## [1] Competitors / peers
The bundled models are captured by different players: issuer float (proven — Tether >$10B net profit 2025; Circle ~$2.64B reserve income FY2025); distributor rewards, the GENIUS loophole (PayPal funds ~3.7→4% on PYUSD, not issuer Paxos; Coinbase keeps 100% of on-platform USDC reserve income, 50% off — Circle paid Coinbase $907.9M in 2024); embedded rails (Stripe/Bridge $1.1B closed 2025-02-04, [[Cross River launches unified stablecoin payment platform]]). → The issuer earns the yield but cedes most of it to whoever touches the customer; a fintech that merely resells rails captures little — only distribution ownership captures margin.

N/A — no company. ## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Thin. The "who captures the yield" lens is correct but under-delivered: reserve-yield capture is durable only at the top of the stack and is compressing as the Fed cuts (3.63% vs ~5% in 2024); the distributor-rewards route is under active regulatory attack the piece appears to miss — OCC NPRM (2026-02-25) + FDIC (2026-04-10) create a rebuttable presumption that coordinated issuer↔affiliate yield is itself prohibited. Traction check: paying yield ≠ winning users (PYUSD cap below its 2024 peak despite the reward). → Only the distribution-owning party captures margin; pure resellers get squeezed.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Adoption momentum real (bank entrants — SoFi's sofiUSD, JPMorgan JPMD deposit token on Base [[JPMorgan launches JPMD deposit token on Base]]), but two structural drivers cut against the thesis: falling rates shrink float economics; regulatory closure of affiliate-paid yield (US) and stricter MiCA (EU CASP transition ended 2026-07-01). Counterintuitive: tighter yield rules could *strengthen* the biggest incumbents (direct float monetisers) and squeeze the mid-tier fintechs the piece addresses.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Total stablecoin supply ~$316bn as of 2026-06-12 (per DefiLlama), up from $308bn end-2025 and ~12x since end-2020 ($27bn) — a large, still-fast market but with visibly decelerating supply growth (+2.5% H1'26). Highly consolidated: USDT ~$187bn (~59%) and USDC ~$75bn (~24%) hold ~83% of supply (as of 2026-06-12, per DefiLlama). Long-run TAM claims are wide and analyst-sourced — Citi base $1.9tn / bull $4tn by 2030; Standard Chartered ~$2tn by end-2028 (via press) — treat as scenarios, not facts. Value in the stack is created almost entirely at the reserve layer: issuers earn ~4–5% on T-bill-backed reserves and pay holders nothing. "Why now": the US GENIUS Act (S.1582) legally bars payment-stablecoin issuers from paying interest/yield/rewards to holders, and the OCC's 2026 proposed rules would extend the ban to affiliates and third parties (via Perkins Coie / American Banker). This is the structural gift the FinTech Futures piece describes: fintechs cannot pay depositors on stablecoin balances, so 100% of the reserve carry accrues up the distribution chain to whoever holds the issuer/distribution relationship.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: circulating supply (float), reserve yield capture (share of the ~4–5% carry a fintech keeps), and transaction/adjusted volume. Key players & basis of competition: (i) pure issuers — Tether (scale/offshore distribution), Circle/USDC (regulated, US-listed; note USDC overtook USDT on 2025 adjusted volume, $18.3tn vs $13.3tn, per press); (ii) fintech-affiliated issuers — PayPal PYUSD, whose cap more than quintupled to ~$4.1bn over the past year and reached 70 markets in Mar'26 (via Fortune/AlexaBlockchain); (iii) new entrant — a bank consortium "Open USD / OpenUSD" shared model directly targeting fintech-affiliated coins (via American Banker). Basis of competition: distribution/network, regulatory license, and payments-stack integration — not price (holders get zero either way). The article's protagonist is the generic fintech, not one company; its position is a fast-follower — it can monetise float but does not control an issuer, so it competes on distribution, not economics.

**Comps & multiples.** Circle (CRCL) is the cleanest public reference for reserve-yield economics: FY2025 revenue & reserve income $2.75bn (+64% YoY), of which reserve income $2.637bn and other revenue only $110mn — i.e. ~96% of revenue is float carry (via cryptobriefing). Critically, distribution cost is enormous: Coinbase takes 100% of reserve income on USDC held on its platform and 50% elsewhere; in 2024 $908mn of $1.01bn total distribution cost went to Coinbase (~54c per revenue dollar; via Decrypt). Trading multiple: per multiples.vc (May'26) mkt cap ~$28bn, EV ~$27bn, LTM revenue ~$2.86–3.0bn → EV/Revenue ≈ $27bn / $2.86bn ≈ 9.4x, EV/EBITDA ~44x; note market-cap sources diverge widely ($17bn stockanalysis vs $28bn multiples.vc, Jul'26) so treat the absolute level as `[UNSOURCED]` and the ~9x EV/Rev as indicative. Rich vs a payments peer set (payment processors ~3–6x EV/Rev) but the entire revenue base is rate-sensitive carry, so the premium is not clearly growth-justified. Internal comps: [[This Week in Fintech Coinbase CFO on USDC and stablecoin revenue]], [[Circle wins final OCC approval for national trust bank]], [[Circle's USDC outpaces USDT growth for second year]], [[Stripe-backed Tempo launches stablecoin transaction framework]].

**Risk flags.**
- Rate compression: the whole model is reserve carry on ~4–5% T-bill yields (Circle ~96% of revenue). A Fed cutting cycle compresses issuer/fintech economics dollar-for-dollar — a fintech "monetising balances" is effectively taking duration/rates risk it doesn't hedge. Why: revenue falls without any change in usage.
- Regulatory clamp on the loophole: GENIUS + OCC extension to affiliates/third parties targets exactly the "reward"/yield structures fintechs would use to retain balances; if the ban tightens, the retention mechanic (pass some yield back) disappears while banks keep deposit tools. Why: asymmetric competitive disadvantage vs banks.
- Distribution-economics capture: the Circle/Coinbase split (~54c/$) shows the party that owns the issuer relationship, not the balance-holder, keeps the carry. A fintech without its own issuer risks being the "Coinbase-side" only if it controls distribution; otherwise the issuer keeps the float. Why: margin captured a layer away.
- Disintermediation by bank consortium (Open USD) + PayPal proprietary rails: incumbents entering pressures every fintech-affiliated coin on both trust and distribution (via American Banker). Why: float, and thus carry, migrates to regulated/branded issuers.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The reframing is that stablecoin balances become the next interchange — a structural margin pool now that interchange has a ceiling — but ownership of that pool depends on controlling the issuer/distribution relationship, not just holding customer float. Falsifiable thesis: fintechs without a captive issuer will see the reserve carry accrue to issuers/distributors, not to them. Trigger to watch: the Circle–Coinbase agreement renewal window (Aug 2026) and OCC final rules on affiliate yield — either could reprice who keeps the carry. Thesis is wrong if fintechs win a legal path to share yield (reward structures survive OCC scrutiny), restoring balance-retention economics.
