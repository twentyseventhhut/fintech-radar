## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
**[0] TL;DR.** On 2026-07-06 the FCA published the **Mills Review** — a landmark, first-of-its-kind (globally, by a regulator) long-term review, led by FCA executive director **Sheldon Mills** and commissioned by the Board in **January 2026** — on how AI could reshape UK **retail** financial services by **2030 and beyond**. It identifies four AI-driven shifts and makes **seven advisory recommendations** to the FCA Board. Crucially it recommends **keeping the principles-based, outcomes-focused framework** (Consumer Duty + Senior Managers Regime) and writing **no AI-specific rules** — a deliberate contrast with the EU AI Act. The digest headline ("FCA publishes new review into how AI could reshape retail financial services") is accurate.

**[1] What's actually new.** The four shifts: (1) transformation of firm operations; (2) evolution of consumer journeys; (3) reshaping of competition and market power (concentration/reliance on a few AI/cloud providers); (4) amplification of fraud and cyber risks. The seven recommendations: (1) secure and adapt the regulatory perimeter; (2) strengthen system-wide coordination/oversight; (3) monitor the transition to autonomous ("agentic") models; (4) scale up the FCA **AI Lab**; (5) enable the foundations for **agentic finance**; (6) build an AI-enabled supervisory model; (7) develop a free, public-interest **AI financial-capability service** for consumers. Key figure: research commissioned for the review found ~**one-fifth of UK adults (~11 million people)** would likely use AI that acts autonomously within pre-set goals, though with trust/oversight concerns. Recommendations are **advisory to the Board, not binding rules**.

**[2] Internal context (this vault).** This slots into a well-documented FCA AI-regulation arc already in the vault:
- [[NatWest, Monzo, Santander among first in FCA AI live testing scheme]] (2025-12) — first AI Live Testing cohort with technical partner Advai.
- [[FCA names eight new participants for AI Live Testing]] (2026-04) — second cohort (Barclays, Experian, Lloyds/Scottish Widows, UBS, GoCardless, Coadjute, Aereve, Palindrome); note explicitly states the FCA "would avoid additional regulations for AI by relying on existing frameworks" and that "In January, the FCA launched a review led by Sheldon Mills" — i.e. **this note IS the output of that pre-announced review.**
- [[The Treasury Select Committee warned of a failure to tackle AI risks]] (2026-01) — political pressure on FCA/BoE to act on AI risk; the Mills Review is partly the response.
- Adjacent regulatory-perimeter moves: [[ESG ratings to fall within FCA regulatory perimeter]] (2026-01), [[FCA completes crypto roadmap with new rules]] (2026-07). The "secure and adapt the regulatory perimeter" recommendation echoes the FCA's broader perimeter-expansion pattern.

**[3] External context / market.** The report is formally titled "AI and the future of retail financial services" (the Mills Review), a corporate document rather than a rulebook. It builds on the **AI Lab**, **AI Live Testing** (technical partner Advai; second-cohort testing concludes end-2026, evaluation report Q1 2027) and the **Supercharged Sandbox** (NVIDIA-supported). On **superscalers/hyperscalers**: the FCA frames concentration risk via the **Critical Third Parties (CTP) Regime**, with commentary pushing to designate critical AI/cloud vendors so regulators can oversee them directly rather than only the banks using them. On **Consumer Duty**: the FCA's stance (reaffirmed since its Sept-2025 Feedback Statement FS25/5) is that the Consumer Duty and SMCR are flexible enough to govern AI outcomes without bespoke AI rules. **Contrast with the EU AI Act (Reg. 2024/1689):** the EU takes a prescriptive, legally-binding, risk-tiered model with fines up to 6-7% of global turnover and detailed high-risk obligations; the UK/FCA takes a lighter-touch, outcomes-based, "designing for the unknown" approach using existing regulators and principles — no new statutory AI obligations. Practitioner commentary (law firms, PwC, Aveni) frames the review as the FCA "waking up" to agentic AI while resisting a rulebook.

**[4] Why it matters / open questions.** This is the UK's clearest signal that it will **not** replicate the EU AI Act inside financial services, betting that Consumer Duty + SMCR + live testing can absorb agentic AI. Live tensions: (a) recommendations are non-binding — adoption/timing by the Board is unconfirmed; (b) "secure the perimeter" hints at wanting **expanded FCA powers** over AI/critical-third-party vendors, which needs government/legislation; (c) principles-based supervision is hard to operationalise for opaque autonomous models — the promised good/poor-practice report (due later 2026) and the Q1-2027 Live Testing evaluation are the real tests; (d) the "public-interest AI financial-capability service" is novel and unspecified (cost, delivery, who builds it). ## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The pick is a regulatory publication, not a deal — the "sector" here is AI-in-finance regulation + the regtech/compliance-tooling market it shapes. The FCA's review is the **Mills Review** (led by exec director Sheldon Mills, commissioned by the FCA Board, published 6 July 2026), billed by the FCA as "the first work of its kind initiated by a regulator globally." It projects how AI reshapes retail financial services by 2030+ across four shifts: firm operations, consumer journeys, competition/market power, and amplification of fraud/cyber risk (per FCA press release, 6 Jul 2026). Notably it does NOT propose new AI rules — it reaffirms that accountability doesn't shift to the machine (Senior Managers Regime + Consumer Duty still apply) and offers seven recommendations to the Board. This is consistent with the FCA's 2 June 2026 confirmation that it "will not introduce new rules for AI" (per Global Policy Watch, Apr 2026). Sizing the addressable regtech layer: global regtech market estimates for 2026 range ~$20–29bn depending on the firm (Straits/Grand View/Precedence, via search, 2026), with the AI-in-regtech slice ~$3.5bn in 2026 growing at ~36.7% CAGR (per The Business Research Company, via search). Consumer-demand data point the FCA itself commissioned: ~11 million UK adults (a fifth) are likely to use autonomous "agentic" AI within pre-set financial goals. **Why now:** the UK's principles-based, pro-innovation stance (existing regulators + five non-statutory principles) is diverging sharply from the EU AI Act's prescriptive, horizontal, risk-tier model with heavy penalties — the review is the UK regulator planting a flag on "designing for the unknown" rather than legislating hard rules, just as agentic AI moves from pilot to production.

**Competitive landscape.** Not a company competition — the relevant KPIs are for the regtech vendors and incumbents who monetize compliance: ARR/NRR for SaaS regtechs, and (unmeasured publicly) time-to-remediation / false-positive rates for AI compliance tools `[UNSOURCED]`. Regulatory "players": the FCA is positioning the UK as the light-touch, innovation-first jurisdiction (AI Lab, Supercharged Sandbox 2nd cohort testing agentic payment/compliance/service agents through 2026, evaluation report Q1 2027) against the EU (AI Act, comprehensive) and the US (fragmented/deregulatory). Recent moves in the corpus: [[Napier AI selected for FCA Supercharged Sandbox]] (Nov 2025), [[Singapore and UK launch AI in finance partnership]] (Nov 2025), and MAS's parallel path [[MAS proposes AI risk management guidelines for financial firms]] (Nov 2025). The protagonist (FCA) is **ahead** on visible thought-leadership / sandbox tooling but is deliberately NOT building a rulebook moat — its "moat" is regulatory soft power and the UK's attractiveness as an AI-finance testbed (analysis).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Divergence / dual-compliance cost.** UK principles-based vs EU AI Act prescriptive vs US fragmented means cross-border retail-finance firms carry parallel AI governance regimes — the same disintermediation-by-regulation risk that stablecoin firms face on MiCA (cf. corpus warnings on EU dual-licensing). Second-order: raises the compliance-cost floor and, ironically, expands the regtech TAM the FCA is trying to keep light.
2. **Accountability gap in agentic AI.** The review insists SM&CR accountability doesn't shift, but with ~11m UK adults ready to delegate to autonomous agents, the liability chain for an agent's mis-selling / erroneous action is untested. If enforcement lags, mis-conduct scales faster than remediation.
3. **Soft-law execution risk.** "No new rules" leaves interpretation to firms and future enforcement — regulatory ambiguity can chill investment (firms wait for clarity) OR invite regulatory-arbitrage, either of which undercuts the pro-innovation goal.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The Mills Review signals the UK is betting on soft-law + sandboxes to win AI-finance capital while the EU legislates — a falsifiable thesis: if the UK approach works, expect regtech/AI-compliance vendors and agentic-finance startups to cluster in London and the Supercharged Sandbox pipeline to convert to production deployments (watch the Q1 2027 AI Live Testing evaluation report). What breaks the thesis: a high-profile agentic-AI consumer-harm incident that forces the FCA to reverse into prescriptive rules, converging toward the EU model. Trigger to watch: the FCA Board's response to the seven recommendations, and whether any hardens into rulemaking.
