# Viva.com wins EU contract to build CBAM payments platform

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **13 July 2026**, Viva.com and European Dynamics announced they were selected by the European Commission's **DG TAXUD** to build, operate and maintain the **Common Central Platform (CCP)** — the payment rail through which **CBAM certificates** (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism) will be sold, repurchased and reconciled across all 27 Member States. Division of labour per PR: **European Dynamics** = platform architecture, development, integration with EU customs/tax systems; **Viva.com** = the regulated payments layer (bank accounts for DG TAXUD + Member States, SEPA connectivity, execution of A2A payments).

De-PR'd corrections:
- The tender is **real and well-defined** (not loose framing): DG TAXUD's CBAM **CCP**, backed by a Joint Procurement Agreement signed by all 27 states on 15 Dec 2025; OJ call published 11 Feb 2026; bids closed 6 Apr 2026. Milestones: testable build by 31 Aug 2026, **go-live 1 Feb 2027**.
- This is the **certificate sales/clearing rail**, NOT the CBAM Registry (declarant portal) — those are separate systems. **→ why it matters:** the value here is settlement/banking plumbing, not the customer-facing compliance system.
- **Timeline caveat that reframes urgency:** CBAM's definitive regime began 1 Jan 2026 (obligations/reporting), but certificate **sales don't begin until Feb 2027**. So this is a "won the build" story, not a live revenue system — the actual payment flows are ~7 months out (analysis).
- **Contract value, exact award date, and duration are UNDISCLOSED.** No TED formal award notice was located. Treat "won/selected" as **PR-asserted by the two parties**; the formal award is unconfirmed/open. Announcement is credible (both parties + Greek press ot.gr), but there is no independent Commission confirmation of a signed contract.

## [1] Competitors / peers
The consortium structure (IT integrator + licensed EMI/acquirer) is the logical answer to a tender needing both customs-integration muscle and a payment licence. The field splits two ways:
- **EU IT integrators** (the ED role): Atos, Sopra Steria, Indra, NTT Data/Everis, Accenture, Capgemini, Intrasoft/Netcompany — the usual TAXUD/customs-systems incumbents.
- **Regulated payments providers** (the Viva role): Worldline, Nexi, Adyen, Stripe, Nuvei, PayU, plus banks. Worldline is the EU's go-to institutional-infra name (selected among ECB **digital euro** providers). **→ second-order:** Viva winning the SEPA/A2A banking piece is the genuinely novel element — a fintech acquirer stepping into core EU public-finance rails, territory normally held by legacy processors/banks. No named losing bidders are public.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Viva.com** (ex-Viva Wallet; CEO Haris Karonis): Greek cloud acquirer/EMI; Tap-to-Pay on iPhone/Android, banking/financing. Now **29 European countries** (the "~24" figure is stale). JPMorgan bought **48.5% for ~$800M in 2022**; majority (51.5%) held by WeRealize. A €5bn-by-June-2025 valuation trigger let JPMorgan seek full control; valuations clashed (EY ~€3bn vs Houlihan Lokey ~€1bn). Litigation escalated; a London court granted an **anti-suit injunction (Aug 2025)** restraining JPMorgan's Greek proceedings — see [[UK courts block JPMorgan's legal action against Viva Wallet]]. **→ why this win matters:** first EU-institutional / central-government mandate found for Viva — a credibility marker independent of the JPMorgan drama, potentially useful in Viva's own valuation narrative. Fits its "tech bank for businesses" + pan-EU expansion arc (cf. [[Viva.com and BlueStar EMEA partner on European commerce]], [[Viva.com launches Tap to Pay on iPhone in Spain]]).
- **European Dynamics**: Greek/Luxembourg ICT vendor; long EU-institutional track record (Parliament, Court of Auditors, EMA, EUIPO, Europol, Publications Office). Also historically a prolific **tender litigant**. No prior Viva partnership found — this appears a first pairing. For its role here (routing EU cross-border payments) cf. the pan-EU payments push in [[Poland's BLIK joins pan-European cashless payments push]].

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real novelty:** Viva into core EU public-finance settlement rails — symbolically significant. ED's role is business-as-usual (another TAXUD-adjacent build).
- **Traction = zero yet.** This is an award/announcement; go-live is Feb 2027. No transaction volumes, no revenue, no disclosed value. **→ who captures margin:** the CCP serves only CBAM declarants (thousands of importers, not consumer scale), so volumes are inherently limited; financial upside is modest and undisclosed. High *strategic/symbolic* value, uncertain *financial* value (analysis).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Watch for a **formal TED contract-award notice** (would confirm winner, value, duration) — not yet located.
- **31 Aug 2026:** testable pre-operational CCP. **1 Feb 2027:** go-live + start of CBAM certificate sales — the real proof point.
- **Risk:** procurement challenges by losing bidders (ED itself is a serial challenger) could delay. Tight build timeline. Viva's unresolved JPMorgan governance overhang.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This sits at the intersection of European acquiring/payments and govtech (regulated public-sector money movement), not a commercial-acquiring win. Europe payment-processing market ~$22.5bn in 2026 (per marketdataforecast, as of 2026); top-5 processors hold ~55% of value, leaving room for verticalized/niche players (per popularfintech "European merchant acquiring landscape", 2026). The relevant demand driver is regulatory, not consumer: EU CBAM entered its definitive phase 1 Jan 2026 (cement, steel, aluminium, fertiliser, electricity, hydrogen); the central payments platform goes live 1 Feb 2027 when certificate sales begin, priced off the EU-ETS quarterly average (Q1 2026 official price €75.36/tCO2, per European Commission via integritynext). "Why now": CBAM certificate revenue could exceed ~$80bn/yr by 2040 (per S&P Global Sustainable1, spglobal.com) — a new, mandatory, pan-EU payment flow across all 27 member states that did not exist before and needs regulated A2A/SEPA rails. This is regulation *creating* a payments rail, the strongest possible "why now."

**Competitive landscape.** Acquiring KPIs: TPV/processed volume, take rate, per-transaction margin, merchant count. Incumbents: Worldline (Europe's largest merchant acquirer by revenue), Nexi (largest by merchants — 2.9m), Adyen (2nd by volume, ~$523bn European processing), plus Worldpay/Global Payments/Fiserv (per TSG 2024 directory). But this contract is not won on acquiring KPIs — it is a DG TAXUD tender where the bid is a consortium: European Dynamics (customs-IT systems integrator; the same firm has held multi-year DG TAXUD/EU IT frameworks, e.g. a prior €96m 5-yr consortium contract, per eurodyn.com) supplies domain/IT, while Viva.com supplies the regulated payments layer — bank accounts for DG TAXUD and member states, SEPA connectivity and incoming/outgoing A2A execution (per financialit.net / athens-times, 2026-07). Position: Viva is a niche entrant in govtech payments here, not a challenger to Adyen/Nexi/Worldline on commercial acquiring. Its edge is a full EU e-money/banking licence footprint (23 countries) + JPMorgan backing — credibility for handling sovereign flows `(analysis)`. Recent peer moves: Adyen agreed to acquire Talon.One for €750m (2026-05, [[Adyen agrees to acquire loyalty platform Talon.One for €750 million]]); Nexi under a potential ~€9bn CVC take-private (Apr 2026, per gurufocus); Worldline mid-turnaround with a €500m raise ([[Worldline plans €500M capital raise in turnaround]]).

**Comps & multiples.** Public acquiring peers (external):
- Adyen — 2025 net revenue €2,364.2m (+18%, +21% cc); EBITDA €1,245.7m, 53% margin; ~$30bn mcap → EV/Revenue ≈ $30bn / ~$3bn ≈ 6.0x; EV/EBITDA ≈ 11.2x (per multiples.vc / adyen.com, as of Jun 2026). Growth-justified, in-line for a scaled profitable name.
- Nexi — ~$5.0bn mcap, ~$7.08bn TTM revenue → EV/Revenue ~sub-1x on mcap alone (heavily levered; enterprise value higher); CVC take-private mooted at ~€9bn (per gurufocus, Apr 2026). Cheap/distressed vs Adyen.
- Worldline — turnaround/dilution, €500m raise, SIX taking ~CHF-550m accounting hit on its stake — a value-destruction comp, not a valuation anchor ([[Worldline plans €500M capital raise in turnaround]]).
- Mollie (private) — 2025 net revenue €147m, +29% ([[Mollie posts 29% net revenue growth to €147 million]]) — closest SME-acquiring growth comp to Viva.
Internal Viva comps: [[Viva.com and BlueStar EMEA partner on European commerce]], [[Viva.com launches Tap to Pay on iPhone in Spain]], [[Viva.com extends BLIK tap-to-pay across Poland]], [[UK courts block JPMorgan's legal action against Viva Wallet]]. Viva's own multiple = no data (private): last priced at >$2bn on JPMorgan's Nov-2022 $1.15bn-for-48.5% deal; a €5bn July-2025 valuation trigger governs JPMorgan's call option (per sifted, 2025-07). Peer distribution not computed — comparability weak (public scaled acquirers vs private SME neobank vs a one-off govtech contract); qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Single-client concentration / one-off nature.** Revenue is one government mandate (DG TAXUD), not recurring merchant flow. Contract is two-phase with go-live only 1 Feb 2027 — "announced/awarded," not yet live or generating fees. Slippage in CBAM implementation (repeatedly softened/simplified by the Commission) directly hits the economics; second-order: no disclosed contract value = unquantifiable to Viva's P&L `[UNSOURCED]`.
2. **Shareholder overhang / governance.** The unresolved JPMorgan–WRL dispute (€917m damages claim, €5bn call-option litigation still in court per sifted) is a live distraction and control risk at the top of the cap table; a sovereign-grade counterparty (EU) awarding work to a company whose ownership is under litigation is itself a fragility.
3. **Execution / operational risk on regulated sovereign flows.** Handling A2A/SEPA settlement of mandatory levies for 27 states carries near-zero tolerance for outage or misdirected funds; reputational and penalty exposure is asymmetric to the (likely modest) fee. Prime contractor is European Dynamics — Viva is the sub on payments, so it carries operational liability without controlling delivery.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Signals an emerging "govtech payments" niche — regulation minting new mandatory flows (CBAM, and by extension future EU digital-levy / customs rails) that go to consortia pairing a customs-IT integrator with a licensed EMI, not to the big commercial acquirers `(analysis)`. Thesis: this is a reputational/reference win for Viva (sovereign validation), not a financial re-rating — watch for (a) disclosed contract value or renewal, and (b) whether it becomes a repeatable template Viva wins elsewhere in EU public infrastructure. Falsifier: if the CBAM platform is delayed past Feb-2027 or Viva's JPMorgan control dispute forces a change of control, the "reference win" thesis breaks.
