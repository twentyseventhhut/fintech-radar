# Context-enrichment: Revolut launches Hungarian bank accounts
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**FRESHNESS VERDICT: STALE / DUPLICATE.** This is a re-report of the SAME Hungarian local-accounts / HU-IBAN rollout already covered in [[Revolut launches Hungarian IBANs for customers]] (2026-05-20, story sa1c7294f), which itself followed the branch plan in [[Revolut plans to open a branch in Hungary]] (2025-08-12). Web sources confirm the rollout **began in May 2026** and the migration of the ~2.3M Hungarian base **continued through July 2026** — this July note captures a later stage of the same migration, plus a 2025-traction stat, not a new event. Mark `duplicate_of: [[Revolut launches Hungarian IBANs for customers]]`.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
De-PR'd: Revolut's Hungarian branch (Revolut Bank UAB, Hungarian branch) rolled local Hungarian IBANs / HU bank-account numbers to its Hungarian retail base. By early July 2026 trade press reported >2 million Hungarian customers had been migrated to local accounts (Trademagazin; the base is ~2.3M). The digest text carries no new mechanism, date, or product detail — it recycles the same "local presence" framing and pads it with a growth stat (nearly 500k customers added in Hungary in 2025; MAU +40%, salary-account holders +30%).
**+ Why framed this way:** The PR anchors to a flattering 2025 growth base and to a "milestone" word to make a mid-rollout status update read like a launch. The actual capability (local HU IBAN) was already announced May 2026; the branch regulatory approvals (MNB register, registration dated 23-Oct-2025) predate that. So the "launch" is a migration-progress marker, not day-one.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Hungarian retail banking is dominated by **OTP Bank** (incumbent, traditional bank with a modern app) — the true share to defend. Digital challengers: **Wise** (multi-currency, cheap FX, but foreign-IBAN routing via partner banks), **Gránit Bank** (local digital), and smaller neobanks. Revolut's local HU IBAN closes the single biggest gap vs OTP: salary/direct-debit/utility acceptance that foreign (Lithuanian) IBANs frictioned. Wise still routes via partner banks (per-transfer caps/delay); Revolut now has a native local rail.
**+ Second-order:** The local IBAN is table stakes to move from a "travel/FX card" to a primary account. It matters because Hungary's SZÉP/utility/employer plumbing keys off domestic account numbers. But local IBAN is a commodity capability — OTP already has it, Wise/Gránit are converging — so this narrows a disadvantage rather than opening a moat.

## [2] Company history / fit
Trajectory: branch plan (Aug 2025) → MNB approvals / branch registration (Oct 2025) → HU IBAN launch announced (May 2026) → mass migration through mid-2026 (>2M moved by Jul 2026). Fits Revolut's broader CEE/EU localisation drive: local IBANs already live in Portugal (Jul 2025 deposits), plus Barcelona store + Paris HQ + French licence pursuit, alongside the full UK licence secured Mar 2026.
**+ Why:** Revolut operates most of the EU under the single Lithuanian banking licence (Revolut Bank UAB) and passports/branches into markets rather than acquiring local licences. Structural logic: reuse one licence, add local IBANs + local branches to capture primary-banking relationships (salary, deposits) without the cost/time of a national licence in each country.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Novelty is LOW — the capability was announced two months earlier. What is genuinely new here is TRACTION: >2M Hungarian customers actually migrated to local accounts by July 2026 (vs the May "will roll out" announcement). That is a real "announced → adopted" data point.
**+ Deeper:** Value-add is real for user stickiness (primary-account = deposits, salary inflow), but who captures margin? Deposits sit under Revolut Bank UAB and are covered by the **Lithuanian** DGS up to €100k — NOT a Hungarian scheme — so the balance-sheet economics stay Lithuanian; only the account identifier is local. The multiple case rests on converting travel/FX users into salary/deposit primary accounts; it breaks if OTP retains payroll relationships or if users treat the HU IBAN as a secondary account.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Next: complete migration of the ~2.3M base, expand HU-language support/team, add instant payments, payment requests, QR payments (per Revolut help/branch comms). Regulatory backdrop: passported under Lithuanian licence; MNB oversight of the branch. Risk: a separate report ("Revolut introduces major upgrade, but may become pricier") signals pricing pressure that could dent the primary-account conversion thesis.
**+ Why:** The structural driver is deposit-gathering: local IBANs unlock salary inflows → cheap funding. Counterintuitive second-order: because deposit protection remains Lithuanian, a domestic-trust question persists — Hungarian users get a local number but not a local safety net, which incumbents like OTP can exploit in messaging.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Europe neobanking is a large, fast-growing pool: valued ~$13.3bn in 2025, est. ~$19.7bn in 2026, ~14% CAGR to 2031 (per Mordor Intelligence / MarketDataForecast, via web search, as of 2025). Revolut alone reports ~10m customers in CEE and 40m across Europe incl. UK (per Statista/market reports, via web search) — so its Hungary push is a densification play inside an already-won market, not a greenfield entry. Structure of CEE retail banking is concentrated around domestic incumbents (in Hungary, OTP holds >25% share, 17.5m clients, ~1,100 branches — per Wikipedia/OTP, via web search) but rapidly disrupted at the retail-current-account layer. Why now: Revolut added ~500k Hungarian customers in 2025 (note text); OTP's own then-CEO Csányi stated ~30% of domestic customers already use Revolut (via web search, 2025 AGM). The local-HU-IBAN + Hungarian branch (Revolut Bank UAB Hungarian Branch) is the localisation step that converts a foreign-account "second card" into a primary salary/direct-debit account — the friction that previously kept a foreign LT-IBAN off utility and payroll rails.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for retail neobanks: customer count, monthly active users (MAU), salary/primary-account share, customer balances (deposit funding). Note reports Hungary MAU +40% and salary-account holders +30% in 2025 — primary-account traction is the metric that matters here. Key players in Hungary: OTP (incumbent, >25% share), GRÁNIT (local digital-first, MNB-regulated, HUF-IBAN, generous OBA deposit insurance ~EUR 255k), Wise, and Revolut (dominant challenger by user count). Basis of competition: distribution/brand + fee structure (Revolut avoids Hungarian domestic transaction fees and bank taxes that hit OTP — a structural cost advantage, via web search) rather than price of a single product. Recent moves with dates: branch launch announced 2026-03-17; HU-IBAN rollout began ~May 2026 (internal note [[Revolut launches Hungarian IBANs for customers]], 2026-05-20); Revolut confirmed migration of >2m eligible Hungarian retail customers to the local branch (via web search, 2026). Position: **ahead** — dominant challenger with primary-account momentum; moat is scale + brand/network effects among younger urban users, but it operates via a Lithuanian licence/branch, not a full Hungarian bank (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** No deal/valuation attaches to this specific news, so multiples are qualitative. Revolut group valuation ~$75bn (secondary share sale, Nov 2025 — round valuation, not market cap; per Sifted/TechCrunch, via web search), up ~60% from ~$45bn a year prior. Group 2025 results (see IR below) put this at ~$75bn / $6.0bn revenue = **~12.5x P/S (round)** — rich vs listed peer Wise at ~$12.25bn market cap on a much lower multiple (per companiesmarketcap, as of Dec 2025), but Revolut grew revenue +46% and PBT +57%, so the premium tracks growth (comps-analysis growth-multiple logic). Divergent private peer N26 has re-rated down toward ~$3bn from a ~$9bn 2021 peak (via web search) — illustrating that neobank multiples are earned by profitable scale, which Revolut has and N26 lost. Internal comps for the localisation/local-IBAN market-entry pattern: [[Revolut launches Hungarian IBANs for customers]], [[3S Money launches French IBANs and Paris office]], [[Greek neobank Snappi goes live]], [[Revolut says French growth not dependent on banking licence]]. `[UNSOURCED]`: Hungary-segment revenue/deposits/unit economics — not disclosed.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Deposit-guarantee mismatch.** HU customers get local IBANs and a Hungarian branch, but deposits remain covered by the **Lithuanian** DGF up to €100k (via web search), not Hungary's OBA — weaker perceived protection than local rivals (GRÁNIT's OBA ~EUR 255k). Second-order: if a Hungarian retail-protection or "local-licence" narrative gains political traction (as bank-tax friction already has), it becomes a marketing/regulatory wedge for incumbents.
2. **Regulatory/fiscal target.** Revolut's cost edge rests on sitting outside Hungarian bank taxes/transaction fees; with ~30% of the population already users and >2m migrated, it is now large enough to attract levelling regulation or a new tax perimeter — the same advantage that drives growth invites the risk.
3. **Deepening but not diversifying.** This is intensification in an already-dominant market; incremental users are marginal, and reliance on non-interest demand deposits under a foreign licence means funding/product depth (mortgages, local credit) still lags true local banks (analysis).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Marginal for the group's story but a clear signal for Hungarian retail banking: the challenger is crossing from "supplementary card" to **primary account**, directly threatening OTP's payroll/deposit base — the highest-value banking relationship (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if HU salary-account share keeps compounding (+30% in 2025), expect OTP/local banks to respond on fees or push a "local deposit guarantee" message within 12 months; trigger to watch — any move by Revolut to secure a Hungarian (MNB) licence or local guarantee, or an incumbent fee cut. Thesis breaks if migration stalls or a Hungary-specific tax neutralises the cost advantage.

The Hungary launch is a live execution step on the "become a real local bank across Europe" thesis, backed by an accelerating-scale, still-profitable Group.

**Key figures (Group FY2025, from the consolidated accounts).**
- Revenue **£4,515,770k, +46.1% YoY** (2024: £3,090,043k). Mix: fee income £3,429,229k (+54.7%), interest income £974,159k (+23.3%), other income £112,382k (+35.4%). Fee income now ~76% of revenue — growth is diversifying away from rate-driven interest income (interest income growth 23% vs fee 55%).
- Gross profit **£3,517,641k, +41.3%** (2024: £2,489,142k); gross margin **78%, down from 81%** — modest compression as cost of sales rose to £998,129k (+66.1%) faster than revenue (scaling/processing/interest-expense costs).

Net profit **£1.3bn** (2024: ~£0.8bn). `(web-confirmed; the group Highlights page OCR'd garbled — "£986B/£589B" is a scanning artifact, the audited/reported figure is PBT £1.7bn vs £1.1bn)`.

Transaction volume ~£1.7T (+57%). 11 product lines each >~£100m revenue.

**By segment / driver (CEE / Hungary backdrop).** The Hungarian branch is booked under **Revolut Bank UAB (RBUAB)**, the Lithuania-licensed EEA bank — the entity that actually holds CEE deposits. RBUAB FY2025 (own filing): KYC'd customer base **52.7M (2024: 38.6M)**, MAU **29.4M** (conversion 55.7%), gross revenues **+57.4% to EUR 2.8bn**. Hungary specifically: ~**2M customers** / ~25% market penetration (July 2025), **~500K net adds in 2025**, MAU **+40%+** and salary-account holders **+30%+** in 2025 (per the news). The launch converts these 2M users to domestic Hungarian IBANs — Hungary joins the local-banking footprint (FR, IE, IT, NL, RO, ES, DE, PT, BE), turning cross-border flows into domestic ones and unlocking deposit/lending economics.

**Guidance / forward.** None formally given. Management commentary points to global expansion and a stated ambition of ~100M customers by mid-2027 (75M+ retail already, ~1M new every ~17 days) `(web, management framing)`. The Hungarian full-branch move (MNB-approved, registered Oct 2025 as "Revolut Bank UAB Magyarországi Fióktelepe") is the local execution of the licence-led strategy — no revenue guidance attached to Hungary specifically [UNSOURCED for any Hungary P&L].

**Thesis-flags.**
1. **Deposit engine is the strategic prize, and this launch feeds it.** Customer balances +66% to £50.2bn → domestic Hungarian IBANs for 2M users let Revolut on-board local salary/deposit flows → cheaper funding + a path into local lending (Revolut already runs mortgages in the EEA bank). Second-order: more balances lift interest income, partly offsetting the rate-cycle drag now visible (interest income +23% vs fee +55%).
2. **Local-bank strategy is de-risking revenue mix.** Fee income (+55%) outpacing interest income (+23%) → less rate-dependence → CEE local-banking build-out (Hungary the latest) is what sustains fee/transaction growth as deposit rates fall. Why it matters: reduces the "neobank margins evaporate when rates drop" bear case.
3. **Gross-margin compression to watch (81%→78%).** Cost of sales +66% vs revenue +46% → becoming a licensed local bank in each country carries regulatory/infrastructure cost. Second-order: geographic licence expansion (Hungary, and 30 licensed markets) trades some margin for durable local deposit access — the model favours scale over margin near-term.
4. **Execution/regulatory dependency.** Each market needs its own regulator sign-off (MNB here); the CEE "one new Hungarian customer per minute" traction only monetises once the local licence + IBAN infrastructure is live — this launch flips Hungary from "announced" to "live," a positive de-PR tick.
