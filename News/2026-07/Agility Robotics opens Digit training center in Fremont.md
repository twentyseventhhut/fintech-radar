Agility Robotics plants its flag in Tesla’s backyard
Agility Robotics is opening a 60,000-square-foot facility to train its humanoid robots in Fremont, California, just up the highway from the factory where Tesla is expected to start manufacturing its Optimus robots this year.
Tesla has increasingly bet on Optimus. Elon Musk recently said he expects it to be “the biggest product ever” once it’s “useful outside of Tesla sometime next year.”
While Agility doesn’t have Tesla’s capital, it does have a robot, Digit, that is already useful in the real world. The robot is already generating revenue, carrying totes and bins in manufacturing and warehouse settings for customers like Amazon, GXO, Schaeffler, and Toyota Motor Manufacturing Canada . The company says it has secured $300 million in contract orders for its robots.
“It’s great to have [Tesla] in the same area as us, because really, for a long time Agility was out there alone, and it’s good to have others in the humanoid space,” CEO Peggy Johnson told TechCrunch. “We have commercialized. We now know what it takes to walk into these facilities and meet their safety bars, their regulatory bars, compliance, plug into their IT infrastructure, plug into their warehouse management system.”
Agility hasn’t disclosed how many Digits that it has built or deployed, but outside observers estimate that dozens have worked in pilot or revenue-generating deployments. The company has said, for example, that Digits have moved 100,000 totes at a GXO logistics facility.
Johnson is currently leading Agility through a reverse-merger that is expected to make it the first pure-play humanoid robot company on the public markets later this year. Founded in 2015 by a group of researchers who developed new techniques that allow robots to safely walk on two legs, Agility is trying to capitalize on its lead over a newer generation of AI-inspired robotic startups like Figure, 1X, the Bot Company, or Sunday Robotics.
While the arrival of transformer-based neural networks that helped give rise to LLMs also promises major advancements in robotic behavior, Agility is taking a practical approach to autonomy.
“When you think about self-driving cars, you know, as a non-humanoid example, you really don’t want the anti-lock brake controller under AI control,” Agility co-founder and chairman Damion Shelton told TechCrunch. “The analog with humanoids is all the safety stuff needs to go through a path that’s not generative AI, right? You don’t want to get creative with your safety stack.”
What AI does do, however, is deliver on the promise of scale. 
“One of the first times [Bruce Leak, the Quicktime inventor who serves on Agility’s board] asked us how we were going to go about coding applications for the robot, we didn’t really have a good answer,” Shelton said. “The number of things you can imagine a robot doing is far larger than the number of engineers who can program robots. And generative AI answers that question definitively.”
The new facility is designed to accelerate the company’s robotic deployments. Johnson says more than 30 customers are in talks with the company about deploying Digit, and the new facility will be where the six-foot-tall robot learns new skills in environments similar to those it will experience in the field.
Unlike many of the newer entrants to the humanoid space, Agility isn’t planning to offer in-home humanoid robots anytime soon. It’s a view that jibes with that of most independent robotics experts, who believe today’s most powerful robots aren’t safe enough for consumer use. Digit operates in a human-free space right now, but the version 5, expected to be unveiled this fall, will have the ability to sense humans and won’t need to be kept in a robot-only zone.
Co-founder and chief robot officer Jonathan Hurst said there is plenty of work to keep Agility busy in manufacturing and logistics alone.
“Let’s start with the bins and the totes, and then let’s do the picking and the kitting,” Hurst told TechCrunch. “And then let’s like start working on cardboard, which is really hard, and loading and unloading tractor trailers and things like that. Okay, now we’re at 100 million robots, you know? A trillion-dollar company.”

# Context-enrichment: Agility Robotics opens Digit training center in Fremont
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Agility Robotics opened a **60,000 sq ft facility in Fremont, CA** (47221 Fremont Blvd) — a **software / "Physical AI" development hub**, NOT a factory. Its RoboFab manufacturing plant stays in Salem, Oregon. The site is where Digit "learns new skills" in field-like environments; Agility says it will hire **~200 people** (AI/ML software eng + field ops). TechCrunch's "plants its flag in Tesla's backyard" is the PR frame — Fremont is where Tesla is converting Model S/X lines to Optimus. The site opening was **16–17 Jul 2026** (press release 16 Jul).
- **Why framed this way (the real story is the SPAC, not the building):** the Fremont opening is a *proof-point drop* timed into a live public-listing process. On **24 Jun 2026** Agility signed a definitive merger with **Churchill Capital Corp XI (Nasdaq: CCXI)** at **~$2.5B implied valuation**, ~$620M gross proceeds (~$420M trust + **$200M PIPE at $10/sh led by Foxconn**), targeting ticker **AGLT**; a confidential Form S-4 draft was submitted **14 Jul 2026**. The "first pure-play public humanoid company" and "we have commercialized" messaging is a **de-risking narrative for public-market investors** — a Silicon Valley AI hub + named blue-chip customers make the SPAC look like operating substance, not a pre-revenue moonshot (analysis).
- The **$300M "contract orders"** is soft: filings/PR call it **multi-year orders for Digit v5, "subject to the realization of certain contractual milestones"** — i.e. contingent bookings, not banked revenue.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Tesla Optimus** — the headline foil. No standalone valuation (division); Tesla converting Fremont to up to 1M Optimus/yr, Giga Texas to 10M at buildout; Musk guided public sales only ~end-2027 ([[Morgan Stanley raises forecast for China humanoid robot market]] cites Musk: no public Optimus sales until end-2027). Optimus leads on **manufacturing-scale ambition**; Agility leads on **live paying deployments today**.
- **Figure AI** — most valuable pure-play at ~**$39B** (OpenAI-led round; Sept-2025 $1B Series C w/ Nvidia, Qualcomm, T-Mobile, Salesforce, Brookfield). Paid Western pilots (BMW). ~15x valuation vs Agility's $2.5B, but Agility argues it has *more documented deployment hours*.
- **Apptronik** ~$5.5B, **1X** ~$10B (shipping $20k Neo home robot), **Unitree** (price/units leader, China). Home-robot entrants: [[Sunday Robotics unveils ACT-2 model for home robot Memo]], [[UK robotics startup Humanoid raises $150M Series A tranche]] ($1.2B pre-money target). **Boston Dynamics** now fully Hyundai-owned ([[Hyundai to buy SoftBank's remaining Boston Dynamics stake for $325M]]). China market re-rating: [[Morgan Stanley raises forecast for China humanoid robot market]] (50k units 2026, $15B by 2030).
- **Position:** Agility is the **traction/commercialization leader in the West** but a **valuation and capital minnow** — $2.5B vs Figure's $39B and Tesla's balance sheet. Its whole pitch is "revenue now, safety/compliance moat, boring logistics use-case" against better-capitalized rivals selling a bigger AGI-flavored vision.
- **Why the landscape is this way (second-order):** valuation is being awarded for **AI-narrative + capital**, not deployed hours. Agility's edge (Digit walking bipedally since 2015, real WMS/IT integration) is the *least* rewarded attribute in a hype cycle → hence the SPAC route (cheaper-than-IPO public access) and the Fremont hub (buys an "AI" story to close the multiple gap).

## [2] Company history / fit
Founded **2015** (Oregon State spin-out; bipedal-walking research). Amazon invested **$150M in 2022 Series B**; ran Digit pilots but is **not currently an active deployment customer** — waiting to restart with **Digit v5**. Named live customers: **GXO, Schaeffler, Toyota Motor Manufacturing Canada, Mercado Libre** (PR); TechCrunch adds Amazon historically. Milestone: **Digit moved >100,000 totes at GXO's Flowery Branch, GA facility by Nov 2025** — the most-documented humanoid commercial deployment. CEO **Peggy Johnson** (ex-Magic Leap, ex-Microsoft) leads the SPAC; co-founders **Damion Shelton** (chairman), **Jonathan Hurst** (chief robot officer). **Digit v5** to be unveiled this fall; can sense humans (no longer needs a robot-only cage), lift ~50 lb, reach 7.2 ft, run up to 22 hrs/day.
- **Why the company acts this way:** a hardware company with contingent bookings and heavy cash burn needs **public capital + an AI multiple**. Fremont hub = manufacture the AI story; SPAC = fastest public access; "logistics only, no home robots" = a **credibility/safety wedge** distinguishing it from Figure/1X/Tesla consumer promises that experts call unsafe.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new:** almost nothing in the *event* itself — it's a leased software office + hiring plan. Real substance is the **operational track record** (100k totes at GXO, 4 named enterprise customers, Digit v5 with human-sensing) and the **safety architecture** — co-founder Shelton's line that "the safety stack must NOT go through generative AI" is a real, differentiating engineering stance vs end-to-end-neural rivals.
- **Traction is real but modest:** Agility has **not disclosed unit count**; outside estimates are "dozens" in pilot/revenue deployments. $300M orders are **milestone-contingent**. So: real revenue, real integration know-how — but scale is still pilot-to-early-commercial, not mass deployment.
- **Who captures the margin (second-order):** the durable moat isn't the humanoid form factor (commoditizing fast, esp. from China/Unitree) — it's **integration into enterprise WMS/IT + clearing safety/regulatory bars**. That's a services/systems-integration moat, not a hardware one. Risk: if end-to-end AI models (Figure/Sunday's ACT-2 style zero-shot learning) close the capability gap, Agility's "practical, non-AI safety stack" edge could invert into a **speed-of-learning disadvantage**. The central question shifts from "who has revenue today" to "does deployment-hour data compound into a durable lead, or does a better AI model leapfrog it before scale?"

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Near-term:** Digit v5 reveal this fall; SPAC close "later in 2026" (pending shareholder vote + SEC review); trades as **AGLT**. >30 customers "in talks." Foxconn PIPE + potential Foxconn manufacturing partnership is the sleeper — could solve Agility's scale/cost gap vs Tesla's vertical integration.
- **Sentiment:** frothy humanoid cycle (Figure $39B, MS doubling China forecasts twice in 2026). Agility positions as the "adult in the room" — revenue, safety, no home-robot vaporware.
- **Risks / counterintuitive second-order:** (a) **SPAC dilution + trust redemptions** — the $420M trust is only realized net of redemptions, which run high in 2026 SPACs; the "$620M" is a ceiling, not a floor. (b) Public-market scrutiny will expose **actual revenue vs the $300M contingent bookings** — a valuation reset risk. (c) Being first-public pure-play makes Agility the **sentiment proxy for the whole humanoid trade** — it will be re-rated on peers' news, not just its own. (d) Capital gap vs Tesla/Figure means it must win on **capital efficiency**, so Foxconn is the most important name in the release, not "Tesla's backyard."

## Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Is the event actually material, or is it a SPAC proof-point?** — The Fremont "training center" is a 60k sq ft leased software office + a ~200-person hiring plan, opened weeks after the 24-Jun Churchill Capital XI merger and 14-Jul S-4 draft. Answer: the building is marketing; the real event is the **$2.5B public listing**. The note headline undersells this.

2. **Is "$300M in contract orders" real revenue?** — No. Filings say "multi-year orders for Digit v5, **subject to realization of certain contractual milestones**." Contingent bookings, not banked revenue. Open: what is Agility's *actual trailing revenue*? (GeekWire filings suggest it's small.)

3. **How many Digits are actually deployed?** — Undisclosed by the company; outside estimates "dozens." The only hard number is **>100k totes moved at GXO (Flowery Branch, GA) by Nov 2025**. Traction is early-commercial, not scale.

4. **Is Amazon still a customer?** — No, not currently active. Amazon invested $150M (2022 Series B) and ran pilots but paused; deployment restart is contingent on **Digit v5**. TechCrunch listing Amazon among current customers is misleading.

5. **What does the $620M SPAC proceeds actually net out to?** — ~$420M trust (**before redemptions**, which run high in 2026 SPACs) + $200M Foxconn-led PIPE at $10/sh. Realistic floor could be far below $620M. Open: assumed redemption rate?

6. **Is "Tesla's backyard" a strength or a vanity frame?** — Vanity. Proximity to Tesla's Fremont Optimus line has no operational benefit; it's a recruiting/PR angle. Competitively, Agility ($2.5B) vs Tesla (mass-production ambition + balance sheet) is asymmetric.

7. **Is the valuation defensible vs peers?** — Agility $2.5B vs Figure ~$39B, 1X ~$10B, Apptronik ~$5.5B. Agility argues *most deployment hours*; the market rewards *AI narrative + capital*. The multiple gap is the whole reason for the Fremont "Physical AI" rebrand.

8. **Is the "safety stack must not run through generative AI" stance a moat or a liability?** — Both. It's a genuine differentiator for enterprise safety/compliance today, but if rivals' end-to-end AI (Figure, Sunday's ACT-2 zero-shot) closes the capability gap, Agility's practical approach could become a **learning-speed disadvantage**. This is the pivotal open question.

9. **Where is the durable moat — hardware or integration?** — Integration (WMS/IT plumbing + clearing customer safety/regulatory bars), not the humanoid form factor, which is commoditizing (Unitree/China). This is a systems-integration moat, thinner than it looks.

10. **How important is Foxconn (vs the Tesla headline)?** — Very. Foxconn led the PIPE and is the plausible manufacturing/cost partner that could close Agility's scale gap vs vertically-integrated Tesla. The most load-bearing name in the release is Foxconn, not Tesla.

11. **Does being first-public help or hurt?** — Double-edged: first-mover public access, but Agility becomes the **sentiment proxy for the entire humanoid trade** — re-rated on peers' news and hype cycles, amplifying downside.

12. **Is Digit v5 shipping or announced?** — Announced; unveiling "this fall." Human-sensing (no cage), 50 lb lift, 7.2 ft reach, 22 hr/day are **spec claims**, not yet field-proven at scale. Most of the $300M orders are for this unshipped generation.

13. **What's the burn / path to profitability?** — Open. Humanoid hardware is capital-intensive; the SPAC exists because private capital at Agility's scale is expensive. No disclosed path to positive unit economics.

14. **Freshness — is this a duplicate?** — No. The SPAC (24 Jun) and the Fremont opening (16 Jul) are distinct, genuinely new events; no prior note covers Agility. Related-but-different notes exist (Figure/1X/Sunday/Humanoid/China), so this is a **fresh** node in the humanoid cluster.

15. **Does the "trillion-dollar company / 100M robots" quote signal anything?** — Only ambition (Hurst's framing). Discount as vision-selling into a live capital raise; no bearing on near-term weight.

**Sector & drivers.** Humanoid robotics — sub-vertical of physical/embodied AI, protagonist targeting warehouse & manufacturing logistics. Size: the global humanoids market is put at ~$3.93bn in 2026 → ~$17.8bn by 2031 at ~35% CAGR (per Mordor Intelligence, as of 2026); China alone is forecast by Morgan Stanley at ~$2bn in 2026 → ~$15bn by 2030 with 50,000 units shipping in 2026 (~446k by 2030) — see [[Morgan Stanley raises forecast for China humanoid robot market]]. Cumulative sector funding surpassed ~$9.8bn through 2025 (per market trackers). Structure: **early, fragmented, capital-intensive** — value pools split between the robot integrators (Agility, Figure, Apptronik, 1X, Tesla, Unitree) and the component supply chain (e.g. actuator/gearbox maker Leaderdrive, flagged by Morgan Stanley). Barriers are capital, safety/regulatory certification for shared human spaces, and data/deployment flywheel. **Why now:** transformer-based models promise task-generalisation ("the number of things a robot can do far exceeds the number of engineers who can program it," per co-founder Shelton), while a US labor-cost gap makes RaaS pencil out. Second-order: the race is shifting from demo videos to *paid commercial deployment* — the metric that now separates leaders from the pack.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: **units shipped/deployed, operating hours, contracted order backlog $, RaaS $/hr vs fully-loaded human labor, and units/yr manufacturing capacity.** Key players & basis of competition: Figure (~$39B post-money, OpenAI-led; per market trackers) and Apptronik (~$5B, $520M round Feb-2026, Google/Mercedes-backed — [[Proception settles Tesla trade-secret suit, raises $11M]] sits in the same Tesla-adjacent talent orbit) compete on Western paid pilots (BMW, Mercedes, GXO); Tesla Optimus competes on manufacturing scale (Musk: public sales pushed to end-2027); 1X (~$10B) and Sunday Robotics chase the *home* market ([[Sunday Robotics unveils ACT-2 model for home robot Memo]]); Unitree wins on price/volume (5,500+ units shipped 2025, ~$1.3B). **Risk flags.**
1. **SPAC + no financials disclosed.** $2.5bn pre-money set before an S-4 reveals revenue/EBITDA; SPAC redemptions could gut the ~$420M trust, leaving mostly the ~$200M PIPE — funding shortfall risk for a cash-hungry hardware scale-up. Why: capital, not tech, is Agility's weakness vs Figure/Tesla.

**What this changes (idea-lens)** *(analysis)*. Breaks if: S-4 reveals thin revenue / heavy cash burn, or the anchor customer slips — then it re-rates toward "pre-revenue hardware bet." Trigger to watch: **S-4 filing (revenue/burn) and the Digit v5 unveil this fall (human-sensing, exits robot-only zones).**
