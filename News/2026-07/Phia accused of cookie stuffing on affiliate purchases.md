Phia accused of ‘cookie stuffing,’ taking affiliate credit on purchases it didn’t earn
Phia, the shopping startup co-founded by Bill Gates’ daughter, Phoebe Gates, and Sophia Kianni, has been accused of a practice known as “cookie stuffing,” which may have helped the product receive commissions and credit for sales it did not actually generate, according to a Bloomberg investigation . 
The report has sparked controversy and led to Phia’s suspension from Impact.com , a leading affiliate and influencer platform. Other startups have been sued over “cookie stuffing,” notably Honey, which is owned by PayPal and remains the subject of an ongoing class action lawsuit. 
Founded in 2025, Phia has raised more than $40 million in funding and has a star-studded list of investors, including Khloé Kardashian and Hailey Bieber. The startup developed an app as a browser extension that works somewhat like Google Flights, but for shopping. Phia helps customers find the lowest-priced items across various retailers as well as discount codes to use when shopping. The company takes a commission on purchases made through the platform, an industry practice known as affiliate marketing. 
The Bloomberg investigation, as well as findings from an independent consultant and a competitor, found that if a user shopped at an online retailer — even if they arrived at the site on their own or through another affiliate program like Wirecutter — Phia would open a new tab in the background. During the checkout process, Phia would override the referral codes from other affiliates and instead inject its own, allowing it to take credit for and potentially receive a commission on a purchase it didn’t earn. 
Once the issue was flagged to Phia, a spokesperson told Bloomberg that all necessary changes had been made to fix the issue. A check by Bloomberg found the issue had been resolved. It’s unclear if the fix is enough to satisfy the retailers and affiliate partners that work with Phia.
TechCrunch reached out to Phia for comment and has not received a response.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Affiliate marketing is a large, growing performance channel: global affiliate spend ~$19.4bn in 2026 vs ~$17.1bn in 2025 (per digitalapplied/track360 compilations, via secondary blog citations, as of 2026 — non-audited industry estimates, treat as directional). Structure is fragmented on the publisher side (millions of affiliates; per industry data the top 10% of affiliates drive ~67% of program revenue) but concentrated at the network/tracking layer: a handful of networks/SaaS platforms (Impact.com, CJ/Commission Junction, Rakuten, Awin) own attribution, and retailers pay ~5–20% of order value on physical goods (median e-commerce commission ~8–8.4%). Value is created at the point of *attribution*, not discovery — whoever "wins the last click" is paid, which is precisely the seam Phia is accused of exploiting. Why now: the shopping-extension model (Honey, Capital One Shopping, Rakuten, Phia) monetizes 100% via affiliate commission with no user fee, so the only revenue lever is claiming attribution — a structural incentive to over-attribute. Cookie-window shrinkage (Apple ITP/ATT, Chrome third-party-cookie moves) and Google's Mar-2025 Chrome Web Store rule (extensions may not claim affiliate commission without providing a discount) are actively tightening this space.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: attributed GMV, take/commission rate (~8% of order value), monthly active users, and — critically for extensions — attribution *win rate* (share of checkouts where the tool's cookie is last). Key players and basis of competition: Honey/PayPal and Capital One Shopping (incumbent scale + distribution), Rakuten, Wirecutter/affiliate-publisher programs; competition is on distribution (install base) and on how aggressively the tool inserts itself at checkout — a race-to-the-bottom dynamic where the winner is whoever grabs the last click. Recent moves: Google tightened Chrome Web Store affiliate rules (Mar-2025); PayPal won dismissal (with leave to amend, Nov-2025) of the consolidated influencer class action over Honey's alleged cookie-stuffing (`(analysis)` — court noted lost commissions stem from last-click attribution rules, a partial legitimization of the practice). Phia's position: sub-scale niche entrant (~1–1.5m users, launched Apr-2025) leaning on celebrity distribution rather than product moat; no durable moat evident (`(analysis)` — extension install base is low-switching-cost; the "50% lower return rates" and "11x revenue growth" claims are company-stated `[UNSOURCED]`). In-base: [[Bloomberg finds Phia shopping app inflated savings claims]] (sibling story, corroborated by Capital One Shopping + researcher Ben Edelman).

**Comps & multiples.** Phia is private, pre-scale, no disclosed revenue → trading multiples "no data". Last round: ~$35–35.5m Series A (Notable Capital), >$43m total funding, ~$185m post-money valuation (per Forbes/Crunchbase/Tracxn, as of Jan–Jun 2026). No ≥3 comparable disclosed figures → distribution not computed, qualitative comparison only. Flag: a $185m valuation for a <2-year-old zero-fee extension whose monetization is under active regulatory/partner attack is *rich vs justifiable revenue base* `(analysis)`, but is celebrity/hype-driven and not anchored to disclosed economics.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Revenue-integrity / existential channel risk.** Phia's *entire* revenue is affiliate commission, and its Impact.com suspension cuts off a primary attribution rail. Why: if networks and retailers deauthorize it (as Impact.com did), the monetization engine stops — not a fine but a shut-off valve. This is worse than Honey, which had PayPal's balance sheet and diversified revenue behind it.
2. **Litigation/regulatory precedent risk.** Cookie-stuffing is not a grey area at the extreme: eBay v. Digital Point / Shawn Hogan ($28m in commissions, wire-fraud guilty plea, prison, 2012–14) shows criminal exposure; the Honey class action shows civil exposure even if hard to win. Why: "we fixed it" (Phia's stated remedy, Bloomberg-verified) mitigates forward conduct but not past clawbacks or partner trust.
3. **Reputation/diligence risk.** Compounds a prior 2025 privacy issue (extension reportedly logged browsing incl. bank statements per secondary reports `[UNSOURCED]`); for a brand built on celebrity trust, repeated integrity failures erode the one asset (distribution via trust) it actually has.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is a governance shock, not a re-rating of the sector — affiliate/agentic-commerce economics are intact and the Honey dismissal arguably *narrows* liability (last-click attribution is court-tolerated). But it sharpens a real thesis: as AI/agentic shopping tools proliferate ([[Visa expands agentic commerce access for merchants in Europe]], [[Robinhood claims AI-agent shopping Guinness World Record]]), the value capture will be fought at the attribution layer, and networks (Impact.com) plus platform gatekeepers (Google Chrome Web Store) are the enforcers. Watch trigger: whether major retailers/networks reinstate Phia or permanently deauthorize it — the latter would be a falsification of the "just fix the code" narrative and pressure the $185m mark. Second-order: expect networks to codify anti-cookie-stuffing rules for AI shopping agents, raising compliance barriers for new entrants.
