На этом фоне регулирование фокусируется на финансовом UX: важно не только что раскрыто, но и как, где и в какой момент. # Context-enrichment: Russian Duma backs bill on clearer financial product ads
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

The Финтехно item conflates TWO distinct legislative moves the Duma acted on around 6-7 Jul 2026. Separate them or the story is misread:

- **Bill A — advertising simplification (FIRST reading, 7 Jul 2026).** Lets ads of ANY financial service show only "basic conditions" and push full detail to the provider's website, provided the ad carries a warning "Study all conditions of the financial service" ("Изучите все условия финансовой услуги") pointing to a specific site section. Format rules: warning ≥3 sec on radio; ≥3 sec + ≥5% of frame area on TV/video; ≥5% of ad space in other media. Authors: Anatoly Aksakov (chair, Duma Financial Market Committee) + Arkady Svistunov. Mechanism: extends to ALL banking/insurance/other financial ads the regime that ALREADY applies to consumer-credit ads (full-cost-of-credit / ПСК range, provider name). ([nbj.ru](https://nbj.ru/news/v-gosdumu-vnesyen-zakonoproekt-o-raskryt/73332/), [1prime.ru](https://1prime.ru/20260707/reklama-871357964.html), [interfax.ru](https://www.interfax.ru/russia/1090727))
- **Bill B — transfers-cost disclosure (SECOND + THIRD reading, 7 Jul 2026; law).** Obliges credit institutions from **1 Sep 2027** to publish the cost of money-transfer services (incl. SBP/СБП fast-payments and e-money terms) and all changes to them. Amends the laws "On the National Payment System" and "On Consumer Credit (Loan)". Order/method/timing/composition of disclosure set by credit-institution activity standards. ([ria.ru](https://ria.ru/20260707/perevody-2103330396.html), [vz.ru](https://vz.ru/news/2026/7/7/1433094.html), [osnmedia.ru](https://www.osnmedia.ru/ekonomika/gosduma-prinyala-zakon-o-raskrytii-uslovij-bankovskih-uslug))

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Two things on the same day, opposite in direction but same theme (financial-product transparency/UX):
- Bill A DEREGULATES the ad medium (less clutter mandated in the ad itself) while shifting the disclosure burden to the website — first reading only, so terms can still change. It does NOT create new penalties; it re-uses the existing consumer-credit ad regime. Net: relief for advertisers, conditional on a standardized warning + working site link.
- Bill B ADDS a hard duty: publish transfer tariffs, live 1 Sep 2027 (long runway). It bites hidden commissions on transfers/cards specifically.

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** the Финтехно framing ("advertising will get a bit easier AND disclosure more transparent") papers over that A loosens and B tightens — they are not one coherent "transparency package." A's real driver: FAS data show financial-services ads are ~50% of all Ad-Law violations (2023); the current rule that ALL conditions must sit inside the ad is unworkable for today's conditional products (rate depends on purchases/subscription/status/tenor/limit/client tier). So A is a pragmatic climb-down — move detail online rather than keep failing an impossible in-ad disclosure standard. B's driver: Bank of Russia's behavioural-supervision push against hidden fees/misselling. Neither is a landmark; both are incremental plumbing.

## [1] Competitors / peers (comparable regulatory precedents)
- **Consumer-credit ad regime (pre-existing):** already requires provider name + full-cost-of-credit (ПСК) range and the warning "Assess your financial capacity and risks." Bill A simply generalizes this. So A is catch-up/harmonization, NOT novelty. ([garant.ru](https://www.garant.ru/news/2101189/))
- **FAS enforcement track:** a 2024/25 law (Presidentially signed) DOUBLED fines for credit/loan ad violations — legal entities now RUB 600k-1.6m (was 300k-800k). FAS is "unambiguously for" higher fines. ([fas.gov.ru](https://fas.gov.ru/news/33193), [iz.ru](https://iz.ru/1766730/evgenii-grachev-natalia-bashlykova/fas-odnoznachno-za-povyshenie-shtrafov-v-reklame-finansovykh-uslug))
- **CB misselling fines (in development, separate):** Bank of Russia proposes bank fines of 0.1-1% of own funds (capital) for consumer-rights violations, proportional to benefit gained. ([en.iz.ru](https://en.iz.ru/en/1864902/lubov-lezneva/protective-measures-central-bank-proposes-increase-fines-banks-violating-consumer-rights))

**Why the landscape is this way:** Russia runs a two-pronged consumer-finance-conduct model — FAS on advertising, Bank of Russia on point-of-sale conduct (behavioural supervision, misselling). Bill A is FAS-adjacent (Ad Law); Bill B and the fines are CB-adjacent. Misselling complaints reportedly fell >2x since 2023 when the CB got power to suspend sales for information violations — so the state reads the model as working and is fine-tuning, not overhauling.

## [2] Company/actor history / fit
No single company — sponsor is Aksakov's Financial Market Committee, the standing author of Russian fintech/payments law. Fits a clear 2026 cadence of Aksakov-led financial-consumer bills in this corpus: [[Финтехно Russia approves new private-savings mobilization law]] (housing-savings, 2nd+3rd reading, live 2027), [[Russian government backs installment-market bill with delay]], [[Russia advances plan to add cooling-off period on crypto transfers]]. Same playbook: standardize disclosure/UX, phase in with a long runway.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Low novelty. Bill A = generalization of an existing consumer-credit ad rule; Bill B = a disclosure mandate with a 15-month lead time (Sep 2027) and the actual content delegated to future "activity standards" — i.e., the teeth are TBD. **Traction = zero yet:** A is first-reading (can change/die); B is passed but not in force. The value-add is real but modest and deferred: consumers gain a mandated, comparable transfer-cost page in 2027; advertisers get regulatory relief now. Who captures value: advertisers (cleaner creative, lower compliance-copy risk) short-term; consumers only if website disclosure is genuinely findable — the classic risk that "see site for details" becomes a compliance fig-leaf (FAS itself warns "подробнее на сайте" does not excuse in-material disclosure of material terms). (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Bill A needs 2nd/3rd readings; format/warning specifics may shift. Bill B: banks have until 1 Sep 2027; the operative disclosure standard is still to be written by credit institutions' self-regulation, so watch that standard, not the headline. ([kubnews.ru](https://kubnews.ru/amp/obshchestvo/2026/07/08/banki-obyazhut-raskryvat-vse-komissii-po-perevodam-i-kreditam-do-soversheniya-operatsii/))
- Backdrop: rising FAS fines + CB misselling-fine proposal + behavioural supervision → the compliance cost of sloppy financial marketing is going up even as the in-ad disclosure burden goes down. Second-order: shifting detail to websites concentrates disclosure quality into UX/design, which is HARDER to police than a fixed ad template — enforcement may lag, partially offsetting the consumer benefit. (hypothesis)

## Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Are these one bill or two?** TWO. Bill A = ad-simplification (1st reading); Bill B = transfer-cost disclosure (2nd+3rd reading, law). The Финтехно item merges them — a reader could wrongly think one "transparency law" passed.
2. **Does Bill A make disclosure MORE or LESS transparent?** LESS in the ad itself (details move to website), MORE only if the site page is findable. So "clearer ads" is half-true — the ad gets cleaner, not more informative.
3. **Is Bill A actually new?** No — it generalizes the EXISTING consumer-credit ad regime (ПСК range + warning) to all financial services. Harmonization, not novelty.
4. **When does anything bind?** Bill B: 1 Sep 2027. Bill A: not yet passed. Traction today = 0. Answered.
5. **What are the penalties in these bills?** Neither bill sets new fines. Fines live elsewhere: FAS 2024/25 law doubled credit-ad fines to RUB 600k-1.6m; CB proposes 0.1-1%-of-capital misselling fines (separate, in development). Open whether those attach to Bill A breaches.
6. **Who defines Bill B's actual disclosure content?** Credit-institution "activity standards" — delegated, not yet written. The teeth are TBD; headline overstates certainty.
7. **Does "see website" become a compliance fig-leaf?** Risk is real — FAS explicitly says a site link does NOT excuse omitting material terms. Whether Bill A's warning-format rules prevent abuse is open.
8. **Who benefits, who loses?** Advertisers gain relief now; consumers gain a comparable transfer-cost page in 2027. Net-neutral-to-mildly-positive for consumers; positive for banks' marketing.
9. **Is this a corpus duplicate?** No prior note covers this advertising bill or the transfers-disclosure law; grep over region/ru returned none. FRESH.
10. **Regulator alignment — FAS vs Bank of Russia?** FAS owns ad rules (Bill A); CB owns conduct/misselling (Bill B + fines). Coherent division of labour, but the two moves point opposite directions (loosen ad / tighten transfer disclosure).
11. **Why loosen ad rules now?** Financial-services ads were ~50% of all Ad-Law violations (FAS 2023) because in-ad full disclosure is unworkable for conditional products — pragmatic climb-down, not consumer generosity.
12. **Second-order enforcement risk?** Moving detail to websites shifts disclosure quality into UX/design, harder to police than a fixed ad template → enforcement may lag, offsetting benefit. (hypothesis)
13. **Cross-border / systemic weight?** None — purely domestic RU retail-conduct plumbing; no international spillover, no company-level P&L impact.
14. **Does it change the central question?** Yes: from "did Russia make ads clearer" to "which direction — and does 'clarity' just relocate offline detail to a website nobody checks."
15. **Would a reader be misled by the source framing?** Yes on the conflation and on "clearer"; correcting both is the main value-add of this note.

Importance: 2/5 — fresh but low-weight domestic retail-conduct regulation. Bill A is incremental harmonization (still 1st reading), Bill B is a deferred (Sep-2027) disclosure duty with content delegated to future standards. No penalties in either bill, no company impact, no cross-border relevance; matters mainly as a marker of Russia's ongoing FAS-ad + CB-misselling conduct agenda.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Domestic RU retail-conduct / financial-marketing regulation — not a market with a TAM; sizing is by regulatory workload and enforcement statistics. Structure: a two-agency split — **FAS** owns advertising rules (Ad Law 38-ФЗ), **Bank of Russia** owns point-of-sale conduct (behavioural supervision / misselling). Entry barrier for the "market" being regulated (financial marketing) is not the point; the load-bearing dynamic is that in-ad full disclosure has become **unworkable for conditional products** (rate depends on purchases/subscription/status/tenor/limit/client tier), so the state is re-plumbing *where* disclosure lives. Dated facts: (1) Financial-services ads were **14.23% of all Ad-Law violations in 2024** (2nd-most-common category; ~15% in H1-2024) — per FAS report via pravo.ru — NOTE: this materially corrects the "~50%" figure carried in [0]; the ~50% almost certainly conflates categories or an older cut, and 14.23% is the sourced 2024 number. (2) FAS issued **2,202 Ad-Law penalties totalling RUB 161.3m in 2024** (vs 2,597 / RUB 134.4m in 2023) — pravo.ru. (3) Credit/loan-ad fines for legal entities are **RUB 600k–1.6m** (raised from RUB 300k–800k by a Presidentially-signed 2024/25 law); FAS + CB are separately floating **turnover/capital-based fines** (share of own funds) for improper bank ads — iz.ru, comnews.ru. "Why now": a pragmatic climb-down (Bill A) meeting a behavioural-supervision tightening (Bill B) on the same day.

**Competitive landscape.** No companies compete here; the relevant "KPIs" are enforcement metrics: (a) share of financial ads in Ad-Law violations (14.23%, 2024); (b) misselling/imposed-services complaint trend; (c) fine size trajectory. Behavioural supervision is working on its own terms — imposed-services complaints in consumer lending fell **45.2% in 2024** and misselling/imposed-services complaints fell a further **31% in 2025** (65% in auto-lending) — cbr.ru, urbc.ru. Key regulatory actors: **FAS** (ad simplification + rising ad fines), **Bank of Russia** (behavioural supervision, welcome-calls, proposed 0.1%-of-capital misselling fines), the **Duma Financial-Market Committee (Aksakov)** as legislator, and industry self-regulation (ABR/NSFR) writing the operative disclosure standards. Protagonist "position": Bill A is **catch-up/harmonization** — it generalizes the pre-existing consumer-credit ad regime (ПСК range + warning) to all financial services; Bill B is a **deferred disclosure duty** (live 1 Sep 2027) with content delegated to future credit-institution "activity standards." Confirmed 07 Jul 2026: Bill A recommended for 1st reading (Aksakov + Svistunov); Bill B passed 2nd+3rd readings — 1prime.ru, ria.ru, vedomosti.ru, interfax.ru.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/deal/multiples — regulatory, not a company. Comps = precedent RU conduct-regulation moves in-base:
- [[Финтехно Russian banks need memorandum on honest deposit disclosure]] — closest analogue: same 1–3 Jul 2026 CB Financial Congress backdrop, same FAS+CB co-drafting, same "fair disclosure" theme (deposit terms); voluntary memorandum (7 banks signed) vs Bill A/B statutory. Shows the disclosure agenda is running on *both* soft-law and hard-law tracks simultaneously.
- [[Russian government backs installment-market bill with delay]] — same Aksakov-led playbook: amend an already-in-force base regime, pace implementation with a delay, add consumer-protection constraints. Same "long-runway, delegated detail" pattern as Bill B.
- [[Финтехно Q3 brings debt-load and installment-market rule changes]] — parallel CB tightening track (ПДН/DSTI haircuts, macroprudential limits) — same conduct-supervision cadence, different instrument.
Distribution not computed (no numeric comparables); qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Compliance-cost asymmetry, not relief.** Bill A lowers the in-ad burden but the *backdrop* raises cost: doubled credit-ad fines (RUB 600k–1.6m) + proposed turnover/capital-based fines + CB's 0.1%-of-capital misselling fines. Net compliance cost of sloppy financial marketing is **rising**, so treating Bill A as pure deregulation misreads the direction of travel.
2. **Enforcement uncertainty / "see-site" fig-leaf.** Moving detail to websites shifts disclosure quality into UX/design, which is harder to police than a fixed ad template; FAS itself warns a site link does NOT excuse omitting material terms. Enforcement may lag the deregulation, partially offsetting the consumer benefit. (hypothesis)
3. **Scope ambiguity / TBD teeth.** Bill A is 1st-reading only (format/warning specs can shift, or the bill can die); Bill B's operative disclosure content is delegated to yet-unwritten credit-institution "activity standards" and does not bind until 1 Sep 2027 — headline certainty overstates the actual, deferred, still-to-be-defined obligation.
4. **Low cross-border relevance.** Purely domestic RU retail-conduct plumbing — no international spillover, no company-level P&L impact, no read-across for non-RU fintech. Weight is as a marker of the FAS-ad + CB-misselling conduct agenda, not a standalone event.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Not a re-rating or a new market — it is fine-tuning of a conduct regime the state reads as *working* (complaint volumes down 31–65% in 2025). The real signal is the **bifurcation of the disclosure stack**: ads get lighter, mandated disclosure migrates to websites/tariff pages, and the enforcement frontier moves from ad-template compliance to digital-disclosure UX. Falsifiable thesis: if Bill A passes 2nd/3rd reading without tightening the site-link/warning specs, expect a rise in FAS "материальные условия не раскрыты despite site link" cases in 2027–28 — the trigger to watch is the FAS enforcement mix (in-ad vs website omission), not the headline. Watch also the credit-institution "activity standard" for Bill B (the actual teeth) rather than the 1 Sep 2027 date. (analysis)
