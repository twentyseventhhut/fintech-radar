Meta considered buying Kalshi before developing its own prediction market app

 hide caption 
 
Before Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg directed employees to build a standalone prediction market app, he proposed buying Kalshi, the leading company in the prediction market sector, according to three people with knowledge of the discussions who were not authorized to speak publicly.
 Planet Money 
 Do prediction market bettors make anything better? 
Zuckerberg met with Kalshi CEO Tarek Mansour about a possible takeover last year as Kalshi's popularity surged, but the negotiations never advanced, according to one of the people who had direct knowledge of the meeting.
There are competing narratives about why the talks broke down, with some saying Mansour would not move forward with a sale and others indicating Meta considered the legal and ethical questions surrounding Kalshi too messy.
 Technology 
 Meta plans to release AI-powered prediction market app, documents show 
Whatever made the discussions fall apart, Meta still wants to tap into the prediction market craze. Zuckerberg has stood up a team that is now working to release its own prediction market app called Arena, which internal documents reviewed by NPR show will allow people to make guesses about future events.
Unlike Kalshi and its main competitor, Polymarket, Meta's app will not take bets using real money. Instead, users will wager "play money" on the outcome of happenings in the news and topics trending online. Meta's documents say the company's artificial intelligence systems will power the questions and determine who wins or loses based on something happening or not.
Neither Kalshi nor Meta would provide NPR with a comment when asked about the acquisition talks.
Prediction markets have become one of the fastest-growing parts of the tech industry in recent years. The sites allow people to place bets on everything from sports to elections to whether Iran will develop a nuclear weapon.
The massive influx of users into prediction markets makes the space an obvious target for Zuckerberg, according to Tim Wu, a Columbia University law professor who advised the Biden White House on tech policy.
"Meta seems to clutch at every shiny object," Wu said. "With the help of their advertising cash cow, they've been able to fail again and again without consequence," he said, citing Meta's pullback from the so-called " metaverse ," and the abandonment of its cryptocurrency project, Libra. "I can't imagine a casino app with fake money is going to be much of a thrill," he said. "But maybe it's something my children would like, I don't know."
 Technology 
 2 young billionaires are behind the prediction market boom. They hate each other 
Thanks in part to a permissive regulatory environment in Washington, prediction markets have seen staggering growth.
In June 2025, about $28 billion was traded every month on Kalshi and Polymarket. A year later, monthly volume on the sites is nearly $220 billion, driven mostly by sports-related betting, according to The Block , a news and research company that tracks prediction market data.
Kalshi, which is overseen by commodities regulators in Washington, was valued at $22 billion in its latest funding round in May, up from a $2 billion valuation last year. Polymarket, which operates an overseas exchange outside the reach of U.S. regulators, is valued at $10.7 billion, according to the private market data firm PitchBook.
 Technology 
 Minnesota becomes first state to ban prediction markets 
The rise of prediction markets has set off dozens of legal battles pitting the tech companies against state gaming officials , who insist the sites are gambling under a different name.
President Trump has vowed to protect prediction market companies, even as controversies over insider trading and market manipulation plague the industry.
 Law 
 U.S. soldier charged with using classified information to bet on Maduro's removal 
Justice Department officials have opened two criminal cases over alleged insider trading on Polymarket. One involves a special forces soldier who allegedly profited from classified information about the capture of Venezuelan leader Nicolás Maduro by U.S. forces. In the other case, DOJ accuses a Google employee who earned more than $1 million of using confidential data about search trends to correctly guess the most-Googled people of 2025.
Meta's "buy or bury" strategy
Zuckerberg's interest in acquiring Kalshi follows a familiar corporate pattern. Meta has amassed a user base of more than 3 billion worldwide through the takeover of emerging social media platforms. Notably, Meta's purchases of Instagram in 2012 and WhatsApp in 2014 supercharged its reach and allowed it to become a colossal force in digital advertising. More recently, Meta bought AI wearable company Limitless and Moltbook , a social network for AI bots.
Meta's takeovers have attracted scrutiny from federal regulators. The Federal Trade Commission alleged at a trial last year that Meta engages in a "buy or bury" strategy in which nascent rivals are either acquired by the company, or Meta introduces a service cloning the competitor to squash their business.
 Business 
 Judge sides with Meta in antitrust trial, will not spin off WhatsApp and Instagram 
A judge sided with Meta, ruling that the company did not violate any competition laws when it gobbled up Instagram and WhatsApp. Lawyers with the FTC are appealing the decision.
While the acquisition talks never advanced, Meta did strike a partnership with Kalshi in March, allowing for easy integration of Kalshi markets on Meta's social media app Threads.
Wu, the former White House tech policy adviser, said Meta became a company worth more than $1 trillion by acquiring apps rather than building its own. He argues Meta throws its power and money around like a monopoly and distorts the competitive field for everyone else.
"WhatsApp and Instagram have given them never-ending profits, but normal companies cannot fail five times in a row," he said. "Meta looking to take over Kalshi fits in with the company's long-standing practices."

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
NPR (Bobby Allyn, 2026-06-30) reports — via three unnamed people, one with direct knowledge — that **Zuckerberg met Kalshi CEO Tarek Mansour "last year" (2025) about a possible acquisition**, but talks **never advanced**. Two competing narratives on why: (a) Mansour wouldn't sell; (b) Meta found the legal/ethical questions around real-money prediction markets "too messy." Neither Meta nor Kalshi commented. Meta instead stood up a team building **Arena**, a standalone app (first scooped by the same reporter on 2026-06-24 from internal documents).

De-PR'd reality — three separate things, only one of which is "live":
- **Acquisition talks: dead, 2025, unconfirmed by either party** (single-sourced on the meeting itself). This is a *rumor/leak*, not a transaction. Weight it as reporting, not fact.
- **Arena: PLANNED, NOT LIVE.** Per NPR's document scoop, no release date; a prototype must still be built and internally tested for iOS/Android. Key mechanic: **play money** (a "daily virtual allotment"), NOT real bets — this is what structurally separates it from Kalshi/Polymarket. Resolution is done by **Meta's Llama LLM** with **no human review and no dispute/challenge window**. Arena is a **rebuild of Meta's shut-down "Forecast" app**; the stated reason Forecast died was the "operational cost of manual question curation" → AI resolution is the cost fix.
- **Threads–Kalshi partnership: LIVE, March 2026** — integrates Kalshi markets into Threads. Terms undisclosed.

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The play-money design is not a product philosophy — it's a **regulatory hedge**. Gaming lawyer Daniel Wallach (per NPR): launching without real-money wagering buys Meta time to pursue a federal license and lets the contested legal landscape settle, then flip to real money later. The AI-only, no-appeal resolution is a **cost/scale play** (the thing that killed Forecast), and it exports resolution risk onto an LLM — a live liability question nobody is addressing. The through-line NPR draws: talks → clone (Arena) → "buy or bury."

Series F **$1B at $22B**, announced 2026-05-07 (Coatue-led), doubling from **$11B (Dec 2025)**, itself up from **~$2B (June 2025, Paradigm-led $185M)** — see [[Kalshi raises $1 billion Series F at $22 billion valuation]], [[Kalshi raises $1bn at $22bn valuation]], [[Kalshi valuation reportedly jumps to $11B after $1B round]], [[Kalshi raises $185M at $2bn valuation]]. Rumored ~$40B target round (June 2026) — unconfirmed, not closed.
- **Polymarket** — global leader outside U.S. regulatory reach; **$10.7B per PitchBook** (NPR's cite), but was in talks at a **$15B target** in April 2026 ([[Polymarket in talks to raise at 15B valuation]]). Backed by **ICE ($2B first tranche Oct 2025 at ~$9B — [[Polymarket raises $2B from ICE at $9B valuation]] — plus a fresh $600M add-on 2026-03-27**). Built a compliant U.S. on-ramp via the **$112M QCEX acquisition** (July 2025).
- **Distribution incumbents** already reselling Kalshi rails: **Coinbase** ([[Coinbase rolls out Kalshi prediction markets across US]]), **Robinhood** ([[Robinhood and Kalshi launch football prediction markets]]), **Cash App** ([[Cash App Pay now available on Kalshi]]).

**Position:** Meta is **behind on product (nothing shipped) but unmatched on distribution** (3B+ users across FB/IG/WhatsApp/Threads). Everyone else who entered — Coinbase, Robinhood, Cash App — did it by *reselling Kalshi*, not building. Meta is the first big platform trying to **build rather than partner** — after failing to buy.

**Why the landscape is this way / second-order:** The duopoly's moat is real-money liquidity + regulatory positioning (CFTC DCM status). Meta can't cheaply replicate that, so it enters at the **only layer it can win at speed — engagement/play money — which is also the layer with the weakest monetization**. That is why DraftKings and Robinhood shares reportedly dipped on the Arena news but Kalshi/Polymarket's core (real-money, institutional) is barely threatened: play money doesn't compete for the liquidity that makes prediction markets valuable.

## [2] Company history / fit
Meta's pattern (NPR's framing, largely factual): grew to 3B+ users via **acquisitions** — Instagram (2012), WhatsApp (2014) — and more recently **Limitless** (AI pendant, Dec 2025) and **Moltbook** (AI-bot social network). The FTC alleged a **"buy or bury"** strategy; Meta **won** the antitrust trial when Judge Boasberg ruled **2025-11-18** that it holds no social-networking monopoly and need not divest IG/WhatsApp (FTC appealing).

**Why Meta acts this way:** Post-antitrust-win, the legal cost of "buy or clone" just dropped — the ruling effectively blessed the historical playbook. Kalshi (a $22B, fast-growing, regulator-entangled asset) is exactly the kind of target Meta would *buy* if clean; when it isn't clean (or the founder won't sell), Meta defaults to **clone**. Arena also fits Meta's serial "shiny object" pattern (Tim Wu, ex-White House: metaverse, Libra/crypto — both abandoned) — funded by the ads cash cow, so failure is consequence-free. This is a **cheap optionality bet**, not a strategic pivot.

**Genuinely new (not in corpus):** (a) the **Zuckerberg–Mansour acquisition talks** and (b) **Arena's existence/mechanics**. The corpus has extensive Kalshi funding/partnership coverage but **nothing on Meta building a prediction market or trying to buy Kalshi** → this is a fresh development, not a reprint.

**Why the value-add is thin, one level deeper:** Prediction markets create value from **real-money liquidity** — the incentive to be right is what makes prices informative and pulls in volume ($28B/mo → ~$220B/mo combined, per NPR citing The Block). **Play money strips out exactly that incentive**, so Arena can't produce the informational or financial product Kalshi/Polymarket sell; it's an **engagement toy**, competing with Meta's own feed for attention, not with Kalshi for volume. Who captures the margin? Kalshi/Polymarket capture the real-money take-rate + institutional flow; Meta at best captures ad engagement — the low-monetization end. The **LLM-only, no-appeal resolution** is a genuine novelty but a **liability, not a moat**: at scale, disputed/wrong AI calls on elections and geopolitics are a reputational and possibly legal problem with no human backstop.

- **Next:** Prototype → internal test → public iOS/Android launch, date TBD. Watch for a later **pivot to real money** contingent on a federal (CFTC) license (Wallach's read). Kalshi's ~$40B rumored round and Polymarket's $15B round test whether the duopoly keeps compounding while Meta is still pre-launch.
- **Regulatory backdrop (the real story):** CFTC oversees Kalshi/Polymarket-US as DCMs; **>a dozen states** contest CFTC preemption; **Minnesota became the first state to fully ban** prediction markets. DOJ has **two insider-trading cases** (a U.S. soldier, ~$404K, on the Maduro capture; a Google engineer, >$1.2M, on search-trend data) plus a reported ex-Rep. Santos/Kalshi probe. Trump administration broadly permissive federally.
- **Sentiment:** Skeptical. Tim Wu (NPR): "Meta seems to clutch at every shiny object… I can't imagine a casino app with fake money is going to be much of a thrill." DraftKings/Robinhood shares reportedly fell on the news; commentators frame Arena as a possible "poison golden egg" for Meta's ad business (gambling-adjacent brand risk).

**Why the market goes this way / counterintuitive second-order:** The load-bearing risk for Meta isn't competition — it's **brand and regulatory contagion**. Bolting a betting-flavored product onto the ads machine invites the same "gambling under another name" scrutiny that gave Kalshi/Polymarket their state-level legal battles, while play money caps the upside. So the counterintuitive effect: **Meta's biggest asset (the ad cash cow) is what makes Arena risky, not safe** — it has everything to lose (brand, regulatory goodwill) and little incremental revenue to gain. The more strategically interesting fact buried here is that **Meta tried to buy the category leader and was rebuffed/deterred** — a rare case of the "buy" half of "buy or bury" failing, forcing the weaker "clone" half.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Prediction markets are one of the fastest-growing corners of consumer fintech. Combined monthly volume on Kalshi + Polymarket (+ Polymarket US) hit ~$44.8bn in June 2026, up ~75% MoM from ~$25.7bn in May, with Kalshi alone at ~$31.5bn (+87% MoM) — driven mostly by sports (per The Block, as of 2026-06). The note's own figure: ~$28bn/month traded across Kalshi + Polymarket in June 2025 vs ~$220bn a year later (per The Block, via NPR) — an inflated headline that likely conflates notional/turnover metrics; the more conservative Block series ($25–45bn/month in 2026) is the safer anchor. Structure: a **duopoly** (Kalshi + Polymarket), so this is a consolidated, network-effect market — liquidity begets liquidity, which is exactly why an ad-funded entrant with 3.56bn daily users (Meta) is tempting but not automatically winning. Why now: (1) a permissive US regulatory posture (Trump administration vowed to protect the category; Kalshi sits under CFTC/commodities oversight rather than state gaming law) opened the door; (2) sports betting became the growth engine, blurring the line with regulated sportsbooks (~$14bn/month wagered via legal US sportsbooks in 2025 — prediction-market sports volume now rivals it). Headwind: dozens of state-level legal battles (Minnesota banned prediction markets; DOJ opened two insider-trading cases) — the "gambling under a different name" fight is unresolved.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: monthly notional volume, take rate on contracts, and (for a social entrant) DAU/engagement. Key players: Kalshi (CFTC-regulated US exchange), Polymarket (offshore, crypto-settled), now Meta's **Arena** as a new entrant. Basis of competition: liquidity/depth for the incumbents; distribution for Meta. Recent moves: Meta struck a Kalshi integration partnership on Threads (March 2026); Arena development surfaced via NYT/NPR (June 2026). Meta's positioning is **deliberately niche/adjacent, not head-on**: Arena uses "play money" (daily game-style tokens), AI-generated questions and AI-adjudicated outcomes — no real-money wagering. That keeps it outside CFTC jurisdiction (per gaming lawyer D. Wallach, via NPR) and lets Meta test engagement before seeking a license. Moat read (analysis): Meta's edge is distribution + ad monetization, not a real-money order book; play-money removes the very take-rate economics that make Kalshi/Polymarket valuable, so this is a top-of-funnel engagement play, not a direct revenue attack. The Kalshi talks reportedly failed over price (Mansour unwilling to sell) and/or legal/ethical "messiness" (per NPR) — consistent with Meta's classic "buy or bury": it partnered, cloned, and left the door open.

**Comps & multiples.** No public trading multiples for the prediction-market names (all private), and Meta's own figures are irrelevant as a valuation comp for Arena. Private round valuations (private-round marks, NOT market caps):
- **Kalshi** — $22bn, Series F (May 2026), $1bn raised → [[Kalshi raises $1 billion Series F at $22 billion valuation]]; trajectory: $2bn (June 2025) → $11bn (Nov 2025) → $22bn — an ~11x step-up in ~11 months → [[Kalshi valuation reportedly jumps to $11B after $1B round]].
- **Polymarket** — ~$10.7bn (per PitchBook, via NPR); was in talks at ~$15bn in April 2026 → [[Polymarket in talks to raise at 15B valuation]].
- Implied price-per-volume sanity check (analysis): Kalshi at $22bn on ~$31.5bn *monthly* volume ≈ ~$378bn annualized run-rate → ~0.06x valuation/annualized-volume; low because volume is notional turnover, not revenue — the multiple that matters (valuation/net-take-rate-revenue) is **not disclosed** for either. So: distribution not computed, qualitative only. Kalshi's ~11x re-rating in <1yr looks rich even for hyper-growth and hinges entirely on the regulatory tailwind holding.
- **Meta (IR-grounded, for scale/funding context, not a comp):** Q1 2026 total revenue **$56.311bn (+33% YoY)**; income from operations **$22.872bn**; net income ~$26.77bn (per Meta Q1 2026 earnings release / 10-Q, 2026-04-29/30). Reality Labs: **$402m revenue vs ~$4.03bn operating loss** in Q1 2026. The RL line is the tell — Meta funds serial "shiny object" bets (metaverse, Libra, now Arena) out of the ad cash cow; Arena's cost is a rounding error against $22.9bn quarterly operating income, so Meta can afford to fail repeatedly (echoing critic Tim Wu, via NPR).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory whiplash for the incumbents** — the entire re-rating (Kalshi $2bn→$22bn) rests on a permissive federal stance; state bans (Minnesota) and DOJ insider-trading cases could reverse it, and a category-wide crackdown would hit private marks hard with no public price to cushion it.
2. **Distribution disintermediation of the incumbents** — Meta plugging prediction-market UX in front of 3.56bn DAU (even play-money) can normalize the behavior and later pivot to real money once licensed; that's the "buy or bury" risk incumbents face even after the failed sale.
3. **Play-money = no proven economics for Meta** — Arena has no take-rate revenue model yet and Meta explicitly hasn't ruled out real money later; execution/regulatory-license risk means this could stall like Libra, so it's optionality, not a needle-mover for Meta's P&L.

**What this changes (idea-lens) (analysis).** Meta entering (even sideways, with play money) validates prediction markets as a mainstream consumer category and pressures incumbents' distribution moat while leaving their liquidity moat intact — a partial re-rating catalyst for the space, not a consolidation. Falsifiable thesis: if Arena gains real-money capability via a CFTC license within ~12 months, it becomes a genuine threat to Kalshi/Polymarket's take-rate economics; if it stays play-money-only, it's an engagement toy and incumbents keep the monetizable market. Watch: any Meta license application to CFTC, and whether Kalshi/Polymarket volume growth (Block series) holds through post-World-Cup seasonality.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
> Earnings layer (Meta Q1 2026, quarter ended March 31, 2026; reported April 29, 2026). Framed around the news that Zuckerberg eyed a Kalshi buy before building Arena — i.e. Meta funding a prediction-markets bet off a hyperscale ad engine. Primary source = Meta's own filing (8-K Ex-99.1 / earnings release); consensus from public aggregators.

**Verdict (headline read).** BEAT · revenue $56.31bn (+33% YoY; vs public consensus ~$55.52bn, a ~$0.79bn / +1.4% beat) · GAAP diluted EPS $10.44 (+62% YoY) but flattered by a one-off tax benefit — ex-benefit underlying EPS ~$7.31 (still ahead of ~$6.66 consensus, ~+9.6%). Fastest revenue growth since 2021. Guidance: Q2'26 revenue $58–61bn, FY total expenses $162–169bn reaffirmed, but FY26 capex RAISED to $125–145bn (from $115–135bn) — the market took the beat as secondary to the capex hike (stock fell ~6–7% after hours despite the top-line beat, on some tapes).

**Key figures (with growth).**
- Total revenue **$56,311M, +33% YoY** ($42,314M in Q1'25); ~2% FX tailwind per the CFO outlook.
- Advertising revenue **$55,024M** (+33%; +$13.63bn) — impressions +19% YoY, average price per ad +12% YoY.
- Other revenue $885M (+74% from $510M); Family of Apps revenue $55,909M (+33%); Reality Labs revenue $402M (-2%).
- Income from operations **$22,872M, +30% YoY** ($17,555M); total operating margin **41% (flat YoY)**.
- Total costs and expenses $33,439M (+35%): R&D $17,699M (+46%), cost of revenue $10,218M (+35%) — cost growth outpaced revenue, offset by scale.
- Net income **$26,773M vs $15,688M** in Q1'25 (per Q1'26 earnings presentation series); GAAP diluted **EPS $10.44 vs $6.43, +62%**.
- Effective tax rate **-23%** (a $5,021M tax BENEFIT) vs +9% ($1,738M expense) in Q1'25 — driven by an **$8.03bn income tax benefit** (U.S. Treasury Notice 2026-7, partly reversing the $15.93bn non-cash charge from Q3'25 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act). This is the single biggest reason net income/EPS look explosive.
- Family DAP 3.56bn avg for March 2026, +4% YoY (slight QoQ dip from Iran internet disruptions and a WhatsApp restriction in Russia).
- Q1 capex $19.8bn (property & equipment purchases $19.00bn); operating cash flow $32.23bn; cash & marketable securities $81.18bn; dividends/equivalents $1.35bn.

**By segment / driver.** Growth is ~100% ads-engine: Family of Apps operating income **$26,900M (+24%)** carries the company; Reality Labs is still an **operating loss of $(4,028)M** (Q1'25 $(4,210)M) — narrowed only 4%. The Arena/prediction-markets push (and the road-not-taken Kalshi acquisition) sits inside this same "spend the ad windfall on optionality" posture as Reality Labs and the AI capex line: FoA prints ~48% segment margin while Meta funds money-losing bets. Arena uses **play money, not real bets** — so it is a product/engagement experiment, not a fintech/take-rate revenue line, and there is **no disclosed revenue or user metric for Arena in this filing** [no data].

**vs expectations / prior period.** Revenue beat public consensus (~$55.52bn as of the late-Apr 2026 print) by ~+1.4%; underlying EPS beat by ~+9.6%. But the HEADLINE EPS beat (+62% YoY) is largely tax-driven — de-PR flag: strip the $8.03bn benefit and profit growth is far more modest. YoY momentum accelerated to +33%, the fastest since 2021, vs the low-20s% range in recent quarters ([[Meta 8-K 2025-04-30]] Q1'25; [[Meta-Reports-Third-Quarter-2024-Results-2024]]).

**Guidance / forward.** Q2'26 revenue **$58–61bn** (introduced). FY26 total expenses **$162–169bn reaffirmed** (unchanged). FY26 capex **RAISED to $125–145bn** (from $115–135bn) — cited higher component pricing plus incremental data-center cost; management reaffirmed it expects FY26 operating income above the prior year. Tone: confident on ads/AI monetization, aggressive on infra spend. What they stay quiet on: any ROI timeline on the capex ramp and any economics for Arena — consistent with prediction markets being an engagement optionality play funded by ads, not a near-term earnings driver.

**Thesis-flags.**
1. *Tax-flattered bottom line.* EPS +62% is mostly the $8.03bn Notice 2026-7 benefit and a -23% tax rate → underlying growth (~$7.31 EPS) is the real number. Second-order: reported EPS momentum is not repeatable next quarter, so a "clean" Q2 can optically decelerate even if the business is fine.
2. *Capex outrunning the P&L.* FY26 capex to $125–145bn while operating margin is flat at 41% and RL still loses ~$4bn/qtr → free-cash-flow compression risk if ad growth normalizes below the +33% pace. This is the same balance-sheet muscle that lets Meta fund Arena/Kalshi-style bets — and the same reason the market flinched.
3. *Ads engine is the entire thesis.* +33% ad growth (impressions +19%, price +12%) funds every experiment (RL, AI, Arena). Any moderation in ad pricing/impressions directly de-funds the optionality portfolio.
4. *Arena is not (yet) a fintech line.* Play-money design + zero disclosed metrics → treat as engagement/AI-distribution experiment, not a prediction-markets revenue bet vs Kalshi; the "Zuckerberg eyed Kalshi" frame is strategic optionality, not a reported segment.
