Atome Philippines closes USD 81 mln loan with Asia United
Paula Albu
10 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Atome Philippines has closed a wholesale facility worth approximately USD 81 million (PHP 5 billion) with Asia United Bank (AUB), securing local currency capital to support the company's continued expansion in the Philippines.
Funding to support card expansion
Proceeds from the facility will primarily support continued growth of the Atome PayLater Anywhere Card, which has been issued to more than 3 million Filipinos. According to Atome, up to 80% of cardholders are first-time users, and 65% are women, with the majority using the card for everyday expenses such as groceries, food, household items, and telecommunications and utility bill payments. Card adoption has extended beyond Metro Manila to customers across Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao.
Broader product portfolio and funding diversification
In addition to the PayLater Anywhere Card, Atome's product portfolio in the Philippines includes lending, savings, and insurance offerings, aimed at expanding access to financial services for Filipinos who remain underserved by traditional banking channels. The facility, denominated in Philippine pesos, adds a local currency funding line to Atome's broader funding base, which the company said reflects AUB's assessment of its credit profile and operating model.
Company commentary
A company official at AUB said the credit facility reflects the bank's approach to supporting fintech platforms that contribute to the Philippine financial ecosystem, while maintaining credit standards. Another AUB official said the partnership is intended to advance financial inclusion while preserving rigorous credit assessment practices.
A company official at Atome Philippines said the facility validates the company's market position and provides local currency funding at scale, adding that it is intended to support the company's growth while diversifying its funding mix with local capital. The official said the facility is also intended to support the Philippine economy and the company's aim of serving underbanked Filipinos with accessible financial tools.
Positioning within the Philippine fintech sector
The facility reflects continued interest from Philippine banks in providing local currency funding to fintech platforms operating in consumer lending and buy-now-pay-later services, an area that has seen growing demand among first-time credit users, particularly outside major urban centres. As buy-now-pay-later products continue to expand into segments underserved by traditional banking channels, partnerships between fintechs and local banks may continue to serve as a mechanism for diversifying funding sources while extending credit access more broadly across regional markets in the Philippines.

# Context-enrichment: Atome Philippines secures $81M (PHP 5B) wholesale facility from Asia United Bank
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Atome Philippines closed a **wholesale credit facility of ~USD 81M (PHP 5 billion)** with **Asia United Bank (AUB)**, a listed Philippine commercial bank. This is **debt (a funding line), not an equity round** — it lets Atome lend AUB's balance sheet (or draw against receivables) to fund consumer loans/BNPL, not "raise capital" in the VC sense.

Key de-PR points:
- **It's PHP-denominated.** That is the actual news, not the size. Atome's regional funding to date has been USD (HSBC $200M, Lending Ark $75M, the $345M syndicate). Local-currency funding removes FX mismatch: Atome lends to Filipinos in pesos but had been funding in dollars → this line hedges the currency book. The PR frames this as "validation"; the real substance is **funding-mix diversification and FX de-risking**, which is a treasury move, not a growth event.
- **"3 million cardholders" is a select stat.** The Maya-Bank note (Sep 2025) already claimed "over 4 million BNPL customers in the Philippines." The AUB release cites "3 million+ *PayLater Anywhere Card* holders" — a narrower product cohort, not total users. The 80%-first-time / 65%-women / "beyond Metro Manila" stats are financial-inclusion framing to make the deal palatable to a bank + regulators.
- **Terms undisclosed.** No pricing, tenor, advance rate, or whether it is a term loan vs a revolving warehouse. AUB's quotes ("maintaining credit standards," "rigorous credit assessment") signal a conservative, secured structure — i.e. AUB is lending against Atome's receivables with covenants, not taking unsecured platform risk.
- **Legal entity:** Atome's PH lending runs through **Neuroncredit Financing Company Inc.** (SEC CS201816338, Certificate of Authority No. 1178) — an SEC-licensed financing company. That licence is what lets a bank fund it wholesale.

→ Why structured this way: a **local commercial bank wholesale line is the cheapest, most scalable peso funding** for a foreign-parented lender. Atome is systematically laddering funding sources (fund managers → global banks → parent equity → local banks) so no single source caps its loan-book growth. This deal is the **"local bank" rung** — expected, incremental, low-novelty.

## [1] Competitors / peers
The pattern — **fintech lender takes a wholesale/warehouse line from a local bank** — is the dominant SE-Asia funding template right now:
- [[Akulaku Finance secures $27.5M debt facility from Danamon]] (Jun 2026, Indonesia, PHP-equivalent Rp500B from MUFG-owned Danamon) — same mechanism, smaller, local-currency, working-capital.
- **Finvolution/JuanHand** launched the *luvit* card **with AUB as BIN sponsor** (May 2026) — the SAME bank (AUB) is banking multiple PH fintech lenders; AUB is positioning as *the* fintech-funding bank in the Philippines (analysis).
- [[Maya Bank and Atome partner on $48M Philippine lending]] (Sep 2025) — Atome's *own* prior PH bank tie-up (loan channelling). AUB now adds a second bank relationship.
- Direct PH BNPL/lending rivals: **GCash/GLoan** (distribution moat via the GCash wallet, pre-qualified GScore), **Kredivo PH** (Kredivo Philippines Lending Corp., SEC-registered), **BillEase**, **Home Credit**. GCash is the structural threat — it owns the wallet + credit-scoring rails Atome must acquire customers into.

→ Second-order: the fact that **the same bank (AUB) funds both Atome and Finvolution** means the funding is commoditized — banks compete to lend to fintechs, which is good for Atome's cost of funds but signals the deal is **not a differentiator**. Everyone can get a peso warehouse line; the edge is in origination/underwriting, not funding access.

## [2] Company history / fit
Atome = the BNPL/lending arm of **Advance Intelligence Group** (Singapore; backers SoftBank Vision Fund 2, Warburg Pincus). Philippine funding ladder:
- **Nov 2024:** $200M HSBC-arranged regional facility — [[Atome Financial secures $200 million HSBC-led credit facility]].
- **Jun 2025:** $75M asset-backed facility from **Lending Ark** (CLSA Capital Partners fund) — PH-specific.
- **Sep 2025:** $48M **Maya Bank** loan-channelling — [[Maya Bank and Atome partner on $48M Philippine lending]].
- **Jan 2026:** **$345M** upsized regional syndicated facility (HSBC + DBS, +SMBC/Cathay/Fubon/SPD) — [[Atome secures $345M debt facility for SE Asia BNPL]]; covers SG/MY/PH.
- **May 2026:** **$149M** parent equity injection from Advance Intelligence — [[Advance Intelligence injects $149 million into Atome Financial]].
- **Jul 2026:** **$81M PHP AUB** wholesale line ← this note.

Financials (from prior notes/web): FY2024 operating income +63% YoY to $236M, >$2B GMV (+50%); FY2025 annualized net revenue >$500M, annualized GMV >$6B, Dec-2025 monthly GMV +70% YoY. Group reached profitability.

→ Why Atome acts this way: a consumer lender's growth is **funding-constrained, not demand-constrained** — every peso lent must be funded. With GMV compounding ~50-70%/yr, Atome needs a continuous pipeline of ever-cheaper, longer, local-currency funding. The Philippines is its flagged strategic PH market; hence a **cadence of PH-specific raises roughly every quarter**. This AUB line fits that cadence exactly — it is business-as-usual balance-sheet plumbing.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Novelty: low.** This is Atome's ~4th distinct PH funding event in ~13 months and the Nth SE-Asia fintech-bank warehouse line in the corpus. The only genuinely new elements: (a) a **new lender relationship (AUB)** diversifying counterparty risk, and (b) **peso denomination at scale** (FX hedge). Neither is a product, market, or capability launch.

Traction is **real but pre-existing**, not created by this deal: the card is live, 3M+ holders, expanded across Luzon/Visayas/Mindanao — that traction is what *unlocked* the facility, not what the facility produces. AUB is lending *because* Atome already has a performing book.

→ Who captures the margin: In a wholesale/warehouse structure, **AUB captures a low-risk senior spread**; Atome keeps the (riskier) consumer-credit spread and the customer relationship. The value-add to Atome is **lower/cheaper/hedged funding → protects net interest margin as it scales** — a defensive, not offensive, gain. What breaks it: rising PH consumer-credit delinquency (BNPL loss rates), or SEC/BSP tightening of financing-company rules (as OJK did in Indonesia in Dec 2025, per the Akulaku note) — regulatory risk is the live tail.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Expect **more of the same**: additional local-bank warehouse lines across SG/MY/PH, continued push of the PayLater Anywhere Card, and Atome's stated expansion into **savings, insurance (Chubb partnership), and cash loans (Atome Cash, up to ₱55k)** — a super-app-ification of a BNPL name. Parent Advance Intelligence has reportedly weighed a ~$200M raise, so a group-level equity event / eventual IPO is the real catalyst to watch — this facility is a step toward IPO-readiness (clean, diversified, local funding).

Sentiment/backdrop: SE-Asia BNPL is structurally growing (~22.5% CAGR cited; regional digital economy heading past $1T GMV by 2030) but **regulators are tightening** — Indonesia's OJK restricted BNPL to banks/financing cos (Dec 2025). The PH SEC/BSP could follow. 

→ Counterintuitive second-order: the flood of cheap bank funding into fintech lenders **inflates loan books faster than credit cycles have been tested** in these first-time-borrower cohorts (80% first-time users here). The "financial-inclusion" framing that wins bank funding is the same cohort most exposed in a downturn — so easy funding today can amplify losses tomorrow. The risk isn't access to capital; it's the **quality of the book that capital is funding** (analysis/hypothesis).

## FRESHNESS / DUPLICATE VERDICT
**FRESH** (but low-importance). This is a **distinct new facility**: new lender (AUB, not HSBC/DBS/Lending Ark/Maya), new currency (PHP), new size ($81M/PHP5B), July 2026. It is NOT the $345M Jan-2026 syndicated facility ([[Atome secures $345M debt facility for SE Asia BNPL]]) nor the $75M Lending Ark line — no existing note covers this AUB deal. Fresh, but it is the 4th PH funding item, so weight is capped: importance 2/5.

3. **What are the actual terms?** — OPEN. No disclosed pricing, tenor, advance rate, or secured/unsecured structure. AUB's "rigorous credit assessment" language implies a secured, covenanted line against receivables. Without terms, the deal's economics can't be judged.

4. **Why does PHP denomination matter?** — It removes FX mismatch (Atome lends in pesos, had funded largely in USD). The real substance is FX de-risking + funding diversification, not scale.

5. **Do the "3M cardholders" reconcile with the "4M BNPL customers" claimed in Sep 2025?** — Different cohorts: 3M = PayLater Anywhere *Card* holders; 4M = total PH BNPL customers. Not contradictory, but the release cherry-picks the card metric. Total-user growth is not disclosed here.

6. **Is AUB taking real risk on Atome, or is this low-risk senior lending?** — Likely low-risk: AUB captures a senior spread against a performing receivables book; Atome retains consumer-credit risk. AUB also banks Finvolution (luvit card) — it is building a fintech-funding franchise, not making a concentrated bet.

7. **How many PH funding events is this now?** — ~4th in 13 months ($200M HSBC Nov-24, $75M Lending Ark Jun-25, $48M Maya Sep-25, $81M AUB Jul-26) + the $345M regional syndicate + $149M parent injection. High cadence → this one is incremental, not a milestone.

8. **What does the facility actually produce vs merely fund?** — It funds already-existing traction (live card, 3M+ users). The traction unlocked the loan; the loan doesn't create the traction. No new product/market/capability.

9. **Where's the regulatory tail?** — Live. Indonesia's OJK restricted BNPL to banks/financing cos (Dec 2025, per [[Akulaku Finance secures $27.5M debt facility from Danamon]]). PH SEC/BSP could tighten financing-company/BNPL rules; Atome operates via licensed Neuroncredit Financing Company Inc.

10. **Is the "80% first-time users / 65% women" stat a strength or a risk flag?** — Both. It wins bank + regulator goodwill (inclusion), but first-time-borrower cohorts are the least credit-tested and most loss-prone in a downturn. Easy funding into an untested book is a second-order risk.

11. **Who's the real competitive threat in PH?** — GCash/GLoan (owns wallet + GScore distribution), plus Kredivo PH, BillEase, Home Credit. Atome must acquire into wallets it doesn't own. Funding access is not the moat; origination/underwriting is.

12. **Does cheap, commoditized bank funding help or commoditize Atome?** — It lowers cost of funds but signals funding is undifferentiated (AUB funds rivals too). Edge must come from underwriting, not funding.

13. **What's the group-level catalyst behind the cadence?** — IPO-readiness. Advance Intelligence has weighed a ~$200M raise; building clean, diversified, local-currency funding lines is classic pre-IPO balance-sheet grooming. The facility is a step, not the story.

14. **Could delinquency data change the read?** — OPEN. No disclosed loss rates / NPLs for the PH book. Without book-quality data, "validation" is unverifiable.

15. **Bottom line — does this shift the central question?** — No. It's routine balance-sheet plumbing. The central question stays: can Atome's underwriting hold up as it scales an untested first-time-borrower book faster than credit cycles have been tested — not "can it raise money" (it clearly can).

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** PH BNPL is a fast-growing, structurally underbanked market. Per a Feb-2026 ResearchAndMarkets databook (via GlobeNewswire), the Philippines BNPL sector was ~$7.37bn in 2025 and is forecast to ~$18.81bn by 2031 (2026–31 CAGR ~16.1%; 2022–25 CAGR ~25.9%) — treat as one analyst-house estimate (competing sources put 2026 GMV materially lower, so the level is soft; the growth direction is the robust signal). Drivers/"why now": low credit-card penetration + a large unbanked/underbanked base → cards/PayLater become a first credit product (Atome says up to 80% of its PH cardholders are first-time users, 65% women — DATA from PR, not independently verified). Structure: fragmented, many local players (BillEase, TendoPay, Cashalo, UnaPay) plus regional platforms; entry barrier is not tech but *funding cost + credit underwriting on thin-file borrowers* and regulation — Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) is tightening BNPL transparency/risk rules. This deal exemplifies the "why now": the binding constraint for SEA lenders is local-currency wholesale funding, and PH universal banks are now willing to supply it. `(analysis)`

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: GMV, receivables/loan-book size, funding cost & mix (local-currency vs USD), first-payment-default / NPL on thin-file cohorts, active users. Key players in PH BNPL/lending: BillEase, TendoPay, Cashalo, GCash (GGives), Home Credit PH, Akulaku, plus Atome. Basis of competition: distribution (merchant/e-com integrations, wallet reach) and cost-of-capital, not product features. Recent moves: Atome closed a **US$345M upsized syndicated debt facility (Jan 2026)** for SEA (IR: annualised net revenue >US$500M, annualised GMV ~US$6B at FY2025) and a **US$149M equity injection from parent Advance Intelligence (May 2026)**; this AUB PHP 5bn (~US$81M) line (10 Jul 2026) is incremental local-currency funding on top. Atome's position in PH: scaled challenger, not the leader — the AUB facility validates its credit profile and diversifies funding into pesos (removing FX mismatch on peso-denominated receivables). Moat: distribution + parent balance-sheet backing (switching costs/network low) → `(analysis)`. Note the "3 million cardholders" figure here vs "2 million+" cited by PH press in Jan 2026 — either fast growth or PR rounding; flag, don't reconcile.

**Comps & multiples.** No public equity multiple for Atome (private). This is a debt/warehouse-facility event, so the relevant comp is funding structure & scale, not P/E:

**Risk flags.**
1. **Credit/underwriting on thin-file borrowers** — 80% first-time users means limited repayment history; a PH consumer-credit downturn or rate move hits BNPL NPLs first; the PR is silent on delinquency/NPL and on facility pricing (cost of the peso funding). Second-order: if losses rise, wholesale lines get repriced or pulled.
2. **Regulatory** — BSP is actively tightening BNPL disclosure/risk rules; stricter affordability/consumer-protection rules could slow first-time-user acquisition, the exact growth engine here.
3. **Funding-dependence / thin equity cushion** — growth is debt-financed (US$345M + this line) on a business only recently at full-year profit (FY2024); reliance on external wholesale funding + parent injections is the model's structural fragility if capital markets tighten.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Not a re-rating — a routine, positive funding data-point confirming a SEA-wide pattern: local universal banks (here AUB) are becoming the funding layer for fintech consumer-lenders, de-risking FX and extending credit-access reach. Falsifiable thesis: PH banks keep expanding local-currency lines to BNPL platforms through 2026–27. What would break it / trigger to watch: a BSP tightening or a visible NPL spike at a PH BNPL peer (BillEase/Cashalo/Home Credit) that makes banks pull back — watch AUB/peer disclosures and BSP circulars.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
This is a funding-facility deal, not an earnings print — no results are reported *in the news* itself. But both counterparties have real reported figures that ground the lending-facility thesis (funding capacity + credit quality). The IR DB covers `atome` (private); `asia-united-bank` (PSE: AUB) is a listed bank with public results. Lead entity = AUB.

**Verdict (headline read on the facility, not the deal).** AUB — IN-LINE / modest BEAT: Q1 2026 net income record P3.2bn (+1.8% YoY vs P3.14bn), but the quality is in the mix — NIM 5.3% (+20bps YoY), loans +9.4%, NPL 0.44%. A well-capitalised (CAR 19.3%), high-margin, pristine-asset-quality lender is extending an ~US$81M (PHP 5bn) wholesale line to Atome — this is a low-cost-of-capital, credit-disciplined bank choosing to underwrite Atome's BNPL book. Atome (private): FY2025 annualised net revenue >US$500M, annualised GMV ~US$6B, record full-year profitability — a funding-worthy borrower, not a cash-burning one.

**Key figures — Asia United Bank (PSE: AUB), Q1 2026 (reported 2026-05-18).**
- Net income: **P3.2bn, +1.8% YoY** (from P3.14bn Q1 2025) — record quarterly.
- NIM: **5.3%, +20bps YoY** (from 5.1%) — supported by CASA-to-deposits climbing to 78% from 69% (lower-cost funding).
- Total loans & receivables: **P276.2bn, +9.4% YoY** — the loan book is growing, and wholesale lines to fintechs like this Atome facility are part of that book expansion.
- Deposits: P335.5bn, +8.9% YoY.
- ROE **19.3%** · ROA **3.2%** · cost-to-income **30.4%** (from 32.6%).
- Asset quality: **NPL ratio 0.44%**, NPL coverage 111.2%. CAR 19.3%, CET1 18.7%.

**Key figures — Atome Financial / Advance Intelligence Group (private, borrower).**
- FY2025: **annualised net revenue >US$500M**, **annualised GMV ~US$6B**, record full-year profit (per 2026-01 syndicated-facility disclosure). Dec-2025 record monthly GMV, +70% YoY.
- Trajectory: FY2024 net revenue US$280M (+45% YoY), GMV US$2.5bn (+35% YoY) → FY2025 revenue >US$500M / GMV ~US$6B implies ~+79% revenue and ~+140% GMV run-rate acceleration. Operating income record US$236M, first full-year profit in FY2024.
- Philippines-specific: PayLater Anywhere Card issued to >3M Filipinos; 80% first-time users, 65% women (per the news) — a thin-file, first-credit borrower base, which is exactly the credit-quality angle AUB is underwriting.

**By driver.** AUB's +1.8% net-income growth understates the operating story: the profit was near-flat only because the base (P3.14bn) was already record; the *drivers* — NIM expansion, +9.4% loan growth, falling cost-to-income — are all improving. The Atome line sits inside that +9.4% loan-book growth as incremental, spread-accretive lending. For Atome, this PHP-denominated wholesale facility diversifies a funding stack that in Jan-2026 was upsized to a US$345M syndicated facility (BlackRock/InnoVen US$80M in 2024, EvolutionX up to US$100M in 2024, US$149M equity injection from Advance Intelligence in May-2026) — local-currency funding removes FX mismatch against a peso-denominated receivables book.

**vs expectations / prior period.** AUB — no public consensus pulled for a Philippine mid-cap bank [UNSOURCED for consensus]; vs prior year the print is a qualitative modest beat (record net income, margin up, asset quality intact). Atome — private, no consensus; vs prior year FY2025 is a clear acceleration on both revenue and GMV. Related base reports: [[Atome upsizes syndicated debt facility to US$345M]] context and prior FY2024 profit milestone are the internal trend markers (no in-corpus earnings note found for either entity to wikilink).

**Guidance / forward.** AUB gave no formal guidance in the Q1 release; management framed the result as resilience "despite permacrisis" / Middle East shock — cautious tone, defensive framing, but backed by 0.44% NPL and CASA gains (analysis). Atome gave no forward guidance; the deal commentary is validation-style ("validates market position", "funding at scale"). Neither party disclosed the facility's pricing/tenor — cost of the PHP funding is not in the public domain [UNSOURCED], which is the single most thesis-relevant undisclosed number (spread between AUB's cost of funds and the on-lend rate to Atome's cardholders).

**Thesis-flags.**
1. **Funding capacity — confirmed, low-cost.** AUB's 78% CASA + 5.3% NIM means it funds cheaply and lends at spread; a bank with that funding base extending an ~US$81M peso line signals Atome can access *local, low-cost, FX-matched* capital → reduces Atome's blended cost of funds and FX risk → supports margin on the Philippine card book. Second-order: de-risks Atome's path to sustained profitability post the FY2024 first-profit milestone.
2. **Credit quality — the underwriter's asset quality is the tell.** AUB runs a 0.44% NPL / 111% coverage book; it "maintains rigorous credit assessment" (per its own commentary) yet still underwrote Atome. A pristine-asset-quality bank taking Atome exposure is a third-party credit endorsement of Atome's BNPL underwriting — more informative than Atome's own self-assessment. De-PR flag: neither side disclosed *Atome's* loss rates / NPLs on the card book — the 80%-first-time-user, 65%-women mix (news) is a thin-file cohort; the facility's quality ultimately rides on Atome's collections, which are the silent number here.
3. **AUB growth is loan-led, not margin-led erosion.** +9.4% loan growth with NIM *up* 20bps means AUB is adding volume without sacrificing spread; fintech wholesale lines (like Atome's) are a way to deploy its excess CASA into higher-yield receivables → sustainable if NPLs hold.
4. **Atome acceleration is real but on annualised/run-rate figures.** FY2025 ">US$500M annualised net revenue / ~US$6B GMV" are *run-rate/annualised* self-reported numbers (not audited FY figures like the US$236M FY2024 operating income) — treat as directionally strong but softer-sourced than the FY2024 audited print (analysis).
