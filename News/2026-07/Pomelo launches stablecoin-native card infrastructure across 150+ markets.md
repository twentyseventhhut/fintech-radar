# Context-enrichment: Pomelo Goes Global with Stablecoin-Native Card Infrastructure Across 150+ Markets
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Disambiguation (settled first).** There are three "Pomelo"s. This is **Pomelo the Buenos Aires / LatAm card-issuing infrastructure firm** (pomelo.la, CEO/co-founder Gastón Irigoyen) — NOT the fashion brand, and NOT the California-based cross-border remittance start-up "Pomelo" that Zepz acquired in Jan 2026. The stablecoin/remittance framing in this note belongs to the LatAm Pomelo, which is expanding *out of* LatAm; its whole model is BaaS card-issuing for banks/fintechs (Santander, BBVA, Bancolombia, Western Union, Rappi, PicPay, PayJoy, Stori, AstroPay, Binance). Internal corpus `company/pomelo` = this same entity — confirmed via prior notes below.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On ~2026-07-03 Pomelo announced it is taking its card infrastructure global — a "stablecoin-native global card" that lets clients issue USD- and stablecoin-denominated cards to users in 150+ markets without a local entity/license in each country. Bundled alongside: **agentic payments** (single-use cards for AI/OTA flows), **tokenization-as-a-service**, and **automated chargeback management**. Rails are Mastercard and Visa; Spanish-language press (El Cronista, Diario Financiero, Bank Magazine) says the global card is already **operational** with virtual issuance, Apple/Google Pay, physical-card production/distribution, and **real-time settlement in USD or stablecoins** (initially Circle's USDC).
- **Why this framing / what it reveals:** This is the *activation* of a roadmap Pomelo pre-announced with its Jan 2026 $55M Series C, when it said the round would "give rise to a stablecoin-native global card offering, alongside tools for payment tokenisation and chargeback management." So July 3 is "announced → launched," not a fresh strategy. The PR leads with "150+ markets" — but that number tracks Mastercard/Visa network reach and Pomelo's existing ~150 clients, not 150 markets of live cardholder adoption. "Stablecoin-native" is doing marketing work: the differentiated piece is real-time USDC settlement; the card itself still runs on Visa/Mastercard rails (the actual value capture layer).

## [1] Competitors / peers
Stablecoin-card infrastructure is now crowded and better-capitalized above Pomelo:
- **Rain** (Puerto Rico) — issuance + program mgmt + settlement; **$250M Series C (Jan 2026)**; positions as a generalized stablecoin payments abstraction layer.
- **Bridge / Stripe** — Stripe acquired Bridge (~$1.1B, 2025); Bridge got a conditional OCC national trust charter (Feb 2026); card-issuing largely in private beta.
- **RedotPay** — consumer-facing stablecoin card, 6M+ users, 100+ countries, **$107M Series B (Dec 2025)**.
- **Nium** — launched stablecoin card issuance early 2026, 190-country payout net, single-API Visa+Mastercard, Coinbase liquidity.
- **Mastercard/Visa themselves** — Mastercard acquiring BVNK (up to $1.8B); Visa reported ~$4.6B annualized on-chain stablecoin settlement (Q1 2026). Networks are moving up the stack.
- **Position:** Pomelo is a **regional (LatAm) leader going global into a category where global-first players already have scale and more capital.** Ahead of Rain/RedotPay on real bank/enterprise clients in LatAm; behind on global footprint, funding, and US regulatory standing (Bridge's OCC charter). Parity at best on the "single-API multi-network issuing" pitch (Nium says the same).
- **Why the landscape is this way / 2nd-order:** The margin sits with whoever controls (a) the card network rails and (b) the settlement/FX spread. Pomelo owns neither — it rides Visa/Mastercard and settles in someone else's stablecoin (USDC/Circle). So "stablecoin-native" risks being a *feature*, not a moat; the durable question shifts from "can Pomelo build a stablecoin card?" (yes, everyone can) to "what does Pomelo capture that Rain/Nium/networks don't?" — answer is its LatAm client relationships and credit-processing depth, which are regional, not global.

## [2] Company history / fit
Pomelo trajectory: founded 2021 (ex-Naranja X / Mercado Pago people); Series A $35M → Series B $40M → **Series C $55M (Jan 2026)**, ~$160M total. ~150 clients, claimed +250% revenue over two years, 2.8M+ Stori cards issued in Mexico. Prior corpus milestones: AstroPay regional rollout (Jun 2025), Binance crypto cards in Brazil (Oct 2025), postpaid credit in Brazil (Oct 2025), GOcuotas card in Argentina (Mar 2026), Chile CMF prepaid-issuer license (Jun 2026).
- **Why it acts this way:** Card-issuing BaaS is a commodity take-rate business with regional licensing moats. To earn a *software* multiple and escape LatAm TAM limits, Pomelo needs (a) global reach and (b) a higher-value narrative — stablecoin settlement + agentic payments provide both. The Chile CMF license (Jun 2026) and the global-card launch are the same strategy: reduce the "you need a local entity per market" friction that is Pomelo's core sales objection. Logical fit — not a pivot.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Genuinely new **for Pomelo**, not for the market. The value-add is packaging: an existing LatAm issuer-processor now offers cross-border USD/USDC settlement + tokenization + agentic single-use cards through one API, letting a fintech launch globally without per-country entities.
- **Traction reality check:** The Spanish press says the global card is "operational" and lists a client roster that spans regions (Western Union, BBVA, Santander, Coppel, Kavak, Didi, Binance, AstroPay, PayJoy) — but these are Pomelo's *existing* clients; there is **no disclosed count of cards issued abroad, GMV, or live cardholders in the new 150-market footprint.** So "live product, unproven global adoption." No numbers on the stablecoin-settlement volume, no named USDC/Circle contract terms, no FX-spread economics.
- **Who captures the margin:** Visa/Mastercard (rails), Circle (USDC float/redemption), and the client bank (interchange). Pomelo takes a processing/platform fee. "Stablecoin-native" doesn't change who captures the margin — it just adds a settlement option. The multiple story rests on volume of clients going global through Pomelo; it breaks if networks (BVNK/Visa) or global-first issuers (Nium/Rain) commoditize the same single-API global-issuing pitch.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Expect Pomelo to court non-LatAm fintechs/banks and publish adoption metrics to validate the "global" claim; watch for a named Circle/USDC deal and additional network-country certifications. Sentiment on stablecoin cards is hot (Visa $4.6B annualized on-chain settlement; stablecoin supply >$270B, 40x since 2020) — but capital and regulatory standing are concentrating at the top (Stripe/Bridge OCC charter, Mastercard/BVNK, Rain $250M). 
- **Why / 2nd-order (analysis):** The tailwind is real, but it is being captured by the networks and the best-funded infra players. Pomelo's edge is regional trust and credit-processing, which don't automatically travel. Counterintuitively, launching "global" against better-capitalized global-first rivals can *dilute* focus from the defensible LatAm franchise. The bull case is that Pomelo's real global buyers are LatAm-adjacent (remittance corridors — Western Union is already a client), where its relationships do travel. Risk: regulatory (stablecoin/US), FX and settlement-liquidity, and commoditization by Visa/Mastercard moving up-stack.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Stablecoin-linked card issuing sits at the intersection of two markets: card-issuer processing (Marqeta/Lithic/Stripe Issuing) and stablecoin payment rails. No clean TAM is publicly verifiable — as a proxy for scale, stablecoins processed ~$26tn of on-chain volume in 2025 with a ~$322bn market cap (per insights4vc, via Substack, as of 2026; both figures are third-party estimates → treat as directional, not audited). Structure: the issuing-infra layer is fragmenting fast — pure-play issuer-processors (Marqeta, Lithic), network-owned rails (Visa/Mastercard settling in USDC), and a new cohort of stablecoin-native issuers (Rain, Bridge/Stripe, Pomelo). Barriers are high: card-network membership, per-market licensing/BIN sponsorship, and regulatory/compliance surface — which is exactly what these players sell as a service. "Why now": networks are actively pulling stablecoins into settlement (Mastercard Crypto Partner Program lists Pomelo; Visa/Mastercard exploring their own stablecoin per Forbes/CoinDesk, Jun 2026), and issuers want cross-border reach without stitching together local rails. Second-order effect: if settlement moves to USDC, the value migrates from correspondent-banking FX spreads to whoever owns the issuing + conversion API.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: card TPV/annualized volume, interchange take (issuer interchange ~1–2% of ticket, per insights4vc), number of live markets/BINs, and conversion/FX spread. Key players & basis of competition: **Rain** — stablecoin-native issuing across 150+ countries, ~$3bn annualized card volume, credit+prepaid with Mastercard (May 2026), $250M Series C at $1.95bn (Jan 2026, per Bloomberg/Crunchbase); **Bridge (Stripe, acq. ~$1.1bn Feb 2025)** — stablecoin-backed cards via Stripe Issuing, live in ~30 countries targeting 100+ by end-2026, "Open Issuance" platform; **Marqeta/Lithic** — incumbent issuer-processors adding stablecoin support. Competition is on distribution (network + geographic footprint) and product breadth, not price. **Pomelo's position: catching up / niche-strong.** It is the LatAm issuing leader (Series C $55M Jan 2026, co-led Kaszek/Insight; ~$160M raised to date; ~150 customers incl. Santander, BBVA, Bancolombia, Western Union, Rappi; +250% revenue over two years, per Insight Partners/Bloomberg). Its stablecoin-native global card + agentic payments + tokenization-as-a-service is the extension of that base beyond LatAm. Moat (analysis): switching costs from deeply embedded issuer integrations at Tier-1 LatAm banks + Mastercard/Visa membership; weaker on stablecoin-native track record vs Rain/Bridge who already run live global volume.

**Comps & multiples.** Private names, no earnings — round valuations only, revenue undisclosed → EV/Rev = no data for all. Internal comps: [[Rain raises $58 million Series B for stablecoin cards]] (Aug 2025, pre-$1.95bn), [[Rain expands Visa membership into Asia-Pacific for stablecoin cards]] (Mar 2026), [[Rain and Lithic partner on stablecoin payments]] (Sep 2025), [[This Week in Fintech Bridge powers Shopify, MetaMask, Remitly]] (Oct 2025). Rough scale anchor: Rain $1.95bn post-money on ~$3bn annualized card volume ≈ 0.65x valuation/annualized-TPV (`$1.95bn / $3bn = 0.65x` — TPV is gross flow, NOT revenue; this is a crude size gauge, not a real multiple). Pomelo Series C post-money [UNSOURCED] (not disclosed in Bloomberg/Insight coverage); with ~$160M raised and +250% two-year revenue growth but no absolute revenue → any multiple = no data. Distribution not computed (only one usable figure); qualitative: Rain is the priced leader, Bridge is capital-backstopped by Stripe, Pomelo is smaller and earlier on the stablecoin axis. In-line vs peers on regional issuing; behind on stablecoin-native scale.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Headline overstates reach — "150+ markets" is really ~150 customers.** Insight Partners/Bloomberg describe ~150 clients, and the stablecoin-native global card was framed at Series C (Jan 2026) as a forthcoming/expanding product. De-PR: "announced/launching" ≠ live global TPV. Why: if adoption lags Rain/Bridge, Pomelo defends its LatAm base but doesn't win the global layer.
2. **Disintermediation by the networks themselves.** Visa/Mastercard exploring an own stablecoin (Forbes/CoinDesk, Jun 2026) could compress the independent issuer-infra margin — Pomelo depends on their rails and BIN sponsorship. Why: margin captured one layer up leaves issuers as thin pass-through.
3. **Execution/geography stretch + regulatory surface.** Moving from LatAm issuing to 150+ markets multiplies licensing, compliance and fraud/chargeback liability (note itself leads with "automated chargeback management" — a tell that fraud economics are the hard part). Who's silent: unit economics, FX/conversion spread, and fraud-loss ownership are undisclosed.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is a new-entrant move into an already-forming global stablecoin-card oligopoly (Rain, Bridge/Stripe), not a category creation — expect the theme to consolidate toward whoever pairs live global TPV with network settlement. Falsifiable thesis: Pomelo's global stablecoin card gets real traction only if a Tier-1 LatAm bank client goes live cross-border on it within ~12 months; trigger to watch = a named live volume/market count (not customer count) or a Mastercard/Visa co-announcement of live settlement. What breaks it: if disclosures stay at "customers served" with no live TPV, the launch is positioning ahead of product.
