Adyen joins European Payments Initiative as principal member
Sinziana Albu
14 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Adyen has joined the European Payments Initiative (EPI) as a principal member, a step aimed at expanding merchant acceptance of the Wero digital wallet across Europe.
According to the announcement, the move is intended to give consumers a fast, secure and straightforward way to pay at checkout, while helping to accelerate merchant adoption of the wallet across the region.
EPI was founded in 2020 by a group of European banks and payment service providers with the aim of building an independent, pan-European payment system based on instant account-to-account transfers. The initiative operates Wero, a digital wallet that runs on the SEPA instant credit transfer scheme and is designed to support P2P transfers, ecommerce payments, and, eventually, POS transactions.
Wero has been rolled out progressively across several European markets, beginning with peer-to-peer transfers in Germany and Belgium, followed by France. Ecommerce functionality has been introduced more recently, with in-store payments expected to follow. EPI has positioned Wero as an alternative to established international card schemes, as well as to domestic solutions such as iDEAL, Bizum, Blik, and Swish, some of which EPI has already acquired or absorbed into its offering.
Merchant acceptance and market context
Adyen's addition as a principal member follows a pattern of EPI extending its membership base beyond its founding bank shareholders to include payment platforms and acquirers with a role in merchant acceptance. Other companies that have joined EPI in a similar capacity include payment technology providers focused on integrating Wero into merchant-facing infrastructure.
For EPI, broadening its network of principal members is presented as a way of ensuring that Wero can be deployed efficiently at the point of sale and online, rather than relying solely on the founding bank shareholders to drive distribution. In addition, for Adyen, the membership extends its existing involvement in European payments infrastructure, adding a further A2A payment method to the range it already supports for merchants.
Implications for the European payments ecosystem
The announcement frames Adyen's membership within EPI's broader ambition of building what the initiative describes as a sovereign, competitive European payments ecosystem, one intended to reduce reliance on non-European payment schemes. Both organisations said they intend to continue working together on the development of payments in Europe, though no further operational or commercial details were disclosed at the time of the announcement.

# Context-enrichment: Adyen joins European Payments Initiative as principal member
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- 2026-07-14 (The Paypers): **Adyen joined EPI as a principal member**, framed as a step "aimed at expanding merchant acceptance of the Wero digital wallet across Europe." No dated go-live, no merchant numbers, no economics disclosed — announcement, NOT adoption.
- **What "principal member" actually means (de-PR'd):** EPI is tiered — 16 founding **shareholders** (ABN Amro, ING, Rabobank, BNP Paribas, Deutsche Bank, Sparkassen, Worldline, Nexi, etc.) who OWN and GOVERN EPI, vs. **Principal Members** (PSPs that either distribute Wero to consumers or, on the acquiring side, enable merchant acceptance). Adyen is an **acceptor-side Principal Member** (acquirer/PSP surfacing Wero at checkout/POS) — NOT taking an ownership stake and NOT governing EPI. The "principal" badge is a low-commitment, marketing-friendly membership, not a shareholder seat. → Why it matters: the substance is "Adyen will offer Wero as one more A2A method to its merchants," not "Adyen helps build/own the rail."
- **Provenance caveat (red-team flag).** Parallel web research could not corroborate a standalone "Adyen joins EPI as principal member" press release and noted a possible conflation with **Adyen joining the EPC (European Payments Council)** effective 1 Jul 2026 — a *different* body (SEPA scheme management). Adyen IS listed on EPI's member page (unlabeled as "principal"). The primary source here (The Paypers, dated 14 Jul 2026) explicitly says EPI/Wero, so we treat the reported EPI acceptor-membership as the event, while flagging the EPC-vs-EPI ambiguity as unresolved. (analysis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **This is a well-worn template.** Prior corpus notes of the SAME "PSP/acquirer becomes EPI Principal Member to bring Wero to merchants" pattern: [[Airwallex becomes principal member of EPI to bring Wero to merchants]] (2025-12, acquirer side), [[Mollie joins EPI Company to accelerate Wero]] (2026-01, PSP/merchant side), [[Van Lanschot Kempen becomes Principal Member of EPI, adds Wero]] (2026-06, consumer/wealth side), plus processing/acceptance deals [[ACI Worldwide and EPI to power instant payments in Europe]] (2026-06) and [[Nexi implements Wero for eCommerce in Germany]] (2026-04). External: ACI Worldwide, Nopan (~Jun 2026) also announced as principal members; German acceptance already spans Nexi, Nuvei, Worldline, Stripe, PAYONE, Unzer, PPRO.
- **Position: LATE follower, but the heaviest logo so far.** Adyen is NOT first (Worldline/Nets were first acquirer-shareholders; Mollie/Airwallex/ACI already principal members). Its edge over those peers is SCALE: Adyen is a top-3 global enterprise acquirer (~€1.3tn+ annual processed volume), so its acceptance footprint is far larger than Airwallex's or a niche PSP's. → Why the lay of the land is this way: EPI is methodically signing every acceptance channel so Wero can appear at checkout without leaning solely on shareholder banks. Adyen closes a big gap in enterprise-merchant reach.
- **vs. cards/incumbents:** Wero's pitch is A2A (instant SEPA, interchange-free) vs. Visa/Mastercard interchange rails. Near-term this is coexistence, not displacement — Wero rides SEPA Instant and remains a P2P-heavy scheme; card checkout displacement is aspirational, not evidenced at scale.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Adyen is a merchant **acquirer/PSP**, not a consumer bank; its Wero interest is purely **acceptance-side** — offering Wero as a checkout/POS method to enterprise merchants (corpus: Adyen recently added SAP Unified Payment, Uber, Starbucks, GOV.UK Pay, Tap to Pay via Starling; see [[Adyen selected as payment provider for GOV.UK Pay]], [[Adyen and SAP launch SAP Unified Payment for Commerce Cloud]]).
- **Why Adyen acts this way (structural, partly defensive):** Adyen is Amsterdam-HQ and iDEAL-heavy; **iDEAL is migrating into Wero (NL, 2026 co-branding → full by end-2027)**. Any acquirer with Dutch merchants MUST support Wero or lose a core NL method — so this is as much a migration necessity as a strategic bet, dressed in "European sovereignty" language. Adding one more local A2A rail also fits Adyen's "single platform, all methods" positioning at near-zero incremental cost. (analysis)
- Fit is logical and low-risk: expands the method set, aligns with EU payment-sovereignty optics, no capital committed.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty: low.** The mechanism (acquirer becomes Principal Member, enables Wero acceptance) is identical to Airwallex/Mollie/ACI. The only genuinely new elements are the **brand/scale** (Adyen's enterprise merchant base is materially larger than prior acceptor sign-ups) and — if it materialises — a bigger acceptance funnel for Wero's still-nascent e-commerce phase.
- **Traction: this is an announcement, not adoption.** No Adyen merchant count, no live date, no economics. Background Wero traction (external, for weight, NOT Adyen's): ~43.5M users in year one → **>50M registered**, >100M transactions, >€7.5bn transferred (targets ~60M / ~€15bn by end-2026). BUT that strength is **P2P**; the e-commerce/POS phase Adyen serves is early — e-com live DE (late 2025), FR/BE (Jan 2026), LU (Jun 2026), NL via iDEAL migration through 2027; in-store QR from 2026, NFC slipped to 2027.
- **Who captures the value (analysis):** A2A is interchange-free, so there's no rich interchange pool; Adyen monetises processing/acceptance regardless of rail. Value accrues to **EPI/Wero** (a large new acceptance node) and to Adyen's **method-completeness/retention**, not to a new high-margin P&L. Adyen remains a price-taker on someone else's rail.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Watch for an actual **Adyen Wero go-live** and any merchant-acceptance figures (likely undisclosed — the absence of numbers signals early/immaterial volume today).
- Structural backdrop: the needle-movers for European payment sovereignty are the **iDEAL→Wero migration** (NL, through 2027), the **Digital Euro** progressing in the EU ([[Digital Euro clears key hurdle in EU parliament]], 2026-06), Wero e-com/POS actually converting, and shareholder-tier expansion (Austria: [[EPI's Wero adds Erste and Raiffeisen as shareholders in Austria]]). Adyen's acceptor membership is a breadth confirmation, not a driver.
- **Second-order (analysis):** with a top-tier global acquirer now onboard, EPI has largely completed the "collect every acceptance channel" phase; the open question shifts from "can Wero be accepted?" to "will consumers CHOOSE Wero at checkout over cards?" — i.e. from distribution to demand-side conversion. Membership count is a lagging signal; active e-com usage past the €7.5bn base is the real test.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** European "payments sovereignty" is the through-line: a policy push (ECB, national regulators) to reduce reliance on US card schemes (Visa/Mastercard) by building homegrown account-to-account (A2A) rails on SEPA Instant. EPI's Wero is the flagship, backed by ~16 European banks/PSPs. Traction (sourced): Wero passed ~44m users at one-year mark (Sep 2025, [[Wero wallet reaches 44 million European users in year one]]) and by late 2025 cited "over 45m users and >€7.5bn in transactions" (Airwallex/EPI PR, [[Airwallex becomes principal member of EPI to bring Wero to merchants]]); 2026 external estimates put it at ~43.5m registered users, >100m transactions and ~€5–7.5bn transferred — figures are inconsistent across sources, so treat magnitude (tens of millions of users, single-digit €bn volume) as the confident read, exact counts as "no data". Why now: Wero is transitioning from P2P (Germany, Belgium, France live since 2024) into the harder e-commerce/POS phase in 2026, and iDEAL (NL) is being folded into Wero from 2026 ([[iDEAL to phase into Wero starting in 2026]]) — the merchant-acceptance land-grab is on, which is exactly what "principal members" like Adyen are recruited to deliver. Structure: European acquiring is fragmented (Adyen, Worldline, Nexi, Stripe, Checkout.com, PPRO, Nuvei + domestic schemes iDEAL/Bizum/Blik/Swish); barriers are regulatory (licensing, PSD2/instant-payments mandate) and network effects (two-sided merchant↔consumer adoption). Wero's ~0.7% estimated merchant fee vs ~1–3% for cards is the structural driver (per paymentexpert.com / ecommerce-nation, 2026) — cheaper rails that also route interchange away from the card duopoly.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: processed/settled volume (TPV), net revenue take-rate, EBITDA margin, and (for A2A) merchant acceptance breadth. Adyen's own reported KPIs are strong (Q1 2026 Business Update, 2026-05-06): net revenue €620.8m, +16% YoY (+20% cc); processed volume €382.0bn, +21% YoY; FY26 guide +20–22% cc net revenue growth; EBITDA margin guided broadly in line with 2025 and toward >55% by 2028 (per the update, via tradingview/quartr). Basis of competition here is distribution + product breadth: Adyen adds Wero as one more A2A method on its single-platform rails alongside cards, iDEAL, etc. Recent peer moves onto Wero: Airwallex became a principal member (Dec 2025, [[Airwallex becomes principal member of EPI to bring Wero to merchants]]); Nuvei launched Wero for e-commerce merchants (Nov 2025, [[Nuvei and EPI launch Wero payments for European merchants]]); payabl., Van Lanschot Kempen, Mollie ([[Mollie joins EPI Company to accelerate Wero]]), Nexi, PAYONE and PPRO are all onboarding Wero. So Adyen is **catching up, not leading** on this specific move — several acquirers signed as EPI members before it. Adyen's moat is scale/single-platform switching costs (enterprise merchants: Uber, LVMH, Starbucks); Wero membership is incremental method coverage, not a differentiator `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** No deal/round attaches to this news, so this is a positioning comparison of listed European acquirers (external, as-of ~mid-2026; treat as indicative — figures pulled from aggregators, currency/ADR inconsistencies flagged):
- **Adyen (AMS:ADYEN):** market cap ~€26bn (aggregators cite ~$30bn); reported ~6x EV/Revenue on ~$3bn net revenue base — a growth-premium multiple justified by +16–21% growth and >50% EBITDA margin (per stockanalysis.com / multiples.vc). In-line/rich but growth-backed → not a flag.
- **Nexi (BIT:NEXI):** EV/EBITDA ~5.5x, EV/Revenue <1x — deep-value/low-growth (per gurufocus/stockanalysis).
- **Worldline (PAR:WLN):** distressed — EV/EBITDA ~6.7x on a collapsed equity base after fraud-concealment allegations ([[Worldline shares plunge on fraud-concealment allegations]]); a cautionary comp, not a peer to benchmark against.
- **Stripe (private):** ~$159bn Feb-2026 tender valuation on ~$5bn net revenue → far richer implied multiple, but private/round valuation, not market cap (per techcrunch/cnbc).
Distribution not computed (mixed currencies, <3 clean comparable figures). Qualitative read: Adyen sits at the top of the European acquirer multiple stack — the gap vs Nexi/Worldline is a growth-and-margin story, not obviously mispriced. Internal comps: [[Adyen forecasts 20% annual net revenue growth after 2026]], [[Adyen shares fall on Cleveland Research margin concerns]], [[Retailers and payment firms clash over iDEAL successor Wero]].

**Risk flags.**
1. **A2A cannibalises Adyen's own card economics (disintermediation of take-rate).** Wero's ~0.7% fee vs ~1–3% cards means merchants shifting volume to Wero compress the fee pool Adyen earns on — Adyen participates in a lower-margin rail. Second-order: as A2A scales, blended take-rate faces downward pressure exactly as margins are already under scrutiny ([[Adyen shares fall on Cleveland Research margin concerns]]).
2. **Adyen is on record with cost/fraud/dispute concerns about Wero — economics undisclosed here.** In Apr 2026 Adyen (and Mollie) publicly flagged Wero's higher costs, fraud risk and weeks-long chargeback/dispute exposure vs iDEAL ([[Retailers and payment firms clash over iDEAL successor Wero]]). Joining as a principal member while those concerns are unresolved, with "no commercial details disclosed," suggests defensive/optional positioning rather than conviction — fraud liability and dispute-window economics are the silence to watch.
3. **Adoption/execution risk — Wero is announced, not proven at the merchant checkout.** User counts are P2P-heavy and inconsistently reported; e-commerce and POS are the unproven 2026 test. If merchant adoption stalls (the "solution in search of a problem" critique, [[Opinion Wero wallet a solution in search of a problem]]), principal membership is a low-cost option that delivers little volume.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** For the sector, Adyen's membership is a de-risked hedge, not a re-rating: it signals that even A2A-skeptical acquirers now see European A2A/Wero as inevitable enough to plug in, accelerating the shift of European e-commerce volume off card rails `(analysis)`. Falsifiable thesis: Wero e-commerce volume through acquirer members (Adyen/Nuvei/Airwallex) becomes a disclosed line by 2027; trigger to watch — Wero's actual e-commerce/POS transaction volume in Germany/France/Belgium in 2026 and whether Adyen names it in a business update. What breaks the thesis: Wero merchant acceptance stays P2P-bound and the ~0.7% fee advantage fails to overcome card ubiquity/rewards, leaving membership a headline with no volume.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
_This news is a partnership (Adyen joining EPI as a principal member to expand Wero acceptance), not an earnings release. The section below is the latest-results financial backdrop that grounds the EPI move — Adyen's scale, growth, margins and capital position give it the merchant-acquiring reach EPI needs at the point of sale and online._

**Latest results backdrop (not an earnings event).** Most recent print: **Q1 2026 Business Update (2026-05-06)** — a trading statement (net revenue + volume only, no full P&L). The last full P&L is **H2 / FY 2025 (2026-02-12)**. Trajectory reads as sustained ~20% top-line growth with margin expansion — a healthy backdrop, not a trigger for this A2A/Wero distribution move.

**Q1 2026 (reported).** Net revenue **€620.8m, +16% YoY (+20% constant-currency)**; processed volume **€382.0bn, +21% YoY**. By segment: Unified Commerce (in-store/omnichannel) **€196.2m, +24% (+28% cc)** and Platforms **€75.0m, +35% (+40% cc)** led; Digital **€349.6m, +9% (+13% cc)** lagged. The in-store/omnichannel strength is the relevant read for EPI: Adyen's growth engine is exactly the merchant point-of-sale + ecommerce acceptance layer EPI needs to push Wero into. Source: Adyen Q1 2026 Business Update.

**FY 2025 (reported, full P&L).** Net revenue **€2,364.2m, +18% YoY (+21% cc)**; EBITDA **€1,245.7m, +26% YoY**, EBITDA margin **53%** (vs 50% in 2024 — ~3pp of operating leverage); net income **€1,062.5m, +15% YoY**. Processed volume **€1,394.3bn, +21% ex a single large-volume customer (+8% incl.)**; POS volume **€311bn, +34%**. **vs prior period / momentum.** Growth is holding around the ~20% cc line rather than re-accelerating: FY25 net revenue +18% (+21% cc), H2 25 +17% (+21% cc), Q1 26 +16% (+20% cc). Margin is the clearer improvement (50% → 53% FY, 55% in H2 25). No public consensus variance computed (this is not an earnings event) — direction assessed vs prior periods. Cf. **Guidance / forward.** Reaffirmed at Q1 2026: **FY 2026 net revenue growth of 20–22% YoY constant-currency**; **2026 EBITDA margin broadly in line with 2025 (~53%)**; medium-term target **EBITDA margin above 55% by 2028**; **capex up to 5% of net revenue**. Guidance was not raised — management is steering to a steady-state ~20% growth / margin-ramp story rather than a step-change, so the EPI/Wero move is incremental optionality, not a re-rating catalyst.

**Thesis-flags.**
1. *Margin scrutiny is the live debate.* In June 2026 the shares fell on Cleveland Research margin concerns ([[Adyen shares fall on Cleveland Research margin concerns]]). Fact: 2026 margin is guided flat vs 2025, not up → why: the >55%-by-2028 target back-loads the ramp → why it matters: bears read flat 2026 margin as investment/competition pressure → second-order: adding A2A rails (Wero) that bypass card interchange could pressure per-transaction economics if it cannibalises higher-take-rate card volume, though at current scale it is immaterial.
2. *Growth decelerating toward ~20%, not below.* +16% reported / +20% cc in Q1 26 is at the low end of the 20–22% cc guide — a miss below 20% cc would be the flag to watch; not present yet.
3. *Capital allocation is turning acquisitive.* Recent Talon.One (€750m) and Orb deals ([[Adyen agrees to acquire loyalty platform Talon.One for €750 million]], [[Adyen to acquire Orb to unify billing and payments infrastructure]]) sit alongside 87% FCF conversion — the balance sheet easily absorbs an EPI membership, which carries no disclosed financial commitment.
4. *A2A/Wero is strategic hedge, not a P&L line.* No revenue/economics disclosed for the EPI membership; treat as distribution/optionality in European sovereign payments, backed by Adyen's #1-in-Unified-Commerce growth (+24–35% by segment) — the operational credential that makes Adyen a useful acceptance partner for EPI.
