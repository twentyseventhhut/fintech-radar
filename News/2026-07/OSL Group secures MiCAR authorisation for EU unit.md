OSL Group secures MiCAR authorisation for EU unit
Sinziana Albu
09 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
OSL Group's European subsidiary, OSL EU1, has received CASP authorisation from Austria's FMA under the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCAR).
The approval follows the 1 July 2026 deadline for firms to convert national crypto registrations into full MiCAR authorisation, a transition that has significantly narrowed the number of licensed operators in the region.
According to the company, more than 1.200 crypto firms held national registrations across the EU prior to the deadline, but only around 210 (roughly 17%) converted to full CASP authorisation in time. Firms that did not complete the process have either withdrawn from the EU market or lost the legal basis to serve clients there. MiCAR replaces the previous patchwork of 27 national regimes with a single harmonised framework, meaning a CASP authorisation obtained in one member state can be used to offer services across the entire bloc.
Under the FMA authorisation, OSL EU1 is permitted to provide custody and administration of crypto-assets, spot trading, fiat on- and off-ramp and conversion services, and crypto-asset transfers to institutional and eligible clients across the EEA. In addition, the authorisation adds to a MiCAR licence already held in the Netherlands by another OSL Group subsidiary, EU Internet Ventures B.V.
The legal entity behind OSL EU1 is currently registered as CIGE vierte PGG GmbH, and the company said a renaming process to OSL EU GmbH is underway.
OSL Group's regulatory history begins in Hong Kong, where its subsidiary, OSL Digital Securities Limited, was among the first virtual-asset platforms licensed by the Securities and Futures Commission (SFC). The Austrian authorisation extends that licensed model into Europe, giving the group standing under both the Hong Kong and EU crypto-asset frameworks.
The company said it holds or is pursuing more than 50 trading and payment licences globally, with regulatory presence spanning Asia, Australia, the US, and Canada, alongside its Hong Kong and EU authorisations. Kevin Cui , Executive Director and Chief Executive Officer of OSL Group, said the Austrian authorisation builds on the company's Hong Kong foundation and its recent Australian licence, describing it as part of a longer-term approach to regulated infrastructure.
Market implications
With MiCAR authorisation in place, OSL Group can pursue European banking relationships, access local payment infrastructure, and engage institutional counterparties that require dealings with fully licensed entities. As the post-MiCAR European crypto market consolidates around authorised platforms, the Austrian authorisation positions OSL Group among a comparatively small group of firms able to operate across the EEA under a single licence, at a point when many previously registered competitors have exited the region.

# Context-enrichment: OSL Group secures MiCAR authorisation for EU unit
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Freshness verdict: FRESH.** This is a genuinely distinct grant — a full MiCAR **CASP authorisation from Austria's FMA** for OSL Group's entity **OSL EU1** (legal entity currently *CIGE vierte PGG GmbH*, renaming to *OSL EU GmbH*), announced 9 Jul 2026. It is NOT a re-run of another firm's licence: the recent corpus MiCA grants are for different companies — [[Coinbase secures MiCA licence to serve EU crypto customers]] (Luxembourg/CSSF), [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]] (Luxembourg), [[Ripple receives preliminary MiCA CASP licence in Luxembourg]], [[Flowdesk Europe gets AMF CASP authorization under MiCA]] (France/AMF). It is also distinct from OSL's own prior corpus note [[OSL and Anchor Point test HKDAP stablecoin with Standard Chartered]] (HK stablecoin test, May 2026) — a different jurisdiction and event. Framing piece: [[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]]. Verdict: fresh, low novelty (one more name on a long, thinning CASP register).

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- OSL Group's EU subsidiary **OSL EU1** received a full **CASP authorisation under MiCAR from Austria's FMA** (Financial Market Authority). Permitted services to **institutional and eligible clients across the EEA (30 countries)**: custody & administration of crypto-assets, spot trading, fiat on/off-ramp & conversion, and crypto-asset transfers. Passportable bloc-wide.
- **Not OSL's first EU MiCAR licence:** the group already holds a MiCAR licence in the **Netherlands** via another subsidiary, **EU Internet Ventures B.V.** So the Austrian grant *adds* an entity/jurisdiction to an existing EU footprint rather than opening the EU for the first time. The PR's "from Hong Kong to Europe" framing is thus slightly overstated — Europe was already entered.
- **Timing is the whole story:** the grant lands right after the **1 Jul 2026** MiCAR transition deadline, when only ~210 of ~1,200 nationally-registered EU crypto firms (~17%) converted to full CASP status. OSL uses this cull to claim membership of a "comparatively small group" able to passport across the EEA. **Why structured this way:** the value is scarcity/positioning, not a new capability — the FMA licence lets OSL pursue EU banking relationships, local payment rails and institutional counterparties that require a fully licensed entity.
- **PR to discount:** the note lists *capabilities the licence unlocks* and a "50+ licences globally" tally — not live EU clients, EU volumes or EU revenue. "Positioned to pursue" = authorisation, not adoption. The entity is even mid-rename (still legally *CIGE vierte PGG GmbH*).

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Already MiCA-licensed and live:** Coinbase (Luxembourg/CSSF), Kraken (Luxembourg + CBI Ireland), OKX/Crypto.com/Gemini (Malta), Bitpanda (Austria FMA + CSSF Luxembourg), Bitvavo (NL/AFM), Bitstamp (Luxembourg). On the infra/stablecoin side: Bridge/Stripe, Ripple, Circle. → OSL is a **late, small entrant** relative to these; it is on-time for the deadline but well behind the volume incumbents.
- **Austria FMA as a hub:** Bitpanda (a top-tier EU retail exchange) and AMINA ([[AMINA secures MiCA license in Austria]]) also chose Austria — so OSL's jurisdiction pick is unremarkable, not a differentiator. **Why the landscape is this way:** MiCA rewards early, well-capitalised applicants who can absorb compliance cost; the second-order effect is that the register concentrates around a few large names, and a sub-$1.5bn-cap Asian entrant joining post-deadline gains *access* but not *position*.
- **OSL's actual peer set** is arguably other Asia-rooted, compliance-first platforms building a global licence lattice (e.g. HK/SFC-licensed venues expanding outward), not the EU volume leaders it is listed beside.

## [2] Company history / fit
- OSL Group (**HKEX: 863**) began as a HK-regulated digital-asset platform — its subsidiary **OSL Digital Securities** was among the first virtual-asset platforms licensed by Hong Kong's **SFC**. It has since built a "compliance-driven global strategy": HK foundation, a recent **Australian AFSL** (ASIC, wholesale clients), US/Canada presence, and now the EU. It positions as "a global stablecoin payment and trading platform" (OSL StableHub, OSL BizPay, Banxa on/off-ramp; see [[OSL and Anchor Point test HKDAP stablecoin with Standard Chartered]]).
- **Fits logically:** the Austrian CASP is the EU tile in a deliberate multi-jurisdiction licence lattice ("50+ licences held or pursued across 10+ jurisdictions"). **Why the company acts this way:** OSL's differentiation IS regulatory breadth — being the licensed, institutional-grade rail in each market — so a MiCAR CASP is table-stakes for the "regulated everywhere" thesis rather than a discretionary expansion. Structurally, a loss-making platform (FY2025 net loss ~HK$387M on ~HK$489M revenue) is buying optionality and institutional credibility, funding it via equity (reported ~$200–300M raise tied to European/OSL Pay expansion).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty is low.** MiCAR CASP grants are now routine (~210 firms); OSL is one more name, and it already had a NL MiCAR licence. The genuine increment is a *second* EEA-passportable entity plus the specific FMA-authorised service scope.
- **Traction: none disclosed for the EU.** No EU AUC, no EU client count, no EU revenue. OSL's global AUC (~HK$5.7bn, +50% YoY) and OSL Pay growth are group-wide/HK-Asia-driven, NOT EU. → treat as "green light," not adoption.
- **Who captures the margin:** OSL's edge is being the *licensed institutional gateway* — custody/settlement/on-off-ramp fees. But in the EU it enters a market where custody and trading are commoditising and where distribution (existing exchange user bases, bank relationships) is owned by incumbents. → the licence buys *the right to compete*, not a moat; the durable value depends on whether OSL can win EU institutional counterparties who value its Asia/HK connectivity (a plausible but unproven wedge — bridging EEA institutions to HK/Asia digital-asset flows).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Expect OSL to convert the licence into named EU institutional custody/trading relationships and to lean on the Australia + HK + EU "tri-framework" story for cross-border (Asia↔EEA) institutional flow. The renaming to OSL EU GmbH and go-live of services are the near-term milestones to watch.
- **Regulatory backdrop:** MiCA's post-deadline consolidation is a tailwind for any licensed player — the field thinned to ~210 CASPs. Counterintuitive second-order effect: as MiCA concentrates the field, *being licensed* stops being a differentiator (everyone left is licensed) and the real battle reverts to distribution, liquidity and balance-sheet — where a ~US$1.4bn-cap, loss-making Asian entrant is disadvantaged vs Coinbase/Kraken/Bitpanda.
- **Risks:** no EU traction yet; small scale and continuing losses; late entry into a market with entrenched incumbents; execution gap between licence and live volume; the "50+ licences" narrative is breadth without proven depth in any single large market.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The EU crypto-services market is now defined by MiCAR's **1 Jul 2026** transition cutoff: of ~1,200 nationally-registered EU crypto firms, only ~**210 (~17%)** converted to full CASP authorisation; the rest exited or lost the legal basis to serve EU clients (per the note / GlobeNewswire, 9 Jul 2026). ESMA's interim register showed ~**204 CASPs** as of 18 Jun 2026 (via finance.yahoo.com). Structure: a single harmonised regime replaces 27 national ones, so **one CASP authorisation passports across all 30 EEA states** — this shifts the value chain from "27 local licences" to "1 licence + distribution," and the binding barrier becomes **regulatory capital/compliance cost** (a permanent tax favouring well-capitalised incumbents). **Why now:** the deadline is a forced consolidation event — a licensed CASP is now scarce and therefore a moat *only until* the field settles, after which distribution and liquidity re-take primacy `(analysis)`.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: assets under custody (AUC), trading volume / take rate, and float on fiat/stablecoin balances. Key players and basis of competition: **volume incumbents** — Coinbase (Luxembourg/CSSF), Kraken (Luxembourg + CBI Ireland), Bitpanda (Austria FMA + CSSF), OKX/Crypto.com/Gemini (Malta), Bitvavo (NL/AFM) — competing on brand, liquidity and existing EU user bases; **infra/issuer** players Bridge/Stripe, Ripple, Circle competing on distribution/issuance. Recent dated moves: [[Coinbase secures MiCA licence to serve EU crypto customers]] (Jun 2026, operational go-live), [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]] (Jul 2026), [[Ripple receives preliminary MiCA CASP licence in Luxembourg]] (Jun 2026), [[Flowdesk Europe gets AMF CASP authorization under MiCA]] (Jun 2026), [[AMINA secures MiCA license in Austria]] (Nov 2025). **OSL's position: late, niche entrant** — on-time for the deadline but well behind the incumbents on scale; it already held a NL MiCAR licence, so Austria is a second EEA entity. Moat `(analysis)`: OSL's differentiation is regulatory breadth ("50+ licences across 10+ jurisdictions") and a plausible Asia↔EEA institutional bridge (HK/SFC roots + Australia AFSL), NOT EU liquidity or distribution — which it lacks.

**Comps & multiples.** Comparability is weak: peers are far larger and mostly private or differently-structured, so a clean peer multiple is not computable.
- **OSL Group (HKEX:863, public):** market cap ~**HK$10.76bn (~US$1.4bn)**; FY2025 revenue ~**HK$488.8m** (~US$62m), **net loss ~HK$387m**, AUC ~HK$5.7bn (+50% YoY) (via simplywall.st / OSL results — secondary aggregators, directional). → P/S ≈ HK$10.76bn / HK$0.489bn ≈ **~22x** `(analysis)` — optically rich, but the firm is loss-making and revenue small, so the multiple reflects growth optionality/AUC scarcity, not earnings; EV/EBITDA and P/E **not computable** (EBITDA-negative). Flag: **rich on P/S, but a loss-maker — valuation rests on the compliance-lattice growth story, not current economics.**
- **Coinbase (COIN, public):** the scaled read-across, but ~2 orders of magnitude larger by revenue/cap — not a like-for-like comp; excluded from arithmetic.
- **Circle (CRCL, public):** issuer, different model; see comp discussion in [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]]. Not comparable to a custody/trading platform.
With only OSL's own figures verifiable, **distribution/median not computed — qualitative comparison only.** OSL screens small and unprofitable vs the EU cohort it is listed beside.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Access ≠ adoption (execution risk).** The licence unlocks the right to serve EU institutions but no EU AUC, clients or revenue is disclosed; the durable value depends on winning counterparties in a market where incumbents own distribution — second-order: a late, sub-scale entrant may earn only thin fees while incumbents keep the flow.
2. **Scale / profitability.** ~US$1.4bn cap, loss-making (~HK$387m FY2025), funding expansion via equity — limited balance sheet to compete on liquidity/marketing against Coinbase/Kraken/Bitpanda; dilution and cash-burn risk if EU traction is slow.
3. **Commoditised differentiator.** Post-deadline, *every* surviving competitor is licensed — "being a CASP" stops being a moat; OSL must fall back on distribution/liquidity it does not have in the EU, or on an unproven Asia-bridge wedge.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Not a re-rating — a small land-grab confirming that the post-MiCA EU crypto market is consolidating around ~210 licensed players, with OSL adding a second EEA-passportable entity to the count. Falsifiable thesis: OSL's EU licence pays off only if it converts its HK/Asia institutional network into disclosed EEA custody/trading AUC over 2–4 quarters; the thesis breaks if 12 months out there is still no EU volume disclosure while incumbents entrench. Trigger to watch: any OSL disclosure of EU institutional AUC/clients, or the OSL EU GmbH go-live.
