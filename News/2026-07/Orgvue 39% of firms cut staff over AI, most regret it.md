# The ‘AI boomerang’: Why some companies are rehiring employees they laid off due to AI

Managers are realizing the rapidly evolving technology can’t completely replace humans after all, research suggests.

AI-related layoffs have captured everyone’s attention lately. Last month, Meta laid off 10% of its workforce—just months after committing up to $135 billion toward AI development this year. 

Other companies, such as Cloudflare, Coinbase, and PayPal, have also laid off or plan to lay off employees as they become “AI native.”

Last year, a report from Forrester Research highlighted that 55% of employers regretted their decision to lay off staff due to artificial intelligence. And a Gartner prediction published earlier this year claims that 50% of all companies that replaced customer service or operational employees with AI will be forced to restaff those roles under different titles by next year.

A trend called the “AI boomerang” effect is emerging: Companies that laid off employees due to AI or automation are now rehiring for those roles for a number of reasons.

According to new research from the consulting firm Robert Half that was reviewed by _Fast Company_, nearly a third (32%) of hiring managers say that their organizations eliminated a role or let someone go primarily due to productivity gains from AI or automation, only later to rehire for that exact role.

“Companies that moved too quickly on AI are now seeing where it falls short in practice,” says Megan Slabinski, district president of technology talent solutions at Robert Half. “While they may have seen early efficiency gains, those efforts also surfaced gaps in quality, oversight, and decision-making, especially as business demands picked up.”

“In many cases, organizations have had to reassess their expectations, recognizing that while AI can be effective in certain areas, it’s not the end-all-be-all solution some initially believed it would be,” Slabinski adds. “That’s prompting leaders to consider the shift from cutting roles to rethinking them instead, with a clearer understanding of where AI works best alongside their employees, not in place of them.”

The survey is based on responses from 2,000 U.S. hiring managers across a range of industries. Of the group, finance (44%), tech (32%), and HR (35%) were the sectors with the most hiring managers who say they have rehired for the same roles—but marketing and creative, legal, healthcare, and administrative and customer support followed closely behind.

# Context-enrichment: Orgvue — 39% of firms cut staff over AI, most regret it
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
An **Orgvue** (London-based org-design / workforce-planning SaaS vendor) survey report says **39% of business leaders made staff redundant because of AI deployment**, and of those, **55% now admit the decision was wrong**. The digest also cites **Robert Half** (32% of hiring managers eliminated a role due to AI/automation productivity gains, then rehired for the exact same role) and a **Gartner** prediction (50% of companies that replaced customer-service / operational staff with AI will restaff those roles under different titles within a year). Framed as the "AI boomerang."

**Key de-PR caveats:**
- **The 39% and 55% are the SAME Orgvue survey, chained**: 39% cut staff → of *that* subset, 55% regret it (so ≈21% of all leaders both cut AND regret). The headline "most regret it" is technically "most of those who cut," not "most firms."
- **Sample / method (from Orgvue press release + PR Newswire):** conducted by **Vitreous World**, commissioned by Orgvue, fieldwork ~**Feb–Mar 2025** (this is a ~2025 dataset re-surfacing in mid-2026 press), **~1,000–1,163 C-suite / senior decision-makers** at medium/large orgs (min 2,000 seats in US/Canada/UK/Ireland; min 500 in AU/HK/MY/SG). It is **self-reported executive opinion**, not headcount data — no verification that regretted cuts were actually reversed.
- **Vendor incentive (analysis):** Orgvue sells "measure roles before you cut them" software. The report's real punchline — **23% of AI-linked layoffs were based on general assumptions about AI, not role-by-role analysis** — is precisely the pain its product addresses. This is PR-adjacent research: directionally plausible but self-interested.
- **The Fast Company source in the note actually attributes the 55%-regret stat to a 2025 Forrester Research report, not Orgvue** — meaning two different studies produced the same 55% figure, or the digest merged them. Treat "55%" as corroborated-ish but not from a single clean source.

## [1] Competitors / peers (comparable & contradicting studies)
- **Robert Half** (survey of 2,000 US hiring managers): 32% eliminated then rehired the same role — **corroborates** the boomerang from the HR-manager angle. Finance (44%), tech (32%), HR (35%) most affected.
- **Gartner:** 50%-restaffing prediction — a **forecast**, not observed data; supports narrative but unfalsifiable yet.
- **Forrester (2025):** 55% of employers regretted AI-driven layoffs — near-identical to Orgvue's number.
- **CONTRADICTING — Ramp × Revelio Labs (Jun–Jul 2026):** across ~22,000 companies, the **heaviest AI spenders grew total headcount +10.2%** (entry-level +12%) over two years — the direct counter-narrative. See [[Report heavy AI adopters grew headcount 10.2%]]. The two can coexist: rushed, assumption-driven cutters regret it; disciplined heavy adopters *grew*. The real variable is **execution quality, not AI itself**.
- **Company-specific AI layoffs in the corpus** that this report contextualizes: [[Crypto.com cuts 12% of staff citing AI]], [[Block to lay off over 4,000 employees citing AI]] (which already **rehired some**), [[Coinbase cuts about 700 jobs in AI-driven restructuring]], [[Rapyd restructures with hundreds of layoffs in AI-driven shift]], and the umbrella [[TechCrunch The AI layoff wave is becoming a powder keg]]. Counterweight: [[Evident AI headcount at 50 major banks up over 25% since 2023]] — banks *adding* AI headcount.

## [2] Company history / fit (Orgvue's angle)
Orgvue is a workforce-planning platform (HQ London; offices Philadelphia, The Hague, Toronto, Sydney) that builds a "digital twin" of an org to model restructuring scenarios before cutting. Its recurring PR cadence is survey-driven thought-leadership: earlier releases include "**92% invested in AI but 78% say projects stalled or failed**." **Why it acts this way (analysis):** as a niche SaaS vendor competing for enterprise HR/finance budget, Orgvue uses "cautionary AI-layoff" data to sell rigor. The research is genuine but its framing is a lead-gen instrument — its conclusion ("analyze roles first") = "buy Orgvue."

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Low novelty as an event.** This is not a new corporate action; it's **a survey report re-entering the news cycle** (data collected early 2025; picked up by CNBC/Fast Company late Jun–Jul 2026 as part of the "AI boomerang" theme). The value-add is the *quantification* and the naming of the boomerang, plus the corroboration cluster (Orgvue + Robert Half + Forrester + Gartner all pointing the same way). **Traction = zero verified reversals**: none of these numbers are audited headcount; they are executive self-reports and one Gartner forecast. **Who captures the value:** the framing benefits (a) org-design vendors like Orgvue, (b) staffing firms like Robert Half — both sell the "rehire / plan better" solution.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
The dominant 2026 narrative is bifurcating: "**AI layoffs are backfiring**" (Orgvue/Robert Half/Forrester) vs. "**AI adopters are hiring more**" (Ramp/Revelio). **Second-order read (analysis):** these aren't contradictory — they describe different cohorts. Firms that cut on *hype/assumption* (23% did no role analysis) rehire; firms that integrate AI properly grow. Expect the story to shift from "AI kills/creates jobs" to "**restructuring discipline separates winners from boomerang losers**." Risk to the thesis: survivorship / self-report bias, a 2025 dataset dressed as current, and vendor-sponsored provenance. Regulatory backdrop: rising scrutiny of AI-justified redundancies in EU/UK could make "we cut because AI" a legal liability, incentivizing quieter rehiring.

## Freshness verdict
**FRESH.** This is a distinct study (Orgvue/Vitreous World) with its own figures, not a reprint of the counter-narrative headcount item and not previously covered in the corpus. It should, however, be read *against* [[Report heavy AI adopters grew headcount 10.2%]] rather than in isolation. Note the underlying data is ~Feb–Mar 2025 fieldwork.

## Challenge / red-team questions

1. **Is "most firms regret it" accurate?** No — 55% is of the **39% who cut** (≈21% of all leaders). The headline overstates by dropping the base. **Answered.**
2. **When was the data actually collected?** Fieldwork ≈**Feb–Mar 2025** (Vitreous World, commissioned by Orgvue); it re-surfaced in Jun–Jul 2026 press. So a ~15-month-old dataset framed as current. **Answered.**
3. **What is the sample?** ~**1,000–1,163** C-suite/senior decision-makers at medium/large orgs across US/CA/UK/IE (≥2,000 seats) + AU/HK/MY/SG (≥500 seats). Self-reported opinion, not payroll data. **Answered.**
4. **Are any of the "regretted" layoffs verified as reversed?** No — every figure is executive self-report or (Gartner) a forecast. No audited rehire counts. **Open / weakness.**
5. **Does the note misattribute the 55%?** Yes: Fast Company credits the 55% to **Forrester (2025)**, while Orgvue independently reports 55% too. Either two studies coincide or the digest merged them. **Answered — flag.**
6. **Is Orgvue a neutral source?** No — it sells org-design/workforce-planning software; the report's core finding (23% cut without role analysis) is exactly its sales pitch. Directionally credible but vendor-sponsored. **Answered.**
7. **Does this contradict the Ramp/Revelio "+10.2% headcount" study?** Not really — different cohorts. Hype-driven cutters boomerang; disciplined heavy adopters grow. Variable = execution, not AI. **Answered (analysis).**
8. **Is this a genuinely new event or a re-report?** It's a **survey report re-entering the cycle**, not a corporate action. Fresh as a distinct study, but low event-novelty. **Answered.**
9. **What's the base rate for layoff regret generally (non-AI)?** Unknown from sources — companies often rehire after any over-aggressive cut; the "AI" attribution may be partly narrative. **Open.**
10. **Are the corroborating sources independent?** Partly: Robert Half (staffing firm, sells rehiring) and Orgvue both have commercial incentive in the "rehire/plan better" message; Gartner is a forecast. Only loosely independent. **Answered.**
11. **What share of the 39% cut based on real analysis vs assumption?** 23% admitted the cut rested on general AI assumptions, not role-by-role analysis — the strongest, most defensible finding. **Answered.**
12. **Which sectors/regions are most affected?** Robert Half: finance (44%), tech (32%), HR (35%) rehired most. Orgvue sample skews large enterprises. **Answered.**
13. **Does "fintech" relevance hold?** Indirect — the theme maps onto fintech AI layoffs (Crypto.com, Block, Coinbase, Rapyd). Report itself is cross-industry, not fintech-specific. **Answered.**
14. **Could survivorship/social-desirability bias inflate "regret"?** Yes — admitting a mistake in a survey is cheap; may over-report regret vs. actual reversal behavior. **Open.**
15. **What changes the central question?** From "does AI cut or create jobs?" to "**does the firm restructure with role-level discipline or on hype?**" — the boomerang is an execution failure, not an AI verdict. **Answered (analysis).**

**Importance: 3/5** — A well-corroborated, quotable data point on the "AI layoff regret / boomerang" theme with a clean vendor + Robert Half + Forrester + Gartner cluster, and it usefully anchors the counter-narrative to [[Report heavy AI adopters grew headcount 10.2%]]. Docked below 4 because: it is a re-surfaced ~2025 survey (not a new event), the provenance is vendor-sponsored self-report with zero verified reversals, the headline overstates its own base, and it is cross-industry rather than fintech-specific. Fresh, informative, but not market-moving.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a research-report data point, not a company event — Orgvue is a workforce-analytics / org-design SaaS vendor (org planning, headcount modelling, skills mapping) and the "39% cut staff, 55% regret it" figure is its own commissioned survey, which is marketing collateral for its category. Sector: workforce/people analytics + HR-tech. Market size is contested and small: per Allied Market Research the workforce-analytics market is ~$2.7bn (2026), while other trackers cite ~$4.0–4.5bn (2025–26) — no single reconciled number, so treat as ~$3–4.5bn, "no data" on a firm TAM. Structure: fragmented, tiered — HRIS-native modules (Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, Oracle) at the top, dedicated enterprise analytics platforms (Visier, One Model, Orgvue) in the middle, point tools below. Barriers are low-to-moderate (data integration, enterprise sales cycles), not network effects. Why now: the survey rides the 2026 "AI boomerang" narrative — Robert Half found 32% of firms rehired AI-eliminated roles; Gartner predicts 50% of firms that swapped support/ops staff for AI will restaff within a year (per Fast Company, 06-05-2026). Orgvue's angle: sell "plan before you cut" tooling into that regret.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs (SaaS): ARR, NRR, seats/enterprise logos; the survey itself is a demand-gen KPI. Key players: Visier (raised ~$220m, ~$1bn valuation, Vancouver, dedicated people-analytics leader), Workday/SAP/Oracle (HRIS-native analytics — distribution moat via installed base), One Model, ChartHop, Cornerstone. Basis of competition: depth of analytics + integration breadth vs bundled-into-suite convenience. Orgvue position: niche (org-design/scenario-planning specialist), catching-up on scale vs Visier and structurally disadvantaged vs suite incumbents that give analytics "for free" inside the HRIS. Moat: (analysis) modest — methodology/IP and enterprise relationships, but disintermediation risk from HRIS vendors is real. Recent Orgvue research cadence is heavy (also "92% invested in AI, 78% stalled/failed"; "72% of HR leaders say AI is dominant driver") — a content-marketing engine, so numbers should be read as vendor-sourced, not independent.

**Comps & multiples.** Orgvue is private — total funding ~$56.4m over 2 rounds; last public revenue proxy £34m (FY to Mar-2022, dated, [UNSOURCED] for current). No post-money disclosed → valuation "no data"; EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA, P/E all **not computable** (no public numerator or denominator). Nearest external comp: Visier ~$1bn round valuation on ~$220m raised — a round mark, not a market cap; no revenue disclosed so no clean multiple. Distribution not computed (fewer than 3 comparable figures) — qualitative only. Internal comps (this is a labour/AI-jobs data point, so comps are peer AI-headcount events, not deals): [[Standard Chartered to cut 7,800 back-office jobs by 2030]] (2026-05, first to attach 15%+ to a functional area), [[Solaris cuts 20% of workforce in AI-native restructuring]] (2026-04), [[Crypto.com cuts 12% of staff citing AI]] (2026-03, ~180 roles), [[Fintech Wrap Up how banks move from AI pilots to production]] (2026-06, pilots-to-production gap). Contradicting corpus signal: [[Citrini Research scenario on AI economy spooks markets]] frames AI-labour as scenario, not realised fact.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Vendor-sourced / conflicted data.** The headline stat comes from a survey commissioned by Orgvue (via Vitreous World, fieldwork Feb–Mar 2025, n≈1,163 decision-makers, US/CA/UK/IE + APAC) — Orgvue sells the tooling that "prevents" the mistake the survey highlights. Self-serving framing; second-order effect: the "regret" number may be inflated by leading questions and should not be read as an independent labour-market measurement.
2. **AI is NOT the primary layoff driver — even in Orgvue's own data.** Orgvue's release itself states 43% cite economic conditions and 31% restructuring (=74%) as the reason for redundancies; external evidence agrees — AI was named in only ~55k of US cuts in 11 months of 2025 (~4.5% of the ~1.2m total; "market/economic conditions" were ~4x larger, ~245k), and Oxford Economics/HBR (Jan 2026) call AI-attribution partly "convenient corporate fiction" masking cyclical cuts. Denmark + US national studies find no discernible AI-adoption/employment link (juniors excepted). Second-order effect: the "AI boomerang" is real but small; treating it as a structural signal overstates the case.
3. **Category disintermediation.** Orgvue's specialist niche is squeezed between HRIS incumbents (Workday/SAP/Oracle) bundling analytics into the suite and better-capitalised Visier — margin/pricing pressure; its research-marketing volume signals it's competing on mindshare, not defensible product moat.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is a narrative-inflection, not a re-rating: 2026's story shifts from "AI replaces workers" to "AI over-cutters rehire" — bullish for workforce-planning/skills-intelligence tooling (Orgvue, Visier, One Model) and for staffing (Robert Half). Falsifiable thesis: if the boomerang is genuine, 2026 job-openings data in finance/tech/HR support roles should tick up and AI-attributed cut counts should fall as economic conditions dominate. What breaks it: if 2026 macro layoffs keep rising with AI cited but no measurable rehiring in official data, the "regret/boomerang" is vendor-and-media narrative, not a labour-market turn. Watch: BLS/Challenger AI-attribution counts and whether Gartner's "restaff within a year" call verifies.
