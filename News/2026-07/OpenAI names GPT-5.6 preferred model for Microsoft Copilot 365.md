OpenAI says GPT 5.6 is the ‘preferred model’ for Microsoft Copilot 365 amid breakup chatter
Earlier this week, Bloomberg reported that Microsoft was replacing some of OpenAI’s software with its own in-house models to cut costs. Those in-house models, known as MAI, were being used to power apps like Word and Excel, the outlet noted.
The story raised an increasingly common question about the two companies, which were once seemingly inseparable and have recently sent mixed signals about their situationship: Were the two companies drifting apart?
Now, OpenAI is attempting to put any insinuations of such a break to rest. During OpenAI’s launch of GPT 5.6 on Thursday, the company announced that it would become the “preferred model” powering Microsoft’s 365 Copilot.
OpenAI noted in a related blog post that GPT 5.6 would support Microsoft users across the company’s suite of productivity apps, including Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and Cowork.
“Our partnership with Microsoft has always been about bringing the benefits of advanced AI to more individuals and organizations, and we’re excited to continue building on that shared commitment,” OpenAI wrote.
What being a “preferred model” actually means isn’t entirely clear, other than that OpenAI’s software will continue to power Microsoft’s apps.
That said, it was never reported that ChatGPT’s software would stop powering Microsoft’s apps — merely that Microsoft was relying increasingly on its own software in an effort to reduce costs. The new “preferred model” disclosure doesn’t appear to negate that previous reporting.

# Context-enrichment: OpenAI names GPT-5.6 "preferred model" for Microsoft 365 Copilot
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **Thu 2026-07-09** (reporting spilling to 07-10), during OpenAI's launch of the **GPT-5.6 family** (Sol/Terra/Luna — the separate model-release event, see [[OpenAI releases GPT-5.6 family and ChatGPT Work agent]]), OpenAI announced that GPT-5.6 will be the **"preferred model" powering Microsoft 365 Copilot** across Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Chat and Cowork. Microsoft's own blog language: Copilot "may automatically use GPT-5.6 when it's best suited," users "can also choose GPT-5.6 directly from the model selector," and "availability may vary by region and tenant configuration."
- **De-PR'd:** This is **not** an exclusivity deal, not new commercial terms, and not a reversal of any prior arrangement. It is a **co-marketing / relationship-defense statement**. TechCrunch is blunt: "What being a 'preferred model' actually means isn't entirely clear, other than that OpenAI's software will continue to power Microsoft's apps," and the disclosure "doesn't appear to negate" the prior reporting. Copilot **remains multi-model** — Anthropic's Claude (in Copilot since 2025-09-24) and Microsoft's own MAI models still sit in the same model selector.
- **Why structured exactly this way → what it reveals:** The announcement landed **two days after** a damaging **Bloomberg report (Mon 2026-07-07)** that Microsoft was replacing OpenAI *and* Anthropic models with its own in-house **MAI models in Excel and Outlook to cut costs** ("tens of thousands of prompts/week" already on MAI). → OpenAI bundled the "preferred model" line into its GPT-5.6 launch to **rebut "breakup" narratives** without either party disclosing new terms. → Second order: the *real* signal is the tension, not the reassurance — a company that has to publicly declare it is still "preferred" is a company whose default status is being visibly eroded. The PR frame ("still inseparable partners") is the inverse of the underlying trajectory ("Microsoft is diversifying away from OpenAI on cost").
- **What is genuinely unclear (the silence):** OpenAI/Microsoft say nothing about *which* apps GPT-5.6 is default in vs where MAI/Claude win, no economics, no seat/routing share, no exclusivity term. "Preferred" ≈ **default/first-party-blessed model surfaced in Copilot's router**, layered on an explicitly multi-model stack. It does **not** reverse the cost-driven MAI substitution in Excel/Outlook.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Inside Copilot itself the "competitors" are the other models Microsoft has already wired in:
- **Anthropic Claude** — added to M365 Copilot **2025-09-24** (Researcher + Copilot Studio; Nadella: "our multi-model approach goes beyond choice"); Claude reached **GA in Microsoft Foundry (June 2026)** on Azure. But per Bloomberg 07-07, Suleyman's stated goal is to **reduce and "ultimately eliminate"** what Microsoft pays Anthropic.
- **Microsoft MAI (in-house)** — **seven MAI models** launched at Build **2026-06-02** (MAI-Thinking-1, MAI-Code-1-Flash, MAI-Voice-2, etc.), trained from scratch. Already handling "tens of thousands of prompts/week" in Excel/Outlook. Microsoft claims MAI-Thinking-1 matches Claude Sonnet/Opus 4.6 on coding via human evals — **but the-decoder notes published benchmarks contradict this** (roughly DeepSeek-V3.2 level). Treat parity claims skeptically.
- **Google Gemini (Workspace / Gemini Enterprise)** — Gemini 3 Pro at lower TCO (~$48–60/user/mo vs Copilot ~$66–87). Copilot still leads among Global-2000 CIOs (directional, secondary comparison sites).
- **Position:** OpenAI's GPT-5.6 is the **default/premium tier** of Copilot but is being **flanked from below by MAI (cost) and Claude (choice)**. Its status is "still preferred, no longer sole."
- **Why the lay of the land is this way → second order:** Microsoft's multi-model architecture (Nadella has pushed "open, cheaper AI," [[StrictlyVC Nadella pushes for open, cheaper AI model approach]]) is a deliberate **de-risking of single-vendor dependency**. → The routing layer, not the model, is where Microsoft captures value: whoever owns the router decides which model serves each prompt, so Microsoft can keep OpenAI "preferred" for prestige/hard tasks while silently migrating high-volume cheap prompts to MAI. → The central question shifts from "is GPT still in Copilot?" (yes) to **"what share of Copilot inference volume — and margin — does OpenAI keep as Microsoft's router optimizes for cost?"**

## [2] Company history / fit
The OpenAI–Microsoft relationship is mid-decoupling from a once-exclusive partnership:
- **2025-10-28** — OpenAI's for-profit restructuring: Microsoft ~**27% stake (~$135B)**, IP rights to OpenAI models/products **through 2032** (incl. post-AGI models), **$250B** incremental Azure purchase commitment — **but Microsoft loses cloud right-of-first-refusal**; revenue-share runs until AGI is verified by an **independent expert panel** (defusing the old AGI-trigger clause).
- **2026-04-15** — OpenAI pulls back from Stargate Norway/UK; Microsoft absorbs the capacity; OpenAI reportedly now renting *from* Microsoft — compute ties loosening but not severed.
- **2026-06-02** — Build: MAI models + Claude GA in Foundry (Microsoft's multi-model push formalized).
- **2026-07-07** — Bloomberg: MAI replacing OpenAI/Anthropic in Excel/Outlook to cut cost. **WSJ same week:** "Microsoft must chart its own AI path" ([[WSJ Microsoft must chart its own AI path]]).
- **Why the companies act this way:** Microsoft is under structural pressure to **cut inference cost** on 20M+ paid Copilot seats (frontier-model tokens are the COGS line on a fast-scaling product) → hence in-house MAI + multi-model routing. OpenAI, needing to protect its flagship enterprise distribution channel (Copilot is its largest embedded B2B surface) and its revenue narrative ahead of a delayed IPO ([[OpenAI IPO delayed to potentially 2027, behind Anthropic]]), needs to be seen as **still central** → hence the "preferred model" declaration. The two press releases are the same event viewed from opposite incentive sides.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's actually new:** Very little of substance. The **new fact** is GPT-5.6 (the newer generation) becoming the *default* Copilot model — a routine model-version refresh of an existing integration. The **framing** ("preferred," "amid breakup chatter") is the news, not a new capability or term.
- **Traction (real, Microsoft-disclosed):** Copilot has **20M+ paid seats** (FY26 Q3, disclosed 2026-04-29), up from **15M** the prior quarter (~5M net adds/qtr, ~160% YoY at the 15M mark); AI run-rate **>$37B (+123% YoY)**. >60% of Fortune 500 run ≥10,000 seats; **Accenture 740,000+** (largest). Still low-single-digit % of ~450M commercial M365 seats — so large *and* early. This scale is why "preferred model" matters commercially at all: it is OpenAI's biggest embedded enterprise distribution channel.
- **Why the value-add is fragile, one level deeper:** OpenAI's benefit from being "preferred" is **distribution, not defensible margin**. → In a multi-model Copilot, the model is a **substitutable component** behind Microsoft's router; the moment MAI reaches "good enough" on a task class, that volume migrates for cost (already happening in Excel/Outlook). → So the durable value OpenAI captures ≈ the share of *hard/high-value* prompts where GPT-5.6 stays the quality leader, minus whatever Microsoft can commoditize. **Who captures the margin in the stack:** Microsoft (owns the router, the customer, the seat) captures the enterprise margin; OpenAI captures per-token inference revenue that is now being actively competed down. "Preferred" does not change who holds the pricing power — Microsoft does.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Plans/watch:** Whether GPT-5.6's "preferred" status is honored app-by-app vs Microsoft's cost-driven MAI substitution in Excel/Outlook — the two are on a collision course and neither side reconciled them. Watch Microsoft's next earnings for any disclosure of model-mix / Copilot COGS, and whether Anthropic's Copilot presence shrinks as Suleyman signaled.
- **Sentiment:** Press read this as **defensive PR** (TechCrunch, the-decoder), not a de-escalation of the underlying decoupling. WSJ's "chart its own AI path" and Nadella's "open, cheaper" posture point the same way.
- **Why the market goes this way → counterintuitive second-order effect:** The louder OpenAI insists it is "preferred," the more it **confirms** that default status is now contested and cost-pressured. → A frontier lab whose flagship enterprise channel is owned by a partner actively building a cheaper substitute is **structurally exposed**, not secure — Microsoft's 27% stake and 2032 IP rights mean OpenAI cannot easily walk, while Microsoft can progressively substitute. The "preferred model" designation is best read as a **marker of the relationship's transition from exclusive to transactional**, not a reaffirmation of it.

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** Enterprise-AI assistant market: ~$19.1bn in 2025, ~19.6% CAGR to 2035 (per GM Insights, via gminsights.com, as of 2026). Microsoft's overall AI business is at a ~$37bn annualized run-rate, +123% YoY (Q3 FY26 commentary, MSFT IR). Structure is an oligopoly across two layers — the *model supplier* layer (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and now Microsoft's own MAI) sitting under the *distribution/app* layer (Microsoft 365 Copilot, Google Workspace/Gemini). Value in the assistant business accrues to whoever owns distribution (installed seats, data gravity, admin/compliance), not necessarily the model. Why now: with Copilot seat monetization scaling (20M paid seats), the compute/COGS bill under those seats has become large enough that Microsoft is actively re-sourcing the model layer to protect margin — the direct backdrop to the "preferred model" announcement.

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs the sub-sector runs on: paid seats, ARPU ($/seat/mo), attach rate (paid Copilot ÷ commercial M365 base), and gross margin per query (compute COGS). State of play:
- Microsoft 365 Copilot: **20M paid seats** as of Q3 FY26 (up ~5M QoQ, fastest since launch), Enterprise list ~$30/user/mo → ~$7.2bn annualized list run-rate (`20M × $30 × 12 = $7.2bn`) (per Directions on Microsoft / MSFT IR, Apr 29 2026). Attach is still only ~4.4% of ~450M commercial M365 seats — long runway *and* long dependence on the model bill.
- Google: Gemini bundled into Workspace/Cloud; ~27.9% web-visit share vs ChatGPT 53.9%, Copilot 1.3% (May 2026, various trackers) — Copilot's consumer web share is tiny; its moat is the enterprise Office install base, not standalone chat.

- OpenAI's position (protagonist): still the named "preferred model" for Copilot 365 (Word/Excel/PowerPoint/Cowork), but "preferred" ≠ exclusive. Moat = incumbency inside Microsoft's stack + the 27% Microsoft equity stake `(analysis)`; the moat is eroding as Microsoft's own MAI models take production traffic.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Disintermediation by the distributor.** Microsoft is substituting its own MAI models for OpenAI/Anthropic in Word/Excel/Outlook to cut COGS (Bloomberg, 2026-07-07). "Preferred model" is a label, not volume commitment — second-order: OpenAI risks becoming a swappable component whose share of Copilot inference (and thus revenue) shrinks even as Copilot seats grow.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is early-stage **disintermediation of the model layer by the app owner**, not a partnership renewal — watch the *mix* of Copilot inference that runs on GPT vs MAI, and any move from "preferred" to a named revenue/volume floor. Trigger to watch: MSFT FY26 Q4 earnings + any updated OpenAI/Microsoft commercial terms.

**Strategic/financial significance (why the numbers matter).**
- For Microsoft, the "preferred model" framing defends the growth engine of its single largest segment: M365 Copilot monetization (20m+ seats, +250% seat adds) is the visible driver of the +19% M365 Commercial line and, indirectly, the $37bn AI run-rate. The de-PR read (per the note) is that this rebuts "breakup" chatter after Bloomberg reported Microsoft shifting Word/Excel to in-house MAI models to cut cost — so it protects the Copilot revenue narrative without denying that Microsoft is diversifying away from OpenAI on cost grounds. "Preferred," not exclusive.
- For OpenAI, keeping GPT-5.6 embedded across Word/Excel/PowerPoint/Cowork protects a material slice of its ~25% API/enterprise revenue at exactly the moment enterprise is approaching consumer parity — and matters ahead of a potential IPO (confidential S-1 filed). Losing the M365 default would have been a direct hit to its ~$25bn run-rate.
