## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
[0] What happened (2026-07-17)
Russia's FinTech Association (Ассоциация «ФинТех», АФТ) formally asked the Bank of Russia (ЦБ РФ) to accelerate a federal law making open banking APIs (multibanking) mandatory. Per Известия/РГ/РИА, the appeal was written up after a Financial Congress (Финконгресс) discussion involving T-Bank, Sber, VTB, Sovcombank and Gazprombank. The core complaint: technical standards exist and pilots run, but data-sharing rests on voluntary bilateral agreements, so projects cannot scale or pay back the investment already made.

[1] The underlying regime (why this matters)
- In December 2025 the ЦБ published/updated a set of ~10 Open API standards. Crucially, these standards were developed on the АФТ's own platform (fintechru.org / CBR press release, Dec 2025) — so АФТ is not an outside lobbyist here; it is the standards author now asking for legal teeth.
- Compliance is currently VOLUNTARY. Over 20 major banks test data transmission.
- Original phased mandate plan: systemically important banks, brokers and insurers from 2026; MFOs, depositories, DFA information-system operators and financial platforms from 2027.
- BUT in October 2025 the ЦБ POSTPONED the mandatory-implementation deadline. That deferral is the direct trigger for this АФТ push — investments were made against a 2026 timeline that then slipped.

[2] АФТ's specific proposal
A phased mandate mirroring the original roadmap: first oblige systemically important credit organizations (СЗКО) to expose Open APIs, then connect the rest of the market. This lowers first-mover risk and forces the largest data holders (Sber, VTB, etc.) to open first — precisely the actors with the most to lose from free data-sharing and the most to gain from status quo.

[3] Market grounding (adoption today)
- Multibanking already has 700k+ users; more than half via T-Bank's app, where users see Sber/VTB/Alfa accounts in one interface. See [[T-Bank multibanking pilot reaches 700k users]].
- VTB has laid out five conditions to scale Open API market-wide, condition #1 being exactly this legislative base; it named АФТ as a connection-standards venue. See [[VTB sets five conditions to scale Open API across market]].
- Corporate multibanking is already productized (VTB↔Alfa and a hub sequence). See [[VTB and Alfa-Bank roll out Multibank service for corporates]].
These three are the same 2026 Open-API cluster but DISTINCT events (bank-level pilots / product launches / lobbying wishlists), not the regulatory АФТ push.

[4] Tensions to watch
- Free vs. paid data transfer: T-Bank argues base data must move for free; large incumbents may prefer monetized/limited access. The law's pricing rules are the real battleground.
- SME/challenger banks (T-Bank) benefit from mandatory sharing by incumbents; incumbents (Sber) lose their data moat — hence АФТ pushing the СЗКО-first sequencing.
- ЦБ already slipped the deadline once; a law (Duma process) is slower and more uncertain than a ЦБ regulation, so "accelerate the law" may paradoxically not be the fastest path.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
Red-team questions

1. Is АФTs appeal a formal letter to the ЦБ or a public position stated at Финконгресс? Sources say a letter written up "after" the congress — confirm it was actually sent, not just reported.
2. "Accelerate the LAW" vs "accelerate mandatory application": Open API standards are ЦБ regulation-based. Is a separate federal law actually required, or could the ЦБ mandate this by its own act? The framing matters for who holds the delay.
3. The ЦБ postponed the mandatory deadline in Oct 2025 — is that postponement the real story here, and is АФT just asking to reinstate the original 2026/2027 timeline?
4. Who actually opposes acceleration? If all named banks (Sber, VTB, T-Bank, Sovcombank, Gazprombank) back it, why is it stalled — is the blocker the ЦБ, the Duma, or a hidden incumbent objection on data pricing?
5. "700k multibanking users" — is that the whole market or mostly T-Bank's app? Over half is T-Bank, so is multibanking really market-wide or one challenger's feature?
6. Does АФT have a conflict of interest? It authored the standards the ЦБ published — is "accelerate" self-interested standards-lock-in rather than neutral policy advice?
7. Free vs paid data transfer is unresolved. Does the requested law settle pricing, or would a rushed law leave the hardest question (who pays for API traffic) open?
8. СЗКО-first sequencing: does forcing systemically important banks to open first create competitive asymmetry (they open, challengers consume but need not reciprocate at scale)?
9. Security/consent: mandatory data-sharing raises fraud and consent-management risk. Does the note over-index on scaling economics and ignore consumer-protection friction that may be the ACTUAL reason for the delay?
10. Is this genuinely new on 2026-07-17, or a re-run of the same Финконгресс commentary already captured in the VTB (s1f3d7759) and T-Bank (sffaec189) notes from early July?
11. What is the realistic legislative timeline? "Accelerate" is vague — is there a draft bill, a Duma reading date, or just an aspiration?
12. Cross-border/sanctions angle: does Russia's isolated financial system change open-banking incentives (no foreign fintech competition), making this purely a domestic incumbent-vs-challenger fight?
13. Sourcing: this is a single-source (Известия) story amplified by РГ/РИА/ПРАЙМ citing the same original. Is there independent confirmation from the ЦБ or АФT directly?
14. What happens to the "significant resources" already invested by pilot participants if the law does NOT accelerate — is the sunk-cost argument a real risk to the program or rhetorical pressure?
15. Does mandatory multibanking meaningfully shift market share, or do consumers stay with their primary bank regardless of aggregation (limiting the actual competitive impact)?

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Russia's open-banking regime is a top-down, central-bank-designed standard, not a market-led one. The Bank of Russia updated its Open API standards and mandated that the largest banks, brokers and insurers use open APIs from 2026, with MFOs/depositories/DFA operators following in 2027 (per Bank of Russia / AKM, as of 2025). But the underlying *federal law* — which converts today's voluntary bilateral data exchange into a compulsory regime — has slipped: adoption was planned for 2025 with a 2026 mandatory launch, now pushed to after 2026 pending the law (per RIA/RG, 17 Jul 2026). Pilots (personal financial assistant, corporate multibanking) ran from 2024 with Sber, VTB, Gazprombank, Sovcombank and T-Bank. "Why now": the AFT's complaint is an economics problem — pilots are sunk cost, and without a mandate they cannot scale or earn back the invested capital, because any single large bank can refuse to reciprocate data sharing (per RG, 17 Jul 2026). Structurally the RU market is highly *consolidated* (Sber + a handful of systemic banks hold the bulk of retail data), which is exactly why a voluntary regime stalls: the incumbents that own the data have the least incentive to open it.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs here are non-financial: number of mandated participants, API coverage of product types, and consent-platform adoption (Bank of Russia + Mindigital are building a Commercial Consent Management Platform). Two camps: (1) **data-holders** — Sber, VTB, Gazprombank and other systemic banks that own the richest customer datasets and lose relative advantage under forced sharing; (2) **data-consumers / challengers** — smaller banks, aggregators and fintechs (the AFT membership) that gain the ability to surface competitors' products in their own app. The basis of competition shifts from *data ownership* toward *front-end / distribution* (whoever owns the multibanking interface owns the customer). Recent move: the AFT's formal letter to the CB urging the law and proposing mandatory open-API use start with systemically important banks first (per Izvestia/RIA/RG, 17 Jul 2026). Protagonist (the AFT, i.e. the challenger coalition) is *pushing* — it needs regulation as a lever because it cannot compel incumbents commercially. Moat logic: incumbents' moat = proprietary data + scale; a mandate deliberately erodes it (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round/deal in this story — trading multiples not applicable (regulatory event). Cross-jurisdiction comps instead:
- **US** — [[Judge temporarily blocks CFPB open banking rule]] (Nov 2025): CFPB's §1033 rule (mandatory free data sharing) was preliminarily enjoined — showing that even a written mandate is fragile to legal/political reversal. Compare [[JPMorgan strikes data-access fee deals with aggregators]] (Nov 2025): where mandates are weak, incumbents monetize access via fees rather than share freely — the exact outcome RU challengers fear.
- **EU** — PSD2 already forces *free* basic account-data APIs; PSD3/PSR final text expected OJ H1 2026, in force ~2027 after 21-month transition; FIDA (broader open finance) still in trilogue, applicability ~2029-2030 (per Norton Rose / MoFo, 2026). EU shows even a mature mandate takes ~decade to broaden.
- **RU vs peers:** RU is ~2-3 years behind EU on legal footing but has comparable *technical* standards already published (per Bank of Russia). Distribution not computed — qualitative comparison. Verdict: RU regime is "spec-ready, law-missing" — the inverse of the US (rule written, contested) and behind the EU (law live, scope expanding).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Voluntary-regime deadlock (execution/economics).** Without the law, systemic banks can decline reciprocity; pilot investment doesn't scale or amortize — second-order effect: challengers stall, consolidation around Sber persists.
2. **Incumbent capture / fee-gating.** Even with a law, incumbents may follow the JPMorgan playbook — charge for "premium" API access — turning open banking into a toll road that neutralizes the challenger benefit (per RU note + US comp).
3. **Regulatory-timeline / political risk.** Timelines have already slipped once (2025→post-2026); like CFPB §1033, a written mandate can be delayed or diluted, leaving invested fintechs stranded on an uncertain ROI horizon.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is a *re-rating of who owns the customer* if the law passes: value migrates from data-holders (big banks) to front-end aggregators/challengers, mirroring the EU trajectory (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: mandatory open API benefits RU challengers only if the law bars fee-gating of core data; watch two triggers — (a) whether the federal bill actually enters the Duma with a firm 2027 mandate and "systemic banks first" sequencing, and (b) whether the Consent Management Platform launches (without it, consent friction kills adoption regardless of mandate). If incumbents secure a paid-access carve-out, the thesis breaks and open banking becomes an incumbent revenue line, not a challenger equalizer.
