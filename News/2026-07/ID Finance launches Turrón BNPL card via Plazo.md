ID Finance launches Turrón BNPL card via Plazo
Claudia Pincovski
16 Jul 2026 / 4 Min Read
ID Finance, a fintech group operating in Spain and Mexico, has introduced Turrón, a payment solution developed by its brand Plazo. The product combines an e-money account, a debit card and a Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) mechanism, and is designed to let customers split online and in-store purchases into instalments without additional steps at checkout.
How the instalment mechanism works
Unlike many BNPL products that require customers to select an instalment option at the point of sale, Turrón applies the split automatically at partner merchants, both online and in physical stores. Customers do not need to take any additional action during checkout for eligible purchases to be divided into interest-free instalments at no extra cost.
Before completing a purchase, users can view the repayment schedule within the app, including the instalment amount and the collection dates. The standard structure consists of three monthly instalments, while purchases made at selected retailers may qualify for repayment terms of up to twelve months, subject to applicable conditions. The card carries no maintenance fees and does not require a subscription. Customers using the Turrón card at partner merchants also accumulate cashback, which is tracked automatically within the app.
Positioning within ID Finance's platform strategy
According to a company representative, the card is intended to integrate into existing spending habits by applying instalments automatically, reducing friction for customers at the point of sale. The representative added that the launch extends the company's platform to include BNPL and instalment payments as part of its broader financial services offering.
The launch forms part of ID Finance's strategy to build a range of digital financial products addressing everyday customer needs. Plazo was launched in 2021 and has since expanded from a mobile finance application into a platform combining payments, financing and features related to financial well-being. The introduction of Turrón adds an instalment and BNPL layer to that existing offering.
Funding background and profitability targets
The launch follows a EUR 140 million structured funding round completed by ID Finance in 2024. Plazo reported reaching profitability for the first time at the end of 2025. Following the Turrón launch, the company said it is focused on expanding its network of partner merchants. The addition of BNPL functionality is described as a further step towards Plazo achieving full-year operating profitability in 2026.
The move places ID Finance among a group of European fintechs extending card-based products into embedded instalment payments, a segment that has drawn increasing attention from both consumers and regulators across the EU in recent years as BNPL usage has grown.

# Context-enrichment: ID Finance launches Turrón BNPL card via Plazo
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**Freshness: FRESH.** No prior note covers ID Finance, Plazo or Turrón; the internal corpus holds only adjacent Spanish/EU BNPL context ([[WiZink launches Aplazame installment payments in Portugal]], [[Klarna goes live on Apple Pay in Denmark, Spain and Sweden]]). This is a genuine new product launch (announced ~2026-07-14), not a reprint.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
ID Finance (Barcelona-HQ'd online lender, Russian founders Boris Batine/Alexander Dunaev, split from its CIS "IDF Eurasia" arm in 2018 to position as Spain/Mexico) launched **Turrón** under its Spanish neobank brand **Plazo**: an e-money account + debit card with **auto-applied, interest-free BNPL**. Standard split = 3 monthly instalments; up to 12 months at select retailers; no fees; cashback (reported 3% at supermarkets, up to 10% at 170+ brands). The differentiator claimed is that instalments apply **automatically at partner merchants with no checkout action**.

**De-PR'd:** The account + (virtual/physical) card are live and downloadable, but the signature auto-BNPL is **gated on a partner-merchant network the company itself admits it is "still expanding"** — and **no merchant names were disclosed**. So "launched" = soft-launched; the headline feature is only as real as an undisclosed network. Card issuer/BIN sponsor and network for Turrón are **not disclosed** (Plazo's existing card is Mastercard debit; likely same, unconfirmed). **Why framed this way:** the "auto-applied, zero-friction" angle is a UX story layered onto an established mechanic (card-linked instalments) — it lets a small lender differentiate on experience rather than merchant reach, which it lacks vs incumbents.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **SeQura** (Barcelona) — the domestic incumbent leader in merchant BNPL (~2M shoppers, ~5,000 merchants, ~€1B volume per Stripe). Turrón is far behind on merchant reach.
- **Klarna** — strong on large Spanish merchants/marketplaces; live on Apple Pay in Spain since Nov 2025 ([[Klarna goes live on Apple Pay in Denmark, Spain and Sweden]]), 3× interest-free.
- **Aplazame** (owned by WiZink) — PSP-channel instalments, expanding regionally ([[WiZink launches Aplazame installment payments in Portugal]], up to €6,000 / 3–36 months).
- **Alma, Scalapay, Cofidis, Oney** present; **Afterpay/Clearpay** entered Spain by buying **Pagantis (€50M, Aug 2020)**.
- **Bank encroachment — the real pressure:** BBVA/Santander/CaixaBank/Sabadell rolling out "Plazo Cero" card-linked 0% instalments (pilots late-2025, broader 2026) — the *same* card-linked-instalment mechanic Turrón uses, from players with balance sheets and merchant/cardholder scale. (Note the coincidental name clash: ID Finance's brand "Plazo" ≠ banks' "Plazo Cero".)
- **Position:** catching up / niche. The banks arriving on the exact mechanic is the second-order threat: a UX wrapper is easy to replicate for anyone who already issues the card.

## [2] Company history / fit
Plazo launched 2021, grew from a mobile-finance app to a payments/financing/well-being platform. Traction figures drift across sources (30k users 2021 → 60k+ Mastercard cards Jun-2022 → ~190k customers at the Feb-2024 funding → "close to 500k" in 2025/26 coverage) — directional, company-sourced, unaudited; don't conflate with ID Finance group's "10M+ customers" figure. The launch **follows the €140M structured round of 2024** = the **$150M debt facility led by i80 Group (7 Feb 2024, ≈€139.5M)**, earmarked to scale Plazo. **Why now:** ID Finance says Plazo "reached profitability for the first time at end-2025" and targets **full-year operating profitability in 2026**; adding a BNPL/interchange line is the logical revenue expansion for a lender whose take-rate needs a payments/software layer. Skeptical flag: "first-time profitability" phrasing reads like a single profitable month, and launching a subsidised 0%/cashback product sits awkwardly against a profitability claim.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Partly novel.** "Auto-applied, no user action at checkout" is a real UX differentiator vs SeQura/Klarna (which require selecting BNPL at the till). But the underlying primitive — **card-linked instalments** — is exactly what the Spanish banks' Plazo Cero and other card-linked schemes already do. So it's a UX wrapper on an established mechanic, not a new financial primitive. **Traction: essentially none disclosed** — no named merchants, no live-merchant count for auto-BNPL. **Where's the margin (the real question):** customer pays 0% and gets up to 10% cashback, so revenue must come from (a) **merchant fees/MDR** on partner BNPL, (b) **debit interchange** (thin under EU caps), and (c) cross-sell into Plazo's **interest-bearing credit line**. With subsidised cashback + 0% instalments, unit economics hinge on the merchant-network build and paid-credit upsell — i.e., the very things not yet in place. The value-add is a distribution/UX bet, not a margin innovation.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Company says it's now "focused on expanding partner merchants" — the gating constraint. Spanish BNPL market ~US$9–9.6B (2025), forecast ~+22% to ~US$11.75B (2026), ~US$26.7B by 2031 (~18% CAGR); BNPL ≈5% of Spanish web purchases (Stripe). **Regulatory backdrop is the key overhang: CCD2 (Directive (EU) 2023/2225)** pulls BNPL, small loans and **0%-interest credit into scope** — transpose by 20 Nov 2025, apply from **20 Nov 2026**. Interest-free instalments are **NOT exempt**: Turrón will need authorisation and **affordability/creditworthiness checks**, adding friction/cost precisely as it scales. **Second-order:** CCD2 compliance favours incumbents with licences and underwriting; a subscale lender launching a 0% product into a regulated, bank-contested niche faces a squeeze between compliance cost and thin interchange/MDR. Sentiment: incremental, execution- and network-dependent.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Spain BNPL is mid-sized but fast-growing: per Research and Markets (via GlobeNewswire, 3 Feb 2026), the market is projected to reach ~$26.7bn by 2031, with 2026 spend estimated at ~$11.75bn (+22% YoY) after a 26.7% CAGR over 2022–2025. Structure is fragmented and increasingly two-tier: standalone e-commerce BNPL fintechs (SeQura, Aplazame/WiZink, Klarna, Alma, Scalapay) versus banks turning instalments into a native card feature (CaixaBank, BBVA, "Plazo Cero" card-linked deferral) — squeezing pure-play margins from both ends. "Why now": the EU Second Consumer Credit Directive (CCD2, Dir. 2023/2225) brings interest-free BNPL into consumer-credit scope for the first time; transposition due 20 Nov 2025, application from 20 Nov 2026, and Spain is layering a domestic rate-cap law explicitly covering "compre ahora, pague después" (per Hogan Lovells / A&O Shearman notes). Turrón launches directly into that tightening window (analysis).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: GMV/originations, take rate (merchant discount), loss rate / NPLs, funding cost, and merchant network breadth. Basis of competition: merchant distribution (who's at checkout), automatic vs opt-in split (Turrón's differentiator — auto-applied at partner merchants, no POS action), and cost of capital for the receivables book. Key players: SeQura and Aplazame (WiZink) lead Spanish e-commerce BNPL; Klarna/Scalapay/Alma via large-merchant integrations; banks converting cards to instalment rails. Recent moves: WiZink extended Aplazame to Portugal (Nov 2025) [[WiZink launches Aplazame installment payments in Portugal]]; N26 launched personal loans in Spain (Nov 2025) [[N26 launches personal loans in Spain]]. ID Finance's position: niche challenger — it comes from consumer lending (its edge is underwriting/risk, not merchant reach), so Turrón is a card-issuing + BNPL layer bolted onto an existing e-money app (Plazo, ~190k customers per Silicon Canals, 2024). It is behind SeQura/Aplazame on merchant network; moat is its lending/scoring stack and a captive app user base rather than checkout distribution (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** ID Finance is private → no market-cap multiple; the only sourced datapoint is a €139.5M debt facility from i80 Group in 2024 (Silicon Canals) — a debt line to fund the loan book, NOT an equity valuation, so no EV/Revenue can be computed (no data). Internal comps (BNPL/instalment credit, all lending-model peers): [[Kueski partners with dLocal to expand BNPL across Mexico]], [[Addi secures $71M credit upsize from Goldman Sachs]] (both, like ID Finance, fund BNPL via structured debt not equity), [[Tamara's Q1 profit surges 378% to SAR 123 million]] (a profitable BNPL peer benchmark), and [[Equipifi raises $34 million for bank-embedded BNPL]]. Distribution not computed — no ≥3 publicly verifiable multiples; qualitative comparison only. The signal that matters: Plazo reached first-ever profitability end-2025 and targets full-year operating profit in 2026, which is ahead of most Western BNPL pure-plays that remain loss-making — closer to the Tamara profitability trajectory than to Klarna/Affirm.

**Risk flags (3).**
- **Regulation (primary).** CCD2 + Spain's rate-cap law remove the interest-free BNPL carve-out and force affordability/credit checks from Nov 2026 — directly raising compliance cost and friction on the "no extra steps at checkout" auto-split that is Turrón's whole pitch; the exemption only survives for genuinely cost-free, ≤50-day supplier-direct credit, which a card-issued 3–12 month instalment product likely fails. Second-order: the frictionless UX advantage narrows just as it scales.
- **Credit risk on a debt-funded book.** ID Finance funds receivables with structured debt (€139.5M i80 line), so it carries the loss and the funding cost — unlike merchant-fee-only models. In a Spanish consumer-credit downturn, NPLs hit its own P&L and its funding cost re-prices; "interest-free to customer" ≠ free to ID Finance.
- **Distribution / disintermediation.** As a lender-turned-issuer it lacks the merchant checkout footprint of SeQura/Aplazame and competes with banks embedding the same 3–12 month split natively in existing cards — the margin risk is that value accrues to whoever owns checkout distribution, not to a challenger app.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is a defensive platform extension, not a re-rating: ID Finance is monetising its Plazo user base and lending stack by adding a card + auto-BNPL layer to reach 2026 operating profitability, rather than chasing GMV land-grab (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if it can convert app users to card+BNPL at low incremental CAC, its underwriting edge lets it stay profitable where pure-plays burn. Watch: partner-merchant network additions (the disclosed 2026 priority) and whether the auto-split survives CCD2 affordability rules — if merchant network stalls or the product gets re-papered as regulated credit, the thesis breaks.
