FTC fines Amazon USD 2.25 million for Fair Credit Reporting Act breach
Sinziana Albu
01 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
The US Federal Trade Commission has ordered Amazon to pay USD 2.25 million to settle allegations that it violated the Fair Credit Reporting Act.
The complaint, filed by the Department of Justice (DOJ) on the FTC's referral, centred on Amazon's handling of requests from identity theft victims seeking records of fraudulent transactions carried out using their personal information.
Allegations under Section 609(e)
According to the FTC , Amazon repeatedly failed to comply with Section 609(e) of the FCRA, a provision requiring companies to provide identity theft victims with application and business transaction records related to fraudulent activity within 30 days of a request. The complaint stated that Amazon had no written policy for handling such requests until early 2025, after the company became aware of the FTC's investigation, despite earlier warnings from FTC staff about its compliance obligations.
The FTC alleged that consumers who contacted Amazon to report fraud were frequently told by customer service representatives that records could not be shared for 'security' or 'privacy' reasons. In one instance cited in the complaint, a consumer was asked to correctly guess the name used by the identity thief on a fraudulent account before Amazon would release related records, an attempt the consumer failed to complete after 30 tries. The complaint further stated that Amazon declined similar requests submitted by law enforcement agencies acting on behalf of identity theft victims, and that in some cases where records were eventually provided, Amazon did not meet the 30-day statutory deadline.
Terms of the settlement
Beyond the financial penalty, described by the FTC as the largest imposed to date for a Section 609(e) violation, the proposed order requires Amazon to comply with the provision going forward and to supply requested records to identity theft victims and authorised law enforcement agencies. Amazon must also notify consumers of their rights to request records under the FCRA and contact individuals who sought records since April 2024 but did not receive them, informing them that additional records may be available.
The case was brought under authority the FTC has used only once before, in a 2020 action against Kohl's Department Stores Inc., marking the second instance of enforcement under this specific FCRA provision. The Commission voted 2-0 to refer the complaint to the DOJ, which filed both the complaint and the final stipulated order in the US District Court for the District of Columbia. Once signed by the presiding judge, the order will carry the force of law.
Regulatory context
The action reflects continued FTC scrutiny of how large consumer-facing platforms handle data access obligations tied to identity theft protections. Section 609(e) was designed to give fraud victims a mechanism to obtain documentation needed to dispute unauthorised transactions and support recovery efforts. The settlement signals that the FTC intends to enforce these obligations against major online retailers, alongside its existing focus on data privacy and consumer protection compliance more broadly.

# Context-enrichment: FTC fines Amazon $2.25M over FCRA Section 609(e) breach
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
The FTC referred a complaint (via DOJ) charging that Amazon **knowingly violated FCRA Section 609(e)** — the provision requiring a business to hand an identity-theft victim the application/transaction records tied to fraud committed in their name within **30 days** of a verified request. Amazon settled for a **$2.25M civil penalty** plus injunctive relief.
- Timeline discrepancy to flag: the note/The Paypers dates it "01 Jul 2026"; the **FTC press release is dated June 30, 2026** ("2026/06"). The $2.25M and 609(e) facts match across FTC, Engadget, Law.com, Payments Dive, Techlicious — the substance is solid, only the retelling date drifted by ~1 day.
- The damning specifics (all FTC-sourced, not PR): Amazon had **no written 609(e) policy until early 2025** — and only wrote one *after* learning of the FTC probe, despite prior FTC staff warnings. Reps told victims records couldn't be shared for "security"/"privacy". In one cited case a victim was told to **guess the identity thief's name** and failed after **30 tries**. Amazon also refused law-enforcement requests made on victims' behalf, and blew the 30-day deadline even when it did comply.
- **Why this framing matters:** the FTC deliberately calls this the **largest 609(e) penalty to date** and only the **second-ever** use of this authority (after Kohl's, 2020). The "record" superlative is technically true but rests on a near-empty comparison set (n=2), so it signals *intent to build a precedent line* more than a large absolute sanction. $2.25M is rounding error for Amazon (~$0.6T+ annual revenue) — the value is in the **injunctive tail**: notify all consumers of 609(e) rights and **re-contact everyone who requested records since April 2024** and was denied. That look-back is the real cost/reputational exposure, not the cash.

## [1] Competitors / peers (enforcement landscape)
This is a **regulatory-enforcement** item, so "peers" = comparable actions:
- **Kohl's (June 2020)** — the ONLY prior 609(e) action; $220K penalty ([[FTC Reaches Settlement with Kohl's]] — external). Amazon's $2.25M is ~10x, but Kohl's set the template (same "won't release records without ID'ing the thief" pattern).
- Adjacent FTC/consumer-protection actions in-corpus: [[The FTC asked a federal court to hold payment processor Cliq in]] (Jan 2026, contempt for violating a 2015 order), [[Paddle to pay $5M to settle FTC tech support scam claims]] (Jun 2025), [[California fines Yotta $1 million over false FDIC-insurance claims]] (May 2026), [[Nexo to pay $500K California fine over unlicensed loans]] (Jan 2026).
- **Why the landscape looks this way:** most FTC/state actions target *deception* (Paddle, Yotta) or *unlicensed activity* (Nexo). The Amazon case is different — a **data-access/procedural** duty, not a scam. It shows the FTC pushing an under-used statute against a marquee defendant to set an example (second-order: a $2.25M headline against Amazon deters far more effectively than a $220K one against Kohl's did).

## [2] Company history / fit
Amazon is a recurring subject in the corpus, mostly on the **product/expansion** side, not compliance: [[Amazon launches Prime Business and Amazon Business credit cards]], [[Amazon launches Pay by Bank in the UK]], [[Amazon completes Axio acquisition for India lending business]], [[Nubank and Amazon Brazil expand NuPay checkout partnership]], [[Amazon to end Amazon One palm payments]]. It also faces a separate regulatory front ([[Amazon sues Perplexity over agentic shopping tool]]).
- **Why Amazon acts this way:** the "guess the thief's name / privacy excuse" pattern is the predictable output of a **support org optimized for cost and fraud-deflection**, not for a niche statutory disclosure duty most reps had never heard of. No written policy until a regulator knocked = the classic large-platform failure mode: obscure obligations get no owner until enforcement forces one. Fits Amazon's broader profile of scale outrunning consumer-facing compliance detail.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty of the *news*: low.** This is a settled, single-source-retold enforcement action. No new market, product, or capital.
- **Novelty of the *legal signal*: moderate.** 609(e) has been enforced exactly twice in ~23 years (since its 2003 addition). Establishing a second data point — and against Amazon — makes future enforcement credible and tells every large merchant/platform to stand up a 609(e) intake process. That is the durable "value-add" here.
- **Traction / real effect:** the binding piece is the **April-2024 look-back re-contact** obligation and the force-of-law order once the DC district judge signs. Watch whether it's actually signed/entered.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Order becomes enforceable on judicial signature (US District Court, D.C.); Commission voted **2-0** to refer.
- **Regulatory backdrop (important, and cuts against over-reading this):** the corpus flags a **retrenchment** in US consumer-financial enforcement under the current administration — [[Fintech sector growth meets tougher public-market scrutiny and compliance]] documents a gutted/quiet CFPB. So a 2-0 FTC vote on a *procedural* FCRA matter is a comparatively *safe, bipartisan-friendly* action (protecting fraud victims, not attacking a business model) — which partly explains why it moved while broader consumer enforcement is muted. (analysis)
- **Second-order:** expect compliance vendors and law firms to market "609(e) readiness" off this; expect other large retailers/marketplaces to quietly build intake flows. Low probability of a large follow-on penalty wave, but the *template now exists*.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is not a "credit-scoring / background-screening vendor" story — Amazon is not a CRA here; the news sits in the FCRA-compliance / consumer-data-access subvertical of regtech. The relevant obligation is Section 609(e), a data-access duty that binds *any* company holding transaction records tied to identity theft, not just consumer reporting agencies. Sector size for the adjacent background-screening/consumer-reporting market: ~$5.1bn US in 2025 (per IBISWorld) and ~$7.18bn global (per Mordor Intelligence, 2025), CAGR ~8.6% (per Market Research Future) — but this is context only; Amazon captures none of it and the fine's magnitude is unrelated to that TAM. Why now (regulation driver): the FTC voted 2-0 to refer to DOJ, and this is only the *second-ever* 609(e) enforcement action (first: Kohl's, 2020, per FTC) — signalling the agency is widening 609(e) from thin retail precedent toward large online platforms holding fraud-transaction data (per FTC press release, 2026-06-30). Second-order effect: any platform holding "who-transacted" records now faces a live, if small-dollar, compliance obligation with a 30-day statutory clock.

**Competitive landscape.** Not a competitive-share story — no peer is competing with Amazon on 609(e) compliance. The comparable set is regulatory-enforcement precedent, not market rivals. KPIs that matter here are compliance KPIs, not sector economics: response-rate to 609(e) requests within 30 days, existence of a written policy (Amazon had none until early 2025, only after learning of the probe — per FTC), and breadth of remediation (Amazon must now notify all consumers who requested records since April 2024). Named comparables in enforcement, not commerce: Kohl's Department Stores (2020, first 609(e) action, per FTC). Amazon's "position" is simply that of a repeat large-platform target of consumer-protection enforcement — this is one of many such actions against it, immaterial individually.

**Comps & multiples.** No trading multiple applies — a $2.25M civil penalty is a rounding error, not a valuation event; EV/Revenue / P/E impact = "no data" (not meaningful). **Materiality arithmetic (this is the real comp):** fine $2.25M against Amazon Q1-2026 net income $30.3bn = 2.25 / 30,300 ≈ **0.0074% of one quarter's net income**; against Q1-2026 net sales $181.5bn = 2.25 / 181,500 ≈ **0.0012% of one quarter's revenue** (Amazon Q1-2026 8-K, filed 2026-04-29, drive_url https://drive.google.com/file/d/19EcSV74mXluPrgOy3oiu6h3ADWgwZWAy/view — earnings-release figures: net sales +17% to $181.5bn, operating income $23.9bn, net income $30.3bn). Amazon's SEC filings do not itemise contingencies at this scale; its 10-Q "Legal Proceedings"/"Commitments and Contingencies" disclosure treats such matters as immaterial in aggregate (Amazon 10-Q 2026-04-30, drive_url https://drive.google.com/file/d/19EcSV74mXluPrgOy3oiu6h3ADWgwZWAy/view). Internal enforcement comps from the base (fine size is the only relevant axis; these are AML/reporting, not 609(e), so comparable only as "regulatory penalty scale"): [[Fintrac fines crypto platform Cryptomus C$177 million]] (C$177M), [[FCA fines Barclays £42M over money laundering failures]] (£42M), [[Wise fined $4.2M by US states over AML deficiencies]] ($4.2M), [[FCA fines Sigma Broking £1m for reporting failures]] (£1M), [[Green Dot splits as bank and fintech units get separate buyers]] (Green Dot, $44M Fed fine, 2024). Against this base, $2.25M is at the *small* end and, for Amazon specifically, negligible — flag: **immaterial**, purely reputational/precedential rather than financial.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Precedent-expansion risk (for the sector, not for Amazon):** the FTC explicitly framed this as the largest 609(e) penalty to date and only its second use of the provision — the second-order effect is that 609(e) is becoming a live enforcement lever against any large platform holding fraud-transaction records, raising a compliance-process (written-policy, 30-day-SLA) burden across e-commerce and fintech, even though dollar penalties stay tiny.
2. **Remediation-scope / reputational risk:** the order forces Amazon to re-contact every consumer who requested records since April 2024 and to publish 609(e) rights notices — the operational and reputational tail exceeds the $2.25M cash cost; the "why" is that customer-service refusals (e.g. asking a victim to "guess the identity thief's name") are the kind of detail that drives headlines and further scrutiny.
3. **Immateriality is itself a signal (analysis):** a $2.25M penalty against a company earning $30.3bn/quarter creates no deterrence via the fine amount — deterrence, if any, comes from the injunctive terms and precedent, not the number; that asymmetry is a structural weakness of civil-penalty enforcement against hyperscale platforms.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** For the sector: this is a *regulatory-precedent* event, not a re-rating or consolidation catalyst — it quietly upgrades 609(e) from a dormant provision (one use in 20+ years pre-2020) into an active compliance line-item for platforms holding transaction data (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if the FTC brings a *third* 609(e) action within ~12 months against another large platform, the provision has become a standing enforcement priority and compliance vendors (identity/fraud data-access tooling) gain a real demand driver; what would make the thesis wrong — if this stays a one-off headline with no follow-on cases, it reverts to a symbolic action with zero sector impact (analysis).
