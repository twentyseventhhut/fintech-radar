[0] TL;DR
Julius Baer named Peter Burrill — a nine-year Standard Chartered finance veteran and its former interim Group CFO — as its new CFO and Executive Board member, effective 17 August 2026 (subject to regulatory approval). He succeeds Evie Kostakis, whose departure the bank announced on 10 April 2026. The hire is the latest senior move in CEO Stefan Bollinger's post-Signa turnaround of the crisis-hit Swiss private bank.

[1] What happened (dated)
- 2026-07-07 (note date): FinTech Futures reports "Julius Baer recruits CFO Peter Burrill from Standard Chartered." Julius Baer press release ("Julius Baer appoints Peter Burrill as Chief Financial Officer and Member of Executive Board") confirms the appointment, effective 17 August 2026, subject to regulatory approval.
- Burrill's background: >30 years in finance across major centres; nine years at Standard Chartered, most recently interim Group CFO and Group Management Team member; earlier Group Head of Central Finance and Deputy CFO (led a finance-function transformation — infrastructure, operating model, international org). Prior senior roles at Deutsche Bank. BA in business economics, UC Santa Barbara.
- Predecessor: Evie Kostakis (13 years at JB; deputy CFO then Group CFO since 2022). Her exit was announced 2026-04-10; she is pursuing "another international leadership opportunity" and stays to end-2026 to support an orderly handover.

[2] Why it matters / context
- Turnaround under new leadership: Stefan Bollinger (ex-Goldman Sachs, hired July 2024) became CEO in January 2025 after Philipp Rickenbacher stepped down (Feb 2024) over the Signa debacle. Bollinger is cutting the Executive Board from 15 to ~5, targeting ~CHF 110m cost savings, and reducing headcount ~5%. Installing his own CFO is a keystone of that reset.
- Signa fallout: JB wrote off ~CHF 606m of loans to René Benko's collapsed Signa Group, which halved profit. Ongoing FINMA investigation into the Signa real-estate losses has kept share buybacks paused.
- Continued credit pain: JB booked ~CHF 213m net credit losses in 2025 (mortgage book + residual private-debt loan book): a ~CHF 130m charge disclosed May 2025 and ~CHF 149m of real-estate loan writedowns in November 2025 that it said no longer fit strategy.
- Despite this, FY2025 (reported 2 Feb 2026) was framed as a "successful transition year": AuM +5% to a record CHF 521bn, underlying pre-tax profit +17% to CHF 1.266bn. Bringing in a CFO with deep transformation/regulatory credentials signals a focus on financial discipline and rebuilding credibility.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Swiss/European private banking & wealth management. The pick is a leadership move (CFO hire), not a deal/round — no valuation in the news itself; comps below are positioning, not a transaction multiple. Structure: highly consolidated at the top after the 2023 UBS–Credit Suisse merger — UBS reported ~CHF 5,584bn total AuM in 2025 and ~65% of Swiss private-banking AuM (per ZHAW Wealth Management Blog, as of 2025). Julius Baer sits as the #2 pure-play Swiss wealth manager: AuM grew 5% to a record CHF 521bn in FY2025 on net new money of CHF 14.4bn (per JB FY2025 results, 2 Feb 2026). Barriers: capital, regulation (FINMA), advisor networks, brand/trust — network/switching-cost moats. "Why now": JB is mid-turnaround under CEO Stefan Bollinger, rebuilding risk governance after the 2023 Signa/private-debt blow-up; a heavyweight external CFO (Burrill, ex-interim Group CFO of Standard Chartered, ex-Deutsche Bank Group Controller) signals continued C-suite rebuild. He succeeds Evie Kostakis (departure announced April 2026); start effective ~17 Aug 2026 pending regulatory approval (per Private Banker International / finews).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: AuM, net new money (NNM), gross margin (bps on AuM), cost/income ratio, CET1. Key players (Swiss pure-play tier): UBS (dominant, CHF 5,584bn AuM), Julius Baer (CHF 521bn), Pictet, Lombard Odier, J. Safra Sarasin, EFG International, UBP, Vontobel (private-client AuM ~CHF 111bn in 2024; group market cap ~$5.34bn as of Jul 2026 per companiesmarketcap). Basis of competition: advisor talent, product/discretionary-mandate penetration, Asia/EM inflows, and — post-Signa — credibility of risk controls. Recent moves with dates: JB FY2025 NNM CHF 14.4bn led by Asia/Western Europe/Middle East; underlying cost/income ratio 67.6% (2 Feb 2026). Objectway bought FNZ's Swiss private-banking tech business (Jul 2026, see [[Objectway buys FNZ's Swiss private banking technology business]]) — ongoing back-office consolidation in the same value chain. JB's position: firmly #2 Swiss pure-play but sub-scale vs UBS; catching up on governance credibility rather than leading. Moat = brand + advisor relationships + switching costs (analysis).

**Risk flags.**
1. Governance/credit overhang: CHF 213m net credit losses in 2025 and the 25% IFRS profit drop show the Signa/private-debt legacy is not fully cleaned; a new CFO inherits an unfinished risk-framework rebuild — execution risk on restoring investor trust.
2. C-suite churn: Burrill is the second senior finance change (succeeds Kostakis, departure announced Apr 2026) inside an already-reshuffled executive board under a ~1-year-old CEO — continuity risk; a hire "pending regulatory approval" is announced, not yet live.
3. Scale gap & concentration: at ~1/10th of UBS's AuM, JB is squeezed between a dominant domestic giant and nimble boutiques; NNM leans on Asia/EM, exposing it to geopolitical/FX and single-market flow reversals.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Incremental, not a re-rating: a credible external CFO is a governance-repair signal, consistent with the Bollinger turnaround thesis rather than a growth catalyst (analysis). Watch: (a) whether FY2026 credit losses normalize toward zero and cost/income falls below 67% — the falsifiable test that the cleanup is done; (b) regulatory sign-off and the Aug-2026 start actually landing. Thesis breaks if further loan-book write-downs or another exec exit surface, signaling the reset is still ongoing.
