# a16z: American Dynamism and the Washington-Silicon Valley thaw

> [!info] 2026-07-03 · 2 упоминаний · 0 источника(ов) с текстом
> Каналы: a16z

## Агрегированный текст (из дайджестов)

[a16z] This essay by David Ulevitch originally ran earlier this year, prior to our America Dynamism Summit. Have a great July 4th weekend everyone, we are off next week and we will see you on the 13th! -AD

[a16z] When we launched Fund I four years ago, the relationship between Washington and Silicon Valley had long turned cold. Decades of offshoring and regulatory inertia hollowed out large parts of our industrial base while the “Magnificent Seven” concentrated almost all of the market returns. The U.S. once led in 60 of the 64 most critical technologies. Today we have almost entirely lost that lead, with China now leading in 57 and the U.S. in only 7 technologies. Early American Dynamism pioneers like SpaceX and Palantir made major technological leaps but had to fight to even be allowed to compete for their first government contracts, let alone win them. At the same time, the geopolitical threat from China was growing and the war in Ukraine had not yet begun.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
An **essay by David Ulevitch** (a16z GP, co-lead of the American Dynamism practice), re-run by a16z ahead of its **American Dynamism Summit** (4th annual; the AD Summit series runs 2023→2026, always in Washington DC). It is a **commentary/manifesto**, not a deal or announcement — the analytical weight is in whether its framing is honest, not "what happened."

De-PR'd checks on the essay's own claims:
- **"Fund I four years ago":** the AD *practice* launched **Jan 2022** (Katherine Boyle + Ulevitch, "Building American Dynamism") — so "four years ago" from mid-2026 is consistent. But "Fund I" as a *vehicle* is loosely used: a16z announced a **$500M** AD commitment (May 2023); secondary sources cite a **$600M** dedicated fund and **>$1.1B** total committed by 2024. Exact "Fund I" name/close/size = **open**. → Why it matters: the essay blends "practice launch," "commitment," and "fund" so the founding is bigger/older-sounding than any single verifiable number.
- **The marquee stat — "US once led 60 of 64 critical technologies; China now leads 57, US only 7":** traced to **ASPI's Critical Technology Tracker, published 1 March 2023** (2019–2023 data window; 64 techs). The numbers are quoted *accurately to that 2023 edition*. **BUT the framing is stale/undated in a 2026 essay:** ASPI expanded the tracker (Aug 2024, then 31 March 2026) to **74 technologies, 2021–2025**, now showing **China ahead in 69 of 74**. → Why: presenting a ~3-year-old snapshot as current. Deeper: ASPI itself flags the metric measures **high-impact research citations, not commercialization/manufacturing/deployed capability** — so "lost the tech lead" is a rhetorical leap from "lost the research-citation share." (analysis)
- **"SpaceX & Palantir pioneers who had to fight to even compete for their first gov contracts":** **half-true.** Fair for **Palantir** (In-Q-Tel-seeded ~2005; *sued the US Army in 2016* over DCGS-A procurement and won). **Largely inaccurate for SpaceX** — government seed money was *foundational*: NASA COTS award (Aug 2006, ~$278M) and the $1.6B CRS contract (Dec 2008) that saved the company; SpaceX's real procurement "fight" (suing the Air Force) came *later, 2014*, over launch competition. → Why: both predate the AD practice (2022) and neither is an AD-originated deal — retroactive narrative-building.

Regulatory backdrop cuts both ways — a "thaw" a16z helped *engineer* (donations, summit optics) can refreeze with the next election.
