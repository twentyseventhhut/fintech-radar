Simple. launches bank-facing Global Gateway for FX access
Claudia Pincovski
15 Jul 2026 / 4 Min Read
Simple., a US-based financial company specialising in banking infrastructure and foreign exchange, has launched its Global Gateway for Banks to FX, a bank-facing platform designed to let financial institutions offer cross-border foreign exchange and payment services to their customers.
Single point of access to regulated providers
The Global Gateway connects a bank's authenticated customers to a network of regulated FX and payment providers through one platform. According to the company, this removes the need for a bank to become an FX provider itself, maintain correspondent banking relationships, or operate its own FX marketplace. Customer authentication remains with the participating bank, while execution of the underlying FX and payment services is carried out by the regulated providers in the network.
Simple. positions the offering as distinct from embedded FX providers and institutional FX trading venues. Rather than taking over the bank's customer relationship or requiring banks to build FX infrastructure themselves, the platform is intended to function as a standardised gateway, with banks retaining the customer interface while providers handle execution.
Built on South American FX infrastructure
The platform draws on Simple.'s experience supporting regulated FX providers across South America, where the company has built infrastructure for cross-border payment and FX connectivity. That base now underpins a global platform aimed at banks seeking faster implementation and access to a wider network of regulated providers.
A company official said many banks want to offer improved cross-border payment and FX capabilities but face operational, regulatory, and infrastructure barriers, and that the Global Gateway is intended to give banks a standardised way to connect customers to regulated FX providers while banks continue to focus on their own customer relationships.
Addressing correspondent banking gaps
Cross-border payments continue to depend heavily on correspondent banking networks. While SWIFT remains a central part of international banking, not all financial institutions, particularly smaller and regional ones, have extensive correspondent relationships or broad FX access. Simple. states that around four thousand US banks do not offer SWIFT services, and that non-US institutions typically require a US banking partner and nostro or correspondent banking relationships to settle transactions abroad.
The company also points to regulatory developments in Brazil as an example of the volatility banks and FX providers can face in cross-border markets, citing a new levy on FX payouts of up to 900%, a capital requirement it says could remove around 80% of existing FX providers from the market, and restrictions on USDC and USDT purchases that reportedly pushed vendor costs for these tokens up sevenfold within a single day.
Capabilities and rollout
Key features of the Global Gateway include bank-facing integration, customer authentication managed by the bank, connectivity to regulated FX and payment providers, and an architecture designed to expand as further providers join the network. Simple. says the platform is intended to be deployed without banks needing to build or operate their own FX marketplace.

# Context-enrichment: Simple. launches bank-facing "Global Gateway for Banks to FX"
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**Disambiguation (critical).** This "Simple." (branded with a trailing period) is a **US-based B2B banking-infrastructure / FX company** whose existing infrastructure supports **regulated FX providers across South America (notably Brazil)**. It is **NOT** the defunct BBVA-owned US neobank Simple (simple.com, wound down 2021), **NOT** the Colombian "Simple App" crypto wallet ([[Simple App launches non-custodial crypto wallet in Colombia]]), and **NOT** [[SimpleChain raises $15M for RWA-focused Layer 1 blockchain]]. Beyond the single 15 Jul 2026 Paypers blurb the company has **almost no independent web footprint** — no confirmable corporate domain, founders, HQ, funding, headcount, or prior coverage surfaced. That near-invisibility is itself a finding and caps confidence.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Simple. **announced** (not evidenced as live) "Global Gateway for Banks to FX": a **bank-facing** platform that connects a bank's **authenticated** customers to a **network of multiple regulated third-party FX/payment providers** through one integration. The bank keeps the customer relationship/auth; the regulated providers execute the FX and payment. The bank does **not** become an FX provider, hold correspondent/nostro relationships, or run its own FX marketplace.
- **De-PR'd status: announcement-stage.** No named bank clients, pilots, partners, corridors, volumes, funding, or pricing disclosed. Single-source (The Paypers).
- **Why framed this way:** the pitch is deliberately anchored to a **Brazil regulatory "shakeout" narrative** (see [4]) — that crisis is real and well-timed, and functions as the launch's emotional hook. The company's own magnitude claims ("up to 900%" FX levy, "~80%" of providers removed, token costs "7x in a day") are **self-reported** and should be quoted as claims, not facts. The underlying regulations, however, are genuine 2025-26 Brazilian developments.
- **Why "network of providers" (not a single vendor):** the structural claim is **provider neutrality** — a marketplace of competing regulated providers rather than locking the bank into one white-label counterparty. That is the one genuinely differentiated line; whether neutrality is real at launch is unverified (no providers named).

## [1] Competitors / peers
The standard bank-enablement FX set splits two ways, and **none is a true neutral multi-provider marketplace sold to banks** — that is Simple.'s claimed whitespace:
- **Bank-facing but single-provider infrastructure** (bank keeps customer; ONE vendor executes): **Currencycloud (Visa-owned since Dec 2021)** — closest GTM analog; **Wise Platform** (Nubank, Capitec, Standard Chartered/Mox — but proprietary single rail, the opposite of neutral); **Nium** (2025 "Global Collections for banks", see [[Nium and Circle connect USDC settlement with global payouts]], [[Nium secures payment institution license in Brazil]]); **OpenPayd** ([[Currency.com selects OpenPayd for multi-currency infrastructure]], [[VALR adds multi-currency fiat capabilities via OpenPayd]]); **Banking Circle** (bank-facing but as a licensed bank/clearing member disintermediating correspondents — different model).
- **Direct/embedded FX (vendor IS the counterparty to corporates/consumers):** **Corpay Cross-Border** ([[Corpay signs JP Morgan and BVNK as blockchain settlement partners]]), **Convera**, **iBanFirst**, **TransferMate** ([[QNB integrates TransferMate for multi-currency collections]]).
- **Brazil-adjacent FX infra:** **OpenFX** ([[OpenFX launches in Brazil]], $23M Accel round) modernizes institutional FX rails; **XTransfer** (multiple bank tie-ups: [[XTransfer and BBVA partner on cross-border payments]], [[XTransfer and Societe Generale partner on cross-border payments]]) is the SME-facing analog of embedding into banks.
- **Position:** Simple. is **catching up in category, differentiated in framing.** The aggregator-sold-to-banks concept is not new (Currencycloud/Wise/Nium all chase bank enablement); the neutral **gateway-to-a-network** twist is the only novel structural claim, but it rides on far better-capitalized, live incumbents.
- **Why the map looks like this (analysis):** incumbents chose single-provider white-labels because owning execution captures the FX spread (the margin). A neutral gateway **deliberately gives that spread to the providers** and monetizes access/routing instead — which is why nobody big built it: it caps the operator's take-rate. That is the second-order tension in [3].

## [2] Company history / fit
- Confirmed trajectory: built infrastructure supporting **regulated FX providers in South America / Brazil**, now generalizing that base into a global bank-facing gateway. Everything else (founding year, founders, funding, size) is **open/unverified**.
- **Why this move fits (analysis):** a South-America FX-connectivity operator watching Brazil's regulatory regime cull small providers (see [4]) has both the **motive** (its provider base is under threat) and the **asset** (existing regulated-provider connectivity) to reposition upmarket as a bank-facing aggregator in less turbulent jurisdictions (US regional banks). The launch reads as a **defensive pivot dressed as expansion**.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What is genuinely new:** the **provider-neutral gateway-to-a-network** model sold to banks — banks offer cross-border FX without becoming an FX principal, holding nostro balances, joining SWIFT, or picking a single white-label vendor. Value proposition for **smaller/regional US and non-US banks** is plausible: faster time-to-market, no FX balance-sheet/principal risk, competition among providers.
- **Traction: essentially none.** No named banks, providers, volumes, corridors, funding, or independent coverage beyond one newsletter blurb. "Announced" ≠ "live/adopted."
- **Who captures the margin (analysis, the real question):** in this model the **FX spread accrues to the executing providers, not Simple.** — so Simple. must monetize routing/access/integration fees. That makes it a **thin-margin toll booth** whose value depends entirely on network density (enough banks + enough providers). Without named partners on either side, the network is empty and the flywheel unproven. The central question is **not "is the thesis interesting" (it is) but "can a near-invisible, likely-undercapitalized vendor assemble a two-sided network against Currencycloud/Wise/Nium."**

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Regulatory backdrop (genuine, verified — the strongest part of the story):**
  - **IOF/FX tax hike — REAL.** Decree 12.466/2025 (22 May 2025) raised IOF on outbound remittances ~0.38% → 3.5% and repealed the glide-path to zero. Simple.'s "up to 900%" is a defensible spin on the ~9x rise in that tax line — inflated framing, not a 900% total cost.
  - **Capital requirement culling providers — REAL basis, "~80%" unverified.** BCB Resolutions 519/520/521 (10 Nov 2025, effective Feb 2026) reclassify stablecoin activity as FX, create the SPSAV licensed-VASP category with min. capital ~R$1M–R$35M; a plausible cull of small unlicensed operators. "~80%" is Simple.'s claim.
  - **USDC/USDT settlement restriction — REAL, "7x in a day" unverified.** BCB Resolution 561 (30 Apr 2026, effective 1 Oct 2026) bans eFX providers from settling cross-border remittances via stablecoins/crypto. Related corpus: [[Brazil weighs taxing crypto for cross-border payments]], [[Brazilian tourists use stablecoins to avoid IOF tax abroad]].
  - **~4,000 US banks don't offer SWIFT directly** — directionally plausible (~3,867 US banks hold SWIFT/BIC codes out of ~4,000–4,500 chartered; many small institutions ride a correspondent's SWIFT/Fedwire). Non-US institutions needing a US partner + nostro to settle USD is standard and correct.
- **Sentiment/forecast (analysis):** the Brazilian shakeout is a real tailwind for *any* well-run cross-border infra play, but it cuts both ways — Simple.'s **own provider base sits inside that turbulence**, so its "expand to banks abroad" pivot is as much survival as opportunity. Expect no market re-rating from this: watch-list item pending a confirmable domain, funding, or first named bank/provider partner.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Cross-border payments is a very large, structurally fragmented pool: per Grand View Research (via grandviewresearch.com, as of 2026) the cross-border payments market was ~$187.7bn revenue in 2025, growing to ~$312.1bn by 2033 at ~7.1% CAGR; per FXC Intelligence (via fxcintel.com, as of 2025) B2B cross-border flows were ~$31.7tn in 2024, +51% to ~$47.8tn by 2032. Simple. does not sit in the whole pool — it targets the thin **bank-facing FX-as-a-service / orchestration** layer: banks that lack SWIFT access, correspondent relationships or an FX desk. The note's own datum — "~4,000 US banks do not offer SWIFT services" — is the actual demand thesis, and is a credible structural gap (thousands of US community/regional banks vs a handful of correspondent-scale players). Structure: value chain splits into (a) provider/liquidity layer (regulated FX providers, correspondent banks), (b) orchestration/gateway layer (where Simple. plays — aggregating providers behind one integration), (c) bank customer-interface layer (which Simple. explicitly leaves with the bank). Barriers: regulatory licensing, provider-network breadth, and bank-integration switching costs — not capital-intensive rails ownership. Why now: correspondent-banking de-risking keeps shrinking small-bank access, and orchestration/aggregation (the "connect to many regulated providers via one API" model) is the current answer, mirrored across the peer set.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: FX volume / TPV routed, take rate (bps on notional), number of connected providers/corridors, bank clients onboarded. Basis of competition: distribution (which banks/providers you sign) and product breadth (corridors, compliance) rather than price. Key players by layer: **incumbents / scaled infra** — Corpay Cross-Border, Convera, Nium, Ebury, Airwallex, Wise, XTransfer, TransferMate; most, however, sit as *providers* or compete for the *end-customer*, whereas Simple. positions as a neutral *gateway* that does NOT take the bank's customer — a narrower, more BaaS-like posture. Recent peer moves: Corpay agreed to acquire Alpha Group at ~$2.2bn EV (per SEC 8-K, FY2025); its Corporate Payments segment revenue +27% to $410m in Q3 2025 (~35% of group). [[Nium acquires crypto infrastructure startup Cypher]] and [[Nium and Circle connect USDC settlement with global payouts]] show the provider layer bolting on stablecoin settlement; [[Airwallex raises new funding at $12 billion valuation]] and [[Santander's Ebury eyes £100m Lumon FX takeover]] show capital and consolidation in the same corridor. Protagonist position: **niche / early** `(analysis)` — Simple. has real regional depth (South America FX-provider infrastructure) but is a single-PR unknown at global scale; moat, if any, is provider-network + bank switching costs, not yet demonstrated.

**Comps & multiples.** Simple. is private with **no disclosed round, valuation, revenue or client count** → own multiples = no data. Peer anchors (round/EV, not clean market caps): Corpay Alpha deal ~$2.2bn EV (SEC 8-K, FY2025); Corpay group FY2025 revenue guide $4,405–4,485m (SEC 8-K). Nium last round ~$1.4bn post-money — a ~30% down-round from $2bn (2022), on a $50m Series E (per CNBC, Jun 2024). Airwallex ~$12bn last round (see [[Airwallex raises new funding at $12 billion valuation]]). Clean EV/Revenue only computable for Corpay group: ~$2.2bn Alpha EV is a deal price, not a multiple on Alpha revenue (Alpha revenue not sourced here) → EV/Rev [UNSOURCED]. With only round valuations and one revenue figure, distribution not computed — qualitative only: the peer set spans a wide $1.4bn–$12bn private-valuation range, with Nium's down-round the cautionary marker that cross-border-infra multiples have de-rated since 2022.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Single unverifiable source / no traction disclosed.** The only source is one The Paypers PR (2026-07-15); no named bank client, live volume, provider count or funding is disclosed — "launched" ≠ adopted. Second-order: unclear whether this is a product with signed banks or a positioning announcement.
2. **Sovereign/regulatory volatility in its home base.** Simple.'s edge is South American FX infrastructure, yet the note itself cites Brazil's proposed FX-payout levy "up to 900%", a capital rule that "could remove ~80% of FX providers", and USDC/USDT purchase curbs — its provider network could be gutted by the very regime it grew in. Second-order: gateway value collapses if the underlying regulated-provider pool thins.
3. **Disintermediation from both sides.** As a pure orchestration layer, Simple. is squeezable: scaled providers (Corpay, Nium, Wise, Airwallex) increasingly sell bank-facing APIs directly, while banks' own core vendors (e.g. SBS, Temenos-type stacks) can embed FX — a thin gateway risks having its margin captured above and below it.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Incremental, not a re-rating: another entrant validating the "orchestrate regulated FX providers for under-banked institutions" thesis that Corpay/Nium/Convera already chase — the differentiation is the explicitly neutral, customer-interface-preserving BaaS posture. Falsifiable thesis: if Simple. names ≥1 sizeable US/EU bank client and disclosed volume within ~2 quarters, it's a real gateway; absent that (and given the Brazil regulatory overhang), treat as PR positioning. Watch: first named bank partner, any funding round, and Brazil FX-levy final rule.
