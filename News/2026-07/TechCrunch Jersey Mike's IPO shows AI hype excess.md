Jersey Mike’s IPO illustrates how bad the AI hype has become
I can’t tell the exact tipping point from realistic excitement over a new technology, to hype, to aww-come-on — but I’m pretty sure when a sandwich shop with Danny DeVito as its public face talks about AI in its IPO documents, we must be getting close.
So it is with Jersey Mike’s. 
Because of investor thirst for all things AI these days, I understand why tech companies feel the need to sprinkle AI dust all over their pitches. This is as true for non-AI startups raising venture capital as it is for Bending Spoons’ public debut , a company in the business of buying aging, “not-AI” tech companies to rehabilitate.
Just for kicks, I took a look at Jersey Mike’s IPO documents to see how far this compulsion may go. Surely a sandwich shop would have no need to mention AI in its S-1 . But lo and behold!
The term artificial intelligence and its acronym “AI” were mentioned 22 times. In this case, the company can’t claim to be selling AI software. It sells submarine sandwiches. AI products are what investors are really hungering for (terrible pun intended).
Still, it found a way to mention AI in its investor-risk warnings. That may be even more funny. It doesn’t explain what it’s using AI for that could be dangerous to investors, beyond a hand-wave of a phrase, “We are beginning to use AI Technologies in our business.”
In all fairness, as a company that operates franchisees, it does rely on software (mentioned 52 times) and data (112 mentions), as all businesses do. Its AI risk warning was boilerplate copy, perhaps even necessary, as such disasters have already happened to other food businesses, like the half-baked AI inventory tool that Starbucks rolled out, which couldn’t count and was recently scrapped.
Still, I’m going to go out on a limb here and predict that the risk of an AI disaster for a company that produces real-life sandwiches, not AI slop, is about the same as, say, a franchise shop getting hit by lightning. That actually happened, by the way, to a shop in Texas in 2021. Yet weather was only mentioned five times in the S-1. And lightning? Not once.

# Context-enrichment: TechCrunch — "Jersey Mike's IPO shows AI hype excess"
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
This is an **opinion/analysis column** (TechCrunch, Julie Bort, 2026-07-02), not a deal announcement. The concrete facts under it: **Jersey Mike's Subs** (Blackstone-controlled sandwich franchise) filed a public **S-1** on **2026-07-02** to list on the **NYSE (ticker JMKE)**; underwriters Morgan Stanley, Jefferies, JPMorgan. Reported valuation target **~$10–12bn** (unpriced), vs Blackstone's **~$8bn** 2024 entry. The column's actual claim is narrow and verifiable from the filing text: a sandwich chain that "sells submarine sandwiches, not AI software" mentions **"AI"/artificial intelligence 22 times** in its S-1 — including a boilerplate *risk factor* ("We are beginning to use AI Technologies in our business") with **no stated use-case**. Comparators the author counts: software 52, data 112, weather 5, lightning 0.
- **Why framed this way / what it reveals:** The piece is not a valuation teardown — it uses the S-1's AI name-dropping as a *symptom*, not a cause. The de-PR'd point: the AI mentions add **zero** to the business (a franchise-royalty model), so their presence is pure investor-sentiment signalling. Second order: when a Danny-DeVito-fronted, non-tech, cash-generative franchise pads its prospectus with AI, "AI premium" has leaked from tech into the general IPO discourse — a late-cycle tell (analysis). Do **not** overstate: the column does **not** allege JMKE is *valued* as an AI stock.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Two distinct peer sets, because the column conflates them and the analysis must not.
- **The column's peer set (non-AI issuers AI-signalling):** its own cited example is **Bending Spoons** — see internal [[Bending Spoons prices Nasdaq IPO, hits $25B market cap]] (Milan software conglomerate, priced Nasdaq above range ~$18.4bn, ticker BSP, 2026-07-01). Same pattern: a "not-AI" business courting AI-hungry capital. Position: JMKE is the *more extreme* datapoint (sandwiches vs software), which is exactly why the author picks it.
- **The issuer's true valuation peers (restaurant/franchise):** at ~$12bn / ~$328–339M adj. EBITDA ≈ **~30–37x EV/EBITDA** and **~16x EV/revenue**. CAVA trades ~**60x+** EV/EBITDA (company-operated, still richer); listed QSR franchisors (e.g. Restaurant Brands Intl) sit **~15–16x**; franchisee-level deals **4.5–7x**. So JMKE's ask is **rich for a restaurant** but *below* the frothiest fast-casual name.
- **Why the gap:** JMKE's ~47% franchise-model EBITDA margin justifies a premium over an operator, but a mid-30s EBITDA multiple is **~2x** the best listed QSR comp — a stretch that only "reads normal" against software, which is the column's whole joke (analysis).

## [2] Company history / fit
Founder-led NJ chain, ~**3,300 mostly-franchised units**, 50 states + Canada; grew ~2,000 units over the last decade. **2024:** Blackstone bought a majority stake at ~$8bn, largely debt-financed (~$2.1bn now on the balance sheet). **2026-07-02:** S-1 filed; IPO proceeds partly earmarked to **repay debt** — i.e. a sponsor deleveraging + monetization event, not growth capital.
- **Why act now:** Blackstone is exiting into a **reopened, record IPO window** (US equity issuance hit a record ~$251bn in H1 2026). The AI language in the S-1 is the franchise's way of *not being left out* of that bid. Forbes flagged governance items (~$50M pay to a stepson, $41M aircraft, family on payroll) — classic pre-exit related-party disclosures that argue incentives favour a **high print** over durable public-market value (analysis).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **The business is genuinely strong, and that is the point:** FY2025 revenue **$724M (+11%)**, net income **$55M** (vs $5M prior yr), systemwide sales **$4.3bn (+13%)**, adj. EBITDA **~$328M (~47% of revenue)**, AUV **~$1.4M** (~3x Subway US), pipeline **>1,600 units**. None of this is AI-driven.
- **Novelty of the *story* is media-driven, not fundamental:** the hook is a word-count in a filing, not a business change. "AI at Jersey Mike's" is, per the author, mundane (ordering/labour scheduling) and disclosed only as boilerplate risk copy. Value-add of the *item* = it's a clean, high-signal **sentiment thermometer**, not a fintech/tech event. Who captures the margin: in the *deal*, the sponsor (Blackstone) and selling insiders; in the *narrative*, TechCrunch harvests the AI-hype angle. No traction claim to make on "AI" because none is disclosed.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Next on the deal:** price range + roadshow + pricing still pending; the real signal is whether JMKE prices near the top of the ~$12bn ask and how the first 30 days trade. Watch subsequent non-tech S-1s for rising AI mention-counts (the falsifiable "hype" marker).
- **Sentiment:** 2025–26 is a hot IPO window (Fed ~4.25–4.5%; AI mega-listings clearing). Bubble commentary is concentrated on **AI infrastructure** (e.g. CoreWeave called the "poster child of the AI infrastructure bubble"), not restaurants — restaurant multiples have actually been *compressing* (CAVA down ~30% from highs). Internal IPO-window precedents for cross-checking cycle temperature: [[Chime targets $9.5-11.2bn valuation in IPO]], [[Ripple IPO could reach $30B valuation]], [[Wealthfront prices US IPO at $2B valuation]], [[PayPay valuation could top $20 billion in December US IPO]], [[Bullish raises ~$1.1 billion in upsized IPO]] — all fintech, useful as sentiment markers only, not EBITDA comps.
- **Second-order:** the counterintuitive read is that AI padding in a *sandwich* S-1 is bearish breadth — issuers reaching for a narrative they don't have is a classic sign the window is closing on later, weaker deals. **Thesis-killer:** JMKE prices at a restaurant-appropriate ~12–18x EBITDA and trades flat-to-up → the market disciplined the padding rather than rewarding it, and the "hype" read is overdone.

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** The relevant sector here is NOT fast-casual sandwiches — it is the 2026 equity-capital-markets / IPO cycle and its AI-driven sentiment, which is what the TechCrunch column actually diagnoses (a sandwich franchise put "AI" in its S-1 22 times, incl. risk factors). First-half 2026 US equity issuance hit a record ~$251bn through Jun 26, surpassing the 2021 peak (per Yahoo Finance/Reuters, as of 2026-06-26). The average 2026 IPO valuation is reported at ~3x last year's and ~10x the 2022 average (per iShares/BlackRock commentary). Structure of the driver: a small number of AI mega-deals dominate — SpaceX priced 2026-06-12 at ~$1.77tn ($86bn raised), Alphabet's ~$85bn follow-on, with OpenAI (draft S-1 2026-06-08, ~$920bn) and Anthropic (draft S-1 2026-06-01, ~$965bn) in the pipeline (per iShares/Nasdaq/company reports). **Why now:** capital is chasing an AI narrative, which pulls non-AI issuers (a Blackstone-owned sandwich chain) into "AI-signalling" their prospectuses to ride sentiment — the column's thesis, and a classic late-cycle tell (analysis).

**Competitive landscape.** For the IPO-window "sector," the KPI is issuance quality: AI-narrative concentration, valuation dispersion vs fundamentals, and post-listing performance. For the actual issuer, restaurant KPIs are systemwide sales, AUV, same-store sales, unit growth, franchise-royalty margin. Jersey Mike's fundamentals are genuinely strong and NOT AI-dependent: FY2025 revenue $724M (+11%), net income $55M (vs $5M prior yr), systemwide sales $4.3bn (+13%), adj. EBITDA $327M (~47% of revenue), AUV ~$1.4M (~3x Subway US average), pipeline >1,600 units incl. a UK/Ireland push (per CNBC/Bloomberg/NRN, filed 2026-07-02, NYSE ticker JMKE). Position: a high-margin, cash-generative franchise royalty model — the AI language is boilerplate, not a moat or a business line. The company is silent on WHAT it actually does with AI beyond "We are beginning to use AI Technologies in our business," which is the tell.

**Comps & multiples.** IPO target reported ~$12bn (some reports $10–12bn), vs Blackstone's 2024 entry at $8bn.
- EV/Revenue ≈ $12bn / $724M = **16.6x** — extreme for a restaurant, only "normal" against software, not sandwiches.
- EV/EBITDA ≈ $12bn / $327M = **36.7x** — vs Restaurant Brands Intl (QSR) ~15.9x (as of 2026-06-23) and franchisee-level deal ranges of 4.5–7x EBITDA. Even top-tier fast-casual trades ~8–15x. At 36.7x, JMKE would price ~2.3x the highest-quality listed QSR comp.
- Sanity: EV/EBITDA outlier range guide is ~8–25x; 36.7x breaches it. Growth (systemwide sales +13%, rev +11%) is solid but does NOT mathematically justify a QSR trading at a mid-30s EBITDA multiple — this is a valuation stretch, not a growth-justified premium (analysis).
Internal comps (public-market IPO valuation precedent from the base): [[Ripple IPO could reach $30B valuation]], [[Chime targets $9.5-11.2bn valuation in IPO]], [[Wealthfront prices US IPO at $2B valuation]], [[PayPay valuation could top $20 billion in December US IPO]], [[Revolut targets up to 200B valuation in IPO two years out]] — all fintech, not restaurant, so useful only as IPO-window sentiment markers, not as EBITDA comps. Rich-vs-peers verdict: **rich** on both revenue and EBITDA.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Over-valuation vs comps** — a ~36.7x EV/EBITDA ask for a franchise sandwich chain is ~2x+ the best listed QSR multiple; if AI-cycle sentiment cools before/at pricing, the deal re-rates down toward 15x, i.e. a large gap between the $12bn ask and a fundamentals-anchored value (second-order: post-IPO underperformance risk for allocated investors).
2. **AI-narrative / disclosure quality** — "AI" 22 mentions incl. risk factors with no stated use-case is boilerplate signalling; it does not add value but it does signal a market where issuers pad prospectuses to catch a bid — a late-cycle marker that raises the odds the window closes on later, weaker deals.
3. **Sponsor exit / governance** — Blackstone is monetizing (entry $8bn 2024 → ~$12bn exit), and Forbes flagged related-party pay ($50M for a stepson, $41M aircraft, family on payroll). Sponsor-exit + governance items mean incentives favor a high print at IPO over durable post-listing value (second-order: alignment risk for public buyers).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The pick is a sentiment thermometer, not a fintech event: when a non-AI, Danny-DeVito-fronted sandwich franchise puts AI in its S-1 risk factors, the marginal AI dollar has moved from "excitement" to "hype" — historically a late-cycle signal. Falsifiable thesis: if 2026 H2 IPO breadth keeps widening into low-quality, AI-tagged non-AI issuers AND aftermarket returns turn negative, the window is closing; **trigger to watch** — JMKE's final priced valuation vs the $12bn ask and its first-30-day trading, plus whether subsequent non-tech S-1s keep inflating AI mention counts. What would make the thesis wrong: JMKE prices at a restaurant-appropriate ~12–18x EBITDA and trades flat-to-up, i.e. the market disciplines the AI padding rather than rewarding it.
