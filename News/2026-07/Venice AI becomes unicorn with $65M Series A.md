Venice AI becomes a unicorn with $65M Series A as its privacy-first AI platform takes off
Concerns over the impact of AI chatbots on mental health , personal safety , harassment , and disinformation have forced AI developers to implement safeguards to better control how and what their AI models are allowed to respond or do. 
But concerns and worries can’t erode demand. AI offers a lot of promise, and people don’t want a faceless tech company to restrict their access to that potential. And if they can preserve their privacy while they use AI models however they want, why not?
 Venice AI , which offers access to more than 200 AI models while allowing users to retain their privacy, is raking it in thanks to that demand. Just two years in, the company already has more than 850,000 unique visitors to its website, and serves more than 3 million active users and an average of 1.7 million API calls per day.
The startup hosts “uncensored,” open source models on its own data centers, and routes queries to closed-source models, such as those by OpenAI or Anthropic. All user input is encrypted and unencrypted client-side, and routed through an external proxy before it is processed and returned, with no data stored on Venice’s own systems. It also provides end-to-end encryption on some models, though you have to pay for a subscription to get that feature.
The company is already profitable, with annualized run-rate revenues of over $70 million, its CEO Erik Voorhees (pictured above, in the center) told TechCrunch during an exclusive interview.
Understandably, investors have flocked to get a piece of that traction. Venice AI on Wednesday said it had raised a $65 million Series A at a $1 billion valuation, its first external fundraise. The round was led by crypto-focused venture firm Dragonfly, with participation from Coinbase Ventures, North Island Ventures, and others.
The overlap between Voorhees, Venice’s focus on privacy, and its new crypto investors is hard to miss, especially given the CEO’s background and past work. An early bitcoin advocate, Voorhees has founded a few crypto companies, including bitcoin gambling site Satoshi Dice and cryptocurrency exchange ShapeShift , and has long advocated in favor of preserving users’ privacy.
In fact, when a Wall Street Journal investigation accused ShapeShift , which initially didn’t require its users to identify themselves, of processing millions of suspect funds, Voorhees reportedly said: “I don’t think people should have their identity recorded to catch an occasional criminal.”
He struck a similar note when asked how Venice AI thinks about offering access to AI models in light of recent cases of AI psychosis and resulting harm, saying his team treats their service as a “neutral tool or a neutral platform.” 
“This is the same principle that you have in Bitcoin, where Bitcoin, as a neutral protocol, works the same way for all people,” he said. “I think it’s actually quite dangerous from a safety perspective, for the world to enter this next phase and have everyone be constantly watched. To me that is actually much more dangerous than any particular person asking a controversial question or something that might be considered bad.”
There’s a considerable focus on giving users agency, too. Users can freely choose from AI models that can generate text, images, audio, and video — all of which vary in their performance, quality, and the amount of censorship applied. The website prominently features several AI “characters” that you can customize and chat with, and the company proudly states it offers an “uncensored” experience.
 “We’re optimizing for freedom and actually respecting users as adults, which is, I think, rare these days,” Voorhees said.
The founder said Venice also works on some open models’ system prompts to instruct them to answer more openly, though it doesn’t add any restrictions to the models.
Unsurprisingly, there are two crypto tokens associated with the effort. Venice launched a token called “VVV” in early January, in a bid to attract users, Voorhees said, and in August last year added another, called “DIEM.” Users can buy VVV and then stake it to mint DIEM, which generates $1 worth of AI credits per day that you can spend on Venice. However, Voorhees said only about 8% of the company’s users pay with crypto. 
The founder credited the company’s growth to the good performance of the crypto tokens, though he said the strongest driver was getting close to feature parity with ChatGPT. “When we launched, we were very far away from what ChatGPT could do, but people would use us because it was private. And today, we’re very close to what ChatGPT can do […] so as we’ve closed that gap, it’s become an increasingly compelling alternative,” he said.
Looking forward, Venice AI wants to use the fresh cash to start buying GPUs and building its own data centers so it can stop leasing GPUs and increase its gross margins.

### [0] TL;DR
Venice AI closed a **$65M Series A at a $1B valuation** (announced 2026-07-01) — its **first external raise** since launching in May 2024. The round was **led by Dragonfly**, with **Coinbase Ventures**, **North Island Ventures** and others. Venice is a **privacy-first, "uncensored" AI aggregator**: it fronts 200+ open- and closed-source models, encrypts prompts client-side, routes via an external proxy, and stores nothing server-side. It claims profitability with **>$70M annualized run-rate revenue**, **3M+ active users**, **~850k unique monthly visitors**, and **~1.7M API calls/day**. CEO **Erik Voorhees** (Satoshi Dice, ShapeShift founder) frames the product as a "neutral tool." Two associated crypto tokens (VVV, DIEM) exist, but only **~8% of users pay with crypto**.

### [1] Why it matters
- **Crypto-VC × AI crossover reaches unicorn scale.** A privacy/uncensored consumer-AI play crossing $1B, funded by pure crypto VCs (Dragonfly, Coinbase Ventures), signals the "decentralized/private AI" thesis is now a fundable, revenue-backed category — not just a token narrative.
- **Positioning against Big AI's safety rails.** Venice explicitly monetizes the demand left by OpenAI/Anthropic/Google content filters and logging. It is a bet that a meaningful market wants agency and privacy over guardrails.
- **Revenue-first, not token-first.** Unlike most crypto-AI projects, the equity raise is anchored to real, disclosed SaaS-style revenue (>$70M ARR, profitable), which is unusually strong for a Series A.

### [2] Key facts & numbers
- **Round:** $65M Series A · **$1B valuation** · first external equity raise · announced Wed 2026-07-01.
- **Investors:** Dragonfly (lead), Coinbase Ventures, North Island Ventures, +others.
- **Traction:** >$70M annualized run-rate revenue · profitable (turned profitable ~Q1 2026) · 3M+ active users · ~850k unique visitors · ~1.7M API calls/day.
- **Product:** 200+ models; hosts uncensored open-source models on own capacity, routes to closed models (OpenAI/Anthropic); client-side encryption; no server-side prompt storage; E2E encryption on paid tiers.
- **Tokens:** VVV (launched Jan 2026) + DIEM (Aug 2025). Stake VVV → mint DIEM → $1/day AI credits. Only ~8% of users pay with crypto.
- **Use of funds:** buy GPUs / build own data centers to stop leasing GPUs and lift gross margins.
- **Founder:** Erik Voorhees (co-founder Jesse Proudman, Seattle); early Bitcoin advocate, founder of Satoshi Dice & ShapeShift.

### [3] Context & connections
- **Investor lineage (internal):** Dragonfly leading here fits its capital cycle — see [[Dragonfly closes fourth crypto venture fund at $650M]] (Feb 2026, $650M fund 4). Coinbase Ventures is a recurring crypto-fintech backer — cf. [[Nook raises $2.5M from Coinbase Ventures and others]] and [[Bluprynt raises $4.25M seed from Coinbase Ventures and Robinhood]].
- **Comparable "$1B-valuation" milestones (internal):** [[Mesh raises $75M Series C at $1B valuation]] (crypto payments, Jan 2026) and [[Allica Bank raises $155M Series D at $1.2B valuation]] (Feb 2026) — Venice is a rare crypto-adjacent AI entrant to the unicorn club.
- **Token vs fundamentals tension (external):** VVV peaked at **$21.41 on 2026-06-03**, then slid to the low-teens (~$12–13.62) by late June/early July amid profit-taking and broad AI-token weakness. Venice made its **third emissions cut in three months**, from 4M → **3M VVV/year effective 2026-07-01** (~80% below its original 14M/yr). Equity raise and token price are decoupling.
- **Competitive field (external):** Crowded inference/private-AI space — Fireworks, SiliconFlow, Groq, Cerebras on speed/cost; local-model stacks (LM Studio, Llama/Qwen/DeepSeek/Gemma) on privacy. Venice's differentiation is the *uncensored + no-logging + crypto-native* bundle, not raw inference economics.

### [4] Open questions / what to watch
- Durability of >$70M ARR and profitability once it self-funds GPUs/data centers (capex-heavy pivot compresses cash even if margins improve).
- Regulatory / platform risk of the "uncensored" positioning (AI-safety scrutiny, app-store and payment-rail exposure, given Voorhees' ShapeShift AML history).
- Whether the VVV/DIEM token economy is a growth flywheel or a liability if the ~8% crypto-payer base and falling token price erode the incentive loop.
- Dependence on routing to OpenAI/Anthropic closed models — margin and continuity risk if those providers restrict API resale to an explicitly "uncensored" front-end.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

### Red-team / second-order questions

1. **Is >$70M ARR real recurring revenue or token-inflated?** CEO-disclosed, unaudited, and Venice sells AI credits partly via VVV/DIEM. If a chunk of "revenue" is credits bought with a token Venice itself controls, ARR quality is softer than it looks. **Open** — no independent audit; only ~8% pay in crypto per management, which partly de-risks but doesn't verify the figure.

2. **Why is a profitable, $70M-ARR firm raising only $65M?** A profitable unicorn taking its *first* external capital at Series A is unusual — suggests the GPU/data-center capex pivot is the real driver, or that crypto VCs wanted equity access. **Answered:** stated use of funds is buying GPUs to stop leasing — a genuine capital need, not a distress raise.

3. **Does the equity valuation depend on the token?** VVV market cap and the $1B equity valuation are different instruments, but sentiment bleeds across. VVV fell ~40% from its June high into the raise. **Open** — a sustained token collapse could damage the user-incentive flywheel and future fundraises even if equity is nominally decoupled.

4. **Regulatory tail risk from "uncensored."** Voorhees' ShapeShift was accused (WSJ) of processing suspect funds under a no-KYC stance. An uncensored AI generating harmful/illegal content invites platform, app-store, and possibly criminal-liability exposure. **Open** — no regulatory action yet, but this is the single largest existential risk.

5. **Is the moat defensible?** Venice is an aggregator over others' models plus encryption. Local models (LM Studio, Llama/Qwen/DeepSeek) offer privacy for free; DuckDuckGo/Proton offer no-logging chat. **Open** — differentiation is the *uncensored* bundle, which is a positioning choice more than a technical moat.

6. **Dependency on OpenAI/Anthropic routing.** Venice routes queries to closed models it doesn't control. Those providers can restrict API resale to an explicitly uncensored reseller at any time. **Open** — a supply-side kill-switch risk on part of the product.

7. **Does "no server-side storage + external proxy" actually hold?** Marketing claims client-side encryption and no logging, but queries to third-party closed models must be decrypted somewhere to be served. **Open** — privacy guarantee likely applies to Venice-hosted open models, weaker for routed closed models; needs technical verification.

8. **User quality: 3M "active users" vs ~850k unique visitors.** The active-user figure may be inflated by token speculators/airdrop hunters rather than paying AI users. **Partly answered:** only ~8% pay with crypto and revenue is real, implying a paying non-crypto base — but the "3M active" denominator is unverified.

9. **Emissions cuts as a bullish or bearish signal?** Third VVV emission cut in three months (4M→3M/yr) reads as deflationary engineering. Could also signal weak organic token demand needing supply support. **Open** — deflation only helps if buy-and-burn from real revenue exceeds emissions.

10. **Concentration in crypto VCs.** Dragonfly + Coinbase Ventures + North Island are all crypto-native; no generalist AI or growth investor validated the AI thesis. **Answered (as risk):** signals the round is a crypto-thesis bet; absence of a Sequoia/a16z-AI-type check is notable for a $1B AI company.

11. **What happens to gross margins during the GPU-buildout transition?** Shifting from leased to owned GPUs is capex-heavy and lowers near-term free cash flow before margins improve. **Open** — profitability could reverse during the transition.

12. **Is the TAM for "uncensored AI" large enough to sustain $1B?** The privacy/uncensored niche is real but bounded; mainstream users accept guardrails. **Open** — growth ceiling is a key valuation question.

13. **Comparable-milestone reality check.** Peers hitting $1B (Mesh, Allica) are in payments/banking with clearer regulatory footing; Venice's path is more fragile given content and token risk. **Answered:** valuation is defensible on revenue multiple (~14x ARR) but carries higher qualitative risk than those comps.

14. **Founder-key-man risk.** The brand, thesis, and crypto-VC relationships are tightly tied to Voorhees. **Open** — governance/succession undisclosed.

15. **Is this genuinely new vs. prior DB coverage?** No prior Venice AI note exists in the DB; Dragonfly's fund-4 close (Feb 2026) is background, not the same deal. **Answered:** genuinely fresh — first coverage of this company and this raise.

**Importance: 4/5** — A profitable, revenue-backed crypto-VC × AI unicorn is a meaningful, category-defining datapoint (privacy/uncensored AI now fundable at scale) and a clean marker of the crypto-AI capital crossover, with a named lead (Dragonfly) that ties into tracked fund activity. Held below 5/5 because it's a niche consumer segment with unaudited self-reported figures, heavy regulatory/token tail risk, and a single-source (TechCrunch) origin.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Venice sits at the crossroads of two waves: (1) consumer "uncensored/private" AI (adjacent to AI-companion apps) and (2) crypto-native decentralized AI infra. Sector size is fuzzy and mostly vendor-cited: the generative-AI chatbot market is put at ~$12.98bn for 2026 and the broader AI-companion market at ~$49.5bn (per companionguide.ai / momenticmarketing.com, as of 2026 — secondary blog aggregators, treat as directional, not audited; the tighter "uncensored" niche has no credible TAM → no data). Structure: highly concentrated at the top — ChatGPT is cited at ~68% chatbot share (per LinkedIn/aiforleaders, 2026, unverifiable) — with a long tail of niche players; Venice's wedge is a *product feature* (privacy + no content filter + 200+ models) rather than a frontier model, so its barrier is brand/positioning and a crypto-incentive flywheel, not model IP or capital scale. Why now: (a) safety-driven guardrails on incumbent models create a "freedom" niche; (b) crypto capital is actively hunting a decentralized-AI thesis after a string of 2026 rounds (Nous, Prime Intellect, Gensyn). De-PR: Venice *routes* to closed models (OpenAI/Anthropic) and hosts open models itself — it is a privacy proxy + aggregator, not a model lab; the "own data centers / buy GPUs" plan is announced, not live.

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs the sub-sector runs on: ARR/run-rate revenue, paying-user conversion, gross margin (GPU cost structure), tokens processed, and — crypto-specific — token market cap vs. protocol revenue. Players & basis of competition:
- *Consumer/uncensored AI:* Character.AI (~20M MAU, ~$32M ARR, valued ~$1bn in the reset after peaking at $2.5–5bn; Google's 2024 $2.7bn licensing deal gutted the standalone story) — competes on product/community, loss-making. Replika, and a long tail (337 revenue-generating companion apps per companionguide.ai). Basis: product + distribution, not price.
- *Decentralized/crypto AI:* Nous Research ($50–65M from Paradigm at ~$1bn, per theblock.co), Prime Intellect (~$70M, Founders Fund), Gensyn (~$50M, a16z crypto). Basis: distributed compute / verifiable ML — mostly infra, pre-revenue.
- Recent moves: Venice cut VVV emissions for the third time in three months (to 3M tokens/yr effective 2026-07-01, per cryptobriefing.com) — a deflationary defence as the token corrected from a >$21 ATH in June to the mid-teens.
Venice's position: *niche but unusually monetized* — actually profitable at ~$70M run-rate, which is rare in this cohort. Moat `(analysis)`: weak on tech (it re-sells others' models), moderate on brand ("neutral tool"/Voorhees crypto following) and on a token-incentive flywheel (VVV→DIEM credits) — but that flywheel is also a liability (see risks). Feature parity with ChatGPT is self-asserted by the CEO, unverified.

Read: at **~14.3x run-rate revenue while profitable**, Venice is the *cheapest* name in its loose peer set on a revenue basis (Character.AI ~2x richer and loss-making) — in-line/arguably cheap IF the ~$70M run-rate is durable and not token-subsidised. EV/EBITDA, forward, and margin detail → `[UNSOURCED]` (private, first raise). VVV token market cap (~$600M+, per coingecko/coinmarketcap 2026) is a *separate* value pool from the $1bn equity valuation and should not be added.

**Risk flags.**
1. *Revenue quality / token subsidy.* ~$70M run-rate overlaps with a token-credit economy (stake VVV → mint DIEM → $1/day AI credits). If a slice of "revenue" or usage is really emissions-funded credit rather than cash, the 14.3x multiple flatters a partly circular economy; CEO says only ~8% pay in crypto but the split of paid vs. credit-funded compute is undisclosed → watch for margin/quality erosion as emissions are cut (3rd cut in 3 months). Second-order: falling emissions reduce staking yield → weaker flywheel → churn.
2. *Regulatory / content-liability.* An explicitly "uncensored," privacy-by-design platform ("no data stored," CSAM/harassment/AI-psychosis exposure) invites platform-safety, KYC/AML and jurisdictional risk — Voorhees' own ShapeShift history (WSJ suspect-funds allegations) is a precedent lenders/regulators remember. Second-order: a single enforcement action or app-store/payment-rail deplatforming could sever distribution.
3. *Disintermediation / thin moat.* Venice re-sells OpenAI/Anthropic and open models; its differentiation is a privacy proxy that incumbents or open-source wrappers can replicate, and its cost base is leased GPUs (margin captured up-stack by model & cloud providers). The planned self-owned data centers are unproven capex; execution risk on becoming an infra owner vs. staying a thin aggregator.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Signals a re-rating of the *crypto×AI consumer* thesis: profitability at ~$70M run-rate is the first hard revenue proof-point in a cohort (Nous/Prime Intellect/Gensyn) that is otherwise pre-revenue infra — crypto VCs (Dragonfly, Coinbase Ventures) are underwriting distribution + token flywheel, not model IP. Falsifiable thesis: if Venice sustains >$70M *cash* run-rate and expands gross margin after in-housing GPUs, it validates "privacy-as-a-feature" as a standalone consumer-AI category; watch next quarter's paid-conversion and the VVV emission-cut path. What breaks it: a regulatory/content action, or evidence that run-rate is materially token-credit-subsidised rather than cash — either would collapse both the equity and token narratives at once.
