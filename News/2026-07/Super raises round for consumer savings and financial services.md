# Context-enrichment: Super.com raises $65M Series D for consumer savings and financial services
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Disambiguation.** This is **Super.com** (legal entity Snapcommerce Technologies), a Toronto-founded, San Francisco-headquartered "savings super app," formerly **SnapTravel / Snapcommerce**. It is NOT India's super.money (Flipkart's UPI/lending arm) nor Indonesia's Superbank — those are unrelated companies sharing the "super" tag in the corpus. Founded 2016 by Hussein Fazal and Henry Shi.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **$65M Series D, led by TPG, at a $1.2B post-money valuation** (announced 2026-07-07). Reported by Fortune, The Globe and Mail, and Super.com's own newsroom.
- Company claims: became **profitable**, grew net revenue **>50% YoY to $200M+**, diversified across recurring (subscription/interchange) and transactional (travel commissions/advance fees) streams. **Super+ membership approaching ~1M members**; **over half of US hotel bookings now come from Super+ members**; **>$1B in cumulative direct savings to users since 2016**.
- **Why this framing.** The pitch is explicitly "Amazon Prime for savings" / "flip the script — best rewards historically reserved for high-earners/high-credit; we give them to everyday (lower-income, thin/no-credit) Americans." This is a deliberate re-positioning away from OTA (travel-booking) economics — which carry low, commoditized multiples — toward a subscription-and-fintech membership story that supports a software multiple. At ~$200M net revenue and $1.2B valuation, that is ~6x revenue: a fintech/subscription multiple, NOT an OTA multiple (~1–2x).
- **What's vague.** "$200M net revenue" is self-reported and not audited; the split between low-quality travel-commission take vs. durable Super+ subscription + interchange is undisclosed. "Profitable" is unqualified (net income? EBITDA? one quarter?). Sacra independently estimated ~$200M annualized revenue as of mid-2025, which corroborates scale but not the profitability or growth-rate claims.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Klarna** — launched a global membership program (Premium/Max) in Nov 2025 with the near-identical thesis: "premium perks (cashback, travel, lifestyle) without expensive credit" [[Klarna launches global membership program with cashback and perks]]. Direct thematic collision on the "democratize elite perks" positioning, but Klarna is a licensed bank at vastly larger scale.
- **Chime** — US consumer fintech for everyday earners; IPO'd June 2025 at ~$18.4B, 8.6M members, $1.7B 2024 revenue, launched a cash-back secured credit card [[Chime shares surge 59% in IPO at $18.4B market cap]], [[Chime launches cash-back secured credit card]]. The category leader for the same demographic; overlaps on secured card + credit-building.
- **Dave / MoneyLion** — cash-advance + neobank peers; Dave posted record Q3 2025 [[Dave reports record Q3 2025 results, raises guidance]]; MoneyLion settled a CFPB suit for $1.75M [[MoneyLion to pay $1.75M to settle CFPB lawsuit]] — a reminder of regulatory risk around cash advances/fees.
- **KOHO** — Canadian neobank, raised CAD$130M at CAD$1.33B in June 2026 pursuing a bank charter [[KOHO raises CAD$130M as it pursues Canadian bank charter]]. Comparable valuation, shared Canadian roots, but KOHO is chasing a charter whereas Super.com stays partner-fronted/BaaS.
- **Position.** Super.com is a mid-pack challenger, differentiated by its **travel-acquisition funnel** (a legacy OTA moat that most neobanks lack) but well behind Chime/Klarna on scale, balance sheet, and licensing. **Why the landscape is this way:** everyone is converging on "subscription membership + rewards for the mass market" because interchange-only economics are thin; the winner needs a cheap acquisition channel — Super.com's is travel deals, which is its genuine edge.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **2016** SnapTravel (hotel-deals chatbot over SMS/messaging) → **2021** $85M Series B → **2023** ~$85M Series C + rebrand to **Super.com**, pivoting into a savings super-app anchored by **Super+** ($15/mo membership) → surpassed **$1B annual travel GMV in 2024** → 2026 $65M Series D. ~200 employees.
- **Why the company acts this way.** OTA take-rates are a commodity capped by Booking/Expedia; to escape the low multiple, Super.com uses travel as a top-of-funnel to cross-sell into higher-LTV fintech (Super.com Mastercard interchange, cash advances, credit-building) and a recurring subscription. The Series D "profitable + >50% growth" narrative is precisely the evidence needed to justify a software/fintech valuation on a business that started as travel affiliate.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Not novel** as a concept — "savings/rewards super-app for the underserved" is a crowded 2025–26 theme (Klarna, Chime, Dave). The novelty is **execution + funnel**: travel discounts as a low-cost acquisition wedge feeding a fintech wallet, with **real traction claimed** (>50% of hotel bookings from members; ~1M Super+; profitability). If verified, profitability at $200M revenue distinguishes it from many cash-burning neobank peers.
- **Who captures the margin (analysis).** The durable margin sits in **Super+ subscription dues + card interchange**, not travel commissions (which are pass-through and rate-capped by hotel supply). The valuation only holds if the recurring mix is large and sticky. **Risk:** if most of the $200M is travel-transaction take dressed up as "diversified," the 6x multiple is an OTA wearing fintech clothes — the central question is the recurring-vs-transactional split, which management did not disclose.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Capital earmarked to scale the US "savings super app" and Super+ membership. Sentiment is warm — a **profitable** consumer fintech raising at a flat-to-up valuation is notable in a sector that punished cash-burners post-2022. TPG as lead is a credibility signal (growth-equity, not a distressed round).
- **Risks / regulatory backdrop.** Cash-advance and fee-based products draw CFPB scrutiny (cf. MoneyLion). As a partner-fronted (BaaS) model with no bank charter, Super.com carries sponsor-bank and interchange-regulation exposure. **Second-order:** the "flip the script for low-income users" positioning is politically resonant but also the exact demographic regulators watch most closely on fees — the growth story and the regulatory-risk story point at the same customer base.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Super.com (ex-Snapcommerce/SnapTravel; Toronto→SF, founded 2016) sits in the US consumer-fintech "savings super-app" niche serving near-prime / lower-income households — a bundle of travel-commerce discounts, cash back, cash advances and credit-building (SuperCash card, Super+ subscription). No clean sector TAM sourced [UNSOURCED]; the addressable base is framed by the ~200M US adults, of whom the neobank cohort is <5% penetrated (per Chime, ~10.2M active members as of Q1 2026, via valueaddvc/CNBC). Why now: (1) cost-of-living pressure keeps demand for cash-advance/earned-wage and cashback tools high; (2) a re-rating of the cohort after Dave's +60% revenue growth and Chime's June-2025 IPO reopened funding for profitable consumer-fintech. Structure: fragmented — separate cashback (Ibotta, Rakuten), neobank/cash-advance (Chime, Dave, MoneyLion), and travel-commerce players; Super's bet is bundling these into one subscription. Barrier = distribution + a subscription-loyalty flywheel, not a bank charter (it rides third-party rails/interchange).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: net revenue, members/actives, subscription attach, interchange + cash-advance monetization rate. Super.com disclosed 2025: >$200M net revenue (+50% y/y), profitable, Super+ approaching ~1M members, >half of US hotel bookings from Super+ (per Super.com newsroom / Fortune, 2026-07-07). Key players & basis of competition: Chime (scale/checking, distribution), Dave (ExtraCash cash-advance monetization), MoneyLion (financial-wellness marketplace, bought by Gen Digital ~$1bn, closed 2025-04-17), Ibotta/Rakuten (retail cashback/network). Recent moves: Chime IPO'd Jun-2025 at ~$11.6bn FD (CNBC); Gen Digital closed MoneyLion Apr-2025 (PRNewswire). Position: mid-scale niche — revenue ~1/3 of Dave, ~1/11 of Chime — but differentiated by travel-commerce origin + a paid membership (Super+) that few US cash-advance peers monetize. Moat (analysis): subscription switching cost + travel-supply relationships; thin vs Chime's deposit-account stickiness.

**Comps & multiples.** Super.com round valuation P/S = $1.2bn / ~$0.20bn = **6.0x** (round post-money, NOT market cap).
- Dave (DAVE): mkt cap $2.39–3.58bn / $554M FY25 rev → **4.3x–6.5x** P/S; rev +60% y/y (stockanalysis/companiesmarketcap).
- Chime (CHYM): mkt cap ~$8.0bn (2026-07-02) / $2.2bn FY25 rev → **~3.6x** P/S; rev +31% (Yahoo/valueaddvc).
- Ibotta (IBTA): mkt cap ~$0.83bn / $342M FY25 rev → **~2.4x** P/S; rev −7% y/y, cashback-only (stockanalysis).
- MoneyLion: Gen Digital cash deal ~$1bn, $82.00/sh, closed 2025-04 (Banking Dive) — take-out reference, no clean P/S.
- Rakuten Rewards: ~$1.28bn est. valuation / ~$400M rev → ~3.2x — private, third-party estimate, treat as [UNSOURCED].
Internal comps: no US Super.com peers in base; nearest by tag are foreign "Super" names — [[super.money issues 470K RuPay secured credit cards on UPI]], [[Chime lifts 2025 revenue outlook after Q3 growth]]. Median of 3 verifiable public comps (Dave-mid ~5.4x, Chime 3.6x, Ibotta 2.4x) ≈ 3.6x. Read: Super at 6.0x prints at/above the growth-adjusted range — in-line-to-rich vs Dave (which grows faster, +60% vs +50%) and richer than Chime/Ibotta; a private-round premium, plausibly justified by profitability + growth but not cheap.

**Risk flags.**
1. Valuation vs comps — 6.0x on a round basis tops the public median (~3.6x) and sits above faster-growing Dave; a public exit could compress toward peers (Chime IPO'd 54% below its private peak — the cohort de-rates).
2. Rails/interchange dependence — no bank charter; revenue leans on partner-bank interchange, cash-advance fees and travel-supply take, all captured by other stack layers and exposed to CFPB/state scrutiny of cash-advance/"tip" and overdraft-substitute products.
3. Concentration/cyclicality — heavy tilt to travel bookings (>half of Super+ hotel bookings) makes revenue sensitive to a travel-demand downturn, and the near-prime user base is credit-cycle sensitive on the cash-advance book.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) Signals the "bundled savings membership" thesis for near-prime US consumers is fundable again post-Chime IPO and post-MoneyLion take-out — watch whether Super+ can push net-revenue-per-member up via card interchange + credit-building without loss creep. Falsifiable: if 2026 revenue growth decelerates below ~30% (toward Chime) while staying profitable, the 6.0x looks stretched and the next mark re-rates down; a MoneyLion-style strategic acquisition (a Gen Digital / large neobank buyer) is the more likely catalyst than an IPO at this scale.
