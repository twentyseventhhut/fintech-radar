Google Vids now lets you star in your own AI videos
OpenAI’s Sora may have shut down , but Google apparently thinks there’s still interest in a tool that lets you star in your own AI videos. On Thursday, the tech giant announced an update to Google Vids that will allow you to create a custom digital avatar that looks and sounds like you based on a selfie and a voice recording you upload.
In addition, Google said it’s bringing its multi-modal AI model Gemini Omni to Vids, letting you create videos using a combination of a written prompt and reference images you upload. Omni then mixes those inputs together to create the AI video you want. It can also be used to do things like swap out the background or fix the lighting in a video recorded on your phone, or add effects.
Plus, Omni now supports step-by-step edits, meaning you can make changes to your video as you go instead of starting over from scratch. 
The updates push Google Vids beyond its original role as an AI-assisted workplace presentation tool to become more of an all-in-one video creation platform. By making Vids a part of Google Workspace, the company is telegraphing its use as a business tool for things like company updates or training videos, but personalized avatars and conversational edits could put it in closer competition with other AI video startups and tools like HeyGen , Synthesia , Cap t ions , D-ID , and others.
Google notes that the new AI avatars will be tied to the account holder’s likeness, tied to their Google account, and watermarked invisibly with SynthID. (I suppose that means no one will be using the tool to make bizarre AI videos of Google CEO Sundar Pichai, the way that OpenAI CEO Sam Altman had let users do with Sora when it was available!)
The company also says that access to personal avatars is limited to users in certain regions who are aged 18 or older.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Two features bundled into one 2026-07-16 announcement (Google blog):
1. **Personal avatars** — upload a selfie + a short voice recording, type a script, and a digital twin of you delivers it. Restricted to the account holder's likeness, tied to the Google Account, SynthID invisible watermark on every clip, English-only, 18+, "certain regions."
2. **Gemini Omni in Vids** — text-prompt + reference-image video generation; conversational step-by-step edits (swap background, fix lighting, add effects) instead of regenerating from scratch.

**De-PR'd:** this is a **rollout, not GA-everywhere**. Rapid Release domains start 2026-07-16 (up to 15 days to appear); Scheduled Release start 2026-08-05 — so on announcement day most users couldn't use it. Access limited to "Google AI Pro and Ultra subscribers and Google Workspace business customers" — Google did NOT name which Workspace SKUs (genuine vagueness). No standalone pricing; bundled into existing paid tiers. Avatars were already being layered into Vids for months (presentation→Vids avatars shipped 2026-05; "enhanced avatars" 2026-06); the news here is specifically the *own-likeness* avatar + Omni.
**Why structured this way:** "own-likeness only, tied to your account" + SynthID is a deliberate legal hedge (EU AI Act Art. 50 labeling, likeness/consent law) — it sidesteps the impersonation liability that helped kill OpenAI's Sora. The unnamed regions/SKUs and English-only limits signal EU/regulated markets may be gated at launch.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Own-likeness ("digital twin") avatars are ~2 years old among incumbents:
- **Synthesia** — Personal Avatars GA **Jul 2024**; ~$140M ARR (Feb 2026), $4B valuation on $200M raise (2025-10), claims 90%+ of Fortune 100, 65k+ customers; Adobe reportedly explored a ~$3B acquisition (fell through on price).
- **HeyGen** — Instant Avatar **Aug 2024**; Avatar V from a 15-sec clip **Apr 2026**; ~$100M ARR (late 2025).
- **Canva / D-ID** — upload-photo custom avatars live 2024→2025.
- **Adobe Firefly** — video avatar generator + multi-model hub (Veo 3.1, Runway, Kling) expanded **2026-03-19**.
- **OpenAI Sora — SHUT DOWN (verified):** web/app killed 2026-04-26, API ends 2026-09-24 (~$1M/day burn, users peaked ~1M then collapsed, copyright/deepfake blowback). *Not* a live competitor. The TechCrunch "Sora shut down" framing is correct.

**Google Vids' position: catching up / reaching parity.** The avatar tech is not novel. Google's only real differentiators are **distribution** (bundled into Workspace, Gmail/Drive graph) and **provenance** (SynthID + account-locked likeness). Second-order: bundling into paid Workspace tiers is the one genuine strategic threat to Synthesia/HeyGen — it undercuts them on price for existing Google customers, but English-only vs Synthesia's 140+ languages blunts enterprise appeal near-term.

## [2] Company history / fit
Google Vids: announced at Cloud Next **Apr 2024** as a Workspace video app; Labs testing Jun 2024; **GA 2024-11-07** (behind Gemini add-ons). Since then: auto storyboard/first-draft from stock media, Gemini script help, Veo generation, presentation→Vids avatars (2026-05), enhanced avatars (2026-06). **Omni fits** by moving Vids from "assemble stock clips + templates" toward "generate/edit footage from scratch," directly overlapping the HeyGen/Synthesia editing loop. **Why Google acts this way:** Workspace needs an AI-video answer to justify Gemini add-on pricing and defend against standalone AI-video tools eating the "company update / training video" use case inside the enterprise.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Not new.** Selfie/clip → digital twin is exactly what Synthesia (Jul 2024) and HeyGen (2024→Avatar V 2026) already ship, more maturely and multilingually. Google's lighter input (selfie + short voice clip) is a modest UX edge, not a capability leap. **Traction: none disclosed** by Google for the Vids avatar feature — a telling silence. For contrast, incumbents publish real numbers (Synthesia ~$140M ARR, $4B val; HeyGen ~$100M ARR). **Announced vs live:** feature is live-rolling; traction is entirely unproven. **Who captures the margin:** the value here is distribution/provenance, not model IP — so Google monetizes via Workspace subscription lock-in, not by winning on avatar quality.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
AI-video-generator market estimates diverge 2–4x (~$0.8–0.95B in 2025–26 up to ~$3.35B by 2034 at ~18.8% CAGR) — directionally growing, precisely unreliable; large enterprises ~62% of revenue. **The real story is regulatory:** EU AI Act Art. 50 mandates clear labeling/watermarking of AI/deepfake content (Transparency Code operationalizing through 2026); US state likeness laws (e.g., NY Fashion Workers Act) require written consent for digital avatars — Google's SynthID + own-likeness-only design is a direct compliance hedge, which is *why* it's region-gated. **Risks:** impersonation/fraud (voice+face = social-engineering vector; "tied to your account" ≠ verified it's you), novelty-then-abandon retention risk (cf. Sora's user collapse), incumbents' 2-year head start and enterprise lock-in. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the cheapest, most durable relevance of this launch to finance is *risk-side* — it makes high-quality voice+face deepfakes cheaper again, feeding the exec-impersonation/wire-fraud threat that fintech fraud/KYC teams track.

**Fintech relevance — weak (stated plainly).** No payments/banking/lending/capital-markets angle. Only tenuous ties: generic compliance/provenance tooling for regulated enterprises, and the deepfake-fraud risk vector. Skip for a fintech digest unless framed as "deepfake fraud tooling gets cheaper."

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** AI video generation is a small but fast-growing software market: ~$0.79bn in 2025 rising to ~$0.85–0.95bn in 2026 (per Grand View / Fortune Business Insights, via secondary aggregators, as of 2026), with the AI-avatar sub-segment (digital human presenters — the exact space Vids just entered) estimated at ~$5.1bn growing ~32%/yr (per Market.us-cited aggregators, 2026 — treat as vendor-marketing TAM, not verified). CAGR estimates cluster ~18–36% depending on scope. Structure: fragmented and pre-consolidation — a long tail of point tools (Synthesia, HeyGen, Captions, D-ID, Runway) with no dominant player yet, low switching costs, and the primary barrier being model quality + likeness/identity trust rather than capital. "Why now": (1) enterprise comms/training is the wedge use-case (company updates, L&D) where synthetic avatars replace studio shoots; (2) the frontier-lab overhang — OpenAI's Sora consumer tool having shut down (per note) leaves the "star in your own video" niche open, and Google is filling it by bundling into Workspace rather than as a standalone.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: ARR + ARR growth, seat/usage attach into existing suites, and identity/consent controls (the trust KPI). Key players & basis of competition — Synthesia and HeyGen compete on avatar realism + enterprise trust; Google competes on **distribution** (bundling into Workspace's installed base) rather than best-in-class model. Recent moves: Synthesia raised $200M Series E at $4bn valuation Jan 2026 (led by Alphabet's GV; note the conflict/signal — Google's own VC arm backs the leading independent peer it now competes with); HeyGen surpassed $200M ARR (doubled in ~8mo, per HeyGen/press, mid-2026) at ~$500M last-round valuation (Series A, Benchmark, 2024). Google Vids adds SynthID invisible watermarking, likeness tied to the Google account, 18+/region-gated, 24 languages coming (per Google blog, 2026-07). Protagonist's position: **catching up on model, ahead on distribution** — a fast-follower using bundling as the moat (scale + suite lock-in / switching costs), not product superiority `(analysis)`.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Distribution disintermediation of standalone peers** — Google (and Microsoft, via Copilot) bundling avatar video into the productivity suite at ~no marginal price compresses the standalone AI-video TAM; independents like Synthesia/HeyGen must justify premium ARR against a "good-enough + free-in-suite" default. Second-order: pressure on the very $4bn/27x-ARR round economics GV just underwrote.
2. **Likeness / deepfake liability + regulation** — identity-cloning from a selfie invites consent, impersonation and biometric-data (BIPA/EU AI Act) exposure; Google's SynthID watermark + account-binding + 18+ gating is a de-risking tell that this is the binding constraint. A regulatory tightening or a high-profile misuse event hits the whole avatar segment, not just Google.
3. **Model dependency / commoditization** — Vids' differentiation rides on Gemini Omni; if avatar generation commoditizes across labs, the moat collapses to pure distribution and margin is captured at the suite layer, leaving no defensible video-specific economics `(analysis)`.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Signals the AI-video-avatar market shifting from standalone-tool phase to **suite-embedded feature** — a structural headwind to independents' pricing power, even as the category grows. Falsifiable thesis: within ~12–18mo the leading independents (Synthesia/HeyGen) re-position toward API/agentic/vertical-specialist plays rather than horizontal "make a talking-head video," because the horizontal layer is being absorbed by Google/Microsoft. Trigger to watch: whether Synthesia's next round holds its ~27x ARR multiple, and whether Google/MSFT start disclosing avatar-video usage as a Workspace/Copilot upsell metric. Thesis breaks if enterprise trust/compliance keeps buyers on dedicated vendors despite the bundled free option.
