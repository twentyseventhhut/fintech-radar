Standard Chartered granted MiCA and EMI licences in Luxembourg
Sinziana Albu
01 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Standard Chartered has been granted a Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) authorisation and an Electronic Money Institution (EMI) licence in Luxembourg.
The move forms part of the next phase of Standard Chartered's Financing & Securities Services digital asset custody strategy in Europe.
The authorisations build on the establishment of Standard Chartered's Luxembourg entity in 2025. Luxembourg is now positioned as the hub for the bank's European digital asset custody operations, with services to be rolled out in phases in line with regulatory permissions, including passporting under MiCA where applicable. Passporting would allow the entity to extend services to other EU member states without requiring separate national authorisations in each jurisdiction.
MiCA is the EU's regulatory framework for crypto-asset issuance and service provision, designed to create a harmonised set of rules across member states. An EMI licence separately permits the issuance of electronic money and the provision of related payment services, which supports the operational infrastructure needed for digital asset custody activities involving client funds.
Regulatory engagement in Luxembourg
According to the official press release, Standard Chartered said the authorisations reflect the bank's collaboration with the Commission de Surveillance du Secteur Financier (CSSF), Luxembourg's financial regulator, over the course of the licensing process. The bank's global head of financing and securities services said that securing the licences represents a step in the bank's digital asset activities in Europe, adding that the authorisations reflect a commitment to operating within regulatory frameworks while supporting clients navigating the digital asset ecosystem.
The chief executive officer of Standard Chartered Luxembourg said the licences enable the entity to progressively expand services to clients across Europe, and pointed to Luxembourg as a strategic choice supported by engagement with the CSSF throughout the process.
Part of a broader international rollout
The Luxembourg authorisations follow the earlier launch of digital asset custody services by Standard Chartered in Asia and the Middle East. The bank said the European authorisations support growing client demand for regulated access to digital assets, an asset class it described as still evolving from a regulatory and infrastructure standpoint.
The development adds to a wider pattern among international banks seeking MiCA authorisation since the regulation became applicable, as institutions look to offer crypto-asset custody and related services within a harmonised EU regulatory perimeter rather than through fragmented national regimes.

# Context-enrichment: Standard Chartered granted MiCA + EMI licences in Luxembourg
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Disambiguation up front:** This is Standard Chartered's OWN bank entity (Standard Chartered Luxembourg S.A.), NOT Ripple and NOT Zodia. A separate same-week note — [[Ripple secures full MiCA CASP authorisation in Europe]] (2026-07-06, story s9f8fa262) — is a different company/entity. Do not merge.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Standard Chartered's Luxembourg entity (Standard Chartered Luxembourg S.A., established **2025**) was granted (a) a **MiCA authorisation** and (b) an **EMI (Electronic Money Institution) licence** by Luxembourg's regulator **CSSF**. The bank frames it as the next phase of its **Financing & Securities Services (FSS)** digital-asset custody strategy in Europe.
- **Date caveat:** The Paypers item is dated 01 Jul 2026, but Standard Chartered's own press release is dated **29 June 2026** — timed just ahead of the **1 July 2026** EU-wide end of MiCA's transitional period (the two dates are being conflated). The ESMA/CSSF MiCA-register add was reported ~25 June. (external)
- **What was actually granted:** SC's language emphasises **"digital asset custody."** The itemised MiCA CASP service categories (custody & administration / transfer / exchange etc.) are **not published** in the release or coverage → **open/unconfirmed.** The EMI licence separately permits e-money issuance + related payment services, supporting client-fund handling for custody.
- **Why structured this way:** SC ran custody through its Zodia JV since 2021; the new milestone puts a **regulated, EU-passportable base inside the bank itself** rather than the JV — and it lands as SC is simultaneously **absorbing Zodia** (see [2]). Luxembourg becomes the declared European custody hub; services roll out "**progressively / in phases**," "subject to regulatory permissions, including passporting under MiCA where applicable." That hedged phrasing = a **licensing milestone, not a live book of business.** → why it matters: the value is optionality (passport right), not day-one revenue.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Banks and institutions already holding MiCA CASP authorisation (dated):
- **Coinbase** — MiCA CASP, **Luxembourg / CSSF, 20 Jun 2025** (first US firm via Lux). The direct template SC copied: same jurisdiction, same "EU hub" framing, ~12 months earlier. (external)
- **Kraken** — Ireland / CBI, ~Jun 2025. **OKX** & **Crypto.com** — Malta / MFSA, 27 Jan 2025. **Bitpanda** — Germany / BaFin, Jan 2025. (external)
- **Bank/TradFi wave:** **SG-Forge (Société Générale)** — France / ACPR-AMF, ~Oct 2025 (custody, transfer; order execution added Feb 2026); **DZ Bank** — Germany / BaFin, ~Jan 2026 (retail); **BBVA**, **CaixaBank** (Spain); **Clearstream** (Deutsche Börse, Luxembourg). Ledger Insights: banks are now ~20% of MiCAR CASP licensees. (external)
- Same-week EU MiCA cohort in the corpus: [[Ripple secures full MiCA CASP authorisation in Europe]], [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]], [[FalconX receives EU MiCA authorization]], [[Sygnum Europe secures MiCAR licence for EU expansion]], [[Hercle secures MiCA and EMI licences in Italy]] — plus the losers: [[Tether abandons Europe as MiCA ban wipes USDT from exchanges]], [[Binance struggles after missing MiCA licence, loses users]], [[Revolut to delist USDT for European users under MiCA]].
- **Position:** SC is a **late / catch-up entrant** — behind the exchange wave (Jan–Jun 2025) and roughly contemporaneous with the TradFi-bank wave (SG-Forge, DZ Bank). Not a first-mover.
- **Why the landscape looks like this (analysis):** the 1 July 2026 hard deadline forced a Q2-2026 rush of authorisations. Luxembourg attracted the capital-markets-grade names (Coinbase, Clearstream, Ripple, SC) via its fund-servicing/ICSD depth; Malta grabbed exchanges on speed; Ireland/Germany/France anchored home-region players. → second order: MiCA is consolidating EU crypto into a smaller set of fully-authorised, passportable institutions and pushing non-compliant issuers (Tether/USDT) out.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **2021:** Zodia Custody launched (SC + Northern Trust; SC-Ventures incubation from ~2018); SC majority owner. Later investors: SBI (~20%, 2023), NAB, Emirates NBD. Zodia Markets (OTC) separate. (external)
- Corpus shows SC's steady digital-asset cadence: [[Zodia Custody expands partnership with Deribit by Coinbase]], [[Zodia Custody partners beaconcha.in for Ethereum staking analytics]], [[Standard Chartered backs DeCard stablecoin credit card]], [[OSL and Anchor Point test HKDAP stablecoin with Standard Chartered]], [[StanChart explores reopening Swiss private bank]].
- **Sept 2025:** SBI–Zodia Japan JV disbanded. **Apr 2026:** SC explores full Zodia takeover (Bloomberg). **18 May 2026:** SC agrees to **acquire the rest of Zodia** — regulated custody folds into SC's own business; the tech platform spins out as "Zodia Solutions" (no deal value). **9 June 2026:** Zodia itself secured a Luxembourg **Payment Institution licence** for stablecoin custody/transfer (distinct from this bank grant). (external)
- **Why SC acts this way (analysis):** as a JV, Zodia gave SC crypto custody without full balance-sheet/regulatory ownership. To offer **EU-passportable custody to institutional clients under a single harmonised regime**, SC needs its OWN authorised entity — hence the in-house Luxembourg licence running in parallel with buying out the JV. It's a control + integration move, logically consistent with its Asia/Middle-East custody footprint.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Genuinely new:** a MiCA + EMI dual licence in an EU member state → the legal right to **passport crypto custody across all 30 EEA states** without per-country authorisation. That is real and non-trivial.
- **What's NOT new:** SC has done institutional crypto custody since 2021 (Zodia) and already runs custody in Asia and the Middle East. This is **catch-up / geographic + regulatory extension**, not a first-of-kind capability.
- **Traction — thin:** **no AUC, no client count, no revenue, no EU go-live date, no Zodia deal value** disclosed anywhere → all open. Release language is "progressively expand" / "in phases." Treat "European custody hub" as a **licensing status, not demonstrated volume.**
- **Who captures the margin (analysis):** custody is a low-basis-point, scale business; the value for a bank like SC is **cross-selling** (FX, financing, securities services, stablecoin flows via the EMI) to institutional digital-asset clients, not custody fees alone. The EMI licence hints at stablecoin-adjacent payment rails — but issuance intent is unstated (open). Real differentiation vs Coinbase/Clearstream is SC's **existing institutional banking relationships**, not the licence itself.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **MiCA passporting mechanics:** CASP rules applicable from **30 Dec 2024**; full authorisation confers passporting (host-state notification only). Transitional/"grandfathered" firms could NOT passport — only fully authorised CASPs. **1 July 2026** was the hard EU-wide end of transition (no extension). SC's licence is the entry point; activation is phased and needs local steps. (external)
- **Regulatory backdrop:** the deadline triggered a bifurcation — compliant institutions (SC, Ripple, Coinbase, Bridge, FalconX, Sygnum) in; non-compliant issuers/exchanges out (Tether/USDT delisted, Binance losing users, Bybit limiting EEA). MiCA is doing what it was designed to: institutionalising EU crypto.
- **Risks / watch-items:** (1) integration risk merging Zodia into the bank entity — two parallel Luxembourg custody stories still to be operationally consolidated; (2) no evidence of live EU volume — execution risk on "phased" rollout; (3) commoditisation — custody margins compress as every major bank gets a MiCA licence, so the durable value depends on cross-sell, not custody itself. → counterintuitive second order: as MiCA licences proliferate among banks, the licence stops being a moat and the winners are decided by **distribution and balance sheet**, where SC is strong but not dominant.

4. **Is the MiCA licence the same thing as the EMI licence?** No — two distinct CSSF authorisations (MiCA CASP + EMI). EMI supports e-money/payment-service flows around client funds. (confirmed)

5. **Is this SC's own bank entity or Zodia?** SC's OWN — Standard Chartered Luxembourg S.A. (est. 2025). Zodia is a separate subsidiary being absorbed. (confirmed)

6. **What is happening to Zodia, and does it double-count with this licence?** SC agreed 18 May 2026 to buy the rest of Zodia; custody folds into SC, tech → "Zodia Solutions." Zodia separately got a Lux PI licence 9 Jun 2026 (stablecoins). Distinct authorisations; operational merger post-close is OPEN.

7. **Is EU crypto custody actually LIVE at SC now?** OPEN / likely no. Release says "progressively expand" / "in phases," "subject to regulatory permissions." Licensing milestone, not confirmed live service.

8. **Any AUC, client count, or revenue disclosed?** NONE. All open. This is why importance is capped — no traction evidence.

9. **Is this genuinely new or catch-up?** Catch-up. SC has done institutional crypto custody via Zodia since 2021 and already runs custody in Asia/Middle East. New part = EU-passportable regulated base. It trails Coinbase's Luxembourg MiCA (20 Jun 2025) by ~12 months.

10. **Why Luxembourg over Ireland/Germany/France/Malta?** Fund-servicing/ICSD depth (Clearstream), constructive CSSF, full 18-month MiCA transition, blockchain-friendly law. Mirrors Coinbase, Clearstream, Ripple. (confirmed rationale, external)

11. **Can SC passport across the EEA immediately?** Legally yes (full CASP = passport right, host-state notification), but SC says rollout is phased and needs local steps. Right ≠ live coverage. (confirmed mechanism)

13. **Does the EMI licence signal an SC stablecoin?** OPEN. EMI enables e-money issuance/payment services; not stated as stablecoin issuance. Zodia's PI licence handles stablecoin custody/transfer separately.

14. **What's the durable value if custody is a commoditised, low-bps business?** (analysis) Cross-sell to institutional digital-asset clients (FX, financing, securities services, stablecoin rails) — leveraging SC's existing banking relationships, not custody fees. Moat is distribution/balance sheet, not the licence.

15. **Biggest downside trigger?** Integration risk merging Zodia into the bank entity + no demonstrated EU volume; if the "phased" rollout stalls and rivals (Coinbase, Clearstream, SG-Forge) capture institutional custody first, SC's licence becomes table-stakes rather than an edge.

Not a reprint of any prior corpus note. No duplicate.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Institutional digital-asset custody, now inside the EU's MiCA perimeter. Market sizing is analyst-sourced and wide: one estimate puts assets under digital-asset custody at >$1trn in 2026 rising to >$7trn by 2035 (~23.7% CAGR) — treat as a single third-party projection, not consensus (via cryptobriefing/EY-Parthenon citations, as of 2026). Structure: MiCA's 18-month transition closed 1 Jul 2026, cutting the EU crypto field from ~1,200 formerly-registered firms to 283 fully licensed CASPs across 25 EU/EEA regulators (per casptracker.eu / financemagnates, as of 6 Jul 2026) — a consolidating, licence-gated market where a single authorisation passports across all 30 EEA states. Entry barrier is now explicitly regulatory + capital, favouring balance-sheet incumbents. Why now: the transition deadline forced a "who stays / who leaves" reset; banks are entering the vacated regulated custody lane rather than through fragmented national regimes. Demand tailwind: ~73% of institutional investors report active/planned digital-asset allocation (per EY-Parthenon 2026 survey, via cryptobriefing).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: assets under custody (AUC), fee take on AUC, number of live jurisdictions/passports, client (institutional) count — SC discloses none of these for this entity `[UNSOURCED]`. Basis of competition: regulatory reach + institutional trust/balance sheet, not price. Bank entrants under MiCA: BBVA (Spain), Société Générale-Forge (custody + stablecoin issuance), Clearstream (institutional asset servicing), plus German BaFin-approved bank-brokers (Commerzbank, Baader, flatexDEGIRO) and Trade Republic/N26 (per financemagnates/trendingtopics, 2026). Crypto-native custodians (Ripple — full MiCA CASP via Luxembourg 6 Jul 2026, see [[Ripple secures full MiCA CASP authorisation in Europe]]; BitGo — see [[BitGo prices IPO near $2B valuation for NYSE listing]]) compete on the same passport. SC's position: **ahead among banks**, and this is de-PR'd more than a fresh entry — SC agreed to absorb Zodia Custody (its ~70% SC Ventures-held subsidiary co-founded 2020) into its core Financing & Securities Services division in May 2026 (per coindesk/sc.com); the Luxembourg MiCA+EMI licence (granted 29 Jun 2026) is the EU regulatory vehicle for that consolidated custody business, not a standalone new build. Custody service already live in Asia & Middle East. Moat `(analysis)`: switching costs + bank-grade trust for institutional AUC, plus MiCA passport as a regulatory moat vs. de-licensed rivals.

**Comps & multiples.** No transaction value or AUC/fee figures were disclosed for this licence — a licence grant, not a deal — so a trading multiple is **not computable ("no data")**. Reference points, valuation-only (not applied to this event): SC's separate GSR crypto stake was struck at a ~$1bn valuation (per coindesk, 5 May 2026, [UNSOURCED for this note's economics]); BitGo IPO priced near $2bn ([[BitGo prices IPO near $2B valuation for NYSE listing]]). Internal comps for the "bank/CASP under MiCA" pattern: [[Ripple secures full MiCA CASP authorisation in Europe]], [[Binance applies for MiCA license in Greece]], [[StoneX adds crypto offering under MiCA license]], [[EU's MiCA marks first year with 102 approved CASPs]]. Distribution not computed — qualitative only.

**Risk flags.**
1. *Execution/monetisation gap* — "granted" ≠ revenue. Services roll out "in phases in line with regulatory permissions"; passporting is stated as conditional ("where applicable"). Licence held ≠ AUC won; the release is silent on economics, ticket size and go-live timing.
2. *Late-mover into a consolidating field* — 283 CASPs are already licensed and Ripple/others hold Luxembourg passports; SC competes for the same institutional wallets it already serves, so incremental TAM capture (vs. cannibalising existing Zodia relationships) is unproven.
3. *Regulatory/policy fragility* — MiCA is new and still hardening (Tether/USDT already forced out of EU exchanges); custody economics depend on evolving stablecoin/asset-eligibility rules and CSSF supervisory posture, which can re-scope permitted activities.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Signals the second phase of TradFi verticalising crypto custody — banks folding crypto-native units (Zodia) into core securities-services and wrapping them in MiCA passports, competing on regulatory reach rather than crypto UX. Falsifiable thesis: within ~12 months SC discloses live EU custody AUC or named institutional clients from the Luxembourg entity; catalyst to watch — actual passporting activation into major EU markets and any AUC/fee disclosure in F&SS. What would make it wrong: the entity stays licence-only with no disclosed AUC, confirming this as regulatory table-stakes rather than a revenue driver.
