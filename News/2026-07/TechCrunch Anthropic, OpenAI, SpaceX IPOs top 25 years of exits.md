We’ve talked before about the hot IPO summer, but with SpaceX just launched to public markets and Anthropic and (maybe) OpenAI soon to come, it can be easy to miss the sheer scale of what’s happening.

We got a good reminder of it in Wednesday’s NCVA-Pitchbook Venture Monitor report. Not surprisingly, all of the money in private markets is flooding into AI — but one particular figure stood out. Taking the measure of the pending OpenAI and Anthropic IPOs, the report drops this nugget: “Along with the SpaceX IPO, these exits will generate more value than all U.S. VC-backed exits since 2000.”

That’s quite a claim, and when you add up the numbers, it’s hard to disagree. SpaceX has already gone public at a $1.77 trillion valuation, and with both Anthropic and OpenAI pushing into the trillions it’s likely the trio together will land somewhere north of $4 trillion. By comparison, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission counted just $70 billion in U.S.-based IPO proceeds last year.

Careful readers will notice a few caveats in the language. It doesn’t include non-U.S. companies like Alibaba, and we’re measuring “value created” as opposed to strictly liquid cash. A lot of the major tech developments happened at companies that had already gone public (the iPhone, the debut of Android, and the launches of YouTube and Instagram), so they wouldn’t be captured in the IPO figures.

Still… that was a pretty eventful 25 years. Among other things, that period saw IPOs from Google (2004), Tesla (2010), and Meta (2012), which are now among the most valuable companies in the world. During the same period, LinkedIn, Slack, and WhatsApp were all acquired for more than $20 billion. Uber’s $84 billion IPO seemed like a lot of money in 2019, but it’s less than 5% of what SpaceX just drummed up.

One factor here is that companies are staying private for longer. The Google of today probably would have delayed its IPO and gone public at a higher number. Another factor is the capital-intensive nature of AI training, which has pushed labs into intense fundraising and inflated valuations. But the sheer scale of the public offerings is still way beyond anything the industry has ever done, and is already pushing the financial infrastructure to its limit.

# Context-enrichment: TechCrunch — Anthropic, OpenAI, SpaceX IPOs top 25 years of exits
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

This is a TechCrunch commentary/listicle (Russell Brandom, 2026-07-09) built on one data point from the **PitchBook-NVCA Q2 2026 Venture Monitor**: that the SpaceX + (pending) Anthropic + OpenAI trio will "generate more value than all U.S. VC-backed exits since 2000." The value of enriching it is separating the report's actual claim from TechCrunch's framing, and pinning the live-vs-pending status of each of the three names as of mid-July 2026.

## [0] What exactly is the claim (de-PR'd)
- **Source of the headline:** not TechCrunch's own analysis — it lifts a line from the **NVCA-PitchBook Venture Monitor** (Q2 2026). The report's framing: 81.2% of H1 2026 VC exit value went to just 16 $1B+ IPOs; SpaceX's Q2 IPO alone (~$1.77T val, $75B raised) "generated more value than every US venture-backed exit of the past decade combined." H1 2026 total exit value ≈ **$2.19T** — more than the entire prior decade.
- **The "$4T trio":** SpaceX ($1.77T, already public) + Anthropic + OpenAI (both "pushing into the trillions") → the piece estimates the three land "north of $4 trillion." That is **value created**, not liquid cash raised.
- **The stated caveats (TechCrunch is explicit):** (1) excludes non-US names (e.g. Alibaba); (2) "value created" ≠ liquid proceeds; (3) many of the era's biggest tech moves (iPhone, Android, YouTube, Instagram) happened *inside already-public companies* and never show up in IPO figures. So the comparison is deliberately loaded toward the AI trio.
- **The real structural point (the non-hype takeaway):** companies stay private far longer now, and AI's capital intensity has inflated late-stage valuations. So a huge share of the "value" is a private-market backlog finally clearing through the public window in one 2026 burst — not 25 years of steady value suddenly appearing. Cf. [[CB Insights Q2 2026 was second-biggest venture quarter ever]] (263 mega-rounds = 81% of funding; one company ≈40%) and [[TechCrunch nearly 90 new unicorns minted so far in 2026]].

## [1] Status of the three named companies (dated, verified)

Opened +19% at ~$161, peaked ~$201.80 (>$2.6T). Biggest IPO ever, >3x Alibaba's 2014 $25B. The "just launched to public markets" in the note is accurate.

- **OpenAI — PENDING (slipping).** S-1 filed **2026-06-08**; last round ~$920B; but per Bloomberg (June 26) OpenAI is **leaning toward a 2027 listing**, citing volatility after SpaceX's rocky debut and Altman's hard **$1T floor**. # Challenge / red-team: TechCrunch — Anthropic, OpenAI, SpaceX IPOs top 25 years of exits

1. **"Since 2000" vs "past decade" — which is it?** TechCrunch says the trio beats "all U.S. VC-backed exits since 2000," but PitchBook's own summary phrases the SpaceX comparison as "more than every US venture-backed exit of the *past decade*." Two different, much stronger-vs-weaker claims. Which did the Venture Monitor actually make, and did TechCrunch upgrade it?

2. **Apples-to-oranges: "value created" vs proceeds.** The $4T figure is market cap / value created, compared implicitly against $70B of *IPO proceeds*. Mixing the two inflates the drama by ~50–100x. How much is real cash to sellers vs paper market cap?

3. **Two of three IPOs haven't happened.** Anthropic (Oct target) and OpenAI (possibly 2027) are unrealized. The headline is conditional on two future events, one of which is actively slipping. Is this reporting or forecasting?

4. **Survivorship/selection bias.** Excluding Alibaba and other non-US names, and excluding value created inside already-public firms (iPhone, YouTube, Instagram), is chosen specifically to make the AI trio look maximal. The article admits this — but does the framing survive once you add those back?

5. **Private-mark reliability.** Anthropic's $965B and OpenAI's ~$920B are private round valuations, not public prints. SpaceX itself swung from $161 to $201 to back down post-IPO. How durable are these numbers if public markets re-rate AI?

6. **Concentration = fragility, not strength.** 81% of H1 exit value in 16 IPOs, and one SpaceX deal beating a decade — is this a healthy exit environment or a single-name distortion that collapses if SPCX/Anthropic disappoint?

7. **Does "top 25 years of exits" even mean anything for LPs?** Value created ≠ distributions to LPs. Much of SpaceX/Anthropic/OpenAI equity is locked up, insider-held (Musk/Altman), or non-tradeable. What actually flows back to funds?

8. **Circularity of AI capital.** AI capex is partly funded by AI vendors investing in each other (Nvidia, MGX, cloud credits). Does the "value" double-count intra-ecosystem money? Cf. [[MGX raises $49 billion for one of largest AI funds ever]].

9. **Timing coincidence with the Venture Monitor's promo cycle.** The report has an obvious incentive to headline a record-shattering stat. Is TechCrunch amplifying a marketing line?

10. **Freshness/duplicate check.** Does this add anything beyond [[SK Hynix raises record $26.5B in largest US foreign IPO]], [[CB Insights Q2 2026 was second-biggest venture quarter ever]], and [[TechCrunch nearly 90 new unicorns minted so far in 2026]] — or is it the same "AI IPO summer" thesis re-skinned around one stat?

11. **Infrastructure-strain claim unquantified.** "Pushing financial infrastructure to its limit" — evidence? Which settlement/index/underwriting systems actually strained on the $75B SpaceX deal?

12. **Uber comparison is rhetorical.** "$84B Uber IPO is <5% of SpaceX" compares an operating-loss ride-hailing co to a monopoly-adjacent launch/Starlink business. Is the comparison meaningful or just a hook?

13. **Where's the downside case?** No mention of what happens if the AI IPO window shuts (SPCX volatility, OpenAI delay already signal this). One-sided.

14. **Author scope.** Russell Brandom covers platform policy / emerging tech, not capital markets. Is the financial framing his or lifted wholesale from PitchBook?

15. **"Bigger than 25 years" but the era included Google/Tesla/Meta at IPO.** Those three alone are now multi-trillion. The comparison uses their *IPO-day* value, not today's. Is that a fair yardstick?

**Importance: 3/5** — A well-sourced but ultimately derivative TechCrunch listicle/commentary hanging on one striking stat from the Q2 2026 Venture Monitor. It usefully synthesizes the mid-2026 exit-wave market structure (SpaceX done, Anthropic/OpenAI pending) and ties the exit side to the funding-concentration story, so it has real signal as a market-structure marker. But it reports no new event, two of its three IPOs are unrealized, and the core stat is already covered by adjacent, more concrete notes ([[SK Hynix raises record $26.5B in largest US foreign IPO]], [[CB Insights Q2 2026 was second-biggest venture quarter ever]]). Fresh as synthesis, not high-impact as news.

**Sector & drivers.** The "sector" here is capital-markets structure: the private-to-public liquidity pipeline for AI/frontier-tech. Size of the event: the pending SpaceX + OpenAI + Anthropic exits are framed by the Q1 2026 PitchBook-NVCA Venture Monitor as generating **more value than all U.S. VC-backed exits since 2000** — the report cites a combined ~$2.5tn range (per NVCA/GamesBeat, as of Q1 2026); press guides put the trio's combined valuation nearer $3.6–3.8tn (per Morgan Stanley / iShares, mid-2026). Anchor facts: SpaceX priced its IPO at **$135/share = $1.77tn valuation**, raising **~$75bn** (Nasdaq, ~Jun 12 2026; per CNBC/NBC) — the largest IPO ever, >3x Alibaba; on 2025 revenue of $18.7bn / net loss $4.9bn per its S-1. Anthropic filed a confidential S-1 (Jun 1 2026), targeting an **October 2026** Nasdaq listing at a ~$965bn private valuation (secondary marks reached $1.2tn this week, per TechTimes 2026-07-10). OpenAI filed ~Jun 8 but is reportedly leaning to **2027** on volatility, with Altman's ~$1tn floor (per Bloomberg via Barchart). Contrast: SEC counted just **$70bn** in total U.S. IPO proceeds last year (per the note). **Structure:** highly consolidated at the top — a handful of mega-caps absorb the exit value; barriers are extreme (capital-intensive AI training forcing multi-tens-of-billions raises). **Why now:** (1) companies stay private far longer (the note's Google-would-IPO-higher point) — value that used to accrue to public markets now crystallizes in one late listing; (2) 2026 is on track to be the largest IPO year on record, with AI/AI-adjacent names ~92% of pipeline value (per iShares/Crunchbase) — 2nd-order effect: index-inclusion mechanics and passive flows amplify a few names.

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs that matter for this event: aggregate exit/IPO value, IPO proceeds vs. prior-year baseline, float as % of shares, and top-10 S&P weight (concentration). Players in the 2026 window: **SpaceX** (already public), **Anthropic** (October target), **OpenAI** (2027-leaning), plus a deeper bench — Databricks, and adjacents like **Blue Origin** (seeking $10bn private round, not IPO — per WSJ 2026-07-09 [[Bezos space firm Blue Origin seeks $10B new financing]]) and later-stage AI names re-pricing privately ([[Lovable in talks to double valuation to $13.2B]], $6.6bn→$13.2bn). Basis of competition for the IPO window: scarcity of institutional capital and exchange timing more than product. Protagonist positioning: the trio are the market — SpaceX's ~$75bn raise alone dwarfs Uber's 2019 $84bn *valuation*; Anthropic's private mark ($1.2tn) overtook OpenAI on secondary scarcity (almost no sellers pre-IPO), a moat of narrative + compute scale but a fragile, illiquid price signal `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** Comparability is weak — these are pre-/early-public, mostly non-disclosing names, so most multiples = no data. What is verifiable: SpaceX EV/Revenue ≈ market cap/2025 revenue = **$1.77tn / $18.7bn ≈ 95x** (using price-to-sales as an EV proxy; net-loss-making, so no EV/EBITDA or P/E). That is far above the comps-analysis sanity band (~0.5–20x EV/Rev) — a breach that is *only* partly justifiable by growth and optionality, and rests on Starlink/Starship narrative rather than current cash flow → outlier, rich. OpenAI/Anthropic revenue-multiple = **no data** (revenue not publicly verified; ~$1tn / ~$965bn valuations are round/secondary marks, NOT market caps). Internal comps (base): [[Lovable in talks to double valuation to $13.2B]] (2x step-up in ~7 months — private AI re-rating velocity); [[AI took a third of Singapore's 2025 venture funding]] (capital concentration into AI, 30% of deal value); [[Bezos space firm Blue Origin seeks $10B new financing]] (space capital intensity); [[VIM Investments Pre-IPO funds pay investors 9.2bn rubles]] (pre-IPO liquidity demand). Distribution not computed — <3 comparable public figures; qualitative only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Concentration / market-breadth risk.** A ~$3.6tn AI IPO pipeline lands into a market where the top 10 S&P names already ≈35% of the index (vs ~25% at the 2000 peak; per Morgan Stanley/JPM AM) — new mega-listings deepen a single-theme bet. 2nd-order: a revenue miss at any one AI name could trigger indiscriminate de-risking across the whole cohort.
2. **"Value created" ≠ liquid cash / measurement caveat.** The headline compares *created value* to prior *realized* exits, excludes non-U.S. names (Alibaba) and value at already-public firms (per the note). Risk: the "biggest 25 years" claim is partly an artifact of longer private tenures, not pure wealth creation — over-reading it inflates perceived exit-market health.
3. **Valuation on scarce/illiquid marks + window fragility.** Anthropic's $1.2tn is a thin-secondary print ("almost no shareholders selling"); OpenAI already slipped toward 2027 on volatility. If the window shifts, the "north of $4tn" trio thesis is time-sensitive, and SpaceX's ~95x P/S leaves little margin for AI-capex-vs-revenue disappointment (~$400bn capex vs ~$100bn enterprise AI revenue, per JPM AM).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is a market-structure re-rating, not a normal IPO cohort: exit value is migrating from many mid-cap listings to a few trillion-dollar debuts, pushing "financial infrastructure to its limit" (the note's own words) and turning public indices into levered AI bets. Falsifiable thesis: if Anthropic prices near its ~$965bn–$1.2tn range on/around October 2026 with healthy aftermarket, the mega-IPO window is validated and OpenAI accelerates back toward 2026/early-2027; **trigger to watch = Anthropic's October Nasdaq debut and first-day float/aftermarket**. What breaks the thesis: an AI-earnings or capex-ROI scare (or a broad breadth correction) that pushes OpenAI/Anthropic to delay — which would expose how much of the "$4tn" is unrealized paper, not proceeds.
