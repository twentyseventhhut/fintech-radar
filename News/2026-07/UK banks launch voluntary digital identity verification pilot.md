UK banks launch voluntary digital identity verification pilot
Sinziana Albu
03 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
UK Finance and major banks including Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, Nationwide, NatWest, and Santander have unveiled a voluntary digital identity pilot.
The UK banking industry has introduced a new voluntary digital identity verification service intended to help customers confirm their identity more securely when accessing financial services. The initiative is coordinated by UK Finance and supported by Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, Nationwide, NatWest, and Santander. It has been developed independently of the government's wider digital identity programme and will remain optional for consumers.
The proposed service is designed to give customers a trusted way to verify their identity online. UK Finance has argued that the system could help reduce fraud, streamline customer onboarding and lower verification costs for businesses, while making digital transactions faster. The service is currently being tested through a live pilot involving the participating banks.
Industry commentary on financial crime risk
Commenting on the initiative, Dr Janet Bastiman, chief data scientist at Napier AI, said the value of digital identity lies less in privacy or convenience debates and more in whether it helps organisations make more reliable risk decisions. She noted that a digital identity ecosystem built on secure-by-design principles could strengthen fraud prevention by giving institutions greater confidence in customer verification, making it harder for criminals to exploit stolen or synthetic identities.
Bastiman cautioned that security should be based on a ‘verify, don’t trust’ approach, warning that a digital identity system vulnerable to spoofing or compromise could become a vector for impersonation attacks rather than a safeguard against them. She added that criminal networks are increasingly using AI to generate convincing impersonation attempts and fraudulent documents, meaning identity verification needs to sit within a broader, intelligence-led approach that continuously monitors customer behaviour and transaction risk. She further pointed to explainability as a requirement for financial institutions, both for regulatory compliance and to allow legitimate customers to complete onboarding quickly while suspicious activity is flagged for investigation.
Market context and public debate
The launch takes place against a backdrop of ongoing public debate around digital identity in the UK, with concerns raised over data security, consumer trust and the resilience of digital infrastructure. Recent banking outages and data breaches have increased scrutiny of the reliability of digital verification systems, and questions remain around broader public confidence in digital identity adoption.
The initiative reflects wider efforts across UK financial services to strengthen digital identity capabilities as fraud patterns continue to evolve. UK Finance has reported that criminals stole more than GBP 1.28 billion through payment fraud in 2025.

**[0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd) — and the freshness verdict.** The Paypers (03 Jul 2026) reports UK Finance plus six banks (Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, Nationwide, NatWest, Santander) running a "voluntary digital identity verification pilot." This is a **re-report of the 25–26 June 2026 UK Finance announcement already covered internally** in [[Barclays and Lloyds join UK banking digital ID plan]] (story s453ec627, enriched, importance 3). Same participant list, same UK Finance coordination, same Select ID as technical delivery partner, same "live pilot in the coming months" framing. No new terms, no new reporting period, no "talks→signed" follow-up — **STALE, duplicate_of s453ec627**. The one genuinely additive element here is not a fact about the scheme but external commentary (Napier AI's Dr Janet Bastiman) and a fraud-loss stat (UK Finance: >£1.28bn stolen via payment fraud in 2025). De-PR'd substance: a proof-of-concept (synthetic data) is complete; a real-world **controlled pilot** is next — this is still pre-live infrastructure, not an adopted service. → Why the framing matters: The Paypers headline ("launch… pilot") over-states maturity vs the June primary source, which is explicit that the commercial and governance model is still being designed.

**[1] Competitors / peers.** The bank-led rail sits against: (a) **GOV.UK One Login / GOV.UK Wallet** and the OfDIA **DVS Trust Framework** — the 1.0 version published in pre-release 03 Mar 2026, beta certs expired 31 Mar 2026 (state-backed identity layer the banks are pre-empting); (b) the **EU EUDI Wallet** (eIDAS 2.0) — every member state must offer it by end-2026, private SCA providers must accept by end-2027, positioned as the cross-border KYC standard (a UK divergence risk post-Brexit); (c) reusable-ID vendors — **OneID, Yoti, Signicat ([[Signicat launches reusable digital identity solution ReuseID]]), Zimpler ID+ ([[Zimpler introduces ID+ identification layer for payments]]), Sumsub reusable KYC ([[Noah and Sumsub partner on compliance and verification engine]]), iDenfy ([[iDenfy adds Smart-ID to identity verification for Baltic markets]])**. Position: the banks are **catching up on concept, ahead on trust anchor** — they own the regulated KYC relationship vendors must buy or replicate. → Second-order: OneID's own CEO argues no single wallet wins; credentials will be issued by banks/gov/telcos across many wallets — so the bank scheme is less a "wallet" than an *issuer* of attributes into an orchestration layer (Select ID is the OSP).

**[2] Company / ecosystem fit.** Banks already fund the most expensive identity step (regulated onboarding KYC). A reusable, bank-issued attribute-sharing rail lets them monetise that trust anchor as relying parties pay per verified attribute — the same logic that drove the June announcement. UK Finance is the natural coordinator (neutral trade body avoids one bank owning the rail). → Why now: fraud losses (>£1.28bn in 2025) plus the government's One Login advancing create both a burning platform and a land-grab deadline — banks want to own the identity layer before the state or big-tech wallets do.

**[3] Novelty / value-add / traction.** Novelty is **low and unchanged since June**: no new capability was announced on 03 Jul; the same PoC-complete / pilot-next status stands. TRACTION = **zero live relying parties, zero volume** — it remains announced infrastructure ("piloted" ≠ "live"). The load-bearing open question (who captures the margin) is untouched: open-banking identity data is already largely free, so the monetisable unit is the *liability-bearing verified attribute*, not the data. → Who captures margin: unresolved — if relying parties still carry ultimate CDD responsibility (regulated firms do), they may not pay a premium for a bank attribute they can't fully offload liability onto. That, not privacy, is what decides whether this is a business or a cost centre.

**[4] What's next / sentiment.** Live pilot "in the coming months"; commercial + governance (esp. liability) model still being designed. Three open questions flagged by trade press (Retail Banker Int'l / Yahoo): **who carries liability when a bank-verified attribute is wrong/spoofed/stale; who pays when open-banking data is already free; who takes it to market.** Regulatory backdrop: DVS Trust Framework 1.0 pending go-live in 2026; EUDI Wallet mandate looming for cross-border. → Counterintuitive second-order: broad bank participation looks like strength but concentrates systemic risk — recent UK banking outages/breaches (cited in the source) mean a single ID rail becomes critical infrastructure whose failure is now a national single point of failure, raising the resilience bar the scheme must clear before "voluntary" adoption becomes real.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Sector = digital identity / reusable KYC / regtech. Market size: the global digital identity verification market is put at ~$14–17bn in 2026 with a ~14–18% CAGR (per multiple secondary trackers — market.us ~14% CAGR, Coherent ~16.5% to 2033, Business Research Insights; range only, no single authoritative TAM → treat as `[UNSOURCED]` order-of-magnitude, not a precise figure). Structure: **fragmented** — a long tail of IDV vendors (Yoti, Onfido/Entrust, Veriff, Jumio, iProov, Persona, Didit, Regula, iDenfy) sitting between banks and government data, with no dominant reusable-ID network in the UK yet. Barriers = regulation (UK DVS trust framework certification), access to authoritative data sources, and network effects (a credential is only useful if many relying parties accept it). Why now: (1) UK Finance reports criminals stole >GBP 1.28bn via payment fraud in 2025 (per note source), and AI-generated synthetic/deepfake identities raise onboarding risk; (2) regulatory scaffolding is maturing — GOV.UK One Login has >12m users and an mDL rollout in 2026 (per Biometric Update / GDS), and the EU's EUDI Wallet mandates every member state issue a compliant wallet by 31 Dec 2026 and regulated private-sector KYC to accept it by Dec 2027 (per EC / eIDAS 2.0). The UK bank pilot is a private-sector answer to the same reusable-KYC thesis, deliberately run **separately** from the government programme.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: verifications/month, pass/completion rate, false-accept vs false-reject, cost-per-verification, and relying-party network size (acceptance). Key players & basis of competition: incumbent IDV vendors compete on accuracy + fraud/deepfake detection + coverage; the differentiator here is **distribution/network** — UK Finance's move bundles the UK's largest retail banks (Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, Nationwide, NatWest, Santander) into one consented, bank-app-based verification network, with **Select ID** leading technical design/delivery (per UK Finance, biometricupdate, 25 Jun 2026). Recent moves: unveiled 25 Jun 2026 at UK Finance's Digital Innovation Summit; proof-of-concept completed on **synthetic data**, next stage a **live pilot** "in the coming months" — i.e. NOT yet live/adopted (de-PR: this is announced + PoC-done, not in production). Protagonist's position: the bank coalition is a **new entrant with a structural distribution edge** (analysis) — it starts with the identity data and customer relationships that standalone vendors must acquire; moat = network effects + switching costs if relying parties standardise on it. Risk to that moat: it competes/overlaps with GOV.UK One Login on the consumer side.

**Comps & multiples.** No single covered protagonist (a consortium of banks; none of Barclays/HSBC/Lloyds/Nationwide/NatWest/Santander is a key in ir_coverage.json → IR skipped). Closest pure-play comp = **Yoti** (UK reusable digital ID): revenue GBP 29.0m in 2025, +62% YoY (from GBP 17.9m in 2024, GBP 11.5m 2023) per Biometric Update; total funding >GBP 166m, incl. a GBP 12.5m debt facility from **HSBC** (financialit.net) — note HSBC is also a pilot participant, so the "consortium vs vendor" line is not clean. Current Yoti valuation: **no data** (last public post-money ~GBP 82m dates to 2019; recent figures redacted) → EV/Revenue **not computed**. Internal comps from the base (reusable-KYC / verifiable-credential deals): [[Regula and Indicio partner on verifiable-credential identity verification]] (2025-07, user-held VCs), [[Didit raises $7.5 million for identity fraud infrastructure]] (2026-05, AI-native IDV, 200+ signals/verification), [[Alloy enables perpetual KYC automation for global banks]] (2025-09, pKYC), [[iDenfy launches identity verification app for WooCommerce merchants]] (2026-05). Distribution not computed (private valuations undisclosed) → **qualitative comparison only**. Read: private IDV names are growth-stage and capital-hungry; a bank-owned network could compress their addressable UK KYC spend rather than trade at a premium.

**Risk flags.**
- **Adoption / chicken-and-egg (execution):** value depends on relying parties accepting the credential; a bank-app IDV with no third-party acceptance is just re-onboarding. Still PoC/pilot on synthetic data — no live traction, no disclosed cost-per-verification or relying-party count. Second-order: if uptake is thin, the network moat never forms.
- **Duplication vs GOV.UK One Login / EUDI (regulation & fragmentation):** running "independently of the government's programme" risks a competing/parallel scheme that fragments UK reusable ID; if government or EUDI-style wallets win relying-party mandates, a bank-only network is stranded. Second-order: sunk build cost + standards churn.
- **Spoofing / single-vector concentration (security):** Napier AI's Bastiman (per note source) warns a compromised or spoofable digital-ID system becomes an impersonation *vector*, not a safeguard, amid AI-generated synthetic identities; concentrating verification into one shared network raises the payoff of breaking it. Recent UK banking outages/breaches also cut consumer trust. Second-order: liability/fraud-loss allocation across banks is unstated ("who's silent" on economics and fraud liability).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is an attempted **new entry** by incumbents to own the reusable-KYC layer before government (One Login) or EU-style wallets set the standard — a land-grab on the trust framework, not a re-rating. Falsifiable thesis: if the live pilot converts into acceptance by non-bank relying parties (retail, gambling age-checks, property/lettings) within ~12 months, standalone UK IDV vendors' addressable KYC spend compresses; watch for **named relying parties + a go-live date** as the catalyst. What breaks it: pilot stays bank-only, government One Login/EUDI acceptance dominates, or the consortium can't agree fraud-liability economics — then it's a PoC that quietly stalls.
