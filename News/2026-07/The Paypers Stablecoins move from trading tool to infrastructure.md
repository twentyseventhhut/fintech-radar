[Money20/20 – zerohash interview] Stablecoins: from trading tool to essential infrastructure
Claudia Pincovski
15 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
 Stablecoins are moving from the crypto-native niche into everyday financial infrastructure. At Money20/20, we spoke with zerohash CMO Katie Perry to understand where that shift is heading and what's actually driving it. 
 Founded in 2017, zerohash has scaled into a compliance-first infrastructure provider trusted by Morgan Stanley, BlackRock, and Franklin Templeton, institutions that demand the highest regulatory bar before they'll touch a new asset class. 
 Solving real friction 
 The use cases aren't speculative. Interactive Brokers lets customers fund accounts instantly with stablecoins. Gusto uses them to pay overseas contractors in near real-time, cutting a process that used to take days or weeks down to minutes. According to zerohash's 2026 Stablecoin Momentum Report , institutional volume is climbing, and everyday users are coming back repeatedly rather than trying stablecoins once. 
 
 Use cases in wealth management 
 The most concrete data point from the conversation came from zerohash's ‘ Crypto and the Future of Wealth’ report : 51% of high-net-worth investors aged 18 to 40 have already left an advisor who doesn't offer crypto exposure. For younger affluent clients, digital assets aren't a side bet; they're becoming a core part of how portfolios are built. Wealth platforms that don't integrate this risk will lose clients permanently. 
 Regulation might be the accelerant 
 Perry pushed back on the idea that compliance slows adoption down. zerohash recently secured a MiCAR authorisation and an EMI licence from the Dutch central bank, doubling its regulatory footprint in Europe ahead of anticipated demand. 
 Looking ahead, the roadmap points toward agentic commerce. Perry noted that interest in AI-driven payments is high, but the product layer is still catching up. What stablecoins offer autonomous systems is programmability: money that moves with built-in compliance and rules-based checks, without a human approving every transaction. zerohash is already co-building with companies that arrive with a business problem and land on stablecoins as the answer. 
 About Katie Perry 
 Katie Perry is CMO at zerohash, a crypto and stablecoin infrastructure platform, bringing 15+ years of marketing, brand strategy, and partnerships experience across agencies and public companies. She previously served as VP of Marketing and GM of Issuer Partnerships at Public.com, and founded a fractional marketing consultancy for startups and global brands. A recognised thought leader, Katie has been cited in The Wall Street Journal, Bloomberg, and Forbes. She's the author of Ticker Shock (Wiley, July 2026) and has interviewed 100+ public company CEOs and CFOs.  
 
 About zerohash 
 zerohash provides regulated infrastructure that lets banks, fintechs, brokerages, and payment companies embed crypto trading, stablecoin payments, and tokenized asset services into their own platforms. In the US, zerohash is a licensed money transmitter across 50 states and D.C. In Europe, it holds MiCAR authorisation and an EMI licence from the Dutch Central Bank, anchored by its European headquarters in Amsterdam. Its partners include Interactive Brokers, Morgan Stanley, and Kalshi.

# Context-enrichment: The Paypers — Stablecoins move from trading tool to infrastructure
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Thesis + who argues it.** This is a **thought-leadership / commentary** piece (The Paypers, Money20/20 interview by Claudia Pincovski, 15 Jul 2026) with **zerohash CMO Katie Perry**. Thesis: stablecoins are crossing from a crypto-native / **trading** instrument into everyday **payments infrastructure** (brokerage funding, cross-border payroll, wealth), and — counter to the usual framing — **regulation is the accelerant, not the brake**. The messenger is an interested party: zerohash sells exactly this "regulated stablecoin infrastructure," so the whole frame is a vendor's book. Read it as a directional argument backed mostly by **zerohash's own reports**, not independent data.

**Freshness verdict: FRESH (as commentary), importance modest.** It is NOT a re-report of a discrete prior event. It is distinct from the near-sibling essay this cycle [[FinTech Futures stablecoins hand fintechs a new monetisation model]] (that one argues the *monetisation/revenue-model* angle — float + take-rate for fintechs; this one argues the *infrastructure adoption + regulation-as-accelerant* angle → different central claim, cross-reference not duplicate). The two concrete corporate facts it leans on are already covered as their own notes: zerohash's DNB EMI licence ([[Zerohash receives EMI license from Dutch central bank]], 19 May 2026) and its raise ([[Zerohash pursues funding above $1.5 billion valuation]], 21 May 2026). Because the commentary neither re-announces those nor adds new terms/figures on them, it is not stale *against* them — but its news weight is low; its value is the synthesis, not a new event.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- An **interview essay**, not an announcement. zerohash (founded 2017; regulated crypto/stablecoin/tokenization **infrastructure** provider, US 50-state money transmitter, MiCAR + DNB EMI in the EU) argues stablecoins are now real payment rails.
- **Live use cases it cites (mostly verifiable, some vendor-framed):**
  - **Interactive Brokers** — customers fund brokerage accounts instantly with stablecoins (USDC via zerohash; auto-converted to USD; announced ~15 Jan 2026). This is real and live. Note IBKR is also zerohash's **lead investor** ($104M Series D-2, ~$1B valuation, Sept 2025) → the flagship customer proof-point is not arm's-length.
  - **Gusto** — pays overseas contractors in near real-time via stablecoins (announced ~16 Jan 2026, beta). Real, early-stage.
  - Wealth: "51% of HNW investors aged 18–40 left an advisor who doesn't offer crypto" (from zerohash's *Crypto and the Future of Wealth* report). **De-PR: the verifiable stat is narrower** — the survey (n=500, run by Centiment, fielded Q3 2025) found the **top income sub-segment ($500K–$1M+)** "moved assets away" from advisors with limited crypto access; "left/fired an advisor" and "all 18–40 HNW" are the paraphrase overstating it. Vendor-commissioned.
  - "2026 Stablecoin Momentum Report" (institutional volume climbing, repeat usage) — **self-reported zerohash platform data** (+690% YoY volume, +146% active customers headlines), not independently auditable.
- **Regulation-as-accelerant** claim rests on zerohash's own MiCAR authorisation (~Oct 2025, AFM) + **EMI licence from De Nederlandsche Bank (announced 18 May 2026)**, doubling its EU footprint. That is a genuine, verifiable milestone.
- **Agentic commerce**: framed as roadmap/aspiration ("product layer still catching up"), correctly hedged — zerohash is "co-building," not shipping volume.
- **Why structured this way:** a CMO interview at Money20/20 is a **distribution vehicle for two owned reports and a licence** — it anchors the "infrastructure" narrative to zerohash's blue-chip client roster (Morgan Stanley, BlackRock, Franklin Templeton — but note these are largely **tokenized-fund rails** e.g. BUIDL/BENJI, narrower than "these institutions partner with us on stablecoins").

## [1] Competitors / peers (the infra layer this essay is really about)
- **Circle** — the reference issuer+infra: won **final OCC national-trust-bank approval 10 Jul 2026** ([[Circle wins final OCC approval for national trust bank]]); Circle Payments Network live (~$8.3B annualized volume, ~136 institutions, Q1 2026); IPO Jun 2025. Circle owns the coin *and* reserve float — a deeper moat than zerohash's servicing layer.
- **Stripe/Bridge** — $1.1B acquisition (closed Feb 2025); just got **MiCA CASP + EMI (Luxembourg CSSF, ~2 Jul 2026)** ([[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]]). Bridge's Stripe distribution dwarfs zerohash's funnel.
- **BVNK** — Mastercard definitive agreement up to **$1.8B** (announced 17 Mar 2026); ~$30B/yr, 130+ countries. Directly relevant: **Mastercard walked away from a zerohash deal (~$2B talks) and bought BVNK instead** → zerohash then raised at >$1.5B ([[Zerohash pursues funding above $1.5 billion valuation]]). So zerohash lost the Mastercard exit its main infra peer won.
- **Modern Treasury / Paxos / Partior / Yellow Card** — adjacent infra: e.g. [[Depa Finance selects Modern Treasury for stablecoin payments]], [[Yellow Card selects Turnkey for embedded wallet infrastructure]] (embedded-wallet UX layer, the friction zerohash also abstracts).
- **Position:** zerohash is a credible **regulated B2B2B servicing layer**, ahead on the US 50-state + EU dual-licence compliance moat, but **behind Circle/Bridge on owning the coin/reserve economics** and behind BVNK on the strategic-network exit. Its edge is "compliance-first embed," not scale of float.
- **Why the landscape is this way:** GENIUS Act (signed 18 Jul 2025) legitimized payment stablecoins federally → the value migrates to **whoever holds the regulated licence + the reserve float**. zerohash captures the licence/servicing rung; issuers (Circle) capture the float. Second-order: as banks/fintechs embed, distribution owners and issuers compress the pure-servicing margin.

## [2] Company history / fit
- zerohash trajectory: infra provider since 2017 → Series D-2 $104M @ ~$1B (Sept 2025, IBKR-led) → Mastercard takeover talks (~$2B) fell through → new raise >$1.5B pursued (May 2026) → MiCAR (Oct 2025) + DNB EMI (May 2026) → Staking-as-a-Service (Jun 2026, [[Zerohash launches Staking-as-a-Service via single API]]).
- The interview fits logically: a **compliance-first** vendor whose whole pitch is "regulation is a moat" naturally argues "regulation is the accelerant." **Why:** as a servicing layer without its own coin, zerohash's durable differentiation IS the licence stack + client trust (Morgan Stanley/BlackRock/Franklin Templeton) → thought-leadership that reframes compliance from cost to catalyst directly defends its multiple.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty of the *claim* is low** — "stablecoins are becoming payments infrastructure" is the consensus 2026 narrative (echoed across the corpus this month: [[Circle wins final OCC approval for national trust bank]], [[Visa, Stripe, 140 firms launch Open USD stablecoin]], [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]]). The essay adds **synthesis + two data points**, not a new fact.
- **Real traction underneath the theme (from external research, independent of zerohash):** genuine stablecoin *payments* volume is ~**$390B FY** (McKinsey+Artemis, Feb 2026) — real and growing fast (B2B +733% YoY off a small base) but only ~1.2% of the ~$33T raw on-chain figure and a rounding error vs global payments. Visa USDC settlement ~$7B annualized run-rate (Apr 2026). So "infrastructure" is **live but small**; the "surpassed Visa" framing (not made here, but ambient) is a category error.
- **zerohash-specific traction:** IBKR funding + Gusto payroll are live but early/small; the momentum-report numbers are self-reported. → treat client logos as **distribution proof, not volume proof**.
- **Who captures the margin:** zerohash earns **servicing/transaction fees + the compliance premium**, NOT the reserve float (that's the issuer's, e.g. Circle). This is the structural ceiling: it is the "regulated pipes," not "the dollar" → durable but lower-margin than an issuer, and squeezable if Circle/Stripe embed servicing directly. The central question shifts from "is stablecoin infrastructure real?" (yes, modestly) to **"can a pure servicing layer keep its take-rate once issuers and card networks own both ends?"**

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Roadmap: **agentic commerce** (programmable, rules-based money for autonomous systems) — genuinely the strongest structural pillar (sub-cent M2M micropayments card networks can't match; Coinbase x402 + Linux Foundation x402 Foundation, Apr 2026), but **for zerohash it is aspiration, not shipped volume**.
- **Regulatory backdrop (tailwind):** US GENIUS Act (signed 18 Jul 2025; live effect gated to ~2026–early 2027), EU MiCA final phase (1 Jul 2026, culling unlicensed rivals → licence becomes a moat), UK/BoE **dropping holding caps for a £40bn issuance guardrail** (22 Jun 2026) removing a friction. All support the "regulation as accelerant" thesis — the one place the essay's framing is more right than the skeptic's prior.
- **Risks / what's silent:** no zerohash payment *volumes* disclosed; the wealth stat is overstated vs source; MiCAR/EMI = capability not EU adoption; Mastercard chose BVNK over zerohash (strategic-buyer signal); reserve economics accrue to issuers not to zerohash. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the very regulation zerohash celebrates also lets **banks and card networks in-house** the compliant-servicing layer → the accelerant that legitimizes the category can also disintermediate the standalone servicer.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Stablecoin settlement/payments infrastructure. Sizing (real-economy, not on-chain gross): per BCG/Allium (via CoinDesk), real-economy stablecoin payments ran ~$350–550bn in 2025, +60% YoY, with B2B settlement ~60% of that. Cross-border B2B specifically is small but fast: ~$13.4bn in 2026, projected to $5tn by 2035 (per Juniper Research, via CoinDesk, 2026-04-27) — a projection, treat as directional not hard TAM. Cost/latency is the real driver: institutional rails settle in <3 min at ~0.1–0.5% vs 2–7% for wires (per BCG/vendor guides, 2026); Visa hit a ~$4.5bn annualized stablecoin settlement run-rate by Jan 2026. Structure: two-layer and consolidating fast at the infra layer — issuers (Circle/USDC, Tether/USDT) sit above regulated "embed-the-rails" providers (zerohash, Bridge, BVNK). Barriers are regulatory (money-transmitter licenses ×50 states, MiCAR/EMI) + trust/compliance, not tech. Why now: US GENIUS Act (2025) + EU MiCA July-2026 deadline forced a licensing scramble — the news itself (zerohash MiCAR + Dutch EMI) is a de-PR'd instance of regulation acting as accelerant, not brake.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: settlement volume/TPV, take rate (bps on flow), reserve-income/AUC for issuers, licensed jurisdictions, enterprise logo count. Players & basis of competition: **issuers** — USDT ~59% of stablecoin market cap but USDC ~70% of *adjusted transaction volume* in H1-2026 per Visa on-chain data (compliance/institutional trust is USDC's basis); **infra/embed layer** — zerohash vs Stripe-owned Bridge vs Mastercard-owned BVNK, competing on licenses + distribution, not price. Recent moves (dated): Stripe bought Bridge ~$1.1bn (closed Feb-2025); Mastercard agreed to buy BVNK up to $1.8bn (Mar-2026), then walked away from a zerohash investment; a 140+ firm consortium (Visa, Stripe, Coinbase, BlackRock) unveiled **Open USD** (Jun-30-2026). Corpus corroboration: [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]], [[Circle wins final OCC approval for national trust bank]], [[Standard Chartered and Circle launch USDC minting access]], [[Tether abandons Europe as MiCA ban wipes USDT from exchanges]]. Protagonist (zerohash): niche-leader in *compliance-first embedded* rails — 50-state MTL + MiCAR + Dutch EMI, ~5M users / 190 countries, clients Morgan Stanley, IBKR, BlackRock BUIDL, Franklin Templeton, Stripe, DraftKings. Moat = regulatory footprint + switching costs of embedded integration (analysis); it is a picks-and-shovels supplier, so it can ride multiple issuers rather than bet on one.

**Comps & multiples.** No public revenue disclosed → EV/Rev, EV/EBITDA = no data. Comparison on last-round valuation (private, mark as round valuation not market cap):
- zerohash: ~$1bn post-money after $104M Series D-2 (Sep-2025, led by IBKR); pursuing ~$250M raise at >$1.5bn (May-2026, per CoinDesk/PYMNTS); earlier M&A talks reportedly up to $2bn.
- Bridge: ~$1.1bn acquisition by Stripe (closed Feb-2025).
- BVNK: up to $1.8bn agreed acquisition by Mastercard (Mar-2026).
- Circle (public, CRCL): only listed comp; Mizuho cut to Underperform, PT $50 on Open-USD threat (2026-07-14) — a public read-through on how the market prices this layer.
Read: zerohash's ~$1.5bn mark sits between Bridge (~$1.1bn) and BVNK (~$1.8bn) — in-line for a licensed embed provider, and the step-up from $1bn (Sep-25) to $1.5bn (May-26) tracks the sector's B2B volume growth, so not an obvious outlier (analysis). Median/distribution not computed — private, non-comparable revenue bases. [UNSOURCED]: take rate, TPV, gross margin.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Reserve-economics disintermediation (Open USD).** The 140-firm consortium shares reserve yield with distributors and charges no mint/redeem fee — attacking the single-issuer float-income model. Second-order: if Visa/Stripe/Mastercard route their own rails through OUSD, an independent embed provider like zerohash risks being bypassed by its would-be partners' captive stack. (Note: OUSD is unlaunched as of late-Jun-2026 — announcement, not observed behavior.)
2. **Distribution-partner-turned-competitor.** Mastercard bought BVNK and Stripe owns Bridge; both now compete directly with zerohash while also being ecosystem gatekeepers. The scuttled Mastercard–zerohash deal shows the strategic-buyer exit narrowing.
3. **Regulatory/yield-ban compression.** GENIUS + OCC Feb-2026 rule ban issuer/affiliate yield to holders; Circle's Coinbase reward-share is the flagged loophole. Any tightening compresses the economics zerohash's issuer partners can pass through — and Treasury flags run-risk on ~$281bn outstanding (Mar-2026) with no Fed backstop.
4. **Volume softness cuts both ways.** Aggregate stablecoin market cap shrank ~$10bn since May ([[Stablecoin market cap shrinks $10B since May]]); infra take-rate revenue is levered to on-chain volume, so a crypto-cycle drawdown hits the "infrastructure" thesis even as real-economy B2B grows.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** The center of gravity is shifting from *who issues* to *who controls distribution and reserve economics* — Open USD reframes stablecoins as an ecosystem/network fight, not a ticker race (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: compliance-first embed providers (zerohash) re-rate up if they win neutral-Switzerland status across multiple issuers/consortia; thesis breaks if OUSD's backers bundle settlement into their own captive rails and starve independent providers of flow. Watch: OUSD launch + first named enterprise volumes; whether zerohash closes the $1.5bn round and on what strategic-vs-financial cap table.
