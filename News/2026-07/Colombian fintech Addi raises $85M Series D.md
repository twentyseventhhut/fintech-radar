_Funding round marks BTG Pactual's first Growth investment outside Brazil, underscoring growing investor confidence in Addi and Colombia's financial services market._

BOGOTÁ, Colombia--(BUSINESS WIRE)--Addi announced the close of its Series D, which totals $85 million in equity financing, in a round that marks BTG Pactual's first Growth investment outside Brazil and underscores growing investor confidence in Colombia. The round was led by Citius, co-led by BTG Pactual, and included participation from GIC, Monashees and others.

"Over the past seven years, we’ve built the leading financial and commerce platform in Colombia, creating an AI-first technology company that serves millions of Colombians and tens of thousands of merchants. While we’ve been profitable for two years, we decided to raise this round to accelerate our growth trajectory and bring world-class partners to the table," said Santiago Suárez, CEO and Co-founder of Addi.

"We are proud to support Addi, one of the truly outstanding leaders in Latin America's rapidly evolving financial infrastructure sector," said Oleg Gordienko, Co-founder & Partner of Citius. "We are humbled to be a significant investor and to help drive a massive transformation of Colombia's financial and e-commerce landscape."

The round was led by Citius, a global investment firm focused on backing exceptional technology companies and a long-term shareholder in Addi. BTG Pactual's Private Capital division co-led the round, investing out of its Growth Strategy, which focuses on backing high-growth companies across Latin America. As part of this investment, Addi and BTG Pactual have agreed to collaborate on joint strategic initiatives in the Colombian market.

“We believe Colombia is undergoing a transformation in access to credit and financial services, much like the one Brazil experienced years ago. Addi is setting a new standard in this market by redefining the credit experience for both consumers and merchants through AI deployed at scale. This ability to reshape entire markets is exactly what we look for in our Growth investments,” said Gabriela Lima, Private Capital Director at BTG Pactual.

Previous financing rounds included participation from GIC, Quona, Monashees, Union Square Ventures, Citius and Andreessen Horowitz, among others. The new round deepens those commitments and adds new partners.

This Series D caps a run of milestones over recent months that have brought leading global institutions to Colombia through Addi. In April 2026, the company closed a $150 million structured credit facility led by J.P. Morgan—the first warehouse financing structure the bank has arranged for a company in Colombia—bringing Addi's total debt commitments to more than $680 million. Addi also received authorization from the Superintendencia Financiera de Colombia to operate as a regulated entity, opening the door for deposit-taking activities. Earlier this year, it was named one of the world’s three most innovative fintech companies by _Fast Company_.

Addi will use the Series D proceeds to continue growing the credit platform, strengthen technology infrastructure, and expand the portfolio of financial products for consumers and merchants across Colombia.

**ABOUT ADDI**

Addi is the commerce and financial services platform in Colombia with more than 3 million customers and over 39,000 merchants, ranging from small businesses to large brands. The company drives everyday commerce by expanding access to credit, improving the payment experience, and increasing conversion rates. Its business model is built on a proprietary platform, a dual ecosystem, differentiated solutions, and robust data, allowing it to deliver efficiency, growth, and trust. In 2026, Fast Company named Addi the third most innovative company in the world in Finance and Fintech, recognizing its use of artificial intelligence to transform commerce. Founded in 2018 by Santiago Suárez, Daniel Vallejo and Elmer Ortega, Addi is backed by global investors including Andreessen Horowitz, BTG Pactual, GIC, Citius and Union Square Ventures. For more information, please visit co.addi.com.

**ABOUT CITIUS**

Citius is a globally focused investment firm targeting early and growth-stage tech companies. Firm’s investments include some of the world’s fastest growing companies in LatAm, South East Asia, India and other regions. Citius offices are located in Dubai and Luxembourg. To learn more about Citius, please visit www.citius.vc.

**ABOUT BTG PACTUAL**

BTG Pactual (BPAC11) is the largest investment bank in Latin America and operates in the markets of Investment Banking, Corporate Lending, Sales & Trading, Asset Management, Wealth Management, and Banking.To learn more about BTG Pactual, please visitinstitucional.btgpactual.com.

# Context-enrichment: Colombian fintech Addi raises $85M Series D
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**FRESHNESS VERDICT: FRESH.** This is a NEW **equity** Series D ($85M, led by Citius, co-led by BTG Pactual, +GIC/Monashees), announced ~2026-07-01. It is distinct from every prior Addi note in the corpus, all of which were **debt/credit facilities** (warehouse lines) or a regulatory/licence event — not equity. No prior note covers this equity raise, so it is not a duplicate. Related-but-different priors: [[Addi secures 150M structured credit line from JP Morgan]] (2026-04, debt), [[Colombian fintech Addi obtains banking licence and raises funds]] (2026-05, licence + notes issuance), [[Addi secures $89 million structured financing from Citi]] (2026-03, debt), [[Addi secures $89M financing to expand Colombian lending]] (2026-02, debt).

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **$85M equity Series D**, led by **Citius** (existing shareholder; takes a board seat per LatamList), co-led by **BTG Pactual** — notably BTG's *first Growth-equity investment outside Brazil*. Participation from **GIC, Monashees** and existing backers. Prior equity investors named: a16z, Union Square Ventures, Quona, Greycroft, Citius.
- **No valuation disclosed** (neither the release, LatinFinance, nor LatamList give a post-money). Treat any valuation as unknown.
- **The company frames this as unnecessary capital.** CEO Santiago Suárez: *"This is a round we did not need… Given our profitability and the growth of the business, we expect this to be the last round we will see for quite some time."* Company says it has been **profitable for ~2 years**. **Why structured this way:** a profitable company raising equity it "didn't need" is buying (a) strategic partners — BTG collaboration on "joint initiatives" in Colombia is the real prize, i.e. distribution/balance-sheet access for a would-be deposit-taker; (b) a marquee cap table ahead of a possible late-stage/IPO narrative. The "last round" line signals no near-term dilution appetite and props a high implied valuation without printing a number.
- **Metric discrepancy to flag (not invented — sourced conflict):** the BusinessWire release (in-note) says **3M+ customers / 39,000 merchants**; LatamList's same-day writeup says **5.5M customers / 76,000 merchants across 75,000 POS / 1,034 municipalities**. These cannot both be current. The 3M/39k figure likely tracks *active/credit* customers while 5.5M is *total registered* — but this is (hypothesis); the gap is material and the PR does not reconcile it.
- **AI framing:** Fast Company named Addi #3 most innovative company in Finance/Fintech 2026; CEO credits AI for engineering-productivity gains ("scale faster without increasing headcount"). This is marketing color, not a quantified moat.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Sistecrédito** — the closest domestic BNPL/point-of-sale-credit rival in Colombia; large offline merchant footprint. Addi's edge is claimed to be digital-first + AI underwriting.
- **Mercado Pago (MercadoLibre)** — the structural threat: MELI's regional merchant-credit portfolio exceeds **$2B**, and MELI has a Colombian licence path and vastly deeper balance sheet + marketplace distribution. **Why it matters:** Addi's dual consumer+merchant model competes directly with MELI's embedded credit, and MELI can subsidize credit as a marketplace loss-leader.
- **Nu (Nubank)** — not yet a full Colombian consumer-credit incumbent at Addi's scale, but Nu's Colombia expansion + deposit franchise is the medium-term overhang; the BTG parallel ("Colombia undergoing a transformation like Brazil years ago") is exactly the market Nu wants.
- **Position:** Addi is the **local scale leader in Colombia BNPL**, but a **regional minnow** vs MELI/Nu on capital. The Series D + banking licence is defensive as much as offensive — it needs deposit funding to compete on cost-of-capital before the giants localize.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Founded **2018** (Suárez, Vallejo, Ortega). Funding arc: $2.3M seed (2018) → $12.5M (2019) → Series B in 2021 ($35M equity + $30M debt May; +$75M equity Sept led by Greycroft/a16z; ~$140M over 90 days; valuation then "nearly triple", in the "hundreds of millions"). Then a **long equity gap (2022–2025)** filled entirely by **debt warehouses** (BBVA Spark $35M, Goldman/Fasanara $71M upsize, Citi $89M, JP Morgan/Fasanara $150M → **$680M+ total debt commitments**).
- **Why the equity gap → why now:** post-2021 LatAm fintech valuations reset; Addi pivoted to *funding the loan book with debt* and reaching profitability rather than raising down-round equity. **Structural logic:** a lender's growth is gated by (a) cost of funding and (b) equity to absorb losses. Having maxed debt lines and won a **financing-company licence** (deposit-taking, from Superintendencia Financiera, capital ~COP 38B, process started 2024), the natural next step is equity to backstop a *deposit-funded* balance sheet — cheaper funding than warehouses. The Series D fits that logic exactly.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real traction (sourced):** profitable ~2 years; five consecutive up quarters (per Feb 2026 note); 2.7M active customers / 33k merchants (Feb) growing to 3M+/39k (July release); $3.6B in pre-approved credit; 94% municipal coverage; **60% of transactions at 0% interest**; **47% of users have no credit card** (genuine financial-inclusion angle). This is adoption, not announcement.
- **The actual novelty is not the BNPL** (Sistecrédito/MELI already do POS credit) — it's the **BNPL-to-bank transition**: converting a merchant-acquired credit user base into a licensed, deposit-taking institution. **Why value-add could be real:** deposits are the cheapest funding; if Addi migrates its 3M+ credit users to deposit accounts, its cost-of-capital advantage vs pure BNPL and vs debt-warehouse peers becomes structural. **What breaks it:** deposit-gathering is a *different* muscle (trust, distribution, regulation) than merchant BNPL; MELI/Nu are far ahead on deposits regionally; and the "AI-first" claim is unquantified — no data on loss rates or underwriting edge is disclosed. **Who captures the margin:** if funding shifts to deposits, Addi keeps the credit spread; if it stays warehouse-funded, lenders (JPM/Citi/Goldman/Fasanara) and their advance rates cap the economics.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Use of proceeds: grow credit platform, tech/AI infra, expand product for consumers + merchants; **BTG "joint strategic initiatives" in Colombia** — watch for co-branded credit/distribution or BTG balance-sheet support.
- **Sentiment:** clearly positive — a profitable, marquee-backed round with a "won't need to raise again" narrative; BTG's first ex-Brazil growth bet is a real signal of institutional conviction in Colombia. **Why the market goes this way:** Colombia is the "next Brazil" thesis for credit penetration; capital is concentrating in the local scale leader.
- **Counterintuitive second-order risk:** a "we didn't need it / last round" framing plus no disclosed valuation is a *narrative shield*. If deposit migration stalls or credit losses rise in a Colombian consumer-credit downturn, the absence of fresh equity flexibility (self-imposed "last round") becomes fragility, not strength. Concentration in one country + one currency (COP) is an unhedged macro bet.
- **Regulatory backdrop:** deposit-taking licence opens the upside but adds prudential capital/supervision obligations under the Superintendencia Financiera.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Addi sits at the intersection of Colombian BNPL / installment credit and (increasingly) full-stack digital banking. LatAm BNPL is a fast-growing, still-small pool: per ResearchAndMarkets (via aijourn.com, Jan 2026), the regional BNPL market is projected to grow ~24.8% in 2026 to ~$18.5bn, with mid-teens CAGR forecast to 2031 (report figures — treat as vendor estimate, not audited). Colombia is the structural story here: per Superfinanciera (via larepublica.co, Sep 2025, `[[This Week in Fintech Colombia's fintech market grows 387% since 2017]]`) the country's fintech population grew 387% (84→409 startups, 2017–2023), the fastest in LatAm, driven by low banked/credit penetration + regulatory tailwinds. Structure: fragmented at the wallet/neobank layer (Nequi, DaviPlata ~19m users, Nu, RappiPay, Lulo, and Ualá/Revolut entering per fintechnews.am / c-innovation.eu) but the merchant-installment BNPL niche is thinner — Addi's main structural moat. **Why now:** (1) Addi's Superfinanciera authorization to operate as a regulated (deposit-taking-capable) entity moves it from pure lender toward a funded neobank — cheaper cost of funds `(analysis)`; (2) BTG Pactual's first Growth ticket outside Brazil signals institutional conviction that Colombia is "following Brazil's credit-access curve" (BTG quote in PR — this is a promoter framing, treat as narrative not fact).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for merchant-BNPL/lending: GMV/originations, take rate, net interest margin, loss/NPL rate, funded vs. equity cost of capital. Key players & basis of competition: at the **BNPL/merchant-credit** layer Addi competes with Sistecrédito and Mercado Crédito on merchant distribution and underwriting; at the **neobank/deposit** layer it faces Nequi, DaviPlata (Davivienda), Nu Colombia, RappiPay and Lulo on funding cost + everyday-account ownership. Basis of competition = merchant distribution + underwriting/data (BNPL) vs. deposit gathering + brand (neobanks). Recent moves: Apr 2026 — $150m J.P. Morgan-led warehouse facility (`[[Addi secures 150M structured credit line from JP Morgan]]`), first such structure in Colombia, total debt commitments >$680m; Jul 2026 — $85m Series D. Protagonist's position: **niche leader** in Colombian merchant-embedded credit, catching up as a would-be full neobank. Moat `(analysis)`: merchant network + proprietary underwriting data (39,000 merchants per PR); switching costs at merchant checkout. CAC/LTV, take rate, NPLs → `[UNSOURCED]` (not disclosed).

**Comps & multiples.** No revenue/EBITDA disclosed by Addi → EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA, P/E = **no data**; multiple arithmetic not computable. Round is equity financing of $85m; a post-money "near $1 billion" figure appears only in secondary reporting (LatamList; ground.news) and is **not in the PR** — treat as unverified round valuation, not market cap. Internal comps (round valuations, mark as private post-money not multiples):
- `[[Kapital raises funding at $1.3B valuation]]` (Sep 2025) — LatAm AI-neobank, $1.3bn, 300k customers, US/Mexico/Colombia.
- `[[Ualá raises $195m led by Allianz X at $3.2bn valuation]]` (Mar 2026) — LatAm neobank, $3.2bn post-money, 11m+ customers.
- `[[Bold raises $40M to expand across Latin America]]` (Oct 2025) — Colombian payments, $40m round (no valuation).
- `[[Brazilian BNPL platform Pagaleve raises around $30 million funding]]` (Oct 2025) — Brazil BNPL, ~$30m round.
Distribution not computed (valuations not disclosed for most; different scale/model). Qualitative: Addi's implied ~$1bn (if the secondary figure holds) is below Ualá's $3.2bn on a far smaller/single-country base — in-line-to-rich vs. deposit-scaled neobanks but plausibly supported by claimed 2-yr profitability `(analysis)`; not verifiable without financials.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Single-market concentration.** Effectively 100% Colombia — FX, sovereign, and one-regulator (Superfinanciera) exposure; a Colombian credit-cycle downturn or COP depreciation hits both originations and dollar-debt servicing (>$680m debt commitments). Second-order: leverage amplifies any NPL spike.
2. **Credit/leverage risk masked by "profitable" claim.** 2-yr profitability is self-reported (CEO, PR), no audited numbers, no disclosed NPL/loss rates; a BNPL book funded by warehouse lines is procyclical — losses can turn profit negative fast in a rate/employment shock.
3. **Disclosure inconsistency / disintermediation.** PR cites 3m customers / 39,000 merchants while secondary reports (via web search) cite 5.5m / 76,000 — unreconciled, a data-quality flag on any metric. Strategically, if deposit-taking scales slowly, Addi stays dependent on wholesale debt (J.P. Morgan/Fasanara rails) — margin capture by funders, not a self-funded neobank.

**What this changes (idea-lens) `(analysis)`.** The regulated-entity authorization is the real re-rating trigger, not the $85m: it lets Addi migrate from an expensive wholesale-funded BNPL lender toward a deposit-funded neobank, compressing cost of funds and defending margin as Nu/RappiPay/Revolut push into installment credit. Falsifiable thesis: if Addi launches deposit products and shows funded-deposit growth within ~12 months, it re-rates as a neobank; if it stays warehouse-funded and single-country, the ~$1bn mark looks full and a down-round on the next raise is the tell. Watch: deposit launch, disclosed NPLs, any Brazil re-entry.
