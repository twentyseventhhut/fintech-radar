ECB selects 36 payment providers for digital euro pilot
Sinziana Albu
14 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
The European Central Bank (ECB) has chosen 36 payment service providers (PSPs) from across the euro area to participate in a pilot programme designed to test the technical functionality and operational processes of a potential digital euro. The pilot is intended to support preparatory work for the possible issuance of a digital euro and is scheduled to run for 12 months, starting in the second half of 2027.
According to the official press release, the selection follows a call for expression of interest issued in March 2026, to which the Eurosystem received more than 50 applications from PSPs. Candidates were assessed against a set of pre-defined eligibility criteria. The 36 providers chosen include both banks and non-bank service providers, covering a range of business models, sizes and geographical locations, with the aim of creating a varied testing environment.
An ECB Executive Board member overseeing the digital euro project noted that the level of applications reflects the private sector's willingness to engage with the initiative and contribute to the development of the payments landscape in Europe.
In an exclusive comment for The Paypers, a Revolut spokesperson said: `Following the ECB’s announcement, Revolut confirms its participation in the Digital Euro Pilot phase alongside other selected PSPs. Operating a unified banking infrastructure across all 27 EU markets, we are pleased to support the ECB and national central banks in testing this framework within the designated pilot environment ahead of the project's launch in the second half of 2027.` 
Pilot design and participant roles
The pilot will run on a beta version of the digital euro that is intended to be functionally and technically close to the version described in draft legislation, though it will not carry legal tender status. Participating PSPs will be assigned one of two roles, or both: distributing PSPs will give Eurosystem staff access to beta digital euro services, including account set-up and payments, while acquiring PSPs will support selected merchants in accepting beta digital euro payments.
Testing will involve staff at the ECB and at 19 national central banks, namely those of Belgium, Germany, Estonia, Ireland, Greece, Spain, France, Croatia, Italy, Cyprus, Latvia, Lithuania, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Austria, Portugal, Slovenia, Slovakia and Finland. Selected PSPs may also offer pilot services in countries other than the one in which they are established.
Use cases and next steps
The exercise will include e-commerce merchants and businesses offering everyday services, such as cafeterias and restaurants. Central bank staff will be able to make beta digital euro payments between individuals, both online and offline, as well as payments from individuals to businesses, covering physical points of sale, including software-based POS systems, and online and mobile transactions.
Findings from the pilot are expected to feed into further refinement of the digital euro's design and user experience. The ECB has said it will publish regular updates on its dedicated digital euro pilot webpage. In the near term, the selected PSPs will work with their respective national central banks and the ECB to prepare for the start of the pilot.

# Context-enrichment: ECB selects 36 payment providers for digital euro pilot
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **14 July 2026** the ECB named **36 PSPs** (banks + non-banks) to join a **digital euro pilot** that runs **12 months from H2-2027**. Selection followed a **call for expression of interest in March 2026** (>50 applications). Named participants across sources: **Deutsche Bank, UniCredit, Revolut, Adyen, Stripe, SumUp, Worldline, Nexi, Poste Italiane, Satispay, Banca Sella, Isybank, Numia, Monte dei Paschi, Bank of Cyprus, JCC** — with **8 Italian** firms. PSPs take **distributing** and/or **acquiring** roles; testing runs with ECB staff + **19 national central banks**.

**De-PR'd reality — this is a test harness, not a launch and not adoption.**
- The pilot uses a **beta** digital euro that is **NOT legal tender** and is only "functionally/technically close" to the draft legislation. It is **not consumer-facing**: users are **Eurosystem staff**, not the public. Merchants are limited demo cases (cafeterias, restaurants, e-comm).
- **Sequencing matters (→ why):** pilot starts H2-2027, but **first potential issuance is only ~2029**, and both are legally conditional on the **digital euro Regulation** passing — which is still in **trilogue** (see [4]). So this is preparatory optionality-building, not a countdown to rollout.
- **Why frame it as "36 providers selected" (→ what it reveals):** the ECB deliberately markets *private-sector buy-in* ("50+ applications reflect willingness to engage"). The subtext is political — it counters the bank/lawmaker narrative that the digital euro is an unwanted state project (see [1]). Revolut's on-record "pleased to support" quote is the tell: the ECB needs visible PSP endorsement to blunt resistance.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Two competitive frames, and they point in opposite directions:
- **The digital euro vs private EU rails.** The real "competitor" is **Wero** (EPI, 14-bank-backed A2A wallet, live in FR/DE/BE). In Nov 2025, **14 lenders incl. Deutsche Bank, BNP, ING** publicly warned the digital euro "addresses largely the same use cases as private solutions without clear added value" ([[ECB digital euro plan meets bank and lawmaker resistance]]). **→ Second-order irony:** several of those same banks (Deutsche Bank) and card/acquiring incumbents (Nexi, Worldline, Adyen) are now **inside the pilot**. Participating ≠ endorsing — it is defensive: better to shape the rulebook and own the distribution rails than be disintermediated by them.
- **The digital euro vs dollar stablecoins / non-EU cards.** The stated raison d'être is **monetary sovereignty**: cash fell from 72%→52% of in-store payments (2019–24), US cards + USD stablecoins dominate ([[ECB warns US-backed stablecoins could weaken EU monetary autonomy]]). This is the ECB's stronger argument — the weakness (per [1a]) is that the *retail* design overlaps with Wero, not with the sovereignty gap.

## [2] Company history / fit
This is the **latest in a dense, well-telegraphed sequence**, not a surprise:
- Nov 2023: preparation phase begins → **30 Oct 2025** Governing Council moves to next phase, pencils **2027 pilot / 2029 issuance** ([[ECB advances to next phase of digital euro project]], [[ECB advances digital euro project toward 2029 launch]]).
- Oct 2025: **Feedzai** picked as first-ranked tenderer for the central fraud engine (framework up to €237m) ([[ECB selects Feedzai for digital euro fraud detection]]); ECPC/nexo/Berlin Group standards deals ([[ECB signs digital euro standards deals with ECPC, nexo and Berlin Group]]).
- Dec 2025: **EU Council** position (online+offline) ([[EU Council backs digital euro with online and offline use]]); Jun 2026: **ECON committee** + **9 Jul plenary** mandate ([[Digital Euro clears key hurdle in EU parliament]]).
- **→ Why this cadence:** the ECB is **building all the components (fraud, standards, PSP network) in parallel with, but ahead of, the law** — "modular" work to scale gradually and limit financial commitment until legislation lands. Selecting PSPs now keeps 2027 credible if the Regulation passes in 2026. It is momentum manufacturing.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**What is genuinely new here:** the **named participant roster** and the operational split into **distributing vs acquiring** roles — the first concrete map of *who* would run the retail rails. Everything else (timeline, scope, offline/online, holding logic) was already public.
**Traction test — it fails, by design:** zero real users, zero live volume, non-legal-tender beta, users = central-bank staff. This is **~2 years pre-issuance**. Cipollone himself has said **~2.5–3 years of build remain even after legislation**.
**Who captures the margin (→ the real question):** the digital euro is deliberately **zero-remuneration** with a **~€3,000 holding cap** (excess sweeps to linked bank accounts) to prevent **bank disintermediation**. That cap is the entire economic settlement: it deliberately caps the digital euro's ability to displace deposits, which means PSPs' upside is **thin distribution fees, not balances**. **→ So the central question is not "will it launch" but "why would a PSP invest in rails with a capped, fee-only economic model"** — the answer is defensive positioning + regulatory obligation to accept it, not a profit thesis.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Legislative gate is the binding constraint.** ECON position (43–14, 23 Jun) → **plenary mandate 416–169 on 9 Jul 2026** → **trilogue** with Council + Commission, targeted to conclude **end-2026 under the Irish Presidency**. Parliament's version wants the **holding limit set by the Commission on ECB recommendation, reviewed ≥ every 2 years** — i.e. MEPs clawing authority away from the ECB. The holding limit is the live battleground (Bruegel: "will prove central to the digital euro's future").
- **Risks / counter-narrative:** banks fear deposit flight even at €3k; the **legal-basis question** (does the Treaty let the ECB issue digital cash?) is still contested; and the retail use-case-overlap with Wero remains unrebutted. **→ Second-order:** if trilogue slips into 2027, the H2-2027 pilot start compresses or slips, and the 2029 issuance target — already the *earliest* case — becomes fragile. The PSP selection is real, but it is downstream of a law that does not yet exist.
- **Sentiment:** procedural momentum is real and accelerating (Euro Summit political push, sovereignty framing amid US stablecoin surge), but this specific item is an **incremental preparatory milestone**, not a de-risking event.

## Top challenge / red-team questions

1. **Launched or announced?** — Announced only. A **beta, non-legal-tender** pilot slated to *start* H2-2027, tested by **central-bank staff**, not the public. Zero live users/volume today. (answered)
2. **Is the "selection" itself new, or a re-run of the 2027-pilot plan?** — New: the Oct-2025 note pencilled a 2027 pilot in the abstract; this is the first **named 36-PSP roster** + distributing/acquiring role split. Genuinely fresh event. (answered)
3. **Does the pilot depend on a law that doesn't exist yet?** — Yes. First issuance and even the pilot are conditional on the **digital euro Regulation**, still in **trilogue** (plenary mandate 9 Jul 2026; target end-2026). This is the binding risk. (answered)
4. **Who actually captures economics?** — Almost no one, by design: **zero-remuneration + ~€3,000 holding cap** deliberately limits deposit displacement, leaving PSPs thin distribution fees, not balances. (answered)
5. **Why are the very banks that opposed it (Deutsche Bank) now inside the pilot?** — Defensive: shape the rulebook / own the rails rather than be disintermediated by Wero-substitutes. Participation ≠ endorsement. (answered)
6. **What is the real competitor — USD stablecoins or private EU rails (Wero)?** — Sovereignty framing points at USD/US cards, but banks argue the *retail* design overlaps with **Wero**, offering "no clear added value." Unrebutted. (answered)
7. **Is €3,000 the right cap, and who sets it?** — Live battleground: Parliament wants the **Commission** to set it on ECB recommendation, reviewed ≥2y — clawing power from the ECB. Bruegel calls it decisive. (open — trilogue outcome)
8. **Does the ECB have legal authority to issue digital cash at all?** — Contested (Treaty mandate covers physical cash). Raised by critics; unresolved. (open)
9. **Will the H2-2027 pilot start actually hold?** — Only if trilogue closes in 2026. Any slip compresses/pushes the pilot and stresses the *already-earliest-case* 2029 issuance. (open — timeline risk)
10. **How many of the 36 are systemically meaningful vs padding for "diverse representation"?** — Mix of majors (Deutsche Bank, UniCredit, Adyen, Worldline, Nexi, Revolut) and small/regional (Isybank, Numia, JCC); "broad coverage" is partly optics. (partial)
11. **Does "50+ applications" prove private-sector demand, or ECB PR?** — It signals willingness to *participate in a funded pilot*, not commercial demand for the product. Framing is political (counters resistance narrative). (answered)
12. **What's the delta vs live retail CBDCs (India e-rupee, etc.)?** — India already has live programmable CBDC + 120m+ tx; the euro is still pre-issuance. Europe is behind on *live* CBDC, ahead on institutional design. (answered)
13. **Could this pilot be quietly de-scoped like other CBDCs (Norway declined, S. Africa on hold)?** — Politically harder given Euro-Summit push, but not impossible if trilogue stalls or bank opposition hardens. (open)
14. **Does offline capability actually work at scale?** — Draft supports offline/online; the pilot will test it, but no proof yet it works securely at cash-like small-ticket scale. (open)
15. **Second-order: does forcing PSPs to build capped, fee-only rails make the ecosystem stronger or just compliance-driven?** — Risk that adoption is mandate-driven (PSPs obliged to accept) rather than value-driven, undermining the "platform for innovation" claim. (analysis)

**Importance: 3/5 — rationale.** A real, concrete milestone (first named PSP roster + operational roles) on the euro area's flagship CBDC, adding tangible momentum and notable names (Deutsche Bank, Revolut, Adyen, Stripe, UniCredit). But it is preparatory, non-live, ~2 years pre-issuance, and entirely contingent on legislation still in trilogue. Meaningful for the file's trajectory, not a de-risking or launch event — hence 3, not 4.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a public-infrastructure / CBDC event, not a company deal — no round, valuation or metrics attach to it. Global backdrop: 146 countries/currency unions (over 98% of world GDP) are exploring a CBDC; 77 are in advanced (development/pilot/launch) phase and there are ~41 retail pilots, but only 3 full retail launches (Bahamas, Jamaica, Nigeria) — per Atlantic Council CBDC Tracker, as of 2026. The secular split: advanced economies outside the euro area are *retreating* from retail CBDC (the UK is weighing shelving the digital pound — [[Bank of England considers shelving digital pound]]), while the EU pushes ahead. "Why now": the March 2026 call drew 50+ PSP applications (36 selected); the ECB expects EU co-legislators to adopt the digital-euro Regulation during 2026, targets pilot start H2-2027 and potential first issuance in 2029 (ECB). Structure — the digital euro is a two-tier model: the ECB issues, PSPs (banks + non-banks) distribute/acquire; economic value for private players sits in distribution, merchant acceptance and value-added services, not issuance. Cost is a live drag: ~€1.3bn ECB development cost to first issuance + ~€320m/yr operating from 2029 (ECB), plus bank-side capex that Italian lenders want phased over time ([[Italy's banks back digital euro but want costs phased]]).

**Competitive landscape.** The "competitors" here are the private rails the digital euro would sit alongside or against: (a) US card schemes — Visa/Mastercard run ~61% of eurozone card transactions (via EPI/FT), the sovereignty problem the digital euro is partly meant to solve; (b) EPI's Wero A2A wallet, now live in e-commerce ([[Wero e-commerce goes live in Germany]]) and interlinked with EuroPA/Bizum/Bancomat/SIBS/Vipps MobilePay ([[EuroPA and EPI partner for pan-European payments sovereignty]]); (c) euro stablecoins — MiCA-regulated bank consortium efforts ([[BBVA joins euro stablecoin bank consortium]], [[Bancomat announces EUR-BANK euro stablecoin in Italy]]). Basis of competition: acceptance/ubiquity, cost to merchants, and offline capability. The 36 selected span banks and non-banks (per press: Deutsche Bank, UniCredit, Revolut, Adyen, Stripe, SumUp, Worldline), across 16 of 21 euro states — a deliberately varied test set, not a commercial rollout. Protagonist position: the ECB is *ahead* on political momentum vs the UK, but the pilot is a technical test of a beta with no legal-tender status — "announced/testing," not "live/adopted." Moat = statutory legal-tender status once legislated; without the Regulation it has none.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/multiple applies — this is a central-bank pilot, not a traded or funded entity. Distribution not computed; qualitative comparison only. Internal precedents in-base show the trajectory and the friction: [[ECB advances digital euro project toward 2029 launch]], [[ECB digital euro plan meets bank and lawmaker resistance]], [[Italy's banks back digital euro but want costs phased]]; parallel sovereign efforts [[Türkiye advances digital lira with framework and pilot tests]], [[Australia advances CBDC with Project Acacia]], [[UAE makes first cross-border CBDC payment with China]]; and the private-rail alternatives cited above. Any PSP-level revenue impact = `[UNSOURCED]` (economics/fees for distributing PSPs undisclosed).

**Risk flags.**
- *Timeline/legislative risk.* Issuance hinges on the EU Regulation (expected 2026) AND a separate Governing Council decision; the European Parliament rapporteur (Navarrete) has pushed a *scaled-down* version to protect private payment initiatives — the pilot can succeed technically yet the product be diluted or delayed past 2029. Second-order: PSP capex sunk into a moving target.
- *Bank disintermediation / holding-limit tension.* The ~€3,000 per-person holding cap is the design lever against deposit flight, but critics read it as evidence of limited appeal (via ECB/SUERF) — the cap that protects banks also caps utility; too low kills adoption, too high risks deposit outflows in stress.
- *Adoption / relevance risk vs private rails.* European banks argue Wero + national schemes + euro stablecoins can deliver sovereignty without a state CBDC's cost/risk; if private A2A and MiCA stablecoins scale first, the digital euro launches into an already-contested acceptance layer — spending €1.3bn+ for marginal added utility.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The pilot moves the digital euro from "policy debate" to "operational test," and locks in a private-sector distribution roster — mildly positive for the acquiring/PSP layer (Adyen, Worldline, SumUp, Stripe, Revolut) that would monetise acceptance/value-added services, neutral-to-negative for Visa/Mastercard's long-run eurozone share. Falsifiable thesis: this is a re-affirmation, not a re-rating — the real catalyst is EU adoption of the Regulation in 2026; watch that vote and the Parliament rapporteur's scope. Thesis breaks if the Regulation stalls into 2027 or the holding limit is cut hard, in which case the pilot becomes a well-run test of a product that never ships at scale.
