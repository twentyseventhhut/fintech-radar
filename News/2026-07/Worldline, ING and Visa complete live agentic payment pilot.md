[Worldline, Visa, ING video interview] Europe’s live agentic payment
Vlad Macovei
02 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
 Worldline, ING, and Visa say they have completed a live end-to-end agentic payment transaction in Europe. That matters because the discussion around agentic commerce has so far been dominated by prototypes and future-state thinking.  
 In this interview, we sat down with  Madalena Cascais Tomé ,  Member of Executive Committee, Worldline , Hans Overeem , Head of Payments, ING Netherlands , and Mathieu Altwegg , SVP, Head of Product and Solutions Europe, Visa . Together, the three companies frame the milestone less as a technical demonstration and more as evidence that agentic payments can be built around issuer control, consumer consent, and European regulatory expectations from the outset. 
 
 
 From concept to production 
 The most notable point in the discussion is not simply that an AI-enabled transaction took place, but that it was executed in a live production environment and across the full payment chain. Madalena Cascais Tomé argues that this distinguishes the announcement from earlier market narratives: the transaction connected the merchant side, the network, and the issuer, while preserving consumer authentication and authorisation through a seamless passkey-based experience. 
 That issuer role is central. Hans Overeem explains that the bank remains responsible for authenticating, authorising, and consenting to the transaction, while also recognising the agent through the relevant protocol. In practice, this is the industry’s answer to the core concern around agentic commerce: whether AI can act without eroding trust. The message from the interview is clear. Agentic payments will only gain traction if they feel as safe and intelligible as any other digital payment interaction. 
 Mathieu Altwegg positions Visa's contribution as the framework layer required to scale that trust model. He points to standards within the Visa Intelligence Commerce framework, including a trusted agent programme designed to identify agents and create visibility around the transactions they initiate.   
 What scale will actually require 
 The interview also avoids portraying one live transaction as proof of immediate mass adoption. Instead, the speakers outline four conditions for scale: compelling use cases, pan-European interoperability, common standards and governance, and alignment with European regulation. 
 That final point is especially important. Europe’s payments market does not reward innovation that sits outside consumer protection, strong customer authentication, or established liability models. The companies’ argument is that agentic payments must develop within those guardrails, not around them.   
 Consumer control, meanwhile, remains the decisive design principle. Altwegg describes this through 'trusted intent' : a set of pre-defined conditions that specify merchant types, spending limits, and when verification is required. If that model holds, agentic commerce may become less about autonomous purchasing and more about programmable consent. 
 For the payments industry, that is the more credible path to adoption.

Since October 2025, Madalena Cascais Tomé has served as Chief Financial Institutions & Processing Officer at Worldline. With extensive experience in payments and digital services across financial institutions, merchants, corporates, public entities, and consumers, as well as recognised leadership and strategic acumen, she brings invaluable expertise to guide Worldline’s Financial Services global division through its next strategic phase. Throughout her career, Madalena has demonstrated a strong commitment to innovation, impactful leadership, and the ability to navigate complex and rapidly evolving environments successfully.

Worldline, ING, and Visa complete live agentic payment pilot
Sinziana Albu
02 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Worldline, ING, and Visa have completed a live agentic payment transaction in Germany.
According to the official press release, the transaction was designed to demonstrate how AI-enabled purchasing journeys can operate within existing regulatory requirements, including Strong Customer Authentication (SCA), while keeping the consumer and merchant in control throughout the process. As part of the initiative, Worldline and ING have joined Visa's Agentic Ready Programme.
The pilot was carried out in Germany, where a consumer defined the parameters of a purchase, including what to buy and when, and instructed a merchant AI agent to act within those boundaries. The agent identified a suitable product, after which the user authenticated the purchase intent using Visa Payment Passkeys. The transaction was then confirmed through biometric authentication and authorised by ING as the issuing bank. The full transaction was processed across Worldline's infrastructure, covering the journey from agent instruction to final authorisation.
Throughout the process, the AI agent operated within instructions set explicitly by the consumer, while ING retained control over the authorisation step. This structure was intended to preserve a verifiable record of what was authorised, by whom, and under which conditions, addressing a recurring concern around accountability in agent-initiated commerce.
Implications for merchants and the ecosystem
For merchants, the companies stated that the pilot shows agent-initiated commerce does not require a reduction in fraud protection or trust mechanisms. Because the transaction relied on Visa Payment Passkeys and was authorised by a financial institution under existing SCA requirements, merchants can, according to the companies, accept agent-initiated orders under conditions comparable to conventional online purchases, without developing separate payment infrastructure for AI-driven shopping.
 Madalena Cascais Tomé , Member of the Executive Committee, Worldline, said the company's role is to connect participants across the payment value chain, including issuers, acquirers, merchants, and schemes, to support these emerging models while addressing the trust and security requirements needed for broader adoption. Furthermore, Hans Overeem , Head of Payments, ING Netherlands, added that the milestone shows agent-based payments can be both usable and auditable, citing the combination of biometrics and delayed capture as enabling flexible and secure purchasing. Mathieu Altwegg , Head of Product & Solutions, Visa Europe, said the Agentic Ready Programme, which now includes both Worldline and ING, supports real-world testing of AI-enabled purchasing journeys within defined parameters and existing payment controls.
Agentic commerce, in which AI agents act on a consumer's behalf within predefined limits, is an emerging area of focus for payment networks, issuers, and processors as they assess how existing authentication and authorisation frameworks can be adapted to new purchasing journeys. This pilot forms part of Visa's broader Intelligent Commerce initiative, which focuses on integrating payment credentials, consumer controls, and programmability into agentic use cases. The companies described the transaction as a contribution to the ongoing development of agentic commerce in Europe, intended to build confidence in the underlying technology among merchants, issuers, and consumers ahead of wider deployment.

# Context-enrichment: Worldline, ING and Visa complete live agentic payment pilot
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**FRESHNESS VERDICT: FRESH (but low-novelty / near-repeat of a June event).** This is the **Visa**
variant of a pilot Worldline + ING had *already* run with **Mastercard** four weeks earlier
(see [[Worldline, ING and Mastercard complete live agentic payment]], 2026-06-03), and which
Worldline had *also* run with Crédit Agricole + Mastercard in France days earlier
(see [[Worldline, Credit Agricole, Mastercard complete first agentic payment in France]], 2026-06-29).
Same primary players (Worldline as processor, ING as issuer), same de-PR'd mechanism (consumer sets
parameters → merchant AI agent picks product → passkey/biometric SCA → issuer authorises). What is
genuinely new: a *different network* (Visa, not Mastercard), a *different programme enrollment*
(Worldline + ING join **Visa's Agentic Ready Programme**), and a *different country* (Germany, vs the
NL/BE run on Mastercard). So it is a real new event, NOT a reprint of the same figures — but the
marginal information is "Worldline+ING repeated the identical demo on the other network," which caps
importance at 2/5. Not stale, but not a step-change.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Worldline, ING and **Visa** say they completed a *live, end-to-end, in-production* agentic payment
  transaction in **Germany** on 2 Jul 2026. Worldline and ING **joined Visa's Agentic Ready Programme**.
- Mechanism (from the primary text): a consumer pre-defined purchase parameters (what to buy, when)
  and instructed a *merchant* AI agent to act within them → agent identified a product → consumer
  authenticated purchase intent via **Visa Payment Passkeys** (biometric) → **ING as issuer authorised**.
  The whole chain ran across Worldline's infrastructure. Framed as SCA-compliant, keeping consumer and
  merchant "in control." Part of Visa's broader **Intelligent Commerce** initiative; Visa's contribution
  is the "framework layer" — a **trusted-agent programme** to identify agents and give visibility into
  agent-initiated transactions, plus the "**trusted intent**" concept (pre-defined merchant types,
  spend limits, verification triggers).
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** the entire press framing is *issuer-control-first*
  ("ING retained control over authorisation," "verifiable record of what was authorised, by whom").
  → This is deliberately the *anti-OpenAI/Stripe* framing: European scheme+bank+processor players are
  racing to prove agentic commerce can run *inside* existing SCA/liability rails rather than around them,
  because a US-protocol-led model (ACP, x402) threatens to disintermediate them.
  → Second order: it is ONE transaction, a controlled demo, not throughput. The speakers themselves
  (in the interview source) explicitly refuse to call it mass adoption and list four preconditions for
  scale (use cases, pan-EU interoperability, common standards/governance, EU-regulatory alignment).
  That candor is itself a tell: the hard part (interoperability + governance) is unsolved.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Mastercard** is the direct mirror: same Worldline+ING pilot on Mastercard rails
  ([[Worldline, ING and Mastercard complete live agentic payment]], 2026-06-03) and the France run with
  Crédit Agricole ([[Worldline, Credit Agricole, Mastercard complete first agentic payment in France]],
  2026-06-29). Mastercard also ran live agentic transactions across ASEAN/Hong Kong/Mexico/Ukraine in
  2026-04 → 2026-06 (Krungthai, Getnet Mexico, PrivatBank). So Mastercard was *first* to a live European
  Worldline+ING run; Visa is catching up on the same processor.
- **Visa's own stack:** Intelligent Commerce, Intelligent Commerce Connect
  ([[Visa launches Intelligent Commerce Connect for agentic commerce]], 2026-04-10), the **Trusted Agent
  Protocol** (open framework, Oct 2025, 10+ partners), and the Agentic Ready Programme
  ([[Wirex joins Visa Agentic Ready programme for AI-driven payments]], 2026-06). DBS was first APAC bank
  to pilot Visa agentic commerce ([[DBS becomes first APAC bank to pilot Visa agentic commerce]], 2026-02).
- **Bank/scheme pilots broadly:** [[Mastercard and Visa enlist banks for agentic payment pilots]]
  (2026-02) is the umbrella move; both networks enlisting issuers is exactly this pattern.
- **Rival protocol camps:** OpenAI/Stripe **Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP)**
  ([[Checkout.com adopts Agentic Commerce Protocol]]), Mastercard **Agent Pay**
  ([[PayPal integrates Mastercard Agent Pay into wallet]]), PayPal ([[Mastercard and PayPal partner on
  agentic commerce]]), Ant/Antom ([[Ant International's Antom launches agentic payment solution]]),
  and the **x402** camp (Coinbase/Linux Foundation). Position: Worldline+ING+Visa are **at parity /
  slightly behind** the Mastercard analog on the same rails, and behind the US-consumer-agent players
  on actual merchant reach — but *ahead on the "regulated-EU, issuer-in-the-loop" positioning*.
- **Why the landscape is this way:** networks are commoditisation-averse — if agents route payments via
  a US protocol layer, Visa/MC risk becoming a back-end rail with no consumer relationship. So each is
  building an *agent-identity/trust layer* (Trusted Agent Protocol / Agent Pay) to stay the trust anchor.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Worldline** is mid-turnaround after the 2025 fraud-concealment scandal, share collapse, ~€500m
  capital raise and a large disposal programme ([[Worldline launches €392 million capital raise for
  recovery]], [[Worldline says disposal programme nearly complete after 2025 results]]). It has been
  aggressively signalling forward-looking product wins (Circle/stablecoins, Klarna, agentic commerce —
  [[Worldline adds AI capabilities for agentic commerce]], 2026-01) to rebuild a growth narrative.
  → Why it acts this way: a distressed processor with a commodity take-rate *needs* an innovation story;
  being the "European infrastructure that connects issuer+acquirer+merchant+scheme for agentic" is a
  cheap, high-visibility way to re-rate perception. Fits logically — Worldline sits in the middle of the
  chain and can plausibly claim the "orchestrator" role for both Visa and Mastercard flows.
- **ING** is the recurring issuer partner (NL for Mastercard, now Germany for Visa) — positioning itself
  as the European bank most forward on agentic issuer-authorisation.
- **Visa** slots this into a year-long drumbeat (Intelligent Commerce → Connect → Trusted Agent Protocol
  → Agentic Ready Programme enrollments) — [[Visa projects mass adoption of AI payment agents by 2026]].

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real novelty is thin.** The mechanism (parameterised intent → merchant agent → passkey SCA →
  issuer auth) is *identical* to the June Mastercard run by the same two companies. The only new bits are
  the network (Visa) and programme enrollment. This is network-parity theatre, not a new capability.
- **Traction = zero measured.** No GMV, no live merchants, no conversion, no rollout date — it is a
  single scripted transaction ("demonstrate," "illustrate," "pilot"). "Piloted" ≠ "live at scale."
- **Where the value-add could be real (analysis):** IF the "trusted intent" / issuer-in-the-loop model
  becomes the EU standard, Worldline captures orchestration volume and Visa keeps the trust/identity
  layer. → But *who captures the margin* is unresolved: the agent (OpenAI/merchant) owns the consumer
  intent; the issuer owns liability; the network owns the trust token; the processor is the thinnest
  slice. Worldline risks being the plumbing (lowest margin) while Visa keeps the toll — the same
  "dashboard vs. the-one-who-pays" trap. That shifts the real question from "did agentic work in the EU"
  (yes, technically) to "which layer monetises it" (unclear, and Worldline is weakest-positioned).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Stated next steps: scale requires the four preconditions (use cases, pan-EU interoperability, common
  standards/governance, EU-regulatory alignment) — i.e. governance and interoperability are unsolved.
  Expect more issuer enrollments in Visa's Agentic Ready Programme and parallel Mastercard runs.
- Sentiment: agentic commerce is the dominant 2026 payments theme (318+ agentic-commerce notes in
  corpus). Networks are converging on "identify the agent + programmable consent" as the model
  ([[The Paypers Trust, not tech, will decide agentic commerce]], [[Mastercard Signals report sees AI
  driving trusted intelligent commerce]]).
- Risks / what's unsaid: **fraud liability allocation** in agent-initiated fraud is glossed ("comparable
  to conventional online purchases" is an assertion, not a rule); **economics/interchange** for agent
  transactions undisclosed; **merchant-agent vs consumer-agent** distinction (this used a *merchant* AI
  agent — a narrower, safer case than an autonomous consumer agent shopping across merchants).
- Second-order: the EU push is as much *payment-sovereignty defence* against US-protocol dominance as it
  is product — the corpus's own "Europe's payment sovereignty ambition" theme frames it.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Agentic commerce / AI-agent payments is the payments industry's fastest-moving 2025–26 theme: AI agents transacting on a consumer's behalf within pre-set limits. Third-party sizing is wide and unverifiable in primary form — treat as directional: McKinsey cited (via digitalcommerce360/paz.ai) at up to ~$1tn US agent-driven transactions by 2030; Morgan Stanley cited at ~$385bn US e-commerce by 2030 (attribution second-hand, not the primary notes) — so `[UNSOURCED]` on an authoritative TAM, use only as framing. Live-adoption signal is more credible than the numbers: AI-agent traffic reportedly +805% YoY on Black Friday 2025 and ~$67bn / ~20% of Cyber Week orders "influenced" by agents (via paz.ai, as of late-2025) — "influenced," not "settled," so a soft metric. **Structure:** value chain splits into networks/schemes (Visa, Mastercard — set the trust/standards layer), issuers (ING here — hold authentication/authorisation and fraud liability), and acquirers/processors (Worldline — routes the transaction). Entry barriers are network effects + regulation (SCA/PSD2 in Europe), not capital. **Why now:** the constraint isn't the AI — it's fitting agent purchases inside existing Strong Customer Authentication and liability models; Europe "does not reward innovation outside consumer protection" (note text). This pilot's whole point is regulatory-compatibility, not autonomy.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: for schemes — payments volume / cross-border volume / processed transactions / take rate; for processors — transactions processed, take rate, EBITDA margin. **Key players & basis of competition** (product/standards + distribution, not price): Visa's "Intelligent/Trusted Agent" framework + Payment Passkeys vs Mastercard's Agent Pay/tokenized agent transactions vs Stripe's Agentic Commerce Suite + Shared Payment Tokens (ACP) vs PayPal (ChatGPT/Perplexity Instant Buy, 2025–26). **Recent moves w/ dates:** Mastercard+Visa enlisted banks for agentic pilots (2026-02-20); Mastercard×Stripe/Google/Antom agentic partnership (2025-09-25); Klarna×Stripe BNPL for agents (2026-03-04); Stripe SPT expansion (Mar 2026). **Protagonists' position:** Visa — clear front-runner, sets the standard layer (the moat: network effects + the "trusted agent" registry as an intangible/standard others must adopt) `(analysis)`. Worldline — niche/enabler here: it supplies the rails but does not own the consumer or the standard; strategically fragile (see risk flags). ING — issuer, retains the control point (auth + liability), which is where fraud-economics value concentrates.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Disintermediation / value migrates up the stack.** The economics of agentic payments concentrate at the standards + issuer layers (Visa's trusted-agent registry, ING's authorisation). A processor like Worldline risks being a commoditised rail — participating in the pilot doesn't secure the margin, and Worldline is already financially distressed (impairment, BB, ~30% headcount cut).
2. **"Pilot," not adoption — single live transaction.** One live tx in Germany, de-PR'd. The companies themselves list four unmet conditions for scale (use cases, pan-EU interoperability, common standards/governance, regulatory alignment). No volumes, no economics, no fraud-liability split for agent-initiated disputes disclosed — the hardest question (who eats fraud when an agent misfires) is unresolved.
3. **Standards fragmentation / regulatory dependency.** Multiple competing agent frameworks (Visa Trusted Agent vs Mastercard vs Stripe ACP) with no settled interoperability; the whole model is contingent on SCA/PSD2/PSD3 fitting agent flows — a regulatory shift on agent authorisation could reset the design.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is an incremental credibility datapoint (production, not prototype), not a re-rating trigger — it favours the standard-setters (Visa) over the rails (Worldline). Falsifiable thesis: if agentic payments scale, disproportionate value accrues to the scheme that owns the trusted-agent standard, not to processors. Watch/trigger to confirm or break it: whether Worldline/ING report actual agent-initiated volumes or fee terms (vs more "pilot" PR), and whether a common pan-European agent standard emerges vs the frameworks staying fragmented.
