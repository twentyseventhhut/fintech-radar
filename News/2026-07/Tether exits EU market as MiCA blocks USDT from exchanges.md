Tether exits EU market as MiCA rules block USDT from exchanges
Sinziana Albu
07 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Tether's USDT has lost regulated exchange access in the EU after MiCA's transition period ended, with Circle's USDC and EURC filling the gap.
The European Union's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) framework completed its transition period on 1 July 2026, removing Tether's USDT from regulated crypto exchanges across the bloc after the company chose not to seek authorisation under the new rules. MiCA-licensed platforms, including Coinbase, Kraken, and Crypto.com, have withdrawn USDT trading for European users, ending the token's presence on regulated order books despite its position as the largest stablecoin globally by market capitalisation, at USD 186 billion.
Under MiCA, stablecoin issuers seeking recognition as electronic money tokens (EMTs) must hold at least 60% of reserves in European bank deposits. Tether opted not to apply for this status. A company official has previously stated that the requirement creates systemic risk, given that Tether's reserve model relies primarily on US Treasury securities and other globally diversified assets rather than European bank holdings, making the framework incompatible with its existing structure.
Tether's withdrawal from the European market had been under way for some time before the July 2026 deadline. The company discontinued its euro-pegged EURT stablecoin in 2024, while exchange support for USDT declined progressively over the following months. Coinbase Europe delisted the token in December 2024, Crypto.com followed in January 2025, Binance restricted European USDT trading pairs in March 2025, and Kraken moved users to a sell-only model before ending support entirely.
In addition, licensing data illustrates the selective nature of MiCA authorisation: only 244 MiCA licences had been issued across the EU before the July deadline, and several crypto companies have instead expanded operations from jurisdictions such as Dubai rather than pursue authorisation under the bloc's framework.
USDC gains ground as Tether maintains technology partnerships
Circle took the opposite approach, securing an Electronic Money Institution (EMI) licence in France that can be passported across all 27 EU member states. This has allowed both USDC and EURC to operate under MiCA, positioning them as the primary dollar- and euro-backed stablecoins available on licensed European trading platforms. Liquidity providers that previously quoted USDT pairs have begun rebuilding liquidity around USDC, as regulated exchanges can no longer offer USDT trading within the EU.
Tether has not fully exited the region's digital asset ecosystem, however. StablR and Oobit have launched MiCA-compliant stablecoins, EURR and USDR, built on Tether's Hadron tokenisation platform, allowing the company to sustain technology partnerships without issuing a MiCA-approved stablecoin of its own.
Separately, 37 banks, including BNP Paribas and ING, are developing a joint euro stablecoin known as Qivalis, aimed at providing a regulated euro-denominated alternative as financial institutions expand their participation in digital assets.
Beyond Europe, exchange data points to shifting stablecoin demand: Bybit and OKX have reported higher Bitcoin holdings among users alongside declining USDT balances in their latest Proof of Reserves disclosures, while India's USDT premium rose above 8.5% following enforcement action against crypto remittance firms, underscoring how regional regulation continues to reshape USDT markets differently across jurisdictions.

Revolut to delist USDT for EU users amid MiCA rules
Sinziana Albu
07 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Revolut has announced it will delist USDT for eligible European users in compliance with the EU's MiCA regulation.
The UK-based fintech company has informed affected customers by email that USDT support will be phased out over the coming months. Users can purchase USDT until 6 July 2026, after which no further purchases will be accepted. Furthermore, customers will retain the ability to trade their existing holdings or transfer them to compatible third-party wallets until 31 August 2026. Any balance remaining after that date will be converted into the user's home currency at prevailing market value.
Revolut confirmed the change in a post on X on 3 July 2026, directing customers to a DefiLlama dashboard listing authorised crypto products. The company said the policy applies only to customers who received direct notification of the delisting, and that countries where USDT remains supported are unaffected.
MiCA compliance requirements
MiCA requires stablecoin issuers and crypto-asset service providers to obtain authorisation, hold adequate reserves, meet disclosure obligations, and operate under ongoing supervision before offering services in the EU. USDT has not secured MiCA authorisation, prompting several cryptocurrency platforms operating in the bloc to restrict access for European customers from 1 July 2026. Tether's chief executive officer, Paolo Ardoino, said ahead of MiCA's enactment that the regulation's reserve requirements were not compatible with the stablecoin's operating model, and raised concerns that certain reserve composition and liquidity rules could create difficulties for issuers under the new regime. European service providers have continued to adjust their product offerings to align with the framework.
Wider regulatory pressure on Tether
Separately, Tether suspended transactions on 131 wallets on the TRON blockchain after the US Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) expanded sanctions targeting cryptocurrency addresses linked to ISIS-K. The sanctions list added 134 addresses in total, comprising 131 TRON wallets and three Monero wallets tied to the sanctioned entity. Although unrelated to MiCA, the action reflects broader regulatory scrutiny of cryptocurrency markets across multiple jurisdictions, with implications for how stablecoin issuers manage compliance globally.
Revolut's decision forms part of a wider recalibration among EU-based crypto service providers as MiCA reshapes stablecoin access. Issuers unable to meet licensing, reserve, and disclosure requirements are likely to see reduced availability across regulated platforms in the region.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
De-PR'd: nothing new "happened" on 1 July 2026 that constitutes a distinct event. The substantive act — MiCA barring CASPs (exchanges) from *offering* USDT to EEA users because Tether never sought EMT authorisation — was executed in **Dec 2024–Apr 2025**: Coinbase EU (Dec 2024), Crypto.com (Jan 2025), Kraken (sell-only Mar 2025 → full delist ~Mar 31 2025), Binance EU (Apr 1 2025, balances auto-converted to USDC). Tether wound down its euro EURT (final redemption ~27 Nov 2025). The "1 July 2026" anchor is the end of the CASP transition tail, not a fresh ban.
→ Why framed as a July-2026 "exit": the transition-window closure and the last major holdout (Revolut) leaving in the same week gave outlets a clean "USDT leaves Europe" peg — a narrative milestone, not a new regulatory action. (analysis)
→ Why it matters second-order: the shorthand "USDT banned in Europe" overstates it — MiCA restricts CASP *offering to the public*; users can still **hold/transfer/self-custody** USDT (ESMA clarified custody/transfer ≠ "offering"). Liquidity migrated to DEXs and non-EU venues, not vanished.
Marketing/vagueness flags in the source: "USD 186 billion" cap and "244 MiCA licences" are cited without the fact that the delistings are a year+ old; the piece presents a settled 2024–25 process as a July-2026 headline.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Circle (USDC / EURC)** — the structural winner. Circle took an EMI licence in France, passportable across the EU-27; EURC now ~41–42% of the euro-stablecoin market cap (up from ~17% a year earlier); 8 MiCA-compliant euro stablecoins by mid-2026 (total cap +128% YoY). Corpus corroborates the direction: [[Circle's USDC outpaces USDT growth for second year]] (2026-01).
- **Bank consortia** — Qivalis (BNP Paribas, ING; 37 banks) building a regulated euro stablecoin — corpus: [[Euro stablecoin consortium Qivalis expands to 37 banks]] (2026-05). EURCV (SG-Forge) growing fastest by %.
- **Tether's own EU tech play** — StablR (EURR), Oobit (USDR) on Tether's Hadron platform, plus reported USDT0 variant — lets Tether keep EU technology partnerships without an EMT of its own. Corpus: [[Tether's USDT0 expands to X Layer and OKX]] (2025-09).
→ Second-order: MiCA handed a *US* firm (Circle) a near-monopoly on both compliant USD and EUR stablecoins in the EU — the very outcome that prompted the European Commission's May-2026 MiCA "rewrite" consultation. The regulation's protectionist intent backfired on sovereignty grounds. (analysis)

## [2] Company history / fit
Fits Tether's consistent, deliberate strategy: it never applied for EMT status, objecting to MiCA's requirement that large EMT issuers hold ≥60% of reserves in EU commercial-bank deposits (Ardoino: creates bank-counterparty/systemic risk, citing USDC's SVB depeg). Instead Tether pivoted (a) to the US under the GENIUS Act — USA₮/USAT via Anchorage (corpus: [[Tether announces USAT US-regulated stablecoin]], [[Anchorage Digital to issue Tether's US stablecoin USAT]]) — and (b) to emerging-market distribution and partner-issued EU tokens.
→ Why: Tether's reserve model (US Treasuries, gold, BTC) is structurally incompatible with MiCA's EU-bank-deposit mandate; complying would raise counterparty risk and cut yield. Exiting regulated EU order books costs little — EU is a small share of USDT's EM-driven demand — so Tether rationally chose the US + Global South over EU compliance.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Novelty for THIS note: essentially none. Every hard fact (delisting dates, EURT wind-down, Circle's rise, Qivalis) is already in the corpus across 2024–2026 notes. Traction that IS real and datable belongs to the prior/canonical notes, not this one. The single genuinely-new July-2026 sliver — Revolut, the last major holdout, stopping USDT buys (buy-stop 6 Jul 2026, forced fiat conversion after 31 Aug 2026) — is already captured as fresh in [[Revolut to delist USDT for European users under MiCA]].
→ Value-add of publishing this note again = negative (reader-confusion / repetition). The margin in the EU stablecoin stack is being captured by Circle and licensed bank consortia; Tether's EU value-add is now indirect (Hadron licensing fees), not issuance.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **EU:** European Commission opened a MiCA-rewrite consultation (20 May 2026) — reserve-composition flexibility, third-country equivalence — partly reacting to Circle's dominance. Consultation only; report due ~June 2027, implementation ~2028. Nothing live.
- **Tether:** doubles down on US (USAT/GENIUS) + EM distribution; EU presence stays partner-mediated (USDT0, StablR/Oobit on Hadron).
- **Liquidity:** media estimates of a 70% collapse in regulated-EU USDT depth / $30–35B "gap" are **unverified** directional figures, not audited.
→ Counterintuitive second-order: by pushing USDT off regulated EU venues, MiCA may have shifted EU dollar-stablecoin liquidity toward DEXs/self-custody and a single US issuer (Circle) — reducing, not increasing, EU oversight and sovereignty. That is the tension the rewrite is meant to fix. (analysis)

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** EU stablecoin regulation (MiCA), not a fresh market-size event — a supply-side reshuffle of who is allowed on regulated rails. Total stablecoin market cap topped ~$321bn in 2026 (per bitcoinfoundation.org, as of 2026); USDT alone is ~$184–186bn, more than double #2 USDC (per CoinMarketCap / news.bitcoin.com, Jul 2026). Structure: highly *consolidated* by issuer (USDT ~60%+ of the whole category globally) but now *bifurcating by geography* — EU regulated venues vs everywhere-else. Entry barrier is now regulatory, not technical: MiCA's EMT status demands ≥60% of reserves in EU bank deposits + local supervision. Why now: MiCA's transition period closed 1 Jul 2026, forcing MiCA-licensed CEXs (Coinbase, Kraken, Crypto.com, Binance EEA) to pull USDT order books (note text; coingape, Jul 2026). Second-order effect: the reserve rule is not neutral — it structurally favours issuers willing to hold euros in EU banks (Circle) and disadvantages a US-Treasury-heavy model (Tether), i.e. regulation is picking a reserve architecture, not just a licence.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: circulating market cap (supply), on-chain transfer volume, and — post-MiCA — *regulated-venue liquidity depth* (order-book pairs). Key players: incumbent USDT (largest, now EU-delisted on regulated CEXs, still self-custody/DEX-legal); challenger USDC + euro-native EURC (Circle, MiCA-authorised via Circle Mint Europe SAS EMI licence passported to all 27 states); bank-consortium entrants — Qivalis (37 banks incl. BNP Paribas, ING, per note), plus in-base [[Italy's Bancomat announces EUR.bank stablecoin]] and [[Japan backs yen stablecoin project from top three banks]] showing incumbents building regulated euro/fiat tokens. Basis of competition shifts from network/liquidity (USDT's historic moat) to *regulatory access + reserve credibility*. Recent moves: Circle EURC euro-stablecoin share rose from ~17% to ~41–42% over the 12m to mid-2026 (per stablecoininsider / cryptonewsnavigator, 2026); USDC on-chain volume ~$1.21tn in the transition window, ~2x USDT's (per bingx, 2026); [[ClearBank joins Circle's payment network]] (Oct 2025) extended MiCA-compliant rails. Tether's position in the EU: *retreating*, but retaining a technology foothold — StablR/Oobit issue MiCA-compliant EURR/USDR on Tether's Hadron platform (note), so Tether keeps infra economics without a MiCA coin. Moat (analysis): USDT's global liquidity/network effect is intact ex-EU; inside the EU the moat is gone by regulatory fiat.

**Comps & multiples.** No listed-equity comp for Tether (private, no public float) and no deal/round in this news → EV/Revenue, P/E, price-per-user = **no data / [UNSOURCED]**. Qualitative reserve/economics comparison instead:
- Tether (USDT): ~$184–192bn total assets, US-Treasury-heavy reserves, $1.04bn Q1'26 profit, $8.23bn reserve buffer ([[Tether posts $1.04 billion Q1 profit and $8.23 billion reserve buffer]]). Massive standalone profitability — the EU exit is immaterial to group P&L.
- Circle (USDC/EURC): $740m Q3 revenue, $214m net income ([[Circle explores native token for Arc as Q3 profit surges]]); listed, so a public comp exists but is not quoted in this news → market-cap multiple **not computed** here.
- Credit signal (not a valuation multiple): [[S&P downgrades Tether's USDT stablecoin to weak]] (Nov 2025), citing bitcoin's rising reserve share — the exact reserve-composition concern MiCA codifies. Distribution not computed (only 1 profitability figure per issuer, non-comparable models); qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory disintermediation (structural).** MiCA doesn't fine Tether — it removes its regulated-venue access outright. Why it matters: the largest stablecoin can be legislated off a jurisdiction's regulated rails regardless of size, and other blocs (UK, others) may copy the reserve-composition template, compounding the addressable-market loss beyond the EU.
2. **Liquidity/transition risk (near-term).** USDC/EURC depth may not fully replace USDT liquidity overnight; BitGo's CEO warned of a possible "massive stablecoin crisis" around the deadline (Yahoo Finance, 2026). Why: thin regulated-venue books during the switchover can widen spreads / cause temporary depegs on euro pairs (cryptonewsnavigator flagged competitor depegs while EURC held).
3. **Reserve-model / ratings overhang (Tether-specific).** The Treasury+BTC+gold reserve mix that makes USDT MiCA-incompatible is the same mix S&P downgraded to "weak." Why: EU exclusion + a soft credit signal together cap USDT's penetration of regulated, institution-facing demand — precisely the highest-margin, stickiest segment.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** For the sector this is a *re-rating of regulated euro/dollar stablecoin rails toward MiCA-native issuers* (Circle, bank consortia), not a hit to global stablecoin demand — USDT's supply and profit are set by EM/US remittance and savings use, which MiCA doesn't touch (USAT +540% to ~$140m, [[Tether's US stablecoin USAT grows 540% to over $140 million in April]]). Falsifiable thesis (analysis): EURC keeps compounding EU euro-stablecoin share (past ~50%) while USDT's total market cap keeps growing globally — regulation redraws the map, not the volume. Trigger/what breaks it: a MiCA-compliant USDT variant (USDT0/Hadron-issued) or Tether reversing and seeking an EMT licence would collapse the "USDT locked out of the EU" premise; conversely, a transition-window depeg on a euro pair would validate the liquidity-fragmentation risk.
