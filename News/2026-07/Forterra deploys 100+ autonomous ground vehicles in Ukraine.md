The first American autonomous ground vehicles are fighting in Ukraine
 Forterra , a U.S. builder of autonomous vehicles, revealed today that more than 100 of its self-driving ATVs have been deployed in conflict zones in Ukraine for the past nine months, in what the company believes is the largest deployment of autonomous ground vehicles in combat by any U.S. defense tech company.
“I believe this to be true of every defense technology that’s ever been created — until you hit the realities of combat, you’re just not going to know,” Scott Sanders, Forterra’s chief growth officer and a former U.S. Marine officer, told TechCrunch.
Funded by U.S. defense dollars, the mission is part of a growing effort to transform the U.S. military through its support of Ukrainian resistance to Russian invaders. While aerial drones have garnered much of the attention in the fight, the dynamics they’ve created — extensive no-go zones where surveillance can lead to death from above — have led Ukrainian strategists to seek ground-based autonomy as well.
“There’s nowhere to hide,” Sergeant Major Corey Wilkens, who leads a program developing autonomous vehicles and tactics for the U.S. Army, explained. “You become very, very vulnerable to be able to be attacked by [first-person view drones], other sorts of drones dropping munitions, artillery, mortar, the full range of things that they have.”
Ukraine is already building its own uncrewed ground vehicles (UGVs) to help move supplies and munitions, or evacuate wounded soldiers, but they are typically battery-powered and can only carry up to 250 kilograms, according to a soldier in the Ukrainian army who has worked with the vehicles and who TechCrunch won’t identify for security reasons.
Forterra’s Lancer vehicles, based on Polaris ATVs and equipped with a custom-built sensor and compute stack, are gas-powered and can carry 750 kilograms of cargo, making them more versatile and useful. “The bottom line is that this UGV for logistics and just maintaining our defense is the most important UGV in Ukraine,” the soldier said. “It’s fucking fantastic, and we are dying to get more.”
They didn’t feel that way at first. The Ukrainian Armed Forces have had have mixed experiences with Western contractors bringing new tech to the battle, and at first Forterra’s offerings felt a little too geared for the high-end requirements of the U.S. Army. Modifying the vehicle for the situation — particularly, by adding a Starlink satellite internet antenna — made it a huge value add.
Since arriving in Ukraine last October, the vehicles have driven more than 2,500 miles across more than 1,100 missions, carrying 777,440 pounds of total weight and completing 52 casualty evacuations. Some have been lost in combat, particularly if they get stuck in deep mud or other terrain where Russian forces can target them at leisure.
Forterra has learned some useful lessons — about electronic warfare, updating their software from afar, how to maneuver in challenging conditions, and ensuring their vehicles don’t break down. The company, which has raised more than $500 million in venture funding from funds like XYZ Venture Capital and Moore Strategic Partners, is now better positioned to compete for lucrative national security contracts .
They’ve also seen the limits of autonomy: For now, Ukranian soldiers have mainly been teleoperating the vehicles in combat zones, in part because they’re too valuable to lose and in part because autonomous vehicles aren’t quite ready for the realities of war.
While, for example, the vehicles can navigate autonomously across diverse terrain, they’re not quite at the point where they can identify unexpected enemy forces and react appropriately. “We actually need to be able to respond to the enemy threats, live, while it’s in front of the enemy, which the autonomy doesn’t know how to do yet,” the Ukrainian soldier explained. 
Forterra, which began work on autonomous vehicles 20 years ago, is working on how to combine the kinds of algorithms that gave us self-driving cars with newer generative AI software that allows machines to react to their surroundings in a generalized way. As with other autonomous systems, one of the key obstacles is gathering the right data. 
“There’s a lot of things you have to do that aren’t available in an open source model because they’re not things that humans do, whether that’s figuring out how to navigate a minefield or [operating] a weapon system,” Sanders told TechCrunch. “You need to be able to turn the dials and some things more of a classical robotics approach, and then leverage AI where you need to.”
Competitors in this space are solving similar challenges, like Scout AI , which raised $100 million earlier this year to train foundation models and develop a suite of autonomous platforms for the military that includes UGVs. Other startups like Field AI and Overland AI are trialing UGVs with the U.S. military.
Even with the limitations on UGVs, American military experts are convinced that it’s time to invest in these tools. “Ground autonomy is achievable now and we’ve seen it,” Wilkens said.
Scott Philips, the chief innovation officer at Forterra, visited a Ukrainian unit’s operations center to see the vehicles in action firsthand, winning respect from the unit for visiting an area in range of Russian attacks.
“What struck me most was seeing exactly where the seams are: which steps are still manual, where data has to be re-entered or re-verified by hand, and where the team has already found ways to automate or speed things up,” Philips told TechCrunch. “That’s the kind of ground truth you can’t get from a slide deck because it shows you precisely where better tooling could take pressure off the people doing this work in real time.” One challenge issued by the Ukrainians: Make it cheaper. Forterra’s Lancers aren’t expensive for their category, thanks to relying on Polaris’ commercial supply chain for the vehicle itself, but they are still too valuable to be deployed as freely as UAVs can be.
“Attrition is just a fact of this battlefield, and we have lost a few at this point, and it hurt, and we need more, and therefore we need them cheaper,” the Ukrainian soldier told TechCrunch.

# Context-enrichment: Forterra deploys 100+ autonomous ground vehicles in Ukraine
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**FRESHNESS VERDICT: fresh.** This is the first time Forterra's Ukraine deployment appears in the corpus (grep `company/forterra` returns only this note). It is a genuinely new disclosure (7 Jul 2026): the deployment ran covertly for ~9 months and was only now revealed. It is adjacent to — but not a duplicate of — the corpus's defense-tech cluster: [[Stark raises $569 million at $4 billion valuation for defense drones]], [[Defense startup Dominion Dynamics builds Arctic surveillance systems]], [[a16z American Dynamism and the Washington-Silicon Valley thaw]], [[Marc Andreessen joins Pentagon Defense Policy Board]]. Not a re-run of any of those (different company, different event type — a fielding disclosure, not a round).

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Forterra (US autonomy firm, HQ Clarksburg MD, founded 2002 as Robotic Research; renamed 2024) disclosed on 7 Jul 2026 that >100 of its "Lancer" ground vehicles (Polaris ATV chassis + custom sensor/compute stack, gas-powered, 750 kg payload) have operated in Ukraine since October 2025 — ~9 months. Claimed metrics: >2,500 miles, >1,100 missions, 777,440 lbs hauled, 52 casualty evacuations (TechCrunch primary; some outlets cite 88 casevacs — number is fluid across retellings, treat as approximate). Company frames it as "the largest deployment of autonomous ground vehicles in combat by any U.S. defense tech company."

**The load-bearing de-PR point:** the headline word "autonomous" is doing heavy lifting. Per the primary source and Ukrainian operators, the vehicles are **mainly teleoperated** in combat — kept under human control because they're too valuable to lose AND because "autonomy doesn't know how to" react to live enemy threats yet. So this is a **remote-driven logistics/casevac mule with autonomous-navigation assist**, not autonomous combat. Also de-PR'd: the deployment isn't a commercial win — it was **funded by a $114M US Army prime contract** (awarded ~March 2025) under which Forterra built 105 Lancers (roughly one-third of the award value); only the Lancers went to Ukraine. So "Forterra deployed" = "the US Army paid Forterra to build vehicles it then sent to Ukraine." **Why framed this way:** the reveal is a marketing event timed to convert combat-proof-points into leverage for larger national-security contracts — the real audience is the Pentagon procurement pipeline, not Ukraine.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Crowded US ground-autonomy field, mostly DARPA RACER lineage:
- **Scout AI** — raised $100M Series A (29 Apr 2026; largest US defense-tech Series A on record) for "Fury," a vision-language-action foundation model for unmanned warfare (the AI "brain" layer, platform-agnostic).
- **Overland AI** — raised $100M (2026) to scale its ULTRA UGV; also RACER-derived.
- **Field AI** — RACER spinout, trialing UGVs with US military (foundation-model autonomy stack).
- **Anduril** — the strategic overhang: its Lattice stack already does cross-domain C2, mission autonomy, mesh networking + it fields its own hardware. Any pure-play UGV firm risks being subsumed.
- **Milrem (Estonia)** / Ukrainian domestic UGVs — the incumbent battlefield reality; Ukraine already builds its own UGVs, but battery-powered and capped ~250 kg. Forterra's edge is the 750 kg gas payload + (post-modification) Starlink link.

**Position:** Forterra is ahead on *fielded scale in real combat* (its genuine differentiator vs. Scout/Overland/Field, which are largely pre-deployment or trialing). But it is behind/at-parity on the *AI autonomy layer* — it openly concedes the vehicles can't react to enemies, while Scout/Field are selling exactly that foundation-model capability. **Second-order:** the market is bifurcating into "who owns the platform/integration + combat data" (Forterra) vs. "who owns the autonomy brain" (Scout/Field/Anduril-Lattice). Whoever controls the model layer captures the durable margin; hardware+integration is more commoditizable. Forterra's real asset may be the **proprietary battlefield dataset** from 1,100+ missions — the one thing competitors can't buy.

## [2] Company history / fit
Founded 2002 as Robotic Research (Alberto Lacaze & Karl Murphy) — a 20+ year robotics/autonomy pedigree, not a startup. Rebranded Forterra; raised >$500M cumulatively. Nov 2025: closed $238M Series C (equity + debt) at ~$1B valuation, led by Moore Strategic Ventures with Salesforce Ventures, Franklin Templeton, Balyasny, Hanwha, RTX Ventures, NightDragon, XYZ VC, Hedosophia, Enlightenment Capital. Contract stack: DoD's first ground-autonomy production contract (ROGUE Fires, USMC), the $114M Army breaching/logistics prime, Army GEARS Phase II, and an Army UxS contract. **Why it acts this way:** a two-decade R&D house needs to convert deep tech into recurring program-of-record revenue; a real combat deployment is the single most credible signal for winning large DoD contracts, so revealing Ukraine now (right after a $1B-valuation raise) is a coordinated go-to-market move to justify the valuation and pull forward procurement.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Genuinely new:** actual, at-scale, months-long combat use of US-built ground autonomy — most rivals are still in trials or bootcamps. That's real traction, not an announcement: 105 units built and delivered in <6 months, 1,100+ missions logged, 52 casevacs. The value-add is credible on the *logistics/casevac* axis (payload + range beat Ukrainian battery UGVs; Starlink integration was the unlock).
**But the value-add is narrower than the PR:** (a) it's teleoperation-heavy, so the "autonomy" premium is partly aspirational; (b) attrition is real — units lost in mud/terrain and to Russian targeting, and the Ukrainian operator's blunt ask is "make it cheaper," meaning current unit cost is a barrier to the very attrition-tolerant use that UGVs need. **Who captures the margin:** if the moat is the combat dataset feeding future autonomy, Forterra captures it; if the moat is just an integrated Polaris ATV, the margin erodes as cheaper local UGVs + third-party autonomy brains (Scout/Field) catch up. The Ukrainian "make it cheaper" line is the tell that the current economics don't yet fit expendable-mass warfare.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Forterra will parlay the reveal into larger DoD programs and blend classical robotics with generative AI to close the reaction-to-threat gap (data is the bottleneck). Sentiment is strongly positive in US defense-tech ("ground autonomy is achievable now" — US Army program lead). Backdrop: the a16z-led "American Dynamism"/Washington–Silicon Valley thaw ([[a16z American Dynamism and the Washington-Silicon Valley thaw]], [[Marc Andreessen joins Pentagon Defense Policy Board]]) and a wave of defense rounds ([[Stark raises $569 million at $4 billion valuation for defense drones]]) mean cheap capital and procurement tailwinds. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the same reveal that markets Forterra also hands Russia (and every competitor) a public spec of the system's weaknesses — teleoperation dependence, mud/terrain failure modes, EW exposure, cost — so the disclosure trades near-term operational edge for capital/procurement leverage. And the "make it cheaper" mandate points toward commoditization of the platform, pushing the durable value up into the autonomy/data layer where Anduril and the foundation-model startups are strongest. Risk: Forterra wins the hardware war and loses the software margin.

**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: defense autonomy / uncrewed ground vehicles (UGVs) — logistics/CASEVAC end of the ground-robotics stack, distinct from attack-drone/UAV names. Market size: analyst reports cluster the global UGV market at ~$3.0–4.3bn in 2026 (Mordor Intelligence ~$3.02bn; Precedence ~$4.31bn) growing to ~$13.1bn by 2031 at ~9.7% CAGR (per secondary market-research citations via Yahoo Finance/NatLawReview, as of 2026) — figures are third-party research-firm estimates, methodology unverifiable, treat as directional only, not a hard TAM. North America ~35% share on high defense spend `(per Mordor, 2026)`. Structure: fragmented, early, mostly VC-funded startups plus primes/OEMs (Oshkosh, Polaris) — value is being created at the autonomy/software + sensor-stack layer, with the chassis increasingly a commodity (Forterra rides Polaris' ATV supply chain, Overland/Field ride retrofit kits). Barriers: capital (multi-hundred-$M rounds now table stakes), classified data access, and — the real moat — combat-validated field data. **Why now:** the Ukraine drone war created "nowhere to hide" no-go zones (per note), pushing demand for ground autonomy for resupply/evac; U.S. DoD force-protection + robotic-combat-vehicle procurement cycles; Ukraine alone projected to acquire ~30k UGVs in 2026 `(per secondary reporting, 2026)` `[UNSOURCED — single citation]`.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs (this vertical, not disclosed as clean financials): combat-validated deployment scale (units fielded, miles, missions, attrition rate), payload/range vs battery peers, and — increasingly — foundation-model/autonomy maturity (tele-op share vs true autonomy). Key players & basis of competition: Forterra (gas Lancer, 750kg payload, 100+ units, ~2,500 mi / 1,100 missions / 52 CASEVAC — combat traction is the differentiator); **Scout AI** — $100M Series A Apr-2026, "Fury" VLA foundation model, positions as the autonomy brain layer, largest US defense-tech Series A per PR; **Field AI** & **Overland AI** (OverDrive stack, SPARK retrofit) — both RACER/DARPA spinouts trialing with US Army; Ukraine's own domestic UGVs (battery, ≤250kg) are the incumbent baseline Forterra out-specs. Basis of competition: less price than combat-proof + payload + autonomy roadmap and unit cost. **Protagonist position:** ahead on *demonstrated combat deployment* (likely the largest US UGV combat footprint to date, per company's own claim — de-PR: self-asserted, unverified) `(analysis)`; behind on *autonomy* — the note admits vehicles are mainly tele-operated, so the "autonomous" label is aspirational today. Moat = proprietary combat/EW data + Polaris supply chain (cost), not defensible IP yet.

Growth/revenue undisclosed → cannot test valuation-vs-growth. `[UNSOURCED]` for all revenue-based multiples.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Autonomy gap vs the label.** Vehicles are mainly *tele-operated* in combat (note + market data: tele-op ~69% of 2025 installs) — the "autonomous" thesis and any premium tied to it is not yet field-proven; if generative-AI battlefield autonomy stays years out, Forterra is really a rugged tele-op logistics-hardware vendor, a lower-multiple category.
2. **Attrition + "make it cheaper" = margin/unit-economics squeeze.** The end-customer's explicit ask is lower cost ("we need them cheaper"); combat attrition means the product must be consumable-priced, capping ASP/margin and pressuring the hardware-heavy model. Second-order: pushes value toward the software/autonomy layer, where Scout AI is positioning — Forterra risks becoming the commoditized chassis.
3. **Demand/geo concentration + political dependence.** Traction is concentrated in one theater (Ukraine) funded by "U.S. defense dollars"; revenue durability hinges on continued US/Ukraine appropriations and winning US programs (e.g. GEARS) — a ceasefire or funding shift removes the marquee proof-point and the pipeline.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Combat validation in Ukraine is the sector's first real ground-autonomy proof-point and likely re-rates *logistics UGVs* from concept to procurable category — bullish for the space, but it also exposes the fault line: hardware makers (Forterra) vs autonomy-brain makers (Scout AI). Falsifiable thesis: value migrates to the software/foundation-model layer, so a hardware-only Forterra sees multiple compression unless it owns the autonomy stack. **Watch/trigger:** a named US program-of-record award (GEARS or similar) or a disclosed unit-cost cut = thesis-supporting for Forterra; conversely, a Scout AI / foundation-model vendor winning the autonomy layer across mixed fleets = thesis-confirming that Forterra is the commoditized chassis.
