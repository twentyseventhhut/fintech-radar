## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)

Build, a NY-based (British-U.S.) startup founded ~2024 by architect James Stirrat-Ellis (ex-Heatherwick Studio; worked on Changi Airport T5) and AI researcher Ben McClusky (multi-agent systems / RL background), raised an **$8.5M seed** (≈€7.4M) **led by Index Ventures**, with Pebblebed, Puzzle Ventures and Tiny.vc. Notable angels: **OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar, Blackstone CTO John Stecher**, plus Meta AI Research / Google Maps figures. (Sources: TechFundingNews, Index Ventures, FinSMEs, EU-Startups.)

**What the product actually is (de-PR'd).** The StrictlyVC one-liner ("automate due diligence for infrastructure and data center projects") is accurate but narrow. Build markets itself as an **"agentic real-estate" / built-world automation platform**: a multi-agent AI + middleware layer that runs **site sourcing, power assessment, technical due diligence and early-stage/structural design** for large infrastructure — with **data centers as the flagship use case** (the AI power-buildout is the wedge, not the whole TAM). Claims: pulls **1,600+ data sources**, evaluates planning/environmental/power/political constraints **in parallel rather than sequentially**, cutting DD timelines **"by 95%+"** (weeks → hours).

- **→ Why framed as "due diligence" and not "design":** DD is the sequential bottleneck that stalls projects for weeks–months and is highest-willingness-to-pay for developers racing scarce power. Leading with a quantified time-cut (95%) is a cleaner ROI story than "AI architect."
- **→ Why the "95%" and "1,600 sources" numbers are soft:** vendor-supplied, no baseline/methodology disclosed; "parallel vs sequential" is the real mechanism claim and is credible, but the headline % is marketing until third-party validated.

**Traction that is actually stated (not just announced):** **100+ projects across 15 countries**; customers named include **governments, Fortune 500s, and Tishman Speyer**; **"first hyperscaler client"** recently landed. This is real usage, though "projects" is undefined (paid deployments vs pilots unclear) and revenue is not disclosed.

## [1] Competitors / peers

Two distinct clusters — the note's semsearch mixed them; separate them:

- **Same category (physical-infra / data-center siting & permitting DD) — the real comps:** **Paces** (unifies grid/permitting/environmental data to find viable sites, catch flaws pre-capital), **Transect** (environmental DD / site assessment), **Pearl Street Technologies** (grid-interconnection queue/workflow), **Orennia** (energy-transition + transmission-siting analytics), **LandGate/LandTech** (site selection + utility analytics), plus permitting-automation plays **Lorica, Pulley, Greenlite, Blumen**. Build's differentiator vs these: it spans **site → power → DD → early design in one agentic stack**, rather than owning a single slice (permitting OR grid OR environmental).
- **Different category (PE/deal DD) — NOT comps, do not conflate:** [[Eunice raises $8m to automate due diligence with AI]] / [[Eunice raises $8 million for regulated-market AI]] (regulated-market/alt-asset DD), [[Keye launches from stealth with $5 million seed for PE diligence]], [[AI startup Metal raises $5M for private-equity deal analysis]], [[Pascal AI raises $3.1M for autonomous investment research]], [[Gridline launches AltComply for private markets due diligence]]. These automate *financial/legal deal diligence for investors*; Build automates *physical-project diligence for developers/operators*. Same buzzword, different buyer and data.

- **→ Why the landscape is fragmented:** infra DD spans grid data, permitting, environmental, zoning, geospatial — each historically a separate vendor/consultant. Build bets a single agentic layer beats point tools; incumbents are the **AEC consultancies and in-house dev teams** doing this manually, so the near-term competition is "software vs analysts," not another startup.
- **→ Second-order:** whoever owns the **power-availability + interconnection graph** owns the highest-value node (see [1]/[4]); Build's edge is only durable if its 1,600-source aggregation is genuinely hard to replicate and it moves upstream from DD into being the developer's system-of-record.

## [2] Company history / fit

~2-year-old startup; founders pair **domain (architecture/major infra delivery)** + **AI research (multi-agent/RL)** — a coherent "vertical-agent" team profile. Seed only; no prior disclosed rounds. The pivot in framing from "agentic real estate" (European coverage, June 30) to "infrastructure/data-center DD" (U.S./StrictlyVC framing) reflects **positioning around the AI-datacenter capex wave** to attract Index + hyperscaler-adjacent angels (Blackstone CTO, OpenAI CFO).

- **→ Why this structure:** an architect-led founder gives credibility with developers (Tishman Speyer, governments) that a pure-AI team lacks; the AI co-founder gives the agentic-automation story. The angel roster (Blackstone/OpenAI) is a **distribution signal** — the buyers of data-center DD sit inside those orbits.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction

**What's genuinely new:** applying **multi-agent LLM orchestration to physical-infrastructure pre-development DD** (site/power/permitting/design) as one stack — a thinner-covered vertical than the crowded PE/legal-DD space. Value-add rests on **collapsing a sequential, consultant-heavy workflow into a parallel automated one** at the exact moment power/permitting is the binding constraint on the AI buildout (grid connection takes 5–7 yrs vs 12–18 mo to build the box).

- **→ Why the value-add is plausibly real:** the pain (multi-year interconnection/permitting drag) is severe and expensive; even a partial time-cut has large $ value to hyperscalers/developers. **100+ projects, 15 countries, a hyperscaler client, Tishman Speyer** are above-average seed traction and are usage, not just press.
- **→ What breaks it / who captures margin:** (a) the "95%" is unverified; (b) data-center siting hinges on **utility/grid realities Build doesn't control** — it can *surface* power availability but not *deliver* megawatts, so the margin risk is Build staying an **advisory/DD tool** rather than the transaction/system-of-record layer; (c) incumbents (Paces, Transect, Pearl Street) and AEC consultancies can bundle; (d) "projects" and revenue undefined. (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment

Use of funds: expand engineering/infra teams, R&D, and **North America + Europe** presence. Backdrop is strongly favorable: hyperscalers projected **~$700B in data-center capex in 2026** (Amazon ~$200B, Google ~$175–185B); power is the #1 site-selection constraint — "deliverable power beats land/incentives/fiber." That makes pre-dev DD/siting software a hot, well-funded sub-theme.

- **→ Why the market goes this way:** the bottleneck has shifted from capital to **electrons + permits**; software that shortens siting/DD directly unlocks capex, so demand is structural, not hype.
- **→ Counterintuitive second-order:** the same capex wave that is Build's tailwind also invites **incumbents and hyperscalers to build in-house** and well-funded rivals (Paces et al.) to converge — a seed-stage tool's edge is fragile unless it locks in proprietary data and becomes the developer's default workflow. Regulatory/grid reform (interconnection queue changes) could either enlarge or shrink the pain it monetizes.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Build sits at the intersection of two markets: AI-applied diligence/workflow software and physical data-center/infrastructure development. The demand backdrop is the AI capex supercycle — global data-center capex is projected above $1.0tn for 2026 and toward $1.7tn by 2030 (per Dell'Oro Group, via delloro.com, 2026), with the Big-5 hyperscalers alone at ~$725bn of AI-infra spend in 2026 (per BloombergNEF / al capital advisory, 2026). Why now: for AI-scale sites, power availability has become the gating diligence category (not one item among many), while a single site screen must fuse utility, parcel, zoning, flood, transmission, fiber, incentive and cost data — a manual, sequential process that scales poorly against a wall of new projects. Build claims to cut data-center/infra due diligence by ~95% (per techfundingnews.com / thenextweb.com, 2026-07). Structure: the analytics/diligence layer on top of infra buildout is nascent and fragmented; barrier to entry is less capital than proprietary workflow + data integration and credibility with hyperscaler/developer buyers (Build reports 100+ projects across 15 countries and a first hyperscaler client).

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs this vertical runs on: ARR/seat or per-project revenue, diligence-hours saved, projects/sites processed, logo count (hyperscalers/developers). Distinct positioning vs. the AI-diligence pack: Build's buyer is the infra developer/investor doing *physical* site diligence (site sourcing, power assessment, early design), sold "as a service, not software" (per techfundingnews.com, 2026-07). That is a different lane from the AI investment-diligence cohort automating deal/document review for PE/asset managers — [[Keye launches from stealth with $5 million seed for PE diligence]], [[Eunice raises $8m to automate due diligence with AI]], [[AI startup Metal raises $5M for private-equity deal analysis]], [[F2 raises $10M for AI in private markets]], [[Meridian PE raises $7M for private equity AI system]], [[Pascal AI raises $3.1M for autonomous investment research]] — and from horizontal finance-AI incumbents (Hebbia, Rogo). Basis of competition: distribution + proprietary data pipelines + accuracy on regulated/physical facts, not raw LLM access. Position: niche/early leader in a specific slice (data-center/infra siting diligence) rather than head-to-head with the PE-diligence crowd `(analysis)`. Moat lever: switching costs + data network effects if Build accretes proprietary site/power datasets across projects `(hypothesis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** No revenue/valuation disclosed for Build's $8.5m seed → EV/Revenue and post-money "no data" `[UNSOURCED]`.
