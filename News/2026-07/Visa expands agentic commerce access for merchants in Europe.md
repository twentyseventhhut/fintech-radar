Visa expands agentic commerce access for merchants across Europe
Sinziana Albu
08 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Visa has enabled live agentic commerce transactions across Europe, letting AI agents complete purchases with participating merchants.
The transactions mark a shift from testing within controlled environments to live purchases at participating merchant websites, with agents browsing products, selecting items and initiating payments within parameters set by consumers.
In addition, the transactions are enabled through Visa Intelligent Commerce, Visa's portfolio of initiatives supporting AI-driven commerce. They rely on explicit consumer authorisation, with agents operating within user-defined controls rather than acting independently of the cardholder's instructions. Visa has worked with more than 30 European card issuers to support these transactions, alongside merchants including lastminute.com, Frasers, Cleverbridge and BrickDepot, spanning the travel, retail and ecommerce sectors.
The initiative forms part of Visa's Agentic Ready programme, which the company describes as bringing together infrastructure, standards and partners to support agentic commerce across European markets. Visa has compared the approach to the model it used to scale contactless payments, aiming to establish trust in the system ahead of broader rollout.
Merchant participation has been facilitated through Visa's Trusted Agent Protocol (TAP) and its Agent Directory, designed to let merchants recognise and interact with verified AI agents across platforms. These tools give merchants a way to distinguish authenticated agent traffic from other website activity, while retaining control over how agents access product listings and complete purchases.
Furthermore, Visa has stated that TAP does not require merchants to adopt new infrastructure and is designed to operate alongside existing and emerging commerce protocols. Implementation support is provided by infrastructure providers, including Cloudflare and Akamai, which integrate the protocol into the systems underpinning merchant websites.
Authentication through Visa Payment Passkeys
On the issuing side, participation is supported through Visa Payment Passkeys, which authenticate transactions initiated by AI agents on behalf of consumers. Each transaction is linked to a verified user and their specific instruction, a mechanism Visa states keeps consumers in control of when and how payments are made. All transactions completed under the Agentic Ready programme have used Visa Payment Passkeys, which Visa says supports issuers' compliance with the EU's Strong Customer Authentication requirements.
Issuing banks that have completed live, agent-executed transactions include Barclays, BBVA, CaixaBank, Commerzbank, HSBC UK, ING, Klarna, Lloyds Banking Group, NatWest, Nordea Bank, Revolut and Deutsche Kreditbank, among others.
Wider implications for the payments ecosystem
Visa has said the model underpinning these transactions is also being extended to commercial and B2B payment scenarios, where agent-initiated transactions could apply to business purchasing processes. The company has positioned the live transactions as a step towards wider adoption of agentic commerce across sectors and markets in Europe, contingent on continued cooperation between issuers, merchants and infrastructure providers.

# Context-enrichment: Visa expands agentic commerce access for merchants in Europe
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Visa says AI agents can now complete **live** purchases at **independent European merchant storefronts** (lastminute.com, Frasers, Cleverbridge, BrickDepot — travel/retail/ecommerce) via its **Intelligent Commerce / Agentic Ready** programme. 30+ EU issuers named as having completed agent-executed transactions (Barclays, BBVA, CaixaBank, Commerzbank, HSBC UK, ING, Klarna, Lloyds, NatWest, Nordea, Revolut, Deutsche Kreditbank). Mechanism: **Trusted Agent Protocol (TAP)** + **Agent Directory** (merchant-side: recognise/verify signed agent identity, distinguish from bot traffic) and **Visa Payment Passkeys** (issuer-side: bind each txn to a verified user + specific instruction → claimed EU SCA compliance). Infra via **Cloudflare + Akamai**. Extension to B2B/commercial "being explored".

- **De-PR'd reality:** this is a **choreographed infrastructure milestone, not a market launch**. Primary Visa release dates to **2 Jul 2026** (Visa Payments Forum, Paris); The Paypers (8 Jul) is downstream coverage. It is the **third beat** of a staged 2026 European rollout, not a first: [[Visa expands Agentic Ready program to Canada]] already had "Agentic Ready active in Europe... live transactions taking place today" (Apr/May 2026). Visa's own framing "advance beyond testing at controlled storefronts" is a tacit admission the earlier "live" was controlled.
- **Why structured this way:** Visa explicitly compares the playbook to how it scaled contactless — name issuers + a handful of trophy merchants to manufacture trust ahead of GA. The delta vs April is **merchant-side reach** (TAP/Agent Directory at third-party stores) + a longer named-issuer roster (~30 vs ~20).
- **What's conspicuously absent:** zero transaction volume / GMV / conversion / merchant count beyond 4; and **no named consumer agent app** driving the purchases → rails without a distribution surface.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Mastercard Agent Pay / Agentic Tokens** — the direct rival, and **ahead of Visa on the EU "first"**: Santander + Mastercard completed *Europe's first live end-to-end AI-agent payment in a regulated framework on 2 Mar 2026* — a month before Visa's controlled phase. Agent Pay announced Apr 2025, US-wide Nov 2025; "Agent Pay for Machines" Jun 2026. Internal: [[PayPal integrates Mastercard Agent Pay and comes to ChatGPT]], [[Mastercard and Majid Al Futtaim launch agentic payments in UAE]].
- **Stripe + OpenAI (Agentic Commerce Protocol / Instant Checkout)** — the **only demonstrably at-scale CONSUMER agentic checkout** (live in ChatGPT, Etsy/Shopify) — but **US-only**. Internal protocol context: [[Checkout.com adopts Agentic Commerce Protocol]].
- **Google AP2 (Agent Payments Protocol)** + Coinbase **x402** (onchain settlement) — protocols/frameworks, 60+ orgs, not live consumer EU rails. **EMVCo** working on cross-network agentic specs: [[EMVCo to develop specifications for agentic payments]].
- **PayPal / Amazon (Buy for Me)** — merchant-enablement / US beta.
- **Position:** In Europe, Visa vs Mastercard is a **near-tie on controlled "firsts"** (Mastercard edged the regulated-rail first); Visa leads on named-issuer breadth. Both run near-identical playbooks (policy-scoped tokens + passkeys + demo transactions). **Why:** the networks are racing to make their token/passkey rail the default trust layer before a protocol war (TAP vs AP2 vs ACP) settles — note Mastercard, PayPal, Revolut, Worldpay appear across *multiple* protocols → hedging, not consolidation.

## [2] Company history / fit
Visa Intelligent Commerce unveiled 30 Apr 2025 (partners incl. Anthropic, OpenAI, Microsoft, Mistral, Perplexity, Stripe); TAP with Cloudflare Oct 2025; Akamai Dec 2025; Agentic Ready launched EU/UK Mar 2026; global expansion APAC/LatAm 29 Apr 2026; Canada May 2026 ([[Visa expands Agentic Ready program to Canada]]); earlier AI-payments framing in Asia Nov 2025 ([[Visa leans into AI payments and stablecoins in Asia]]). **Why Visa acts this way:** a commodity take-rate on rails needs a defensible trust/tokenisation moat; owning the **Agent Directory** (who is a legitimate agent) and passkey authentication lets Visa stay "the one who pays / verifies" as value migrates to the agent layer — the same disintermediation risk flagged in the corpus (Ramp/Visa-Mastercard margin-capture debate).

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Genuinely new: agent transactions reaching **third-party merchant storefronts** (not just controlled issuer test storefronts) + Agent Directory as a bot/agent discriminator. Everything else is prior art re-packaged. **Traction = essentially nil disclosed:** no volumes, GMV, conversion; likely a handful of demo transactions (e.g., Worldline+ING+Visa completed "a" live txn). Passkey adoption **not quantified**. **Where the margin sits:** if TAP/Agent Directory becomes the verification default, Visa captures a durable trust-layer toll; if a rival protocol (AP2/ACP) wins the agent side, Visa is back to a commoditised authorisation hop. Value-add is **plausible but unproven** — plumbing proven, market not.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Visa: extend to B2B/commercial agent purchasing; roll toward GA. **Regulatory:** PSD2 SCA still governs — agentic payments break the "human present at checkout" assumption, so the industry leans on passkeys + pre-authorised policies + emerging "Know Your Agent" concepts; PSD3/PSR text (provisional agreement Nov 2025) does **not** yet codify agent delegation → real regulatory risk. **Sentiment:** Gartner (Nov 2025) sees ~20% of digital commerce via agents by 2030 and >$15T B2B AI-mediated by 2028; McKinsey pegs ~$5T ([[McKinsey projects agentic commerce could drive $5 trillion by 2030]]) — but TAMs diverge on the definition of "agentic," so treat as directional. **Second-order:** the central question is not "is there an agentic story" (yes, everyone has one) but **who owns the agent-verification/trust layer in the EU** — and there Visa is one of ~4 credible contenders, not a clear leader.

## Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Is "live in Europe" actually new, or a re-announcement?** → Largely a **re-announcement / next beat**. Visa claimed European "live" activity in the Mar/Apr Agentic Ready phase ([[Visa expands Agentic Ready program to Canada]] says "live transactions taking place today"). The 2 Jul delta is *merchant-side reach* (TAP/Agent Directory at independent stores) + a bigger issuer roster. But it IS a genuine incremental development (third-party merchants, new terms), not a pure reprint → fresh, borderline.

2. **Did Visa or Mastercard reach "first live in Europe" first?** → **Mastercard** — Santander's live end-to-end AI-agent payment (2 Mar 2026) predates Visa's independent-merchant live claim. Visa's edge is issuer breadth, not primacy.

3. **Any real volumes / GMV / conversion?** → **No — undisclosed** in every release. Assume single-digit demo transactions until proven.

4. **Which consumer AI agents are actually driving these EU purchases?** → **Open — the biggest gap.** Release names issuers, merchants, infra — but NOT the agent apps. Rails without a distribution surface (no ChatGPT/Gemini equivalent in the loop).

5. **Does this satisfy PSD2 SCA at production scale?** → **Claimed, not adjudicated.** Passkeys "support compliance"; no regulator has blessed a delegated/agent SCA model. PSD3/PSR doesn't yet codify agent delegation. Regulatory risk live.

6. **Is TAP winning as the standard?** → **Open / contested.** Competes with Google AP2, Stripe-OpenAI ACP, Coinbase x402, EMVCo work ([[EMVCo to develop specifications for agentic payments]]). Multiple players hedge across protocols → no consolidation.

7. **Is the 30+ issuer list "enabled" or "transacting at scale"?** → Mostly **"participated in the programme"** — consistent with controlled test txns, not steady-state production across cardbases.

8. **Passkey adoption numbers?** → **Not published.** No hard figure → treat adoption as unproven.

9. **Does the Cloudflare/Akamai infra dependency create lock-in or a failure mode?** → Plausible: if merchants haven't deployed TAP verification, agents get blocked as bot traffic. Merchant deployment breadth beyond the 4 named = **open**.

10. **Who captures the margin if agentic commerce scales?** → If TAP/Agent Directory becomes the verification default, Visa holds a durable trust-layer toll; if AP2/ACP wins the agent side, Visa reverts to a commoditised authorisation hop. **Open — the central strategic question.**

11. **Does B2B extension have any substance?** → **Announced only** ("being explored"). No named B2B pilots, no numbers.

12. **Is the market size credible?** → Gartner ~20% digital commerce via agents by 2030 / >$15T B2B by 2028; McKinsey ~$5T ([[McKinsey projects agentic commerce could drive $5 trillion by 2030]]). Directional — "agentic" is inconsistently defined; treat TAMs as inflated.

13. **What breaks the thesis?** → Protocol standard war lost; regulator rejects delegated SCA; low consumer adoption of agent checkout; or the agent apps (OpenAI/Google) capture the checkout surface and relegate networks to plumbing.

Importance: 3/5 — Real infrastructure progress from a top-2 network in a strategically pivotal category (agent-verification trust layer), with a named, credible issuer/merchant roster and a plausible margin story. Downgraded from higher because it is a staged re-announcement (not a first — Mastercard led the EU regulated "first"), carries **zero disclosed traction**, names no consumer agent app, and rests on unresolved SCA/protocol-standard risk. Fresh but incremental.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Agentic commerce = AI agents transacting on a user's behalf; the payment-rail layer is the contested part. Sizing is wide and definition-dependent — treat as directional, not a hard TAM: Morgan Stanley (Dec 2025) pegs US agentic e-commerce at **$190bn base / $385bn bull by 2030** (~10–20% of US e-commerce); Bain at **$300–500bn US by 2030** (~15–25% of e-commerce); McKinsey the outlier at **$3–5tn global** ([[McKinsey projects agentic commerce could drive $5 trillion by 2030]]); eMarketer as low as $144bn — a ~35x spread that is a *definition* gap, not a forecasting one (per digitalcommerce360 / Stellagent, as of late 2025). The narrower **agentic-payment infrastructure** slice is what Visa actually sells into: one cited estimate has it growing **$7bn → $93bn by 2032** (via Nevermined, unverified primary — treat as illustrative). Structure: the *rails* layer (Visa/Mastercard) is a consolidated duopoly with network-effect + regulatory moats; the *agent/checkout* layer above it (OpenAI, Google, Perplexity, Amazon) is where consumer distribution and — potentially — margin is migrating. **Why now:** LLM agents crossed the "can complete a checkout" threshold in 2025, and PSD2/SCA forces a network-owned authentication answer (passkeys) that incumbents are better placed to supply than agent apps (analysis).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for the networks: payments/total volume, cross-border volume (highest-yield), processed transactions, net take rate. Key players & basis of competition: **Visa vs Mastercard** compete on the trust/tokenisation standard (TAP + Agent Directory + Payment Passkeys vs Agent Pay + Agentic Tokens), not price; **Stripe+OpenAI (ACP)**, **Google (AP2)**, **Coinbase (x402)** compete to own the *protocol*; **PayPal, Amazon** on merchant/consumer distribution. Recent moves: Santander+Mastercard "Europe's first live end-to-end AI-agent payment" (2 Mar 2026) — Mastercard led the EU regulated "first"; Visa's independent-merchant EU live beat (2 Jul 2026, this pick). **Position:** Visa is a co-leader on rails, not a leader on the agent surface — it names 30+ issuers and 4 merchants but **no consumer agent app** (analysis). Moat = network effects + tokenisation intangibles + issuer relationships; the moat does **not** yet extend to the agent-verification layer, which is still contested.

**Comps & multiples.** The relevant comps are the network duopoly (same model, scale, geo). Arithmetic on P/S (mkt cap / annualised net revenue; multiples/quotes as of ~Jun 2026, per Yahoo/companiesmarketcap):
- **Visa (V):** mkt cap ~$549bn; net revenue run-rate ≈ $11.2bn × 4 = **~$44.8bn** (Q2 FY26 annualised) → P/S ≈ 549 / 44.8 ≈ **12.3x**; forward P/E **~22–24x**. Growth: net revenue **+17% YoY** in Q2 FY26.
- **Mastercard (MA):** mkt cap ~$430–445bn; FY25 net revenue **$32.8bn** → P/S ≈ 437 / 32.8 ≈ **13.3x**; forward P/E **~25–28x**. Growth: net revenue **+16% YoY** (Q1 2026 +16% reported / +12% cc).
Read: the two trade **in-line** with each other; MA carries a modest premium (~1x P/S, ~2–4 P/E turns) consistent with its slightly higher EPS-growth consensus (~16% vs V ~12%) — a normal growth-multiple relationship, **not** an outlier. Both sit near the top of a payments-network band (P/S ~12–14x reflects ~50%+ operating margins, not richness on its own). **Agentic commerce is not in either multiple** — it is optionality, not modeled revenue (analysis). Internal comps: [[PayPal integrates Mastercard Agent Pay and comes to ChatGPT]], [[Mastercard and Majid Al Futtaim launch agentic payments in UAE]], [[Checkout.com adopts Agentic Commerce Protocol]], [[EMVCo to develop specifications for agentic payments]].

**IR grounding (Visa scale).** Per Visa's Q2 FY26 (quarter ended 31 Mar 2026, 10-Q filed 29 Apr 2026): **net revenue $11.2bn, +17%** (strongest growth since 2022); **payments volume +9% cc**; **cross-border volume +12% cc** (ex-Europe +11%); **processed transactions 66.1bn, +9%**; **~$9.2bn returned to shareholders** in the quarter. This is the scale the agentic layer must move to matter: even an optimistic near-term agentic slice is immaterial vs a ~$45bn-run-rate, 66bn-txn/qtr base — agentic commerce is a **defensive optionality/moat play** (protect the authorisation hop from disintermediation), not a 2026–27 revenue driver. Docs: 10-Q 2026-04-29 (https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1403161/000140316126000079/v-20260331.htm); Q2 FY26 earnings release (https://s1.q4cdn.com/050606653/files/doc_financials/2026/q2/Q2-2026-Earnings-Release_vF.pdf).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Disintermediation / margin capture at the agent layer** — if OpenAI ACP or Google AP2 becomes the default checkout surface, the network is relegated to a commoditised authorisation hop and the trust-layer toll (TAP/Agent Directory) never materialises. Second-order: Visa's fresh +17% growth is *today's* rails business; the agentic thesis is about defending, not growing, that stream.
2. **Regulatory / SCA liability unresolved** — passkeys "support" PSD2 SCA compliance but no EU regulator has blessed a delegated/agent SCA model; PSD3/PSR text does not yet codify agent delegation. If a delegated-authentication txn is later ruled non-SCA-compliant, fraud/chargeback liability allocation between issuer, merchant and agent is untested → real adoption brake.
3. **Standards fragmentation + zero disclosed traction** — TAP vs AP2 vs ACP vs x402 vs EMVCo, with peers (Mastercard, PayPal, Worldpay, Revolut) hedging across multiple protocols; combined with Visa disclosing **no volume/GMV/conversion and no named consumer agent app**, "live in Europe" is a plausible-but-unproven infrastructure milestone, not adoption.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is a **defensive standards land-grab, not a re-rating catalyst** (analysis): the networks are racing to make their token+passkey rail the default agent-trust layer before the protocol war settles, because whoever owns agent-verification in the EU keeps the toll as value migrates upstack. Falsifiable thesis: *Visa's agentic push protects its authorisation economics only if TAP/Agent Directory becomes a cross-network verification default.* Trigger to watch: (a) a named consumer agent app (ChatGPT/Gemini-class) routing EU checkout through Visa rails, and (b) an EU regulator explicitly accepting delegated-passkey SCA. Thesis breaks if AP2/ACP wins the agent side or a regulator rejects delegated SCA.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
**Verdict: BEAT (with raised FY26 guidance).** Visa (NYSE: V) — latest reported quarter is **fiscal Q2 2026, ended 31 Mar 2026** (results 28 Apr 2026; 10-Q filed 29 Apr 2026). Agentic-commerce is optionality/moat-defence, not modeled revenue — the reported quarter is what actually drives the equity.

**Reported figures (fiscal Q2 2026) vs consensus / prior year**
- **Net revenue: $11.23bn, +17% YoY** — strongest growth since 2022 (fastest since 2013 ex post-pandemic rebound & Visa Europe acquisition). Beat consensus (~$10.75bn) by ~**$480M / +5.1%**.
- **Adjusted (non-GAAP) diluted EPS: $3.31, +20% YoY** — beat consensus (~$3.09) by **$0.22 / +7.1%**.
- **Payments volume: $3.7tn, +9%** constant-dollar.
- **Cross-border volume: +11%** constant-dollar (ex-intra-Europe); the highest-yield line.
- **Processed transactions: ~66bn, +9%.**
- **Value-added services (VAS) revenue: $3.3bn, +27%** — now ~30% of net revenue. This is the line the agentic thesis maps to.
- **Capital return: ~$9.2bn** to shareholders in the quarter (incl. **$7.9bn buybacks — largest quarterly repurchase in Visa history**); **new $20bn repurchase authorization**.

**Guidance — RAISED**
- FY2026 net-revenue growth outlook lifted from *high-single-digit to low-double-digit* → **low-double-digit to low-teens**.
- FY2026 adjusted EPS growth outlook lifted from *mid-to-high single digits* → **low teens**.
- Drivers cited: resilient spending, higher cross-border volumes, elevated volatility, strong VAS, lower client incentives. Stock rose on the print.

**Thesis-flags — how agentic-commerce expansion fits the growth narrative**
- The Europe agentic-commerce expansion (Intelligent Commerce / Agentic Ready / Trusted Agent Protocol + Payment Passkeys) is a **new-flows + value-added-services** play, not a near-term revenue line. Against a **~$45bn net-revenue run-rate** and **~66bn processed txns/quarter**, any 2026–27 agentic slice is immaterial → this is **defensive optionality** (protect the authorisation hop / own the agent-verification trust layer) rather than a growth catalyst embedded in guidance.
- Where it *would* show up if it scales is **VAS (+27%, now ~30% of net revenue)** and **new-flows** — the fastest-growing, highest-multiple parts of the mix — which is precisely why Visa is racing to make TAP/Agent Directory the default EU verification standard before a protocol war (AP2/ACP/x402) settles.
- **Risk to the flag:** zero disclosed agentic volume/GMV; no named consumer agent app in the loop; PSD2/SCA treatment of delegated-passkey authentication not yet blessed by an EU regulator. So agentic is a credible moat-extension narrative but currently **unproven traction** — it does not move the reported beat.

**Bottom line:** Fiscal Q2 2026 was a clean **beat on both revenue (+17%) and EPS ($3.31, +20%)** with a **guidance raise** and record buyback — the rails business is accelerating on its own; the European agentic-commerce push is a strategic optionality/defence layer riding on top of that strength, not a driver of the quarter.
