This startup pits dealerships against each other to bid on your used car
Selling a car is a pain. You can take the easy route and use services like Carvana, but you may wind up with thousands of dollars less than your car is worth. Or, you could hope for more by going to a dealership, but the dealer’s offer can vary wildly depending on what they’re looking for, never mind the extra time and effort on your part.
A Los Angeles-based startup called Bidbus has spent the last few years trying to combine the best of those options, making it so sellers don’t need to leave their couch to get dealership-level offers. The company has created a digital marketplace where multiple dealers can bid on a car, a process that results in an average offer that’s about $2,000 to $3,000 higher than what Carvana offers, according to Bidbus’ founders.
Now, looking to scale beyond its initial markets of California and Texas, the startup has raised a $15 million Series A funding round led by early-stage mobility fund Ibex Investors. The round also saw participation from Mucker Capital, FJ Labs, Motley Fool Ventures, Data Point Capital, Walter Ventures, and the Car Dealership Guy’s Yossi Levi.
Bidbus’ co-founder and CEO Duke Yan told TechCrunch in an exclusive interview that he came up with the idea after years spent buying and selling cars on his own. When he tried to help his mom get rid of her car, the offers from dealers were insultingly low, so he put them in a group chat. To his surprise, buyers started bidding up the price. 
“Used-car affordability is not a financing problem. It’s a market efficiency problem. Consumers lack real price discovery for trade-ins, dealers struggle to source quality inventory, and much of the best supply is still trapped in people’s driveways,” Yan told TechCrunch.
It’s a clever combination. Dealerships already buy cars at auctions to fill inventory, so the startup is not pitching a foreign idea. Yan said Bidbus helps dealers by bringing them the most highly-valued used cars, which tend to come from private sellers. The startup takes advantage of the spread between what online sellers are willing to pay and the typically higher dealer payout: a difference that can stretch to thousands of dollars.
But netting sellers more money, even after Bidbus takes a cut, wasn’t enough for Yan. He also wants to make the experience fun, and has taken inspiration from stock-trading apps like Robinhood and social media apps like TikTok.
Once a car is accepted on the platform, dealers only have a few hours to bid. Bidbus shows live bids with the corresponding offers displayed in big font. The expectation, Yan said, is for Bidbus users to share screenshots or videos of this activity to raise awareness of the app and draw in new potential sellers.
“Our vision is to make selling a car as transparent and competitive as trading a stock, where price is determined by market competition rather than a single buyer,” Yan said. 
Growing Bidbus hasn’t been easy, Yan said. The company was bootstrapped in the beginning, and while it got some early traction, he admitted he had to ban one of the platform’s largest dealers.
“At that time, he was the only one that bought so many cars, and so he felt like he could get away with haggling or low-balling,” Yan said. “It hurt at first, but now our platform is better than ever. We have five to eight more dealers like him, buying a lot, upholding the standard [experience] that we want our customers to go through.”
Jeff Peters, the partner at Ibex who led the funding round, said he held back on investing in Bidbus’ seed round mostly because the company was only operating in Los Angeles at the time. But once Bidbus started spreading to new markets, bringing on new customers, and signing dealership groups like Lithia Motors and Penske Automotive, Peters wanted in.
“It seems like this is scalable, and that it’s a universal problem and a universal opportunity, at least across the United States. I think it’s gonna be durable, too, because some of the most durable business models are marketplaces,” he told TechCrunch. 
“At the end of the day, they’re providing value to consumers by giving them $2,000 to $3,000 more for their vehicle, as well as allowing dealers to build up their inventory and access new inventory that they never really had access to before.”
Bidbus has so far helped people sell around 10,000 cars on its platform, and Peters said he sees a lot of upside. Carvana proved that selling a car online can be simple and quick, Peters said. Now that vehicle owners are used to that idea, “I think people will be pretty much looking for the best deal, however they can find it,” he added.

# Context-enrichment: Bidbus raises $15M Series A to let dealerships bid on used cars
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Bidbus (LA-based, founded 2022; CEO Duke Yan) closed a **$15M Series A led by Ibex Investors** (partner Jeff Peters), with Mucker Capital, FJ Labs, Motley Fool Ventures, Data Point Capital, Walter Ventures, and Car Dealership Guy's Yossi Levi participating. No valuation disclosed. This follows a **$3.3M seed (closed July 2025, led by Mucker, ~$3M)** — so the Series A lands ~12 months later. Use of funds: expand beyond CA/TX to a national dealer footprint.

The product is a **consumer-to-dealer (C2B) used-car auction**: a private seller lists a car; vetted dealers bid live for a few hours; the highest offer wins. Claimed outcomes: offers "$2,000–$3,000 higher than Carvana" (PR later says **+$2,340 vs instant-offer platforms**), **~10,000 cars sold to date**, and — per the Feb-2026 seed-milestone PR — **$120M annualized GMV five months after the seed**, **>5,000 vehicles, >189,000 registered users**, and **~20-day turn** vs 90+ days for traditional wholesale auctions.

**Why framed this way / what it reveals:** the pitch is deliberately anchored to Carvana's *instant offer* (a deliberately conservative benchmark — instant-buy quotes are the low end of the market), which makes the "$2–3k more" gap look larger than a comparison vs a real dealer trade-in negotiation would. GMV ($120M) is loud but the **take-rate/net revenue is undisclosed** — a marketplace on a big-ticket item can post huge GMV on thin, undisclosed monetization. "Annualized" GMV run-rated off five months is a flattering base. The genuinely notable de-PR'd metric is **inventory turn (~20 days)** and dealer stickiness, not the headline GMV.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Instant-offer / online used-car (C2B side):** Carvana, CarMax, Vroom (wound down retail 2024), Peddle, CarGurus/CarOffer. These are single-buyer instant quotes — Bidbus's whole wedge is turning that into a multi-bid auction.
- **Dealer-to-dealer wholesale auctions:** **ACV Auctions** (public, NASDAQ:ACVA) and Manheim (Cox Automotive), OPENLANE. Bidbus differs by sourcing supply from *private sellers' driveways* rather than dealer trade-in lots — its founders call this the "trapped supply" thesis.
- **Price-discovery / lead-gen:** TrueCar, Droom (India).
- **Auto-fintech corpus peers (different layer — financing, not marketplace):** [[Kavak raises $300 million Series F led by Andreessen Horowitz]] (LatAm used-car marketplace + financing), [[Octane raises $100M Series F at $1.3B valuation]] (recreational-vehicle lending), [[Lendbuzz files for IPO at around $1.5B valuation]] (auto lending), [[Altis raises R$50 million via FIDC for dealership financing]] (dealer credit).

**Why the landscape is this way / second-order:** the used-car chain has two margin pools — (a) the *transaction/marketplace* spread and (b) *financing*. ACV proved the wholesale-auction model is a durable, public-market-scale business but has struggled to earn a rich multiple (commodity take-rate, cyclical volumes). Bidbus is attacking ACV's *supply-sourcing* problem (dealers are starved for quality used inventory as new-car supply normalizes and off-lease volume shrinks post-2021). The strategic question is whether "auction on private-party cars" is a defensible category or a feature ACV/Carvana/Manheim can bolt on — the moat is liquidity (enough dealers per metro), which is exactly why Bidbus had to *ban* its biggest early dealer to protect two-sided balance.

## [2] Company history / fit
Founded 2022, bootstrapped, LA-only initially → seed $3.3M (Jul 2025) → CA+TX, signed franchise groups **Lithia Motors and Penske Automotive** → Series A $15M (Jul 2026). Origin story: Yan put lowball dealer offers on his mother's car into a group chat and watched dealers bid each other up.

**Why it acts this way:** a two-sided marketplace only works once each metro has enough competing dealers to produce real price discovery — hence the geographically staged rollout (prove liquidity in CA, replicate in TX, then national) rather than a national launch. Signing Lithia/Penske (the two largest US public dealer groups) is the credibility unlock that de-risked the Series A — Ibex's Peters explicitly said he *passed on the seed* precisely because it was LA-only, and came in once multi-market + big-group traction appeared. This is a textbook "prove the flywheel before the growth check" trajectory, not a hype round.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Real novelty:** reframing the C2B used-car transaction from a *single instant quote* into a *live multi-dealer auction on private-party supply*. That combination — private-seller sourcing + real-time competitive bidding + a consumer app — is genuinely differentiated vs both Carvana (single buyer) and ACV (dealer-to-dealer only). The gamified "watch dealers bid" UX (Robinhood/TikTok-inspired, screenshot-shareable) is a CAC play, not a technical moat.

**Traction (numbers, not announcements):** ~10,000 cars sold; $120M annualized GMV (run-rated, 5 mo post-seed); >189,000 registered users; ~20-day turn. These are real and fast — but **all GMV/volume, zero disclosed net revenue, take-rate, gross margin, or contribution economics.** 

**Why value-add is real-but-unproven, one level deeper:** the durable value is the *spread* Bidbus captures between the instant-offer floor and the dealer's true willingness-to-pay. That spread exists only while (a) dealers are supply-starved and (b) consumers don't have easy multi-bid price discovery — i.e. it is **an arbitrage on market inefficiency, and marketplaces that succeed compress the very spread they monetize.** Who captures the margin: today Bidbus takes a cut of the spread; at scale, if dealers coordinate or Carvana/ACV replicate the auction, the spread narrows and Bidbus becomes a thin-take-rate transaction rail — the same commodity-multiple trap ACV sits in. The moat has to be *liquidity density per metro*, and there's no evidence yet it holds outside CA.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Plan: national expansion across major regional dealer networks on the $15M. Sentiment is constructive-but-early — a Series A from a specialist mobility fund (Ibex) plus FJ Labs (marketplace specialists) is a credible cap table, and $15M is a modest, appropriate check for a pre-national marketplace. No valuation, no revenue, no unit economics disclosed → this is a Series A on *traction narrative*, not proven scale economics.

**Why the market may go this way / second-order:** off-lease and trade-in used supply is structurally tight through 2026–27 (weak 2021–22 new-car sales feeding into today's 3-year-old inventory), so dealer appetite for a fresh private-party supply channel is real *right now* — timing is favorable. Counterintuitive risk: the tailwind is cyclical. If used-car supply loosens (new-car production normalizes, rates fall), dealer desperation for Bidbus's supply eases and the arbitrage spread compresses — the model is strongest exactly when the market is tightest, which makes revenue **pro-cyclical and fragile**, not defensive. Second risk: incumbent replication (Carvana adding a bid feature, ACV extending to private-party) is the real disintermediation threat, not a new startup.

**Top challenge / red-team questions (second-order)**

1. **Take-rate / net revenue?** $120M annualized GMV is loud, but what fee does Bidbus charge per transaction and what is net revenue? — *open (undisclosed; the single most important missing number).*
2. **Is "+$2,340" honest?** The gap is measured vs *instant-offer* platforms (the deliberate low end), not vs a hard dealer trade-in negotiation. Against a motivated dealer, is the delta still that large? — *likely overstated; benchmark is cherry-picked.*
3. **Is GMV run-rated off 5 months?** "$120M annualized" from a 5-month window flatters a fast-ramping base — what is trailing actual GMV? — *open; annualization inflates.*
4. **Liquidity density outside CA?** The auction only works with enough competing dealers per metro. Proven in LA/CA — is it replicated in TX or just launched? — *open; TX traction unquantified.*
5. **Dealer concentration risk.** Bidbus had to ban its single largest dealer for lowballing; now "5–8 more like him." How concentrated is demand, and what happens if a few big dealers leave? — *partially open; concentration is real.*
6. **Cyclicality.** The value-add is an arbitrage on tight used-car supply. When supply loosens (rates fall / new-car output normalizes), does the spread — and Bidbus's revenue — compress? — *structural risk; model is pro-cyclical.*
7. **Incumbent replication.** What stops Carvana adding a multi-bid feature or ACV extending to private-party sourcing? Auction UX is not patent-defensible. — *open; main disintermediation threat.*
8. **Are Lithia/Penske live buyers or logos?** Signed dealer groups named — are they actively bidding at volume, or announced relationships? — *open; "signed" ≠ transacting.*
9. **Consumer trust / inspection liability.** Who inspects the car, who bears condition disputes and title/fraud risk between private seller and dealer? — *open; silent in coverage.*
10. **Valuation.** No Series A valuation disclosed — flat, up, or down vs seed? Silence usually means not-flattering. — *open.*
11. **CAC vs the "gamified/shareable" thesis.** The TikTok/Robinhood UX is a customer-acquisition bet; what is actual CAC and organic share of new sellers? — *open; unproven.*
12. **Gross margin per car.** Even at +$2,340 seller upside, after Bidbus's cut and ops (logistics, inspection, payments), what is contribution per transaction? — *open.*
13. **Regulatory.** Dealer-licensing / DMV title-transfer rules vary by state; does the C2B auction model need dealer or auction licensing state-by-state, slowing national rollout? — *open; a real friction for "national expansion."*
14. **Defensibility vs corpus peers.** Kavak/Carvana show marketplace + captive financing is where durable margin sits — will Bidbus need to add financing to escape the commodity take-rate trap? — *analysis; likely yes at scale.*
15. **Duplicate check.** Is the $15M Series A already covered? No prior Bidbus note in corpus; the $3.3M seed (Jul 2025) is a distinct earlier round not in the corpus. — *fresh, not a re-run.*

**Importance: 2/5** — Genuinely differentiated model (private-party C2B auction) with fast, real early traction (10k cars, ~20-day turn) and a credible marketplace-specialist cap table. But: sub-scale, US-only, single-region-proven, no disclosed revenue/take-rate/valuation, a small ($15M) Series A, pro-cyclical value-add, and high replication risk from Carvana/ACV. A niche auto-marketplace round of low systemic fintech weight — noteworthy as a model, not as a market-mover.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Consumer-to-dealer (C2D) used-car sourcing sitting on top of the dealer wholesale-auction stack. Structure: the wholesale layer is a public near-duopoly of physical lanes (Manheim/Cox, ADESA/OPENLANE) plus digital challenger ACV Auctions; the *consumer acquisition* layer where Bidbus plays is far more fragmented and still forming. Value in the chain is captured by whoever controls sourcing of quality inventory — "supply trapped in driveways" per the note — because dealers are structurally short of good used stock. Drivers / "why now": (1) CarMax + Carvana already buy ~2M cars/yr direct from consumers, ~13% of the market, and have normalised the "instant digital offer" behaviour Bidbus rides on (per news.dealershipguy.com, Oct 2025); (2) incumbents are actively moving into dealer-to-dealer/peer sourcing — Cars.com acquired dealer auction DealerClub in 2025 (SEC 8-K) — signalling the sourcing layer is the contested prize. No verifiable clean TAM for the C2D niche specifically → no data; don't infer one from used-car-market totals.

**Competitive landscape.** Marketplace KPIs that matter here: GMV, take rate, active dealers/buyers, liquidity (bids per listing). Bidbus disclosed: ~$120M annualized GMV, ~10,000 cars sold cumulative, ~189,000 registered users, live in CA + TX, dealer groups Lithia and Penske signed (TechCrunch, finsmes, PYMNTS, 2026-07-07). Take rate NOT disclosed → [UNSOURCED]. Players / basis of competition: ACV Auctions (B2B wholesale, digital) competes on liquidity/data; Carvana/CarMax (instant offer) compete on convenience/price certainty; Manheim/OPENLANE on scale of lanes; Bidbus differentiates on *price discovery via live multi-dealer bidding* directly to the consumer — a genuinely different auction geometry (many dealers bidding UP one consumer car, vs dealers buying down). Position: niche new entrant, ahead on the specific C2D-auction mechanic but tiny and geographically boxed in (2 states). Moat `(analysis)`: prospective marketplace network effect (more dealers → higher bids → more sellers), but not yet proven at scale — the founder already had to ban his single largest dealer for low-balling, which reveals thin liquidity (a marketplace where one participant can move the clearing price is not yet a real market).

**Comps & multiples.** Best public comp = **[[ACV Auctions]]**-type wholesale marketplace (ACVA, NYSE): mkt cap ~$1.3bn @ $7.43 (2026-06); FY25 revenue ~$758M; Marketplace GMV $10.4bn. Arithmetic: P/S = $1.3bn / $0.758bn = **~1.7x**; mkt-cap/GMV = $1.3bn / $10.4bn = **~0.12x**. ACVA fell **~62% in 2025** (macrotrends), i.e. the market is currently pricing digital used-car marketplaces *cheaply* despite double-digit revenue growth — a caution signal, not a tailwind, for a startup selling a growth story. Applying ACVA's ~0.12x mkt-cap/GMV to Bidbus's $120M GMV = ~$14M — implying a Series A round roughly equal to a public-comp-implied enterprise value, i.e. private-market pricing looks rich vs the listed peer `(analysis)`. Bidbus post-money valuation NOT disclosed → the multiple itself is "no data"; the $14M is only an illustrative anchor, not the deal price. Internal comps from base: **[[Kavak raises $300 million Series F led by Andreessen Horowitz]]** (online used-car marketplace + financing, LatAm), **[[US fintech Lendbuzz prepares for Nasdaq IPO]]** (auto lending, ~$1–1.5bn IPO range). Fewer than 3 comparable GMV/revenue pairs → distribution not computed; qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Thin two-sided liquidity / dealer concentration.** By the founder's own admission one dealer dominated volume and had to be banned; with "five to eight" large dealers now, the bid side is concentrated — collusion or withdrawal by a few dealers would collapse the price-discovery promise (the entire value prop) and the $2–3k premium vs Carvana.
2. **Disintermediation by incumbents.** Carvana/CarMax already own the consumer relationship and instant-offer habit; Cars.com/ACV are buying into sourcing. A well-capitalised incumbent can bolt a "let dealers bid" feature onto existing consumer traffic, capturing Bidbus's mechanic without its cold-start problem.
3. **Cyclical + geographic concentration.** Live in only CA+TX; used-car pricing and dealer appetite are highly cyclical (rate-sensitive, tied to subprime auto credit stress already visible at Carvana). A tightening cycle compresses both dealer bids and the consumer premium that justifies the platform.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is a *new-entry* bet on a specific mechanic (consumer-facing reverse auction), not a re-rating of the sector — and it lands while the listed comp (ACVA) is down ~62%, so the exit math depends on the private premium holding. Falsifiable thesis: if Bidbus's per-listing bid depth and take rate hold as it leaves CA/TX, the network effect is real and it is a credible acquisition target for an incumbent short on sourcing. What breaks it: GMV growth that comes from adding dealers rather than deepening bids per car (breadth without liquidity) — watch bids-per-listing and dealer-concentration, not headline GMV. Trigger to watch: first multi-state expansion metrics or an incumbent (ACV/Cars.com/Carvana) launching a competing C2D-auction feature.
