Acquisition expected to deepen Global-e’s standard logistics capabilities and improve the post-purchase experience for merchants and shoppers

*   _Acquisition focuses on asset-light carrier capabilities and offering of competitive best in class cross-border, domestic and “last mile delivery” standard logistics, supporting sophisticated solutions such as direct injection, consolidated returns and customs brokerage_

*   _Will enable expansion of global offering to merchant segments that require a Non-Merchant of Record solution, leveraging the existing Passport platform_

PETAH-TIKVA, Israel, May 26, 2026 (GLOBE NEWSWIRE) -- Global-e Online Ltd. (NASDAQ: GLBE) (“Global-e” or the “Company”), the platform powering global direct-to-consumer e-commerce, today announced that it has entered into a definitive agreement (the “Agreement”) to acquire Passport Global Inc. (“Passport”), a US-based cross-border e-commerce logistics and solutions company.

The acquisition of Passport will further strengthen Global-e’s logistics capabilities, adding advanced standard shipping solutions powered by an efficient, asset-light, multi-carrier network across cross-border, domestic, and last-mile deliveries. The acquisition will also enhance Global-e’s ability to execute direct injection and consolidated returns, supporting the delivery of scalable and cost-effective logistics solutions.

Passport will also continue to provide a non-Merchant of Record (non-MoR) solution, expanding Global-e’s offering to a broader and more diverse range of merchant segments.

The acquisition is expected to strengthen Global-e’s value proposition by increasing control over critical logistics workflows, including fulfillment, delivery, and returns. We believe this unified experience will enhance the Global-e offering, providing merchants with end-to-end service delivery, including duty clearance, reducing delays and discrepancies, and facilitating customs brokerage.

“We are excited to welcome Passport to the Global-e family,” said Nir Debbi, President of Global-e. “This acquisition represents a significant step forward in our strategy to deepen our logistics and trade compliance capabilities to deliver a seamless, end-to-end logistics experience for our merchants and their customers. By integrating Passport’s advanced cross-border logistics infrastructure and multi-carrier network, we plan to extend our suite of advanced shipping solutions, increase standard shipping reliability, and enhance the post-purchase experience. We believe this combination will further differentiate Global-e’s platform and reinforce our position as the leading enabler of global e-commerce.”

“Joining Global-e marks an exciting new chapter for Passport,” said Alex Yancher, Founder and CEO of Passport. “We built our company to simplify and optimize cross-border logistics, and partnering with Global-e allows us to accelerate that mission on a global scale. Together, we will be able to deliver more efficient shipping solutions, improve transparency, and reduce friction in international trade for merchants and customers alike. We are thrilled to combine our expertise with Global-e’s platform to drive innovation in cross-border e-commerce, and create even greater value for our merchants and their customers.”

Global-e agreed to acquire Passport for $350 million in upfront consideration, funded via approximately equal portions of cash on hand and ordinary shares of Global-e. An additional contingent consideration of up to $75 million is subject to Passport achieving certain financial results in 2026.

Following the closing, Passport’s founder and CEO Alex Yancher is expected to join the Global-e executive management team to lead the integrated Passport business.

Passport is expected to generate approximately $100 million in revenues in calendar year 2026 with a rapid growth rate which is expected to slightly exceed that of Global-e for the year. The acquisition is expected to have a neutral to slightly positive effect on Global-e’s Adjusted EBITDA contribution in H2 2026. The transaction is expected to close in early July 2026, subject to customary closing conditions, including regulatory approvals.

**About Global-e**

**About Passport**

Passport is a cross-border logistics and e-commerce enablement platform helping brands simplify international shipping and scale globally with confidence. # Context-enrichment: Global-e acquires cross-border logistics startup Passport for $350M
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Freshness: FRESH.** Primary source (Global-e/GLOBE NEWSWIRE) dated **26 May 2026**; deal **closed 1 July 2026**; digest surfaced it via StrictlyVC on 2026-07-02. The only prior Global-e M&A note in the corpus is [[Global-e acquires Israeli returns startup ReturnGo]] (Aug 2025) — a **different, smaller deal** (AI returns), not a reprint. Not a duplicate.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Global-e (NASDAQ: GLBE) acquired **Passport Global Inc.** — a San Francisco cross-border e-commerce logistics company (founded ~2017 by Alex Yancher; "9 years old") — for **$350M upfront + up to $75M earnout** (max ~$425M). Upfront is funded via *"approximately equal portions of cash on hand and Global-e ordinary shares"* → ~$175M cash / ~$175M GLBE stock. Close was guided "early July" and delivered on schedule (1 July 2026).
- **Why structured this way (half-stock + earnout):** the ~50% stock component conserves cash and aligns the seller to GLBE equity; the $75M earnout is tied to *"certain financial results in 2026."* Note the skew — the earnout measures **full-year 2026** while GLBE only owns Passport for H2. That is GLBE-favorable: it protects the buyer if Passport underperforms post-close, and defers ~18% of headline value.
- **What Passport actually is (minus PR):** an **asset-light, multi-carrier** cross-border logistics + solutions layer — customs brokerage/DDP duty-and-tax at checkout, direct injection, consolidated returns, last-mile via third-party carriers. ~**1,000 merchants**, ships to **220+ markets**. Crucially a **non-Merchant-of-Record (non-MoR)** model — the opposite of GLBE's core MoR model.
- **Numbers:** Passport ~**$100M 2026E revenue**, growing "slightly faster" than GLBE. Deal is ~**3.5x 2026E revenue** on the upfront (~4.25x with full earnout) — modest, consistent with an asset-light logistics business, not a SaaS multiple.
- **Profitability — carefully hedged:** the release only claims *"neutral to slightly positive"* effect on GLBE **Adjusted EBITDA in H2 2026** — a narrow, non-GAAP, half-year framing. It does **not** disclose Passport's standalone profitability. Read as near-breakeven at best (analysis).
- Founder **Alex Yancher** joins GLBE's exec team as CEO of the Passport division.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Passport's direct rivals (cross-border logistics + DDP):** FlavorCloud, Easyship, Zonos (more landed-cost SaaS), **ESW** (ex-eShopWorld), and **Flow** — now itself part of Global-e. Digital River historically offered a non-MoR global-seller option.
- **MoR vs non-MoR:** GLBE's core model is Merchant-of-Record (GLBE becomes seller of record, absorbs tax/compliance/fraud liability; **Reach** is a notable MoR peer). Passport is deliberately **non-MoR** — framed as *additive*, letting GLBE address enterprise merchants who want to stay their own seller of record, a segment MoR doesn't fit.
- **Position:** GLBE consistently **acquires** cross-border competitors rather than out-competing them (Flow 2021, Borderfree 2022). Passport continues that consolidation playbook; it buys customs/DDP competence GLBE previously orchestrated via third parties.
- **Second-order:** the multiple gap (3.5x logistics vs. GLBE's own software-driven premium) means the deal is *margin-dilutive by mix* even if EBITDA-neutral — logistics revenue carries lower gross margin than GLBE's ~47% non-GAAP blended (analysis; watch FY26 gross-margin guide).

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Passport funding (~$54M total):** seed ~$3M (Oct 2018, Resolute/Precursor + angels), Series A $12M (Dec 2020, led M13), Series B $39M (25 Jan 2022, led **TCV**; Flexport, FJ Labs joined). A ~$200M Series-B post-money is cited only by third-party DBs (Crunchbase/Tracxn) — **unverified**.
- **Global-e trajectory (dated):** FY2025 (20-F filed 26 Mar 2026) GMV **$6,569M** (+35%), revenue **$962.2M** (+28%), first full-year net income **$68.3M** (from a $75.5M 2024 loss), Adj. EBITDA **$198.5M**. Q1 2026 GMV +40%, revenue $252.1M (+33%). Shopify is a strategic partner/warrant-holder. Prior M&A: **Flow (2021), Borderfree (2022), ReturnGo (2025)** — see [[Global-e acquires Israeli returns startup ReturnGo]].
- **Why GLBE acts this way (structural logic):** GLBE historically sat in the software/MoR/checkout layer and *resold* third-party logistics. To lift unit economics it needs to **own more of the shipping/customs margin** rather than pass it through → Passport pushes GLBE down the stack into owned customs brokerage + direct injection + returns, rounding out an end-to-end stack (post-ReturnGo). This is the same "own the margin, earn a software multiple on more of the flow" pressure that drives take-rate-constrained platforms.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Traction is real but mid-scale:** 1,000+ merchants, 220+ markets, ~$100M 2026E revenue — a fraction of GLBE's ~$1.2B FY26 revenue guide. Growth "slightly above" GLBE (~high-30s%).
- **Novelty is modest:** cross-border DDP/customs is crowded (Zonos, FlavorCloud, ESW, Easyship). Passport's edges: asset-light multi-carrier network + customs-broker capability + non-MoR channel + returns/direct-injection depth.
- **Where the value-add is real:** the timely angle is **regulatory** — the 2025–26 end of the US **de minimis / Section 321** exemption (duties now apply to sub-$800 parcels since 29 Aug 2025; indefinite suspension per Feb 2026 proclamation / 24 Jun 2026 Federal Register) makes customs-clearance/DDP competence structurally more valuable. That is exactly Passport's toolset.
- **Skeptical caveat:** the "owned fulfillment / logistics" framing **overstates** what an asset-light, multi-carrier orchestrator delivers — GLBE gains customs brokerage + tech + carrier network, not trucks/warehouses. Who captures the margin: GLBE now captures more of the shipping economics it previously resold, but the same de-minimis repeal that helps the *toolset* is a **headwind to cross-border parcel volumes** (higher landed cost suppresses low-margin demand) — so it can cap GMV growth for both.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Sentiment: cautiously positive / priced-in.** Sell-side reiterated/upgraded Buy (Truist to Buy; Benchmark, Needham, Jefferies Buy, ~$40 PT); GLBE **stock traded roughly flat** on announcement — a sensible bolt-on, not a re-rating catalyst.
- **Watch:** (1) whether the $75M earnout triggers on FY2026 results; (2) blended gross-margin dilution from logistics mix; (3) ~3% dilution from the ~$175M stock component (minor vs ~$5.8–6.1B cap); (4) integration — GLBE's Borderfree monetization only ramped in early 2026 (~4 yrs), so synergies can be slow; (5) whether non-MoR reach cannibalizes GLBE's higher-take MoR economics.
- **Counterintuitive second-order:** the de-minimis repeal is a *double-edged* driver — bullish for Passport's customs competence, bearish for the cross-border volumes that competence serves. Net weight of the deal rests less on "is there a logistics story" than on **whether owning customs/DDP offsets the volume drag it is partly a response to.**

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Cross-border e-commerce enablement — the software+logistics layer that lets brands sell D2C internationally (localized checkout, duty/tax/customs, FX, cross-border shipping & returns). Underlying cross-border e-commerce GMV is large and growing double-digit: estimates diverge widely by firm — e.g. ~$1.34tn in 2026 → $4.30tn by 2035 at ~13.8% CAGR (per Cognitive Market Research) vs ~$636bn in 2026 → ~$2.0tn by 2034 at ~15.4% CAGR (per Precedence Research); treat the absolute number as `[UNSOURCED]`/soft (secondary market-report figures, definitions differ), but the ~13–15% CAGR direction is consistent. Structure: fragmented on the enablement side — a handful of full-stack Merchant-of-Record (MoR) players (Global-e, ESW) vs modular/non-MoR tools (Flow/BigCommerce, Reach, Zonos) and pure logistics (Passport, Flexport). Value concentrates where duty/tax/customs complexity + returns + localized conversion meet; barriers = regulatory/compliance breadth (200+ destinations), carrier network scale and enterprise switching costs. Why now: tariff/customs volatility and de-minimis changes raise the value of managed duty/brokerage; Global-e is buying logistics (Passport) to internalize a cost center it previously outsourced — a vertical-integration move down the stack.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: GMV, take rate (revenue/GMV), adj-EBITDA margin, merchant count/NRR. Players & basis of competition: Global-e (full-stack MoR, ~1,500 brands) vs ESW (enterprise MoR, luxury/apparel, 200+ markets) — compete on cost-to-serve, localized checkout, integrated logistics+returns; Flow.io (BigCommerce, modular/non-MoR), Reach, Zonos (landed-cost SaaS) attack the modular flank; Passport, Flexport, standard carriers on pure logistics. Recent moves: Global-e acquired ReturnGo (returns, Aug 2025) [[Global-e acquires Israeli returns startup ReturnGo]] and now Passport (logistics + non-MoR, announced May 26 2026, close early-July 2026). Passport's investor list (TCV, Flexport, Kleiner Perkins, M13) confirms it as a bona-fide VC-backed logistics platform, not a shell. Protagonist position: **ahead / leader** in full-stack MoR (analysis); moat = compliance breadth + enterprise switching costs + now owned multi-carrier logistics/returns (vertical integration into post-purchase). The Passport deal also opens the **non-MoR** segment Global-e previously did not serve — a distribution/TAM extension, not just cost synergy.

**Comps & multiples.** Global-e (NASDAQ:GLBE): mkt cap ~$5.37bn (as of Apr 2, 2026, per companiesmarketcap.com); TTM revenue ~$0.88bn → **P/S ≈ $5.37bn / $0.88bn = 6.1x** (mkt-cap proxy, not clean EV; GLBE holds net cash so EV/Rev is lower — `[UNSOURCED]` clean EV). On FY2026E revenue guidance midpoint ~$1.25bn → **fwd P/S ≈ $5.37bn / $1.25bn = 4.3x**. Take rate (Q1 2026) = revenue/GMV = $252M / $1.74bn ≈ **14.5%**. Growth context: Q1 2026 GMV +40% YoY, revenue +33% YoY, adj EBITDA +59% to $50.2M ("Rule of 50 again", raised outlook — per IR 6-K 2026-05-13). At ~6x trailing / ~4.3x forward P/S against ~30% revenue growth + expanding EBITDA margin, GLBE looks **in-line-to-reasonable** for a profitable ~30%-grower (not a range outlier vs the ~0.5–20x EV/Rev band; multiple broadly justified by growth). Passport deal metrics: $350M upfront (+ up to $75M earnout) for ~$100M 2026E revenue → **acquisition P/S ≈ $350M / $100M = 3.5x** (upfront-only; 4.25x incl. full earnout) — struck below GLBE's own trailing multiple, consistent with logistics carrying lower-margin, lower-multiple revenue than platform SaaS. Internal comps: [[Global-e acquires Israeli returns startup ReturnGo]] (post-purchase bolt-on, no disclosed price). External MoR peer ESW is private → no public multiple, `[UNSOURCED]`.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Margin dilution / mix shift** — Passport's ~$100M revenue is asset-light logistics, structurally lower-margin than platform take-rate revenue; management flags only "neutral to slightly positive" adj-EBITDA effect in H2 2026, so blended gross margin and take rate likely compress even as GMV grows. Watch reported take rate drifting down as logistics revenue scales.
2. **Integration & retention risk** — second acquisition in <12 months (ReturnGo, then Passport); Passport founder joins mgmt, but "failure to integrate / realize synergies" is explicitly cited in GLBE's own forward-looking risk factors. Execution, not thesis, is the swing factor.
3. **Earnout/valuation & tariff exposure** — $75M contingent on 2026 financials adds pay-up if Passport over-delivers; and the whole cross-border logistics/customs business is directly exposed to tariff, de-minimis and export-control regime changes (a driver that cuts both ways — more duty complexity helps the brokerage pitch but adds volume/volatility risk).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Consolidation signal: enablement leaders are internalizing logistics + returns + non-MoR to own the full post-purchase stack — Global-e is buying its way from "checkout + compliance" toward "checkout-to-doorstep," pressuring pure-play cross-border logistics (Passport-likes, Zonos) and modular tools to either scale or get absorbed (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if the Passport deal is accretive, GLBE take rate holds ≥~14% and FY2026 GMV guidance ($8.53–8.88bn) is met/raised again → re-rating justified; thesis breaks if H2 2026 take rate or gross margin steps down materially or integration slips guidance. Trigger to watch: Q3 2026 print (first full quarter with Passport consolidated) and any updated take-rate/EBITDA-margin bridge.

- GMV: **$1,742M, +40% YoY** (key operating metric).

Fulfillment-services revenue ($131.3M) now slightly exceeds service-fees revenue ($120.8M) — mix continues to tilt toward the lower-margin fulfillment line, yet gross margin still expanded 160bps and AEBITDA margin +330bps, i.e. operating leverage is outrunning the mix drag. Shopify Managed Markets v2.0 adoption rising (Canada early access, UK to follow); Duty Drawback offering expanded — both feed the Passport logistics thesis.

**vs expectations / prior period.** Revenue $252.1M vs public consensus ~$250.8M → beat +$1.3M / +0.9% (a whisker; call it in-line). Non-GAAP EPS ~$0.27 vs consensus ~$0.18 → clear profit beat, driven by the AEBITDA over-delivery, not by revenue. YoY momentum: GMV +40% is an acceleration/steady vs FY2025 trend; AEBITDA +59% > revenue +33% confirms the leverage story. - GMV: **$8,530–$8,880M** (was $8,450–$8,800M) — raised ~$80M at both ends.
- Revenue: **$1,220–$1,280M** (was $1,211–$1,271M) — raised ~$9M.

Management tone confident; Global-e has a history of guiding "at or above top end." No margin guidance withdrawn; no metric cut.

**Thesis-flags.**
1. **Operating leverage is real and accelerating** (AEBITDA +59% on revenue +33%, margin +330bps). Fact → the model scales faster than volume → validates the "Rule of 50" durability → second-order: supports the ~$425M Passport outlay (funded ~50/50 cash + shares) without derailing FY profitability (deal guided neutral-to-slightly-positive to H2'26 AEBITDA).
2. **Mix drift to fulfillment** (fulfillment revenue > service-fees revenue; GMV raise >> revenue raise). Why it matters → blended take-rate compresses; margin gains must come from cost/leverage not price → Passport (asset-light multi-carrier logistics, ~$100M CY2026 revenue) deepens this lower-take-rate, logistics-heavy mix. Watch whether gross margin holds as fulfillment scales.
3. **Guidance raised but revenue beat was thin** (+0.9%). The stock reportedly dipped pre-market despite the beat/raise — the market wanted a bigger top-line surprise; analyst PTs were mixed post-print (Truist upgraded to Buy, PT $34→$39; Jefferies kept Buy but cut PT $50→$40). De-PR: management leads with GMV/AEBITDA/"Rule of 50," is quieter on the FCF-still-negative and the modest revenue upside.
4. **Passport contingency** (up to $75M earn-out on 2026 targets; ~half the $350M upfront in GLBE shares). Adds integration + share-dilution risk on top of an already logistics-tilting mix.
