Kraken to pursue European banking licence via Lithuania
Sinziana Albu
08 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Kraken has reportedly sought a full banking licence in Lithuania, aiming to expand its offering beyond cryptocurrency trading.
According to CoinDesk , cryptocurrency exchange Kraken, which is preparing for an initial public offering in the US, is reportedly pursuing a full banking licence in Europe, with Lithuania identified as the preferred jurisdiction. Kraken has declined to comment on the matter, and the Bank of Lithuania has said that licensing processes for financial market participants remain confidential.
If granted, the company would be following a route already taken by Revolut, which received a specialised European banking licence from the Bank of Lithuania in 2018. That licence allowed Revolut to offer current accounts, consumer lending, and stock trading across the European Economic Area (EEA). Other companies holding banking or specialised banking licences in Lithuania include Revolut Bank, Mano Bank, PayRay, European Merchant Bank (EMBank), AB Fjord Bank, and Saldo Bank.
Part of a broader global licensing strategy
The pursuit of a European banking licence forms part of a wider effort by Payward, Kraken's parent company, to secure additional regulatory approvals across multiple jurisdictions. In March 2026, Kraken Financial became the first digital asset bank to gain access to the US Federal Reserve's payment infrastructure, allowing it to operate on the same settlement rails as traditional financial institutions. In May 2026, Payward secured an authorisation from the UAE's Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority (VARA).
Speaking at Money 20/20 Europe, Kraken CEO Arjun Sethi mentioned that the company intends to pursue banking licences across multiple regions over the next ten years, either by acquiring existing licensed entities or by applying directly for licences in individual markets.
A full banking licence in Lithuania would, subject to regulatory approval, allow Kraken to expand its offering beyond cryptocurrency trading to include services such as deposit-taking and lending. The move also reflects a broader pattern of cryptocurrency firms seeking closer integration with regulated financial infrastructure as the sector matures under evolving frameworks, including the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation.

## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
**[0] TL;DR.** According to a CoinDesk report (7 Jul 2026), crypto exchange [[Kraken to pursue European banking licence via Lithuania|Kraken]] (parent Payward) is reportedly pursuing a **full banking licence in Europe, with Lithuania as the target jurisdiction**. If granted, Kraken would become the only crypto exchange holding a full EU banking licence, letting it move beyond trading into deposit-taking and lending. Kraken declined to comment; the Bank of Lithuania cited confidentiality. This is the EU banking-charter leg of a broader multi-jurisdiction "become-a-bank" strategy Kraken is running ahead of its planned US IPO.

**[1] What's actually new vs. already-known.**
- NEW: The specific EU **banking-licence** ambition and **Lithuania** as the chosen jurisdiction (first surfaced 7 Jul 2026 via CoinDesk; relayed by The Paypers 8 Jul). This is a step *up* from Kraken's existing EU crypto authorisations — a full credit-institution charter, not another CASP/EMI licence.
- KNOWN CONTEXT: Kraken is already **MiCA-authorised via the Central Bank of Ireland** (Jun 2025) covering all 30 EEA states, holds a **MiFID licence via Cyprus**, and holds EMI licences. So the banking licence is additive, not a fix for lacking EU access.
- KNOWN: Kraken has been aggressively stacking charters — [[Kraken parent Payward applies for OCC national trust charter|OCC national trust charter (May 2026)]], its Wyoming SPDI + Fed master account (Kraken Financial, first digital-asset bank on Fed rails), [[Kraken secures VARA authorization to expand in UAE|UAE VARA authorisation]], [[Kraken parent Payward secures British Virgin Islands license|BVI licence]]. CEO Arjun Sethi has publicly framed this as acquiring/building banking licences across regions over ~10 years.

**[2] Why Lithuania.** Lithuania is the EU's dominant fintech licensing hub: the Bank of Lithuania has processed more EMI/PI applications than any other member state since 2017 (200+ licensed EMIs/PIs), communicates in English, runs a fast (3–6 month) digital process, and pioneered the **"specialised bank" licence** — a full CRD/CRR credit institution needing only **EUR 1m initial capital** (vs the EUR 5m CRD minimum elsewhere), with a full EU banking passport and deposit-guarantee coverage, at the cost of a scope restriction on investment/securities activities. The precedent is explicit: **[[Kraken launches Mastercard for UK and European users|Revolut]] got a specialised banking licence from the Bank of Lithuania in 2018** (later upgraded), which is exactly the template Kraken would follow. Other Lithuanian-licensed banks: Mano Bank, PayRay, European Merchant Bank (EMBank), Fjord Bank, Saldo Bank, SME Bank.

**[3] Competitive/strategic frame.**
- **IPO signalling.** Kraken filed confidentially for a [[Kraken files confidentially for US IPO at $20B|US IPO (~$20B valuation, Nov 2025)]] though a listing may [[Kraken cuts 150 jobs after AI deployment; IPO may slip to 2027|slip toward 2027]]. A bank charter is a legitimacy/diversification story that de-risks the equity narrative (recurring net-interest income, "regulated financial institution" positioning) rather than pure trading-fee cyclicality.
- **Peer path divergence.** Most rivals stopped at **MiCA CASP licences**, not bank charters: [[Coinbase]] via Luxembourg CSSF, Gemini/OKX/Crypto.com/Gate via Malta, [[Bitpanda]] via Austria FMA + Luxembourg. Kraken pursuing a *full bank* licence is a differentiator — no crypto exchange currently holds one in the EU. The clear analogue is neobank [[Revolut]], which crossed from EMI to bank.
- **Org build-out corroborates intent.** Kraken has staffed up European regulated operations: [[Kraken hires regtech expert Andreas Roussos to lead Cyprus unit|hired a regtech veteran for its Cyprus unit]] and [[Kraken promotes Stavros Vassiliades to COO of EU business|promoted a CFD/FX veteran to COO of EU business]]. It also bought [[Kraken parent Payward to acquire Reap for up to $600 million|Reap]] for payments/stablecoin rails.

**[4] Watch-items / open questions.** (a) Application not confirmed by Kraken or the Bank of Lithuania — this remains reported/unconfirmed. (b) Which licence class — full universal bank or the lighter **specialised bank** (more likely given Revolut precedent and capital efficiency)? (c) Timeline: Lithuanian bank licensing typically runs ~12 months+ and is ECB-involved for full banks, so any grant is well out. (d) Interaction with MiCA/AML tightening — the Bank of Lithuania has been enforcing MiCA on VASPs and has a mixed history of AML scrutiny on fast-tracked fintechs. (e) Does a deposit-taking crypto bank invite heavier prudential/capital treatment that offsets the strategic upside?

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
**Red-team questions**

1. Is there any confirmation beyond a single CoinDesk sourced-to-"person familiar" report? Both Kraken and the Bank of Lithuania declined/cited confidentiality — could this be a trial balloon or IPO-narrative planting rather than a filed application?
2. Full universal bank vs. Lithuanian "specialised bank" — the note/coverage conflates these. Which is Kraken actually seeking? The capital, scope and passporting implications differ materially.
3. Does Kraken already have enough EU reach via its MiCA (Ireland) + MiFID (Cyprus) + EMI stack that a bank licence adds mostly optics, not new addressable market?
4. What deposit-taking/lending revenue is realistically incremental, and is it worth the prudential capital, liquidity (LCR/NSFR) and CRD/CRR compliance burden of being a credit institution?
5. Timeline reality: full bank authorisation in Lithuania involves the ECB and typically takes 12+ months. Is any grant plausibly pre-IPO, or is this a multi-year story?
6. Revolut analogy — is it apt? Revolut is a neobank with retail current accounts; Kraken is a crypto exchange. Does a crypto-heavy balance sheet face very different regulator/ECB scrutiny than Revolut did in 2018?
7. Lithuania's fintech-hub reputation has drawn AML criticism in the past. Could reputational/AML risk to the Bank of Lithuania slow or block a crypto-native applicant?
8. How does a deposit-guarantee-covered "crypto bank" square with volatile crypto assets on/around the balance sheet — is deposit-taking ring-fenced from trading?
9. Is the ~10-year "banking licences across regions" framing from Sethi strategy or vaporware? What concrete progress backs it (OCC trust charter still pending, Wyoming SPDI live)?
10. Could Kraken instead *acquire* an existing Lithuanian-licensed bank (Sethi flagged acquisition as an option) rather than apply de novo — which would change the story entirely?
11. Competitive read: no crypto exchange holds a full EU bank licence today. Is Kraken genuinely ahead, or is being first a signal that the economics/regulatory cost deter peers for good reason?
12. Does the July 2026 MiCA full-enforcement backdrop actually pressure a *bank* licence, or is that unrelated (MiCA is satisfied by the CASP licence Kraken already holds)?
13. Impact on the IPO: does bank-holding-company status add regulatory complexity/consolidated supervision that could complicate a US listing?
14. Source-quality check: The Paypers note is a secondary relay of CoinDesk; is there any primary-document (application, Bank of Lithuania record) corroboration? No — confidential.

**Importance: 3/5** — A credible, well-corroborated strategic signal that fits a clear, multi-source pattern (Kraken's charter-stacking, EU build-out, IPO run-up) and would be genuinely novel if it lands (first crypto exchange with a full EU bank licence). Docked because it is an *unconfirmed, reported* early-stage intent with no filed application, no timeline, and no confirmation from either party — a directionally important but not yet consequential data point.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Regulated crypto is consolidating around EU frameworks: MiCA became mandatory, with the transitional window for nationally-registered VASPs closing no later than 1 Jul 2026 (per Bank of Lithuania/MiCA transitional guidance) — so the news lands exactly as CASP grandfathering ends and firms decide how far up the regulatory stack to climb. Structurally the space is barbelled: two US-listed/near-listed pure-plays (Coinbase, Kraken) plus MiCA-CASP mid-caps (Bitpanda, Gemini, OKX, Crypto.com). Entry barriers are shifting from technology to **regulatory capital and licensing** — the moat is becoming the charter, not the matching engine. Two drivers, "why now": (1) MiCA maturity pushes exchanges to pick a lane — stay a CASP or become a credit institution to unlock deposit-taking / net-interest income; (2) the IPO run-up rewards a "regulated financial institution" narrative that de-cyclicalises pure trading-fee revenue. Second-order: if a bank charter becomes table-stakes for the top tier, it re-rates the moat toward balance-sheet + compliance scale and away from crypto-native product speed.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: platform/trading volume (TPV), take rate (revenue ÷ volume), funded accounts, and — for the "become-a-bank" thesis — net-interest income and assets on platform. Kraken FY2025 (Payward IR, 3 Feb 2026): adjusted revenue **$2.2bn (+33% YoY)**, adjusted EBITDA **$531m (+26%)**, funded accounts **5.7m (+50%)**, platform volume **$2.0tn (+34%)**, assets on platform **$48.2bn**. Implied take rate ≈ `$2.2bn / $2.0tn = ~0.11%`. Basis of competition is now **licence breadth**: peers largely stopped at MiCA CASP charters — Coinbase (Luxembourg CSSF, then MiCA passport, Jun 2026), Bitpanda (Austria FMA), Gemini/OKX/Crypto.com (Malta). Recent moves: Coinbase secured its MiCA licence (Jun 2026) and a US national trust charter (conditional, Apr 2026) but faced [[Coinbase faces banker pushback on trust bank charter bid|banker pushback]]; Bitpanda is [[Bitpanda leans into bank partnerships and tokenization ahead of IPO|partnering with banks rather than becoming one]] into its Frankfurt IPO. **Kraken's position: ahead / differentiated** — no crypto exchange holds a *full EU bank licence* today; a Lithuanian charter would be first-of-kind and the clearest analogue is neobank Revolut's 2018 EMI→bank crossing (same regulator). Moat = regulatory/scale intangibles, corroborated by a real EU build-out ([[Kraken hires regtech expert Andreas Roussos to lead Cyprus unit|Cyprus regtech hire]], [[Kraken promotes Stavros Vassiliades to COO of EU business|EU COO]], [[Kraken parent Payward to acquire Reap for up to $600 million|Reap rails acquisition]]).

**Comps & multiples.**
- **Coinbase (COIN, public):** market cap ~$43bn (late Jun 2026, macrotrends/companiesmarketcap); FY2025 revenue $7.18bn (+9% YoY). P/S = `$43bn / $7.18bn = ~6.0x`.
- **Kraken/Payward (private):** last round ~**$20bn** post-money ([[Kraken parent Payward raises capital at $20 billion valuation|$800M round, Nov 2025, incl. $200M Citadel Securities]]; earlier [[Kraken raises $800M from Citadel and others|$15bn in Sep 2025]]). On FY2025 adj. revenue $2.2bn: implied P/S = `$20bn / $2.2bn = ~9.1x`; on adj. EBITDA $531m: EV/EBITDA ≈ `$20bn / $531m = ~37.7x` (round valuation, not market cap; EV≈equity assumed net-cash — treat as `[UNSOURCED]` on precise EV).
- **Read:** Kraken's ~9x P/S sits **rich vs Coinbase's ~6x** — but is largely justified by growth (+33% adj. revenue vs Coinbase +9%); the growth-multiple gap, not an over-valuation flag per se. Both inside the sanity range for hyper-growth exchanges. Internal round-comps: [[Kraken files confidentially for US IPO at $20B]], [[Kraken cuts 150 jobs after AI deployment; IPO may slip to 2027]] (listing may slip to 2027). Bitpanda private mark: FY2025 revenue [[Bitpanda 2025 revenue rises 16% to €371 million|€371m (+16%)]] — much smaller scale, not a clean multiples comp.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Regulatory-execution / timeline.** Full Lithuanian bank authorisation is ECB-involved and typically 12+ months; neither Kraken nor the Bank of Lithuania has confirmed a filed application (single CoinDesk sourced report). Second-order: this cannot plausibly close pre-IPO — the "regulated bank" narrative may be sold before it exists, a headline-vs-substance gap.
2. **Prudential capital / CRD-CRR burden.** Credit-institution status brings LCR/NSFR, consolidated supervision and deposit-guarantee obligations; a crypto-heavy balance sheet invites heavier prudential treatment than Revolut faced in 2018. The incremental deposit/lending revenue may not clear the capital cost — margin dilution risk.
3. **Crypto-cycle revenue volatility.** ~0.11% take-rate revenue is trading-volume-driven and pro-cyclical — Coinbase's Q4 2025 revenue fell ~21% YoY and Payward Q1 2026 rose only 3% as trading slowed; a bank charter diversifies but does not immunise the core P&L, and volatile trading income sits awkwardly under a deposit-taking licence.
4. **Competition / first-mover ambiguity.** No peer holds a full EU bank licence — being first may signal genuine lead, or that the regulatory/capital economics deter rivals for good reason; if it works, expect Coinbase/others to follow and the differentiator to erode.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) If it lands, this reframes the top-tier crypto-exchange moat from product to **charter + balance sheet**, and reads as a re-rating catalyst for Kraken's IPO (recurring NII narrative). Falsifiable thesis: Kraken files a *specialised-bank* application in Lithuania (capital-efficient, Revolut template) rather than a full universal bank — watch for a confirmed Bank of Lithuania record or Kraken disclosure; if Kraken instead *acquires* an existing Lithuanian-licensed bank (Sethi flagged M&A as an option) or the story stays "reported/unconfirmed" through the IPO, the bank-charter thesis is more narrative than substance.

This is not a public earnings report — it is Payward's own pre-IPO financial-highlights disclosure. Kraken has **declined to comment** on the Lithuania banking-licence report itself, so there are no results tied to *this* news; the review reads the company's most recent disclosed print instead.

**Key figures (with growth) — company-reported, unaudited (Payward).**
- **FY2025 (reported 3 Feb 2026):** adjusted revenue **$2.2bn (+33% YoY)**; adjusted EBITDA **$531m (+26% YoY)**, ~24% margin; funded accounts **5.7m (+50% YoY)**; platform assets **$48.2bn (+11% YoY)**; revenue mix ~**47% trading / ~53% asset-based & other** (non-trading revenue topped 50% for the first time — the diversification the bank-licence story is meant to extend).
- **Q1 2026 (reported 18 May 2026):** adjusted revenue **$507m (+3% YoY, -19% QoQ)**; adjusted EBITDA **$18m** (down sharply, deliberate through-cycle investment in products/acquisitions/regulation); funded accounts **6.1m** (up from 5.7m at FY2025); platform assets **~$40bn** (down from $48.2bn); total platform transaction volume **$357bn**; **futures DARTs +51% YoY** (NinjaTrader, Breakout, Bitnomial). Management framed it as "one of the most challenging quarters the industry has faced since 2022."
- Implied FY2025 take-rate ≈ $2.2bn / ~$2.0tn platform volume ≈ **~0.11%** (no data on a Q1-specific take-rate).
- EPS / net income / margin bridge: **no data** (private company, not disclosed).

**By segment / driver.** Growth engine is shifting off pure spot trading: FY2025 non-trading (asset-based + other) revenue crossed >50% of the mix, and Q1 2026 leaned on **derivatives/futures (+51% YoY DARTs)** to hold revenue flat-to-up while spot volume collapsed ~38% industry-wide. What under-performed: spot trading revenue and EBITDA, which compressed hard QoQ (-19% revenue, EBITDA to $18m) — the pro-cyclical core doing exactly what the thesis warns about.

**vs expectations / prior period.** No public sell-side consensus exists (private, pre-IPO) → beat/miss assessed vs prior period only, exact "expected" figures [UNSOURCED]. Trajectory: **decelerating** — FY2025 +33% YoY revenue and +26% EBITDA gave way to Q1 2026 +3% YoY revenue and a collapse in EBITDA ($531m FY run-rate to $18m in the quarter). Sequentially -19% QoQ. Prior-period corpus: [[Kraken parent Payward raises capital at $20 billion valuation]], [[Kraken files confidentially for US IPO at $20B]], [[Kraken cuts 150 jobs after AI deployment; IPO may slip to 2027]] — the cost discipline (layoffs) and the possible IPO slip to 2027 read as management reacting to exactly this softer, more cyclical print.

**Guidance / forward.** None given (private company — no formal guidance, no forward consensus). (analysis) On the current trajectory, FY2026 revenue growth almost certainly decelerates sharply from FY2025's +33% unless crypto volumes recover in H2; EBITDA margin stays compressed as management explicitly prioritises investment (acquisitions Backed, Magna, Bitnomial, Reap; regulatory build-out) over near-term profitability. Management tone: candid about the cycle ("toughest quarter in four years"), emphasising diversification and licence-stacking; silent on any hard revenue/margin target and on when EBITDA re-expands.

**Thesis-flags.**
1. **Crypto-cycle revenue sensitivity is live, not theoretical.** Fact: Q1 2026 EBITDA fell to $18m (~3.6% margin) from a ~24% FY2025 margin as spot volume dropped ~38%. Why: ~0.11% take-rate revenue is volume-driven and pro-cyclical. Why it matters: the equity narrative can't rest on trading fees. Second-order: this is *precisely* the vulnerability the Lithuania bank-licence move is designed to address — it strengthens the "we need diversified, recurring income" case rather than undercutting it.
2. **A bank licence diversifies revenue toward net-interest income / deposits.** Fact: non-trading revenue already >50% of FY2025 mix; a full (or specialised) Lithuanian charter would add deposit-taking + lending + NII. Why it matters: NII is balance-sheet-driven and far less correlated to crypto price than trading fees — the single most credible de-cyclicalisation lever in the story. Second-order: but it lands *slowly* (ECB-involved, 12+ months, unconfirmed/reported only) — so it cannot rescue the near-term print or the pre-IPO numbers; it is a multi-year moat/legitimacy play, not a FY2026 earnings driver.
3. **Deliberate margin sacrifice raises IPO-timing risk.** Fact: EBITDA cut to fund acquisitions + regulatory build-out; IPO may slip toward 2027. Why it matters: investing through a downturn is defensible, but a soft-revenue / thin-EBITDA / heavy-spend profile is a harder equity story at a ~$20bn mark (~9x FY2025 P/S). Second-order: the bank-charter and licence-stacking narrative is partly there to justify the multiple while the trading P&L is cyclically weak.
4. **First-mover on an EU bank charter cuts both ways.** No crypto exchange holds a full EU bank licence; being first is a differentiator, but the prudential capital / CRD-CRR / deposit-guarantee burden on a crypto-heavy balance sheet may dilute the very margins it's meant to stabilise — watch whether Kraken files for the capital-light *specialised* bank (Revolut template) vs a full universal charter.
