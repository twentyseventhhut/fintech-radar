Vint Cerf is working on a plan to unleash AI agents on the open internet
Vint Cerf says his favorite place is where he’s never been before.
One of the architects of the protocols behind the open internet, Cerf left Google after 20 years last week, but he’s not done thinking about the digital future. Starting today, he’s advising Innovation Labs, an organization trying to create the open architecture for AI agents to identify themselves.
Innovation Labs is a subsidiary of Identity Digital, a DNS registry company, which sees domain-name infrastructure as a practical way to hold AI agents accountable and position itself for a future where more online interaction happens between agents than people. Cerf joins a handful of other internet luminaries lending their names to the effort.
Most AI agents today stay within proprietary systems, calling on internal resources for specific purposes. But businesses are already envisioning a world where they operate far more autonomously across the internet and interact directly with other agents. So far, a key road block has been a lack of a shared standard for identifying and auditing agents.
A variety of standards are beginning to emerge, and Innovation Labs has proposed DNSid , a system to create identities for agents and link each one to an existing internet domain name, using cryptographic proofs to log its registration over time. Innovation Labs’ interim CEO Allie Kline says the company is trialing the standards with several unnamed hyperscalers and identity companies.
“I felt like I might be able to help them in a period of time when naming and identification is becoming increasingly important,” Cerf told TechCrunch. “This is largely triggered by the notion of AI agents and the question of what authorities they have, where they have derived those authorities, who is accountable for the behavior of an agent in this context, and where and how its identity is established, and why [you’d] trust it.”
Those questions promise to be thorny, Cerf says, because AI agents are so much more active than domains, and it’s not yet clear what commitment an organization is making when they register one.
“It’s going to be a fascinating — and at the same time maybe even exasperating — period in the evolution of the internet and the things that depend on it, because the functionality is so dramatically powerful,” Cerf said.
With multiple solutions to the problem under consideration, Cerf says the key to a wide adoption of any protocol will be its functionality. 
“Company X uses agent Y’s technology, and company A uses agent C’s technology, and then they don’t interwork with each other,” Cerf said. “Nobody can do everything that you might want every agent to do… and so we’re going to have to rely on the pressure coming from the users. This is what happened with TCP/IP.”
One key to Innovation Labs’ proposal is that it does not come with broader plans to do other kinds of AI business or own the registration data, Kline says. “I think there’s a lot of organ rejection to a hyperscaler releasing [a standard] and having that proprietary data,” she told TechCrunch. 
And does Cerf think the agentic economy is the internet’s destiny?
“I don’t think it’s inevitable,” he said. “But what I do think is inevitable is that people will try to do that. We are fundamentally lazy creatures, and if we find a way to have an an agent do something for us, we’re very likely to choose to do that because [it’s] just easier.”

# Context-enrichment: Vint Cerf plans standard to identify AI agents on open internet
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
De-PR'd: this is **not the launch of a new standard** — it is a **name-lending / advisory hire**. As of 2026-07-15, Vint Cerf (TCP/IP co-architect, who left Google the prior week after 20 years) joined the **Advisory Council of Innovation Labs**, a subsidiary of **Identity Digital** (a DNS registry company). The underlying standard, **DNSid** ("DNS-Anchored Durable Identity for AI Agents"), was **already launched 2026-04-27** and **already submitted to the IETF as an Internet-Draft (`draft-ihsanullah-dnsid`) on 2026-06-04**. So the "Cerf plans a standard" framing is TechCrunch's; Cerf is advising an existing proposal, not authoring a fresh one.
- **Mechanism (de-PR'd):** DNSid gives each agent a persistent identifier **anchored to an existing internet domain name**, with **cryptographic proofs** logging registration/ownership/transfer/revocation over time. It is the "durable ownership anchor / accountability layer" — *who stands behind this agent* — deliberately NOT per-request authentication.
- **Traction claim to flag:** interim CEO Allie Kline says they are "**trialing with several unnamed hyperscalers and identity companies**." Unnamed = unverifiable; treat as pipeline, not adoption.
- **Why structured this way / what it reveals:** Identity Digital is a **domain registry** — DNSid extends its existing revenue engine (selling/registering names) into a new object class (agents). The "neutral, non-proprietary, we-won't-own-the-data" positioning (Kline: "organ rejection to a hyperscaler releasing a proprietary standard") is the real strategic wedge: it is explicitly designed to be the **Switzerland** option against Google/Cloudflare/OpenAI-backed alternatives. Cerf's name is the credibility instrument for that neutrality play — a "this is how TCP/IP won: user pressure, not a vendor mandate" narrative.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Agent-identity is a **crowded, fragmenting standards race** — DNSid is one layer of a stack, not a winner:
- **Web Bot Auth** (IETF WG chartered early 2026; backed by **Cloudflare, Amazon, Akamai, OpenAI**) — per-request cryptographic signing via HTTP Message Signatures (RFC 9421). This is the **direct standards rival** but at a *different layer*: per-request authentication (L7) vs DNSid's durable ownership anchor. Complementary in theory, competing for mindshare in practice. Web Bot Auth has far heavier hyperscaler backing already live (Google's AI-browsing agent signs).
- [[Google and Mastercard back FIDO Alliance on agentic commerce standards]] (2026-05-01) — FIDO is the **standards-body analog** for trusted agent interactions, backed by Google + Mastercard. Another parallel effort.
- **AP2 / A2A / MCP stack** — Google's Agent Payments Protocol (Sept 2025, 60+ partners incl. Mastercard/PayPal/Coinbase) sits below MCP/A2A; the Linux Foundation's **Agentic AI Foundation** (Dec 2025; OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, AWS, Block) now houses A2A + MCP. These handle *tools/negotiation/payment mandates*, not *durable identity* — so DNSid is adjacent, not overlapping.
- [[Oak exits stealth with $60M to fix AI-agent identity mess]] (2026-07-15, same day) — a **$60M startup** attacking the identical problem commercially. The startup-vs-standard tension made concrete on the same date.
- [[Vouched launches Agent Checkpoint to verify AI agent identity]] (2026-02-27) — commercial agent-identity verification product; adjacent infra.
- [[UnionPay debuts Agentic Payment Open Protocol framework]] (2026-04-06) — regional open agent-trust protocol.
- **+ Why this landscape / second-order:** the field is splitting by **layer** (durable identity vs per-request auth vs payment mandate vs discovery) AND by **sponsor** (registry-neutral vs hyperscaler vs card-scheme vs startup). DNSid's edge is neutrality + DNS's proven durability; its handicap is that Web Bot Auth already has the hyperscalers who actually generate agent traffic. Standards win on *distribution*, and Identity Digital doesn't control any agent runtime — it controls names. Cerf's TCP/IP analogy ("users force interop") is precisely the bet that distribution won't decide it.

## [2] Company history / fit
Identity Digital = one of the largest DNS registry operators (runs hundreds of gTLDs). **Structural logic:** its core business is renting namespace; as human web-traffic value plateaus, an "agent internet" where *agents outnumber people* is a natural TAM expansion — every agent that needs a durable, resolvable identity is a potential registration. DNSid is a **defensive-offensive land-grab**: reuse the DNS root-of-trust it already operates to become the identity registry for agents. Spinning it into a neutral subsidiary ("Innovation Labs") and recruiting internet luminaries is the standard playbook for making a *commercial registry's* proposal look like a *public-interest standard*.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real novelty:** modest. The novel bit is **anchoring agent identity to DNS + domain ownership** (durable, transferable, revocable) rather than to a key or a per-request signature. That is a genuine architectural distinction from Web Bot Auth. Cerf joining is **zero technical novelty** — it is a credibility/PR event.
- **Traction:** thin and unproven. IETF Internet-Draft = *submitted*, not adopted (drafts are cheap; adoption as a WG item / RFC is the bar). "Unnamed hyperscalers trialing" = no verifiable deployment. Compare Web Bot Auth, which already has a chartered WG and live signing by Google's agent.
- **+ Who captures the margin / what breaks it:** if agent identity standardizes on DNS anchoring, the **registry layer captures rent** (Identity Digital's play). It breaks if (a) hyperscalers route around DNS with their own per-request auth (Web Bot Auth) so durable ownership becomes a niche compliance add-on, or (b) card schemes (AP2/FIDO) bundle identity into the *payment mandate* and the durable-anchor layer never becomes a standalone paid product. The value-add is **not yet confirmed** — it is a credible thesis with a marquee endorser, no adoption proof.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Path:** IETF process for `draft-ihsanullah-dnsid` (BCP-track agent-identity milestones target ~Aug 2026 industry-wide); success needs WG adoption + named production users. Watch for the "unnamed hyperscalers" to become named.
- **Backdrop (why it matters):** agent identity is the gating problem for **agentic commerce/payments**. Card schemes updated operating rules to treat agent-initiated transactions as a distinct category; EU/UK/Singapore regulators are consulting on **liability allocation, consent capture, and access to agent decision logs** (IMF Note 2026/004 flags the same). Liability follows authorization — you cannot assign blame for an autonomous payment without a durable answer to "who owns this agent." That is exactly DNSid's pitch.
- **+ Counterintuitive second-order:** more standards = *less* interoperability near-term (Cerf's own "company X's agent and company A's agent don't interwork" warning). Fragmentation could delay agentic commerce even as it accelerates announcements. Cerf hedged explicitly: agentic economy is "**not inevitable**" — only people *trying* it is. That is unusually candid for a launch-adjacent quote and lowers the hype.

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** This sits in the *agent-identity / "verify-the-bot"* layer of agentic commerce — the trust plumbing UNDER the payment rails, not the rails themselves. Market framing (agentic commerce as the demand driver): per McKinsey (Oct 2025, via Digital Commerce 360) agentic commerce could orchestrate ~$1tn of US retail revenue and $3–5tn globally by 2030; the narrower *agent-payments* segment is put at ~$7bn→$93bn by 2032 (per Nevermined stats compilation) and Juniper at ~$1.5tn agentic-commerce spend by 2030 — figures diverge wildly on scope, so treat as directional, not a hard TAM. Structure: today it is **fragmented and pre-standard** — the news's own thesis ("a key roadblock has been a lack of a shared standard for identifying and auditing agents"). Value chain has three stacked layers now visibly separating: (1) agent reasoning/interop (MCP, A2A), (2) payment authorization (AP2, ACP, x402), (3) **identity/accountability — the layer DNSid targets**. Why now: agents are moving from proprietary walled systems ("most AI agents today stay within proprietary systems") to open agent-to-agent interaction; without a way to answer "who is accountable for this agent," open cross-org agent commerce cannot clear liability/fraud. Barrier to entry here is unusual: it is *legitimacy/neutrality*, not capital — a standard wins by adoption and by NOT being owned ("organ rejection to a hyperscaler releasing a standard and having that proprietary data"), which is exactly why Identity Digital (a neutral DNS registry) recruits Cerf's name rather than a hyperscaler brand.

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs the identity layer runs on (analysis): adoption/registrations (agents anchored), share of hyperscalers/networks committed, and standards-body traction (IETF/EMVCo/W3C acceptance) — this is a network-effect land-grab, not a revenue metric yet. Basis of competition = **distribution + neutrality + being where the rails already are**, not price. Players by sub-layer:
- *Identity/anchoring (direct comps):* **DNSid / Innovation Labs (Identity Digital)** — DNS-anchored durable identity, launched 27-Apr-2026, submitted to IETF (Jun 2026), trialing with unnamed hyperscalers; **Visa Trusted Agent Protocol (TAP)** (launched 14-Oct-2025 w/ Cloudflare, signs agent identity into HTTP headers vs Visa directory); **Mastercard Verifiable Intent** (tokenizes user intent, not the agent); **ERC-8004** (on-chain agent identity, crypto side, Skyfire et al.); Cloudflare/Web Bot Auth on the crawl-auth side.
- *Payment-authorization layer above it:* Google **AP2** (60+ partners incl. Mastercard, PayPal, Coinbase, Amex, Stripe; W3C Verifiable Credentials mandates), OpenAI+Stripe **ACP**, Coinbase **x402**, **EMVCo** (card-scheme spec body, working on agentic-payment specs since Nov 2025).
Protagonist position: **niche but credibly positioned** (analysis) — Identity Digital owns real DNS-registry rails (an existing distribution moat) and plays the *neutral Switzerland* card that hyperscaler standards structurally cannot; but it is one of ≥4 identity proposals with no disclosed adoption numbers ("several unnamed hyperscalers"). Cerf's involvement is a **name/legitimacy asset**, not a technical or commercial moat — note he *left Google last week* and is *advising*, not building. De-PR: this is "announced/trialing," NOT live-adopted; economics, revenue model and who pays for registration are undisclosed.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round attaches to this note (standards initiative, not a financing) → trading multiples **not applicable / no data**. Internal comps from the corpus (agent-payments & identity plumbing):
- [[AEON raises $8 million to build settlement layer for AI agents]] — pre-seed $8m (YZi Labs), 19-May-2026 — settlement layer, adjacent stack tier.
- [[Mastercard launches Agent Pay for Machines for machine-speed payments]] — 12-Jun-2026, 30+ partners incl. Skyfire, Rain, Coinbase, Stripe — a network incumbent building the permissioning/settlement layer DNSid wants to feed.
- [[OpenAI publishes Agentic Commerce Protocol documentation]] / [[Checkout.com adopts Agentic Commerce Protocol]] / [[Worldpay adds support for OpenAI's agentic commerce protocol]] — the ACP rail (Nov 2025).
- [[EMVCo to develop specifications for agentic payments]] (Nov 2025) and [[AAA and Integra Ledger launch agentic commerce legal protocol]] (24-Jun-2026) — parallel standards efforts, evidence of the fragmentation.
Distribution not computed (no comparable valuations) — qualitative only: the space is at pre-seed/standards-formation stage; capital is flowing to settlement layers (AEON) while identity is being contested by incumbents (Visa/MC) and neutral registries (Identity Digital) at *zero disclosed price*.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Standards fragmentation / no winner** — ≥4 competing identity proposals (DNSid, Visa TAP, MC Verifiable Intent, ERC-8004) plus non-interoperating payment rails (AP2/ACP/x402). Cerf's own warning is the risk: "company X uses agent Y's tech… and they don't interwork." Second-order: adoption stalls, and a network/hyperscaler default (Visa TAP inside existing rails, or AP2's 60+ partners) can win on distribution before a neutral standard reaches scale — stranding DNSid.
2. **Disintermediation by the payment/network layer** — value in agentic commerce may accrue to whoever owns authorization + settlement (Mastercard, Visa, Stripe/OpenAI), with identity collapsed into their proprietary stacks; a standalone neutral identity layer risks being commoditized/absorbed rather than monetized. No disclosed revenue model amplifies this.
3. **Adoption/execution risk & key-person dependence** — traction is "unnamed hyperscalers," trialing only; the headline asset is a *name* (Cerf, advising post-Google-exit), which de-risks nothing commercially. IETF acceptance and hyperscaler commitment are the unproven gates.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is the *identity/accountability layer of agentic commerce moving from concept to standards contest* — watch it as the fraud-liability chokepoint that unlocks (or blocks) cross-org agent payments. Falsifiable thesis (analysis): the identity layer will NOT be won by a neutral registry but folded into the payment networks/hyperscalers that already control the rails — trigger to confirm: a named hyperscaler or card scheme adopts DNSid at scale (thesis wrong) vs. Visa TAP / Mastercard Verifiable Intent / AP2 becoming the de-facto identity default via their partner networks (thesis right). Catalyst to watch: IETF disposition of the DNSid draft and any named (not "unnamed") hyperscaler commitment.
