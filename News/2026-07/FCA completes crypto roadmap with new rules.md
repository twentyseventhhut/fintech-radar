## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
**[0] TL;DR.** On ~30 June 2026 the FCA published its final policy statements completing its crypto roadmap — the definitive UK cryptoasset rulebook. The rules apply to firms authorised under FSMA from 25 October 2027 (regime commencement). Headline concession: stablecoin issuer capital floor cut from 2% to 1% after industry feedback. This is the terminal "final rules" milestone, distinct from the earlier consultation/sandbox/commencement-date steps.

**[1] What's new / the milestone.**
- FCA published final rules & guidance (policy statements) finalising the UK cryptoasset regime — "FCA sets landmark crypto rules to cement the UK's place as a global hub."
- **Regulated activities in scope:** operating crypto trading platforms; dealing/arranging qualifying cryptoassets; safeguarding (custody); issuing qualifying stablecoins; arranging qualifying crypto staking.
- **Stablecoins:** issuer capital requirement cut 2% → 1%; requirements on stability, backing quality and governance. UK reserve model reported as ~30% held at bank + 70% short-dated gilts, with no yield passed to holders.
- **Custody:** raised expectations on asset segregation, security, insolvency handling, operational resilience.
- **Trading:** stronger obligations to detect market manipulation, insider trading and abusive order-book activity (market-abuse regime for cryptoassets).

**[2] Timeline / mechanics.**
- Pre-application support meetings (PASS) available from July 2026.
- Authorisation gateway / application window: **30 Sept 2026 – 28 Feb 2027** (gateway stays open for new firms through 2027).
- Mandatory regime commences **25 Oct 2027**. Firms applying in-window that aren't determined in time enter a "saving provision" to keep operating until determined.
- A further policy statement on the regulatory perimeter is due September 2026.

**[3] Internal context — prior FCA crypto roadmap steps (this is the capstone).**
- [[FCA's new cryptoasset regime expected to take effect October 2027]] (2026-05-11) — set the 25 Oct 2027 commencement date; PASS pre-app meetings from May/July 2026; lists all prior consultation/discussion papers (DP23/4 stablecoins, DP24/4 & CP25/41 market abuse, CP25/14 custody+stablecoin issuance, CP25/15 & CP25/42 prudential, CP25/25 & CP26/4 Handbook, CP26/13 perimeter, GC26/2 Consumer Duty).
- [[FCA confirms UK cryptoasset authorisation window]] (2026-04-13) — confirmed the authorisation window mechanics.
- [[FCA publishes approach to UK cryptoasset regulatory regime]] (2026-01-19) — approach document, pre-application meetings, regime design.
- [[UK FCA consults on applying Handbook rules to cryptoasset firms]] (2025-09-18) — CP25/25 consultation stage on Handbook application.
- Sandbox track: [[Revolut to test GBP stablecoin in FCA sandbox]] (2026-03-02) and [[Revolut among firms testing stablecoins in UK FCA sandbox]] (2026-02-27) — 4 firms selected to pilot stablecoins; findings feed the final rules.
- Firm-level: [[Blockchain.com secures FCA registration for UK crypto services]] (2026-02-12) — MLR registration ahead of the FSMA regime.

**[4] Comparison — UK FCA vs EU MiCA vs US GENIUS Act.**
- **Scope:** FCA regime is broad (issuance + custody + trading + staking), broadly mirroring MiCA's market-wide coverage. US GENIUS Act is narrower — payment-stablecoin issuers only.
- **Stablecoin reserves/yield:** MiCA mandates a bank-deposit floor (30% non-significant, 60% significant) plus short-maturity instruments. US GENIUS lets issuers hold short-dated Treasuries and keep the yield (a viable business model). UK: ~30% bank + 70% short-dated gilts, no yield to holders — closer to MiCA's conservatism, less issuer-friendly than the US.
- **Capital:** FCA cut stablecoin issuer capital to 1% (from 2%), a notably light figure vs peers.
- **Authorisation:** MiCA passports across the EEA from a home-state licence; US offers federal (OCC) or state charter routes; UK requires standalone FCA authorisation for firms serving UK customers (no passport in). Reported time-to-authorise: FCA ~6–12 months (stablecoin issuers), MiCA ~9–14 months, GENIUS ~6–9 months.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** On 30 June 2026 the FCA published its final rules/policy statements, completing the UK cryptoasset "roadmap"; the authorisation gateway opens 30 Sep 2026 (window to 28 Feb 2027) and the mandatory regime goes live 25 Oct 2027 — until then FCA oversight stays limited to financial promotions + AML (per FCA, "FCA sets landmark crypto rules…", 30 Jun 2026). The regime is activity-based and broad, capturing trading platforms, intermediaries, custody, staking arrangers and stablecoin issuance under one FSMA licensing gateway (per Skadden / FCA, Jul 2026). Structure: this is a *regulatory-competition* story, not a market-size one — the value chain (issuance / custody / trading / intermediation) is being formalised, and the binding barrier to entry becomes regulatory capital + authorisation, favouring incumbents with compliance teams. Why now: the UK moved *after* the EU's MiCA (live, harmonised) and alongside the US CLARITY-Act track, and is explicitly positioning to be "more proportionate, more predictable and faster to supervise" rather than lighter-touch (per Latham/Forvis Mazars commentary, 2026) `(analysis)`.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs the regime turns on: stablecoin reserve-capital ratio (K-SII), reserve backing/redemption terms, and authorisation lead-time. Key regulatory "competitors" are jurisdictions, not firms: **EU MiCA** (first-mover, passportable across 30 EEA states), **US** (CLARITY/GENIUS stablecoin track), **Singapore/MAS** (licensing of firms serving foreign clients — see [[MAS to require crypto firms serving foreign clients to be licensed]]). Recent UK moves: FCA cut the non-systemic stablecoin capital coefficient from 2% to 1% of issued value after consultation (a $1bn issuer now holds ~$10m vs $20m reserve capital), and the BoE swapped stablecoin holding limits for a £40bn issuance guardrail (per CCN / Global Banking & Finance, Jun–Jul 2026). UK positioning: catching up on *timing* vs MiCA but arguably ahead on *calibration for how markets actually trade* — moat is the depth of London capital markets + rule-of-law/predictability (intangible/regulatory moat), not a first-mover advantage `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation event — this is a regulator publication, so trading multiples are **not applicable / no data**. Beneficiaries are firms already building UK authorisation as an asset. Internal comps (base precedents on the same roadmap): [[FCA confirms UK cryptoasset authorisation window]] (Apr 2026) · [[UK FCA consults on applying Handbook rules to cryptoasset firms]] (Sep 2025) · [[Revolut to test GBP stablecoin in FCA sandbox]] (Mar 2026) · [[Ctrl Alt gets FCA authorisation after tokenising $1.2bn]] (Apr 2026) · [[Blockchain.com secures FCA registration for UK crypto services]] (Feb 2026) · [[FCA approves crypto ETPs from 21Shares, Bitwise and WisdomTree]] (Oct 2025). Read together these show a steady 12-month sequencing from consultation → sandbox → ETP approvals → authorisation window → final rules; distribution not computed (qualitative). One MiCA data point for contrast: MiCA mandates 60% of stablecoin reserves as bank deposits but does not require them unremunerated — the UK approach is described as *more* demanding on reserve economics, which shapes issuer business models (per search, Jul 2026) — verify against primary FCA PS before relying on `[UNSOURCED]` on the exact UK deposit/remuneration term.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Implementation/timing gap (2025→Oct 2027).** Rules are "complete" but not live for ~16 months; a former FCA policymaker (Arredondo) flags an ambition-vs-execution gap, and the transition can incentivise firms to prioritise other jurisdictions first — second-order: the announced hub status may not convert to relocations if authorisation is slow.
2. **Barbell / small-firm disintermediation.** Capital + compliance burden favours large firms with established teams; smaller/lightly-staffed firms face delayed entry or exit — risk that the regime consolidates the UK market and thins the innovative long tail.
3. **Late-mover vs MiCA passporting.** EU offers a live, single-authorisation passport across the EEA today; UK authorisation is single-market and lands ~2 years later — firms may treat MiCA as the default and the UK as optional, blunting the "global hub" thesis.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is a re-rating catalyst for UK-regulated custody/stablecoin/exchange plays and a consolidation signal (compliance-cost moat) rather than a new-entrant wave `(analysis)`. Watch the 30 Sep 2026 gateway open and early authorisation throughput as the falsifiable trigger: heavy, fast approvals + net firm in-migration validates "hub"; thin flow or continued relocations abroad breaks the thesis.
