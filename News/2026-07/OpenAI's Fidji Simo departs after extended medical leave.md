Fidji Simo steps down from OpenAI’s no. 2 role
Fidji Simo, OpenAI’s No. 2 executive, is stepping down from her full-time role, the Wall Street Journal reports .
In a staff note Thursday, Simo said her ongoing medical leave has proven longer and harder than expected, and that she’ll transition to a part-time advisory role instead. Simo joined OpenAI’s board of directors in 2024 and joined OpenAI in May 2025 as CEO of Applications, then a newly created role reporting directly to Sam Altman that consolidated the company’s business and product operations. 
Her appointment came with a broader reporting shift: COO Brad Lightcap, CFO Sarah Friar, and CPO Kevin Weil all began reporting to her, while Altman stepped back to focus on research, compute, and safety.
Simo first disclosed her health issues in April, when she announced she was taking medical leave for a relapse of a neuroimmune condition; that same memo publicly announced that Lightcap was moving into a new “special projects” role and that CMO Kate Rouch was leaving the company to focus on cancer recovery. Weil has since left the company , too.
Simo came to OpenAI from Instacart, where she’d been CEO since 2021 and led the company through its 2023 IPO, and before that spent over a decade at Meta, including running the Facebook app.
Simo’s decision to step back permanently leaves Altman searching for a successor right as OpenAI itself eyes a possible IPO. She’d been widely seen as a likely candidate to take on even more responsibility once OpenAI went public, making this a real vacuum for him to address.
Simo was primarily focused on growing OpenAI’s consumer business. But ChatGPT’s growth cooled late last year, missing internal revenue targets, pushing the company to lean harder into coding tools instead, an area where it has been, and for now continues to be, trailing Anthropic.
TechCrunch has reached out to OpenAI for more information. 
Soon after the Journal story broke, Simo shared the news directly on X, after which Altman responded , also on X: “i am really sad about this and very grateful for all fidji has done for openai, and even grateful for her friendship and who she is as a person. we all wish her the best for a speedy recovery. this sucks.”
Simo’s announcement lands on a busy news day for OpenAI. Earlier Thursday, the company launched its new GPT-5.6 family of models — Sol, Terra, and Luna — alongside a new agent called ChatGPT Work, designed to handle multistep office tasks like drafting documents, spreadsheets, and presentations. Both releases were framed by OpenAI as directly targeting Anthropic.
OpenAI’s executive ranks appear from the outside to be on the thin side for a company that was most recently assigned an $852 billion valuation. In addition to Altman, Lightcap, Friar, and co-founder Greg Brockman (who is also the company’s president and was overseeing product strategy while Simo was out), its bench includes Denise Dresser, who in December joined as the company’s chief revenue officer, overseeing its “global revenue strategy across enterprise and customer success,” per a release at the time. 
It wouldn’t be shocking to see Dresser take on a more expansive role, given she previously spent two years as the CEO of Slack and, before that, spent 14 years with Slack’s parent company, Salesforce.
Simo’s departure comes against another backdrop worth understanding: OpenAI’s shifting approach to employee equity. In April of last year, the same month that Simo joined, the company shortened its vesting cliff — the waiting period before new hires’ stock grants begin vesting — from the industry-standard 12 months to 6 months. Then in December, OpenAI eliminated the cliff altogether for new hires, letting equity start vesting from day one.
The move, described internally by Simo as a way to let employees “take risks” without fear of losing equity if let go early, came amid an escalating AI talent war and reflects just how aggressively OpenAI has been spending to retain staff. The company was projected to spend $6 billion on stock-based compensation in 2025 alone.
None of the aforementioned exits appear tied to compensation. Executive equity packages are typically negotiated individually and could have entirely different vesting terms.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **Thu, July 9 2026:** Fidji Simo, OpenAI's **CEO of Applications** and de-facto No. 2, quit the full-time role and moved to a **part-time advisory** seat. Stated reason: her medical leave "proved longer and harder than expected" (neuroimmune condition; several outlets name POTS — *reported, not primary-verified*). - **The de-PR'd read:** the *stated* cause is health, and no source alleges a forced push-out — but the framing hides two uncomfortable facts. (1) **The org she was hired to build has already dissolved around her.** She was hired May 2025 to consolidate business+product, with COO Lightcap, CFO Friar and CPO Weil reporting up to her. By July: Weil **left** (Apr 17 2026), Lightcap → "special projects" (Apr), CMO Kate Rouch left (Apr). The "No. 2 consolidating the commercial org" structure was **already stale before she formally stepped down** → why it matters: this is less "star exec on leave" and more "the Applications experiment unwound." (2) **Same-day staging with GPT-5.6.** Earlier that Thursday OpenAI shipped the **GPT-5.6 family (Sol/Terra/Luna)** + **ChatGPT Work**, both benchmarked at Anthropic (see [[OpenAI releases GPT-5.6 family and ChatGPT Work agent]]). Burying a No. 2's departure under a launch is standard PR hygiene → the loud product news softens the "thin bench" story. Correlation is loud; causation (performance vs health) is **not** established (analysis).

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Anthropic** is the reference point on every axis Simo owned. ChatGPT growth cooled late 2025 (~810M MAU Nov 2025, decelerating; losing share to Gemini), OpenAI reportedly missed internal revenue targets (WSJ; OpenAI calls it "clickbait"), and Anthropic's revenue run-rate reportedly **overtook OpenAI (~$30B, ~Apr 2026)** on the back of **Claude Code** — the coding surface where OpenAI's Codex is at rough *capability* parity but *commercially* trailing. - **Second-order:** Simo was hired to make OpenAI a product/consumer company (her Meta + Instacart pedigree). But the market's value axis moved to **enterprise + coding + agents** — precisely where the newly-shipped ChatGPT Work + GPT-5.6 are aimed. So her exit isn't just a vacancy; it's a **tacit re-centering of the org away from the consumer thesis she embodied** toward the enterprise fight with Anthropic (analysis).

## [2] Company history / fit

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's actually new/material:** not the leave (April) but the **permanent vacuum at No. 2** right as OpenAI eyes an IPO. Simo was widely seen as the person who'd take *more* responsibility post-IPO; that succession plan is gone.
- **No single successor** — the portfolio is **split**: product strategy → **Greg Brockman** (already ran product during her leave); the rest of the Applications remit → **CFO Sarah Friar + Chief Strategy Officer Jason Kwon**. **Denise Dresser** (first CRO, ex-Slack CEO/ex-Salesforce, joined Dec 2025) is *floated* by journalists for an expanded role — **speculation, not announced.**
- **Why it matters (2nd order):** splitting a consolidator's job back across a CFO, a CSO and the president is a **re-fragmentation** of exactly what Simo was hired to unify → the commercial org loses a single accountable owner *during* the pre-IPO window when investors most want a clean, legible commercial narrative (analysis).

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Altman is now personally searching for a successor while also running the IPO process (2026-vs-2027 debate; sub-$1T a "nonstarter" per Altman, Friar reportedly favors 2027 — see [[OpenAI IPO delayed to potentially 2027, behind Anthropic]]).
- **Risks:** (a) commercial leadership vacuum during the IPO roadshow; (b) the consumer thesis (Simo's mandate) is being quietly demoted vs the enterprise/coding fight it's losing to Anthropic; (c) continued exec attrition despite record equity spend.
- **Counterintuitive second-order:** the same-day GPT-5.6 blitz shows OpenAI can still *ship* frontier product on Altman-led org — so the bet is that **product/compute momentum matters more to the IPO story than a tidy org chart.** Whether public-market investors agree with a five-person effective bench (Altman, Lightcap, Friar, Brockman, Dresser) at an $852B mark is the open question (analysis).

## Market Research

**Sector & drivers.** This is a governance/execution event at the commercial layer of the frontier-AI market, not a financing or metrics event — so sizing is context, not the story. OpenAI carried an ~$852bn last-round valuation (per note text / TechCrunch, 2026-07) and crossed ~$25bn annualized revenue by Feb–Mar 2026, up from ~$20bn at end-2025 (per The Information / Reuters, via Yahoo Finance & Statista). Enterprise reportedly moved to >40% of revenue and is tracking toward parity with consumer by end-2026, on 9M+ paying business users (per press citations, Feb 2026). "Why now": the departure lands as OpenAI (a) eyes an IPO, (b) is re-pivoting from a cooling consumer/ChatGPT engine toward coding + enterprise agents (GPT-5.6 + "ChatGPT Work" launched the same day), and (c) is losing the coding race to Anthropic — the exact battleground the CEO of Applications org was meant to win.

**Competitive landscape.** KPIs for this layer: enterprise seats & ARPU, product-ship cadence, and coding-tool run-rate/share. Simo's "CEO of Applications" role (created May 2025) consolidated business + product, with COO Lightcap, CFO Friar and CPO Weil reporting to her while Altman stepped back to research/compute/safety (note text). Her permanent exit unwinds that consolidation: responsibilities reportedly split across president Greg Brockman, CFO Sarah Friar and CSO Jason Kwon, with CRO Denise Dresser (ex-Slack CEO) a candidate for a wider remit (TechCrunch/Axios/CNBC, 2026-07). Competitive position on the core enterprise/coding fight is *catching up, not ahead*: Anthropic's share of AI coding rose to ~54% by end-2025 vs OpenAI's ~21%, and Claude Code hit ~$2.5bn run-rate by Feb 2026 (per CB Insights / getpanto / Business of Apps). Moat is intangibles + distribution (ChatGPT's ~consumer reach), not switching costs — and the consumer engine is the part that "cooled late last year, missing internal revenue targets" (note text). `(analysis)` the moat sits on the weaker of its two legs right now.

**Comps & multiples.** No public trading multiples — OpenAI is private with essentially no IR disclosure (ir_coverage.json: 1 funding_announcement, no financials); EV/Revenue, P/E = **no data** / `[UNSOURCED]`. As a private sanity check only: ~$852bn valuation / ~$25bn annualized rev ≈ **34x** revenue — extreme even for hyper-growth and only defensible if triple-digit growth holds while enterprise mix improves; a governance vacuum is precisely what erodes that justification. Internal comps (governance/execution precedents, not valuation): [[Fiserv President Dhivya Suryadevara resigns]] (senior operator exit creating an execution gap at a scaled franchise); [[Revolut Trading UK CEO steps down amid wealth push]] (leadership churn mid strategic pivot); [[Paystack fires co-founder Ezra Olubi]] (top-team departure at a fast-scaling private).

**Risk flags.**
1. *Execution/continuity vacuum* — the single owner who unified product + business + revenue is gone with no named successor; splitting her scope across three execs (Brockman/Friar/Kwon) risks slower decisions and diffused accountability right as the enterprise pivot needs a driver.
2. *Thin, churning bench* — Simo caps an 18-month exodus (Murati, Sutskever, Schulman, McGrew, Weil, Peebles, Rouch; only 2 of 11 co-founders remain, per TechCrunch/CNBC). Key-person risk concentrates further on Altman/Brockman heading into an IPO where governance depth is scrutinized.
3. *Losing the fight she was hired to win* — competitor already leads coding (~54% vs ~21%) and enterprise preference; leadership disruption at the commercial org hands Anthropic time on the exact axis where OpenAI trails.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` For the sector, this reinforces that the frontier-AI contest is now an *execution/commercialization* race, not a model-only race — and OpenAI just lost its commercialization quarterback mid-pivot. Falsifiable thesis: if OpenAI names a credible single successor (e.g., Dresser) with consolidated P&L ownership within ~1–2 quarters AND enterprise/coding share stabilizes, the vacuum is a blip; watch for (trigger) either a named Applications/President-of-commercial hire before any IPO roadshow, or continued share loss to Anthropic in coding — the latter would confirm the thesis that the org can't out-execute its rival while its top seat is unfilled.
