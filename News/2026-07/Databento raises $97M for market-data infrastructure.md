Christina Qi spent a decade making money for billionaires,  building Domeyard LP—a high-frequency trading hedge fund that traded up to $7.1 billion a day. Then, she shut the whole thing down to start Databento, a market data infrastructure company.
Databento’s pitch is almost comically unsexy compared to her last venture: sell financial data feeds to the same banks and hedge funds she’d left. The startup just raised a $97 million Series B led by NEA, Fortune learned exclusively, with participation from DRW Venture Capital, Redpoint Ventures, and Tribe Capital. The round drew over $300 million in demand, the company said. And Qi is running it all from a farm in Utah.
With only 24 employees, Databento is already profitable—a combination so unusual that, by Qi’s own admission, her investors keep telling her to just spend more money.
“We haven’t really touched much of that $97 million,” she told Fortune . “Our investors are telling us, spend, spend money, and we’re trying. We’re buying servers, buying stuff like crazy, and yet we’re still profitable every month.”
The raise brings Databento’s total disclosed funding to around $127 million . No valuation has been made public, but the capital is already earmarked: the company plans to expand from its current servers housed directly inside stock exchanges to more than 20 data centers worldwide, and has secured an additional 100-plus petabytes of storage—more than doubling its previous footprint.
The problem Databento is solving has bothered anyone who has ever tried to get institutional-grade market data. Qi recalls sending more than 100 emails over 11 months just to procure data from the largest provider on the planet (Qi wouldn’t say directly, but I can: Bloomberg). Sample data arrived on a thumb drive, via snail mail. Databento’s answer is a service where users can add Microsoft stock data to a cart like an e-commerce order and pay only for what they use—a deliberate contrast to Bloomberg Terminals running $20,000 to $27,000 per seat per year and LSEG’s Refinitiv at comparable rates. Meanwhile, global financial market data spending hit $49.2 billion in 2025, and it’s still growing.
Databento’s edge is partially technical. The company uses specialized chips to capture and clean raw price data (every single buy and sell order, in real-time) from 80-plus trading venues worldwide. It is the only vendor delivering that full picture over a regular internet connection. But the real lever is reach: 400 to 500 people sign up daily, ranging from UC Berkeley students vibe coding trading strategies in their dorm rooms to the Abu Dhabi Investment Authority. 
“90% of revenue does come from the large financial institutions and AI labs,” Qi said. “But the beauty is we’re able to attract the best of both worlds.” That includes Nvidia , which Qi confirmed is both a paying customer and technology partner. OpenAI is also a customer.
Databento’s greatest risk is the one that’s taken down most other scrappy data startups: getting acquired before they could become generational companies. In 2022, MayStreet sold to LSEG, and last year BML was acquired at Series B for around $250 million—exactly where Databento is now. “We’re going to bring this way past a billion,” she said. “This is just the starting line.”

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- **The fact:** Databento (Salt Lake City / Utah, founded 2019 by ex-Domeyard HFT founder Christina Qi) raised a **$97M Series B led by NEA**, with DRW Venture Capital, Redpoint Ventures, Tribe Capital participating. Total disclosed funding now **~$127M** (pre-seed $3.3M 2019, seed $4.25M 2021, Series A built up to $30M through Oct-2024). Round reportedly **oversubscribed >3x** ($300M+ demand). **No valuation disclosed** — a material omission (see [3]).
- **What it actually is:** a growth-equity round into an *already-profitable* company (24 employees, profitable every month per Qi). This is unusual for a Series B and reframes the story: this is **not** "startup needs cash to survive/scale to break-even" but "insiders want a bigger cap table position in a proven, cash-generative asset." The "we haven't touched the $97M... investors tell us to spend" line is a flex, but also a tell: **the money is optionality/land-grab capital, not operating necessity.**
- **Earmarked use:** expand from servers colocated inside exchanges to **20+ data centers worldwide** (currently building EU + APAC coverage, targeted within ~6 months); **100+ petabytes additional storage** (>2x prior footprint). This is a **capex/coverage** story — buying the physical footprint to capture direct exchange feeds globally.
- **Why structured this way:** Leading with NEA (a large crossover/growth fund, not a pure seed shop) + DRW (a proprietary trading firm — i.e. a *customer-adjacent strategic*) signals the round is validation from inside the trading ecosystem. DRW's presence is the interesting tell: a major HFT/market-maker backing its own data vendor. **Flag:** the "$300M demand / >3x oversubscribed" and "985% revenue surge" (that figure is from the *2024* Series A year, not necessarily current) are the classic momentum framings — treat the 985% as a 2023→2024 base-effect number off a tiny base, not a durable growth rate.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Incumbents (the real target):** Bloomberg (~$20–27k/seat/yr Terminal), **LSEG/Refinitiv** (comparable), **S&P Global Market Intelligence**, **ICE Data Services** (now largely under LSEG partnerships / ICE's own feeds), FactSet, Nasdaq. Bloomberg + LSEG together capture **~half** of a **$49.2B (2025) global market-data market** growing ~6.4–6.5%/yr. Americas = ~50% of spend.
- **Direct API-first challengers (Databento's real cohort):** **Polygon.io (rebranded "Massive")** — retail/prosumer flat-rate ($199/mo tier), Atlanta, VC-backed; **Alpaca, IEX Cloud, Alpha Vantage, Intrinio, Tick Data, Kaiko** (crypto). Databento differentiates on **institutional-grade tick/L3 (MBO) order-book fidelity, nanosecond timestamps, direct colocated feeds from 60–80+ venues** — the "we deliver the full raw picture over regular internet" claim. Polygon/Massive is broader/cheaper but explicitly *not* an HFT-grade replay engine → the two occupy different tiers, not a head-to-head.
- **The precedent that matters (M&A comps):** **MayStreet → LSEG (2022)** and **BML acquired at Series B for ~$250M** (per Qi). This maps Databento's likely near-term exit valuation zone (~$250M+) and is the risk Qi herself names: scrappy data vendors get **acquired by an incumbent before becoming generational**.
- **Why the landscape is this way (2nd order):** Market data is a **rents business** — incumbents monetize *access + entitlements + redistribution licensing*, not raw bytes. The exchanges themselves (CME, Nasdaq, ICE) capture the primary economics of proprietary feeds and have been *raising* data fees. So Databento sits **downstream of exchange pricing power**: it can undercut Bloomberg's *packaging/UX*, but it cannot escape paying the exchanges for the underlying feeds. That caps how disruptive the pricing model can truly be.

## [2] Company history / fit
- Trajectory: Qi co-founded **Domeyard LP** (HFT fund, traded up to $7.1B/day), wound it down to start Databento — pitch: sell data to the same institutions. Pre-seed 2019 → seed 2021 → Series A $30M (topped up Oct-2024, with Belvedere Trading, Clear Street, Lightscape) → Series B $97M (Jul-2026).
- **Fit / why it acts this way:** The whole company is an *arbitrage on incumbent friction* — Qi's origin story (100+ emails / 11 months to buy data from "the largest provider," sample data mailed on a thumb drive — clearly Bloomberg) is the product thesis. The e-commerce "add MSFT to cart, pay-per-use" model is a deliberate structural bet that **procurement friction, not data quality, is the incumbents' weak flank.** The 24-employee / profitable posture is core to the pitch: it proves the model doesn't need a Bloomberg-scale sales/support org.
- **Structural pressure driving the raise:** a data business's moat is **coverage breadth + latency + entitlement licensing**. To move upmarket beyond US futures/equities into a global multi-asset Bloomberg alternative, Databento *must* buy physical colocation in 20+ centers now — coverage is a land-grab where being second to a venue is being nowhere. Hence capex-heavy use of funds despite profitability.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new:** self-service, pay-per-use, API-first delivery of **institutional-grade raw tick/order-book data over the public internet** (vs cross-connects/leased lines). That combination — full raw feed fidelity + consumption pricing + no procurement gauntlet — is a real product delta, not just cheaper Bloomberg.
- **Traction (the credible part):** **profitable at 24 people**; **400–500 signups/day**; customers span UC Berkeley students to Abu Dhabi Investment Authority; **90% of revenue from large financial institutions + AI labs**; **Nvidia** (paying customer *and* tech partner) and **OpenAI** confirmed customers; secondary sources cite ~3,000+ firms and ~97% enterprise retention. The **AI-lab customer base is the fresh, non-obvious signal**: LLM/quant-AI training and eval increasingly consume clean historical + real-time market data → Databento is riding an AI-data-demand tailwind, not just displacing Bloomberg seats.
- **Why the value-add is real *but bounded* (2nd order):** Databento captures margin in the **packaging/delivery layer**, not the data itself. The **exchanges own the underlying feed economics** and are raising fees → Databento is a reseller with better UX, structurally squeezed between exchange input costs and customer willingness to pay. Its durable edge is the **engineering** (feed handlers for 70–80 venues, colocation, chip-accelerated capture/cleaning) — genuinely hard, but *replicable by a well-funded incumbent* and *appropriable via acquisition*. **The "no valuation disclosed" is the key silence:** for an oversubscribed, profitable company, hiding the number usually means either (a) it's high and they don't want to anchor an acquirer, or (b) it's flat-ish vs demand and they'd rather not say. **(analysis/hypothesis.)**
- **The central question shifts** from "can Databento out-price Bloomberg?" (unit economics say only partially — exchange fees are the floor) to **"can it stay independent long enough to become a platform, or does it become MayStreet 2.0 — an infra acquisition for LSEG/ICE/Nasdaq at ~$250M–$1B?"** Qi explicitly frames acquisition as the *risk*, not the goal ("bring this way past a billion... just the starting line").

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Plans:** 20+ global data centers within ~6 months; EU + APAC coverage; +100PB storage; broaden asset-class coverage (some sources flag **crypto** market data as an expansion vector). Ambition: multi-asset, global, Bloomberg-alternative infrastructure.
- **Sentiment:** genuinely positive/hyped (>3x oversubscribed, Nvidia/OpenAI logos, profitable-at-24). Backdrop: **$49.2B and growing market-data market** with a structural shift toward real-time + AI-consumable data — a rising tide.
- **Risks / why the market may go this way (2nd order):**
  1. **Acquisition before scale** — the base rate for this cohort (MayStreet, BML) is exit into an incumbent; NEA/DRW may *prefer* a fast strategic exit to a decade-long independent build. Capital concentration + strategic-investor cap table can make an "independence" story *more* fragile, not less.
  2. **Exchange-fee squeeze** — if venues keep raising proprietary-feed fees, Databento's pricing advantage erodes from the input side.
  3. **AI-lab revenue durability** — a chunk of the momentum is AI labs buying training/eval data; if that's project-based rather than recurring operational spend, the "985%/6.65x" growth normalizes hard.
  4. **Incumbent response** — Bloomberg/LSEG can bolt on consumption pricing or acquire a challenger; the moat is engineering + coverage, not a patent.

**Sector & drivers.** Global financial market-data spending hit a record $49.2bn in 2025, +6.5% YoY (per Burton-Taylor/TP ICAP, via Traders Magazine/marketsmedia; 2024 was $44.3bn, +6.4%) — the note's $49.2bn figure checks out. Structure is highly consolidated: the top tier — Bloomberg (~$11bn total revenue, >85% from Terminals, ~325k seats at $31,980/yr for 2026; per Wikipedia/godeldiscount, as of 2026), LSEG Data & Analytics (~$5bn ann. run-rate, +4.9% organic Q3-25; per LSEG/ainvest), and ICE Fixed Income & Data Services (+5% in 2025; per ICE 10-K FY2025) — captures the bulk. Barriers are real: exchange colocation, licensing relationships, and reference/consolidated-feed IP (LSEG says ~90% of data/feeds revenue is IP-shielded). "Why now": (1) the incumbent price umbrella ($20–32k/seat) leaves room for a usage-based challenger; (2) AI labs are a genuinely new demand pool — Databento names Nvidia, OpenAI as customers; incumbents are monetizing the same trend (LSEG signed OpenAI/Microsoft/Databricks/Anthropic distribution deals in 2025), so this is an industry-wide re-pricing of data-for-AI, not a Databento-only tailwind (analysis).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: seats/subscribers, Annual Subscription Value (ASV) growth, logo retention, data-feed coverage (venues/asset classes). Basis of competition = coverage + latency + pricing model + distribution. Incumbents sell bundled per-seat/enterprise licences; Databento's wedge is pay-as-you-go e-commerce-style provisioning + full order-book capture from 80+ venues over standard internet — a genuinely differentiated delivery model, not just a discount. Reported traction (per company, via SiliconANGLE/Utah Business): revenue 6.65x YoY, >97% enterprise logo retention since inception, 400–500 daily sign-ups, profitable at 24 employees. Recent peer/adjacent moves in-base: Nvidia/OpenAI as data buyers signals AI-lab pull; incumbents defending via AI-distribution deals (LSEG, 2025). Position: niche challenger scaling fast, moat still forming — real edge is delivery tech + the fact it's the only vendor pushing full-depth data over ordinary internet (analysis); switching costs and licensing depth still favor incumbents.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Acquisition-before-scale** — the company's own stated top risk (MayStreet→LSEG 2022; BML at ~$250M at Series B). A rich sector with deep-pocketed incumbents makes a takeout at low-hundreds-of-millions the base-case exit, capping the "generational" thesis; incumbents can also just cut usage-based pricing to blunt the wedge.
2. **Customer/segment concentration** — "90% of revenue from large financial institutions and AI labs" (per company). AI-lab demand is early-cycle and lumpy; a training-data pullback or an incumbent locking up an exclusive feed deal hits a concentrated top line.
3. **Exchange/licensing dependence** — the model rests on colocation and redistribution rights from 80+ venues (exchanges are also the incumbents' partners/owners, e.g. ICE). Venues raising data fees or tightening redistribution terms captures Databento's margin from the layer beneath it (disintermediation risk running upward).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Signals the market-data stack is unbundling on the delivery/pricing axis — usage-based, self-serve provisioning against the $20–32k/seat terminal umbrella — with AI labs as a new buyer class the incumbents are also racing for (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if Databento sustains multi-x revenue growth without a strategic takeout through the next raise, it validates a standalone pay-as-you-go data-infra category; trigger to watch = next round/valuation disclosure or an incumbent (LSEG/ICE/Bloomberg) either acquiring it or launching a matching consumption-priced feed. Thesis breaks if the AI-lab cohort proves non-recurring or a takeout lands first.
