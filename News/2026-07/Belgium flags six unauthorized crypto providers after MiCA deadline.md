# Context-enrichment: Belgium's FSMA flags six unauthorized crypto providers after MiCA deadline
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**Freshness verdict: FRESH.** No prior FSMA / Belgian-warning note exists in the corpus, and this is a discrete national-enforcement event (a scam-list update), not a re-run of the EU-level MiCA framework notes we already hold ([[AMINA secures MiCA license in Austria]], [[Circle warns EU stablecoin rules risk dual licensing by March 2026]]). It is, however, a *low-weight* fresh item — a routine consumer-protection warning, not a landmark enforcement action.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- On **Mon 2026-07-06** Belgium's **FSMA** (Financial Services and Markets Authority) published a warning naming **six** entities offering crypto-asset services without CASP authorization: **Aurum Foundation, Bank Bit, Bithf Pro, Dxago, Global Dynamic Trade, ZeriaFunding** (sites: aurum.foundation, bank-bit.com, bitfpro.org, dxago.com, globaldynamictrade.org, zeriafunding.com). All six were added to the FSMA's **list of fraudulent CASPs**; the FSMA stressed the list is non-exhaustive and updated regularly, and urged users to check the official CASP register first. (cryptotimes, cointelegraph, LeapRate, blockonomi)
- **De-PR / reframe:** the digest framing ("post-MiCA-deadline enforcement begins") over-reads the event. These are **six unknown, almost-certainly-scam websites** on a fraud blacklist — not real EU exchanges being wound down. This is a **routine consumer-protection warning** (FSMA publishes these continuously) that has been *tagged* to the MiCA deadline for news value. → **Why it matters:** the second-order signal — "national regulators are now active post-1-Jul" — is real, but this specific action is weak evidence of it; naming six fraud sites is not the same as forcing licensed-but-unauthorized incumbents out.
- **Timing anchor:** the EU's MiCA **transitional period for CASPs ended EU-wide on 2026-07-01** (grandfathering from national regimes). New providers have needed CASP status since **2024-12-30**. So the warning lands days after the hard deadline — hence the framing. (elliptic, AMF/ESMA, zyphe)

## [1] Competitors / peers (other regulators' enforcement)
- **ESMA** (EU-level) set expectations that national competent authorities enforce post-deadline and subjected last-minute applications to heightened scrutiny; warned retail investors to verify against the interim MiCA register. (AMF, ESMA)
- **France (AMF):** required DASPs unable to comply to have an **orderly wind-down plan in place from 2026-03-30**. **Germany and Ireland** closed their transitional periods early, on **2025-12-31** — i.e. months ahead of the EU backstop. (AMF)
- **Position:** Belgium is **in the pack, not ahead**. Germany/Ireland moved earlier; France pre-staged wind-downs in March. Belgium's move is a same-week fraud-list update, which is the *softest* form of enforcement (a warning, not a licence revocation or a wind-down order). → **Why the lay of the land is this way:** MiCA deliberately let each member state set the transition length (up to 18 months); the divergence (Dec-2025 vs Jul-2026) means "one EU rulebook" still has **staggered national enforcement**, and headline "first enforcement" claims are largely a function of who publishes a warning first, not who is toughest.

## [2] Company/regulator history / fit
- The **FSMA runs a standing "companies operating unlawfully in Belgium" warning list** and publishes such alerts routinely (forex/CFD scams historically). Slotting six crypto sites onto that existing machinery is fully consistent with its established modus operandi — low incremental effort, high communications value around the deadline. → **Why it acts this way:** post-deadline, FSMA has clean legal footing (no CASP licence = unlawful), so re-purposing its fraud-list channel is the path of least resistance to *signal* enforcement without yet pursuing costly formal proceedings.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty: low.** Neither the six named firms (obscure, likely scam domains) nor the mechanism (fraud-list warning) is new. What is genuinely new is only the **post-1-Jul legal basis** to treat *any* unauthorized crypto service as unlawful. No numbers on user harm, funds at risk, or enforcement volume were disclosed → **no traction data**. (analysis)
- **Value-add / who captures it:** the real EU story is disintermediation of non-compliant offshore providers toward a small set of MiCA-licensed CASPs (AMINA, licensed exchanges, banks). A six-name blacklist barely moves that; the durable signal is the **register**, not the warning. (hypothesis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Expect a **rolling stream** of similar national warnings and blacklist additions through H2-2026 as NCAs work through non-compliant providers; the substantive enforcement (cease-and-desist orders, revocations, fines) is what to watch, not fraud-list PR.
- **Backdrop risk (counterintuitive second-order):** MiCA's fragmentation — different transition end-dates, plus the stablecoin dual-licensing friction Circle flagged ([[Circle warns EU stablecoin rules risk dual licensing by March 2026]]) — means "compliant EU crypto" consolidates into fewer, larger licensed players, which is bullish for incumbents like AMINA ([[AMINA secures MiCA license in Austria]]) but raises concentration/gatekeeping concerns. This Belgian warning is a minor data point in that arc.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** MiCA crypto-regulation / enforcement, EU. The transitional ("grandfathering") window for pre-existing crypto-asset service providers (CASPs) closed for good on 1 July 2026, with no extension mechanism in the regulation; new providers have needed CASP authorisation since 30 Dec 2024 (per ESMA / Elvinger Hoss, via elliptic.co, as of Jul 2026). Adoption is thin: as of 6 Jul 2026 only ~283 CASPs are MiCA-authorised across 25 EU/EEA states, and by earlier counts only ~210 of 1,200+ firms were licensed at the deadline — i.e. ~80–83% of previously-active firms are now unlicensed / in breach if still serving EU clients (per casptracker.eu / Eastern Herald, as of Jul 2026). Structure: passport-based single market (one home-state licence passports EU-wide), so licensing is highly concentrated — Germany 59, France 31, Netherlands 28, Malta 22, Cyprus 21; top-5 ≈ 60% of entries; Belgium's home register holds just 1 authorised CASP (per casptracker.eu, as of 6 Jul 2026). Driver / "why now": the deadline flipped supervision from "apply-or-wind-down" to active enforcement, and FSMA's fraud-list warning is one of the first visible post-deadline national actions — the barrier to legal operation is now regulatory (licence + home-state supervision), not capital or product.

**Competitive landscape.** This is a regulator action, not a company; the relevant "KPIs" for the enforcement layer are # authorised CASPs, # public warnings / fraud-list additions, and register coverage. FSMA named six firms — Aurum Foundation, Bank Bit, Bithf Pro, Dxago, Global Dynamic Trade, ZeriaFunding — added to its fraudulent-CASP list and urged users to check the official CASP register (per Cointelegraph / LeapRate, as of 6 Jul 2026); it flagged the list may grow. Peer regulator moves in-base, same MiCA cycle: France's AMF warned firms to license by 30 Jun ([[France's AMF warns crypto firms to get MiCA licence by June 30]]); the transitional window's close was pre-flagged ([[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]]); and enforcement teeth are visible in [[Binance faces loss of EU access over Greece MiCA licence]]. Basis of "competition" between NCAs is regulatory attractiveness/speed — Malta, Netherlands, Cyprus, France, Ireland are the crypto centre of gravity, while much of Germany's lead is banks/brokers taking narrow permissions (analysis, per casptracker.eu). Belgium's position: peripheral as a licensing hub (1 CASP) but active on the consumer-protection / enforcement side.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round/deal in this pick — protagonist is a regulator, so trading multiples = no data / not applicable. Internal comps (MiCA-authorisation & enforcement precedents in-base): [[Coinbase secures MiCA licence to serve EU crypto customers]], [[Ripple receives preliminary MiCA CASP licence in Luxembourg]], [[Flowdesk Europe gets AMF CASP authorization under MiCA]], [[Triple-A secures MiCA CASP licence from France's AMF]], [[OpenPayd lands MiCA approval for EU crypto services]], [[BVNK secures MiCA licence in Malta]], [[AMINA secures MiCA license in Austria]] (licensing side); [[Binance faces loss of EU access over Greece MiCA licence]], [[FCA sues crypto exchange HTX over illegal UK promotions]] (enforcement side). Distribution not computed — no financial figures; qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. Enforcement is fragmented and warning-only so far — FSMA's tool here is a public fraud-list warning, not a fine or shutdown; with ~80% of prior firms unlicensed EU-wide, national watchdogs face a large enforcement backlog and uneven capacity, so consumer exposure to unauthorised offshore CASPs persists (second-order: warnings without cross-border takedown power leave gaps that scam operators exploit).
2. Regulatory-arbitrage / passporting risk — because one home-state licence passports EU-wide, supervision quality concentrates in a few fast-licensing hubs (Malta, Cyprus); a weak-link NCA can import risk into Belgium and other states that themselves granted almost no licences (second-order: "race to license" pressure vs supervisory rigour).
3. Named-firm list is low-signal on scale — the six named firms are unknown-size operators with no disclosed users/volumes; the warning signals enforcement intent more than it removes a material market player, and could be gamed by rebranding/new domains (second-order: whack-a-mole enforcement).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) Marks the transition from MiCA rule-making to MiCA enforcement — expect a wave of similar national fraud-list warnings through H2 2026 and consolidation toward the ~283 licensed CASPs, squeezing unlicensed retail-facing operators out of legal EU access. Falsifiable thesis: if enforcement stays warning-only (no fines/blocking) into Q4 2026, the ~80% unlicensed tail keeps serving EU users via offshore rails and the deadline proves largely symbolic. Trigger to watch: first monetary penalty or access-blocking (not just a warning) by an NCA against a post-deadline unlicensed CASP.
