# Turkey pursues membership in Single Euro Payments Area

> [!info] 2026-07-02 · 1 упоминаний · 0 источника(ов) с текстом

[Connecting the Dots in Fintech] 🇹🇷 Turkey is pursuing membership in the Single Euro Payments Area, with government and financial institutions working on the initiative. Joining SEPA would enable faster, lower-cost, and more secure cross-border euro payments between Turkey and participating European countries.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)

On 1 July 2026, Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan said (at a press conference with his Kyrgyz counterpart) that Turkey is "interested" in joining SEPA and that "relevant financial institutions are already working on the issue." Per Turkish Minute (2 Jul 2026), the Finance Ministry has gone one step further and sent a **letter of intent** to join SEPA. That is the actual event behind this note — not a new EU offer.

**De-PR'd:** This is a stated intention plus a letter of intent, NOT an application, accession or membership. No timeline, no eligibility ruling by the European Payments Council (EPC), no confirmed regulatory-alignment plan. The note's aggregated line ("government and financial institutions working on the initiative") is a soft restatement of Fidan's remark and does not mention the letter of intent.

**Why this framing matters (→ why it's structured this way):** The sequencing is the whole story. In February 2026 the EU (Commissioner Marta Kos → FM Fidan) *offered* Turkey SEPA membership; that was reported ~19-20 Mar 2026 (see [[EU proposes Turkey join SEPA to cut euro transfer costs]]). At that time Turkey's response was explicitly "unclear" and the Finance Ministry "declined to comment." The July development is Turkey's **affirmative response** to that March offer — a "proposal → yes, we're interested + letter of intent" follow-up. So this note is a genuine progression of the same thread, not an independent event; the substance is diplomatic signalling, not payments-rail activation.

## [1] Competitors / peers (prior SEPA entrants)

SEPA covers 41 countries. The relevant precedents for a **non-EU/non-EEA** entrant:
- **Albania, Moldova, Montenegro, North Macedonia** — four EU-candidate Balkan states admitted by EPC Board decision (21 Nov 2024), operational from **5 Oct 2025**. EU estimates combined user savings up to EUR 500m. These are the closest peers to Turkey and the template it would follow.
- **United Kingdom** — post-Brexit, the EPC board (7 Mar 2019) kept the UK in SEPA's geographic scope from 1 Feb 2020. UK Finance had to file a country legal opinion demonstrating that UK-onshored EU law met the required standards. Shows the *legal-opinion + strong-economic-relationship* bar for non-EEA members.

**Why the lay of the land matters (→ second order):** SEPA extension to non-EEA countries is governed by EPC's "Criteria for the extension of the SEPA geographical scope," which require a demonstrable "strong economic and legal relationship with the EU" plus market/operational/regulatory alignment (PSD2-equivalent, AML, data protection, ISO 20022, adherence to the SCT/SDD rulebooks). Turkey clears the *economic* test easily (EU is its largest trading partner, >EUR 200bn bilateral trade; large diaspora). The binding constraints are the *legal/regulatory* ones — AML and data-protection alignment — which is exactly where Turkey has historically diverged from EU standards. So Turkey is not "catching up" technically; it is a political-will and rule-alignment question.

## [2] Company/country history / fit

EU-Turkey accession talks have been effectively frozen for years; the customs-union modernisation is stalled (Turkey publicly blames Cyprus for blocking the upgrade, per July 2026 reporting). SEPA is being used by both sides as a **low-politics integration channel** that delivers concrete economic benefit without touching the deadlocked accession file. That is why the EU pitched it and why Turkey is receptive: it is deeper integration "short of accession."

**Why Turkey acts this way (→ structural logic):** For Turkey, SEPA is cheap goodwill with a real payoff — the diaspora and exporters benefit, and it signals Western alignment without accession commitments. The friction is domestic: Turkish banks currently earn fee revenue on cross-border euro transfers (a EUR 1,000-5,000 transfer can cost ~EUR 40 today via services like Western Union); SEPA would compress those margins to near-domestic levels. So the FM's enthusiasm is not automatically the banking sector's — a classic "who's silent" tension the PR frame hides.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction

**Novelty vs the March note: incremental but real.** March = EU *offers*. July = Turkey *says yes + letter of intent*. That is a status change (proposal → expressed intent), so it is fresh — but it is a **low-materiality** step: intent ≠ application ≠ eligibility ruling ≠ accession. No live capability, no ISO 20022 migration, no EPC evaluation started publicly, no date.

**Value-add reality (→ deeper):** The economic prize is genuine (near-zero-cost euro transfers for a huge diaspora and >EUR 200bn trade corridor), but the value only crystallises at the *end* of a multi-year rule-alignment process. Who captures the delta? Consumers and exporters gain; Turkish banks lose remittance fee income; PSPs/fintechs able to plug into SEPA rails (à la BVNK, Papaya direct SEPA access) would gain a new corridor. The "traction" here is zero — this is entirely announcement-stage signalling.

## [4] What's next / sentiment

Next real milestones to watch: (1) a formal application/eligibility submission to the EPC; (2) an EPC Board eligibility decision and an Operational Readiness Date (the Balkan four took roughly Nov-2024 decision → Oct-2025 go-live, ~11 months, and they are EU candidates with closer alignment); (3) Turkish legislative alignment on PSD-equivalent rules, AML and data protection. Given Turkey's regulatory distance from EU AML/data-protection norms and frozen accession politics, a realistic go-live is years out, not 2026. The European Commission has said it is ready to support the alignment (self-interested framing).

**Counterintuitive second order:** SEPA could advance *precisely because* full accession is dead — it is the one integration lever with no political veto attached (unlike customs union, which Cyprus can block). But the same political fragility means it can also be used as leverage and stall. Base case: slow, real, but not imminent.

13. What is the precise mechanism delta for a Turkish user? → Euro transfers to/from SEPA at domestic rates and speeds vs current cross-border fees/latency — but only after go-live, which does not exist yet.
14. Is there any counterparty confirmation from the EPC or ECB? → No public EPC/ECB acknowledgement of a Turkish application in these sources. (open)

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a payments-rails / regulatory-access story, not a company round. SEPA covers 41 countries (per European Commission, via The Paypers, 20 Mar 2026). The EU–Turkey corridor is large: bilateral trade >EUR 200bn/yr (per Reuters/The Paypers, Mar 2026); Turkey's remittance & cross-border payments market ~USD 8bn (per Ken Research, undated, treat as indicative). The event de-PR'd: on 2026-07-02 Turkey's Finance Minister Mehmet Simsek said Turkey submitted a **letter of intent** to join SEPA, after the Turkey–EU High-Level Economic Dialogue with EU Commissioner Dombrovskis in Istanbul (per Turkish Minute / Daily Sabah, 02 Jul 2026). This is a follow-up to the EU's Feb 2026 offer (see internal comp) — so status advanced from "EU proposes" to "Turkey formally signals intent", but **not** to accession: alignment with the Payment Services Directive, AML and data-protection frameworks remains a prerequisite, and PSD compliance is a multi-year lift (analysis). "Why now": EU-Turkey accession talks are stalled; SEPA is a lower-friction integration channel short of full membership (per The Paypers, Mar 2026).

**Competitive landscape.** Rail-level KPIs: per-transfer cost and settlement time. Today a EUR 1,000–5,000 Turkey↔Europe transfer costs up to EUR ~40 via services like Western Union (Reuters, cited Mar 2026). Precedent from the 2025 accession cohort quantifies the shift: in Montenegro the average SEPA-transaction cost was **EUR 6.15** (Oct 2025–Jan 2026) vs **EUR 73.4** in 2024 — a ~92% drop (per World Bank, 25 Mar 2026). EBRD's president said the system could make transactions "basically cost-free" (via Turkish Minute, Mar 2026). Losers if adopted: Turkish banks and remittance/FX incumbents (Western Union, MoneyGram, bank wire desks) that currently monetise the corridor spread; the note itself flags banks "currently generate revenues on cross-border transfer fees that SEPA membership would reduce". Turkish fintechs (Papara — ~20-23m users, ~USD 2bn valuation, remittances to 100+ countries incl. EU, per PitchBook/Fintech Intel; Param; iyzico/PayTR) would gain cheaper EUR rails but lose FX-margin economics on the EU leg (analysis). Note Papara carries idiosyncratic overhang: in 2025 its licence was revoked by the CBRT amid an illegal-betting/AML probe, stayed in December (per Fintech Intel) — a reminder that Turkish AML enforcement is exactly the area SEPA entry stresses.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/multiple applies — this is a regulatory-access event, not a transaction. Comparable **accessions** rather than trading comps:
- Balkan cohort — Albania, Moldova, Montenegro, North Macedonia joined SEPA schemes in Oct 2025 (geographic scope entered Nov 2024 / Mar 2025); EU estimates combined savings up to **EUR 500m** for users (per European Commission / Euronews, 16 Oct 2025). Montenegro realised cost per transaction EUR 73.4 → EUR 6.15 (World Bank).
- Internal comps: [[EU proposes Turkey join SEPA to cut euro transfer costs]] (2026-03, the originating proposal — same story arc, prior stage); [[Blik runs first cross-border test between Portugal and Spain]] (2025-11, non-euro-area A2A reaching into SEPA); [[ECB advances cross-border payments link with India and Asia]] (2025-11, TIPS cross-currency interlinking — the ECB's parallel "cheaper cross-border" agenda). Corridor size / diaspora savings figure specific to Turkey: distribution not computed, qualitative comparison — no per-corridor volume disclosed.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Announced ≠ live.** A letter of intent is an early signal; accession requires full PSD/AML/GDPR alignment which Turkey has not completed, and the Finance Ministry earlier declined to commit (Mar 2026). Timeline and completion are unsourced — treat as multi-year, reversible.
2. **AML/data-protection gate is the binding constraint.** Turkey's AML track record (the Papara case; broader FATF scrutiny) is precisely what the EU must sign off; a compliance shortfall could stall or condition entry (analysis).
3. **Incumbent margin disintermediation.** Turkish banks and remittance/FX players earn the corridor spread SEPA compresses (~EUR 40 → near-zero, by the Montenegro analogue). This is upside for users/fintechs but a revenue hit for banks — and gives domestic banks a lobbying incentive to slow-walk implementation (analysis).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** If completed, SEPA membership re-rails the EUR leg of a >EUR 200bn trade + large-diaspora corridor from ~EUR 40 fixed cost toward near-zero, structurally shrinking the FX/remittance take on Turkey↔EU and favouring app-native players over wire desks (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: watch for a **formal accession application + European Payments Council scheme adhesion** (not just intent) and a published PSD-alignment roadmap within ~12 months; absence of those, or a Finance Ministry walk-back, would show this stays a diplomatic signal rather than an executed rail change. Trigger to track: Turkish banks' disclosed cross-border fee revenue and any EPC adherence date.
