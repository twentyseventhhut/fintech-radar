# Mercor acquires AI simulation startup Deeptune

Brendan Foody started Mercor when he was 19 years old. Now he’s 23, worth billions on paper, and running one of the fastest-growing companies in AI history. On Thursday, his $10 billion business acquired startup Deeptune, a company Foody quietly helped fund months before buying it, Fortune learned exclusively.
 Deeptune , an Andreessen Horowitz-backed startup, builds simulation environments where AI agents practice real-world tasks before touching production systems—think a flight simulator, but for AI agents learning how to use Excel, Salesforce , and Slack. Financial terms weren’t disclosed. And according to Mercor, Deeptune’s team will joining Mercor’s team in New York.
Foody was listed as an angel investor in Deeptune’s $43 million Series A just three months before the acquisition closed. Foody told me the angel check was written with acquisition already in mind: “It was in a lot of ways the main motivation, actually.” Call it relationship-building or call it a strategic hold. Either way, a16z funded Foody’s future acquisition target, and Foody got a front-row seat before making his move.
The deal is also a direct answer to where the AI training market is heading. Frontier labs (and Mercor customers) like Anthropic and OpenAI now need full digital replicas of enterprise software environments where their agents can practice, fail, and learn. Mercor’s network of more than five million domain experts already builds the tasks and scoring rubrics that tell a model whether it did the job right. Deeptune builds the apps those tasks run inside. Together, they cover the full stack.
Foody claims Mercor is already the market leader in building these so-called reinforcement learning environments—and that all of the Mag Seven except Tesla are customers. The company hit $2 billion in ARR in June, up from $1 billion last year. Foody described the jump as “the fastest growth trajectory ever” from $1 million to $2 billion ARR in 24 months.
Mercor’s acquisition comes on the heels of a massive data breach in March. Hackers exploited a supply chain vulnerability in LiteLLM —an open-source Python library widely used to build AI tools—and exfiltrated an estimated four terabytes of Mercor’s data . This allegedly included tax and banking data, passport scans, interview recordings, facial biometrics, and internal records, according to a class action filed in April against Mercor in California. In June, Mercor said that only a very limited subset of sensitive information was affected and there is no evidence that the data has been used fraudulently. The hacking group Lapsus$ claimed responsibility and offered the data for sale on Telegram. 
Foody spoke about the breach like a thing of the past, with the confidence of someone whose customers already voted with their wallets: “Every frontier lab has expanded their relationship with us since the data breach,” he claimed. “The business grew from a $1 billion revenue run rate to $2 billion in ARR in the last four months.”
That’s either a remarkable display of customer loyalty or a reminder that Frontier Labs has nowhere else to go at this scale.

**[0] What happened.** On 2026-07-09 Mercor (AI training-data / RL-environments unicorn, ~$10B valuation) announced its first major acquisition: **Deeptune**, a ~4-year-old New York, a16z-backed startup building high-fidelity simulation environments ("training gyms") where AI agents practice enterprise tasks — replicas of Excel, Salesforce, Slack, ticketing/finance/monitoring tools. Financial terms undisclosed; Deeptune's team joins Mercor in New York. The twist: Mercor founder **Brendan Foody** was an angel in Deeptune's **$43M Series A just three months earlier** (Mar 2026), and told Fortune the angel check "was in a lot of ways the main motivation" — i.e. acquisition was pre-planned. ([Fortune, 2026-07-09](https://fortune.com/2026/07/09/ai-unicorn-mercor-acquires-deeptune-brendan-foody-investor-a16z-openai-anthropic/), [Mercor blog](https://www.mercor.com/blog/mercor-to-acquire-deeptune/), [SiliconANGLE](https://siliconangle.com/2026/07/09/mercor-buys-deeptune-build-training-environments-ai-agents/))

**[1] Strategic logic — vertical integration of the RL stack.** Mercor's ~5M domain-expert network already builds the *tasks and scoring rubrics* that grade whether a model did the job right; Deeptune builds the *apps those tasks run inside*. Together they claim to cover the full RL-environment stack. Foody claims Mercor is already the market leader in reinforcement-learning environments, with all of the Mag Seven except Tesla as customers, serving frontier labs like Anthropic and OpenAI. Deeptune CEO **Tim Lupo**: "we essentially build simulations of digital work that look like the workspace of an accountant or a lawyer or a software engineer." ([Fortune Deeptune Series A, 2026-03-19](https://fortune.com/2026/03/19/andreessen-horowitz-ai-startups-deeptune-series-a/), [Brendan Foody on X](https://x.com/BrendanFoody/status/2075256423211229537))

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
**Red-team questions**

1. Terms are undisclosed — is this a meaningful acquisition or a sub-$50M acquihire dressed up as strategy? No price, no equity/cash split, no headcount disclosed beyond "team joins."
2. Nearly all financials ($2B ARR, "fastest growth ever," "all Mag Seven except Tesla") come from a single source: Foody via Fortune. Any independent confirmation of ARR or customer roster?
3. The angel-then-acquire structure: Foody says the $43M Series A check (Mar 2026) was written "with acquisition in mind." Does that imply the a16z-led round was mispriced, or that a16z knowingly funded an exit to Mercor? Conflict-of-interest / related-party governance risk.
4. Is "$2B ARR" true annual recurring revenue or an annualized run-rate off a peak month? Data-labeling/RL-env contracts are often project-based and lumpy, not recurring.
5. Revenue concentration: if a handful of frontier labs (Anthropic, OpenAI) are most of ARR, how durable is it? Labs are building in-house RL-env teams — is Mercor a bridge, not a moat?
6. The March 2026 breach (~4TB, biometrics, passports, tax/banking data; Lapsus$; class action). Foody says labs "expanded" post-breach — is that verifiable, or spin? What's the litigation/regulatory exposure?
7. "Mercor is the market leader in RL environments" is self-asserted. Surge AI (>$1.2B rev), Scale, Turing, Mechanize dispute this. What objective share data exists?
8. Deeptune was only ~4 years old (some sources imply younger) with a $43M Series A 3 months ago — how mature is the tech, and is "hundreds of enterprise apps recreated" audited or a claim?
9. Does owning the app/environment layer actually create a moat, or does Prime Intellect's open-source Environments Hub commoditize exactly this layer?
10. Valuation: $10B → talks at $20B in ~9 months on $2B ARR = ~10x forward ARR for a services-heavy, labor-arbitrage business (5M contractors). Is the multiple SaaS-like when the cost structure isn't?
11. Retention risk: does Deeptune's team (esp. CEO Tim Lupo) have lockups? Acquihire value evaporates if founders leave.
12. Is the RL-environments TAM ($11.6B→$90B by 2034) real demand or a narrative sized off a ResearchAndMarkets extrapolation?
13. Zuckerberg (see related note) says agents haven't progressed as fast as hoped — if agent capability plateaus, does demand for enterprise RL environments soften?
14. Duplicate check: distinct from the $20B-valuation note (that's the raise; this is M&A) — but does the digest risk double-counting Mercor coverage this week?

**Importance: 4/5** — Well-sourced, materially significant story: names a clear strategic thesis (RL-environments as the new scarce AI input), a marquee $10B→$20B unicorn, first major M&A consolidating a hot 2026 sub-sector, plus notable governance color (angel-then-acquire). Docked one point because it's fintech-adjacent (AI infra, not core fintech), terms are undisclosed, and the standout financials rest on a single self-reported source against an unresolved breach.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Subvertical: AI human-data / expert-labeling / RLHF & RL-environments — the "post-training" layer that sells specialized human-generated training data to frontier labs. Market sizing is soft and definition-dependent: AI data-labeling ~$2.3bn in 2026 → ~$6.5bn by 2031 at ~23% CAGR (per Mordor Intelligence); the RLHF-platform slice ~$2.8bn in 2025 → ~$18.6bn by 2034 (per Lemon.io citation) — both speculative, scope-inconsistent, treat as directional not TAM. *Why now:* frontier labs have exhausted cheap web-scale data and pivoted to expensive expert RLHF/RL-environments (SME reviewers — engineers, MDs, lawyers, scientists at $100–125/hr), so demand shifted from annotation *volume* to expert *depth*. Structure: fragmented and land-grabbing, not consolidated — Mercor, Scale AI, Surge AI, Handshake, Turing all scaling simultaneously; barriers are the two-sided network (curated expert supply × lab demand), not capital or regulation. Second-order: this is a *derivative* of lab capex — demand lives or dies with the frontier-training cycle, not a secular SaaS annuity.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: gross billings (GMV-like), **net revenue after contractor payout** (the real number — experts keep 60–70% of gross), expert-supply headcount, lab-client concentration. Basis of competition: quality/neutrality vs scale-of-expert-network vs specialty (coding/agentic vs academic). Players & recent moves: **Scale AI** ~$29bn (Meta's ~$14.3bn/49% deal, 2025, valuation frozen since), ~$2bn 2026 revenue — but Meta ownership pushed rival labs (OpenAI, Google) toward *neutral* vendors. **Surge AI** — bootstrapped to >$1bn revenue (2025), in talks to raise ~$1bn at $15–25bn (per Reuters/Bloomberg). **Handshake** — ~$1.1bn *gross* ARR Apr-2026 (+349% YoY), net ~$450m (Sacra est.). **Turing** ~$300m ARR (end-2024). Mercor's position: **catching up on scale, fastest grower**, but structurally *younger and thinner-moated* than Scale (govt/enterprise book) or bootstrapped-profitable Surge. Moat = its expert-vetting/matching pipeline + Deeptune acqui-hire (agent-training), but low switching costs — labs multi-source across all five. `(analysis)`

**Comps & multiples.** The load-bearing correction (per Forbes/Sacra): Mercor's "$2bn ARR" is **gross billings**; contractors take 60–70%, so **net revenue ≈ $600–800m**. Multiples on the reported $20bn:
- Mercor EV/gross-rev = `$20bn / $2.0bn = 10x`; EV/**net**-rev = `$20bn / ~$0.7bn ≈ 25–33x`.
- Scale AI EV/rev = `$29bn / ~$2.0bn ≈ 14.5x` (gross basis; valuation stale/frozen).
- Surge AI (reported round) `$15–25bn / >$1bn ≈ 15–25x` (gross).
- Internal corpus: [[Arena AI leaderboard becomes a $100M business]] — adjacent post-training vendor at `$1.7bn / $30m = 57x` (Series A, Jan-2026), and it names Mercor/Surge/Scale as competing "for the same dollar"; [[Patronus AI lands $50M to stress-test AI agents]] carries the same comp set (Mercor $10bn, Scale ~$29bn, Surge $15–25bn); [[Prime Intellect raises $130M Series A for enterprise AI agents]] ($100m ARR at ~$1bn = 10x) — Mercor CEO Foody is an angel there.
- **Flag: rich on the honest (net) basis.** 10x gross looks in-line, but ~25–33x *net* is a hyper-growth multiple that requires 100%+ growth to persist to be justified (Foody claims +100% in 4 months). On a like-for-like *gross* basis Mercor (10x) is actually *cheaper* than Scale (14.5x) and Surge (15–25x) — i.e. the doubling-in-9-months step-up ($10bn Oct → $20bn) is priced to growth, not obviously stretched **if** the growth and net-margin hold. Distribution not formally computed (comps are gross-rev, mixed private-round valuations).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Gross-vs-net optics** — the headline metric ($2bn ARR) overstates the economics ~3x; net revenue ~$600–800m means the take-rate (~30–40%) is the whole business, and it's compressible if experts or labs gain bargaining power → the valuation rests on a number that is 60–70% pass-through.
2. **Customer concentration + demand is a derivative of lab capex** — a handful of frontier labs are the buyers; a training-cycle pause, in-housing of data ops, or one lost account (Mercor reportedly lost Meta post-breach) swings revenue hard. Not a recurring SaaS book.
3. **Execution/reputational overhang** — early-2026 data breach + contractor lawsuits (per Business Insider); a labor-intensive, contractor-dependent model carries ongoing legal/classification and quality-control risk that a $20bn valuation does not price.
4. **Froth/round is early-stage** — talks only, one term sheet, valuation could move; the whole subvertical (5 firms simultaneously chasing $15–29bn) shows concentration of investor enthusiasm in one thin, capex-derivative theme.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` The human-data layer is re-rating toward "Scale AI for X" across the board — this is a *land-grab*, not consolidation (yet). Falsifiable thesis: if labs increasingly in-house expert-data ops or if RL-environments commoditize, net take-rates compress and these gross-billing valuations de-rate fast. Watch: (a) whether the Mercor round actually closes at $20bn or resets; (b) any disclosure of *net* revenue / gross margin (the tell everyone hides); (c) whether a frontier lab acquires a vendor outright (à la Meta/Scale) — that would fracture the neutral-multi-source model the others sell.
