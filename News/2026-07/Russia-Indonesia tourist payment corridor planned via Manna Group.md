# Russia-Indonesia tourist payment corridor planned via Manna Group

> [!info] 2026-07-06 · 1 упоминаний · 0 источника(ов) с текстом
> Каналы: Финтехно

## Агрегированный текст (из дайджестов)

[Финтехно] Между Россией и Индонезией может появиться новый платёжный коридор. PT Manna Indonesia Group заявила о создании системы трансграничных платежей для туристов в национальных валютах двух стран. Система пока не запущена, ей нужен платёжный партнёр. Внутри Индонезии турист сможет платить через приложение LinkAja. Это местный электронный кошелёк, который работает с 2019 года, но из России деньги туда сложно завести напрямую без зарубежной карты: часто приходится покупать криптовалюту, а затем менять её на индонезийские рупии. Новая система должна убрать этот промежуточный криптоэтап и дать прямой обмен рублей на рупии. Для использования сервиса понадобится SIM-карта местного оператора, заявленный лимит операций небольшой — 40 млн индонезийских рупий в месяц (около 170 тыс. рублей). Интересно, что Manna Indonesia Group — не банк и не классический платёжный гигант, а индонезийский холдинг, который позиционирует себя как платформа на стыке торговли, туризма, инвестиций и культурного обмена.

# Context-enrichment: Russia–Indonesia tourist payment corridor planned via Manna Group
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
De-PR'd core: this is **an announcement, not a launch, and not even a signed payment deal**. On **19 December 2025**, PT Fintek Karya Nusantara (**LinkAja**) and **Manna Indonesia Group (MIG)** signed a strategic collaboration — backed by Indonesia's Ministry of Tourism and travel-agent association ASITA — to let *inbound foreign tourists* pay via the LinkAja e-wallet (register with a **Telkomsel SIM**, download the app; usable for tour packages, shopping, transport, ATM cash-out). The Russia angle is a *target market*, not a built rail: MIG explicitly names **Russia, Uzbekistan and Saudi Arabia** as high-prospect source markets. The Финтехно retelling adds the concrete detail that MIG says it wants a **ruble↔rupiah** system for tourists that removes today's crypto workaround, with a stated cap of **IDR 40M/month (~RUB 170k)** — but stresses the **system is not launched and still needs a payment partner**.
- **Why framed this way / what it reveals:** MIG is **not a bank or a licensed payment institution** — it is a self-styled "trade/tourism/investment/culture" holding (founded 2015, CEO Hanzela C. Kusumamenggala). It has **no license, no settlement rail, and no named ruble-side partner**. LinkAja provides the *acceptance* (domestic e-wallet UX) but LinkAja itself does **not** move rubles into Indonesia — the entire cross-border/FX leg (the actual hard part) is the missing "payment partner". So the press frame ("system created for Russian tourists") inverts reality: what exists is a **domestic tourist-acceptance MoU plus an aspiration**; the Russia-specific FX corridor is a marketing pitch to attract that partner and government support. The IDR 40M cap and SIM-card requirement signal a **low-value, KYC-light, e-money-tier** product, not a bank-grade remittance corridor.

## [1] Competitors / peers
The real comparison is **who actually solves "Russian tourist pays abroad post-sanctions"**, where MIG has done nothing yet:
- **UnionPay** (via non-sanctioned Russian issuers e.g. Gazprombank/Russian Standard): already *live* at some Indonesian ATMs (Mandiri/BNI) — imperfect but real acceptance today.
- **Mir card acceptance abroad:** Bank of Russia says international Mir acceptance is "growing," and named Indonesia as being at the *early stages* of accepting Mir (statements ~April 2025) — but **not live** in Indonesia in practice for 2025–26. Cf. [[International acceptance of Mir cards grows despite sanctions]] pattern (Mir works mainly in a handful of friendly states).
- **QRIS interoperability**, the region's genuine cross-border winner: [[Bank Indonesia expands QRIS to Saudi Arabia and India]] and [[Indonesia and China launch cross-border QR payments via Alipay+]] show BI already links its 40M+-merchant QRIS to national systems (Malaysia/Thailand/Singapore/China/Japan). Uzbekistan's HUMO did the same via Alipay+ ([[Alipay+ partners HUMO for cross-border QR payments in Uzbekistan]]).
- **Why the landscape is this way (2nd order):** the successful corridors are **central-bank / national-switch to national-switch** (BI–PBoC, NPCI–BENEFIT). Russia is the odd one out because its national rail (**NSPK/Mir/SBP**) is sanctioned, so no mainstream switch will interconnect — see [[Russia's central bank rejects Sber, Alfa, T-Bank payment system project]] and [[Russia weighs selling up to 5% of NSPK to single investor]] for how contested/isolated Russia's payment plumbing is. That vacuum is exactly why a **non-bank holding + crypto/OTC workaround** shows up to fill it — MIG is a symptom of Russia's exclusion, not a peer to Alipay+ or QRIS.

## [2] Company history / fit
MIG (est. 2015) positions as a cross-sector Indonesia gateway (commerce/tourism/investment/culture) rather than a fintech — so a tourism-payments play "fits" its brand but sits **outside its competence** (no payments license, no FX/settlement infrastructure). LinkAja, by contrast, is a legitimate state-linked e-wallet (Telkomsel/BUMN-backed, operating since 2019) that has been **losing share to GoPay/OVO/DANA** and is logically hunting new volume via **inbound-tourism devisa (FX-earning)** — hence its December 2025 "wisatawan mancanegara" (foreign-tourist) digitalization push.
- **Why they act this way:** LinkAja needs differentiated volume it can't win in the saturated domestic wallet war → inbound tourists (esp. sanctioned-market Russians with *no* card options) are an underserved niche. MIG needs credibility/rails → it borrows LinkAja's license and BI/Ministry endorsement. Each supplies what the other lacks, but **neither supplies the ruble-side settlement** — the binding constraint.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty:** low. Tourist-facing e-wallet onboarding via local SIM is standard (Alipay+/QRIS already do it at scale). The only *specific* novelty claim — a **direct ruble→rupiah exchange removing the USDT intermediation step** Russians use in Bali today — is **unbuilt and unpartnered**.
- **Traction:** **zero verifiable.** No launch date, no transaction volume, no named ruble-side bank/PSP, no central-bank sign-off on the FX mechanism. "Not yet launched / seeking payment partner" is stated explicitly by the parties themselves.
- **Who captures margin (analysis):** if it ever launches, the value sits in the **FX spread + cash-out fees** on a capped IDR 40M/month wallet — thin, retail-tier economics. The hard, defensible layer (compliant RUB in-flow under sanctions) is precisely what MIG *doesn't* own, so any real partner (an OTC/crypto desk or a friendly-jurisdiction PSP) would capture most of the economics and the sanctions risk. Without that partner the value-add is **announcement-only**.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Watch for the **named payment partner and a live pilot** — until then treat as aspiration. Backdrop is favorable in *diplomacy* but stuck in *plumbing*: Russia has sought local-currency (ruble/rupiah) settlement with Indonesia since Indonesia's BRICS accession (Deputy Trade Minister Gruzdev; Ambassador Tolchenov confirm talks ongoing, **no deadline set**), mirroring RUB/CNY and RUB/INR deals. See [[RBI proposes linking BRICS central bank digital currencies]] for the BRICS de-dollarization framing.
- **Why the market goes this way / 2nd order:** the binding risk is **sanctions/correspondent-banking**, not tech. Even a friendly BRICS partner keeps Russia at arm's length in *retail card rails* (Indonesia won't rush Mir/NSPK interconnection because its banks fear OFAC secondary exposure). So the likely path isn't a clean national-currency corridor but a **semi-informal, low-cap, crypto/OTC-adjacent workaround wrapped in a licensed wallet** — which is exactly the IDR-40M-cap, SIM-gated shape being announced. Counterintuitive read: government/tourism endorsement raises *visibility* but not *bankability* — the more official it looks, the more compliance scrutiny any Western-exposed settlement partner faces, which may keep the ruble leg permanently informal.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Cross-border retail payments / tourist FX-and-spend rails, framed here as a bilateral ruble↔rupiah corridor. Structure: highly fragmented at the "last mile" (each market has its own dominant wallet — LinkAja/GoPay/DANA/OVO in Indonesia, national schemes in Russia) with the value chain fought over at the wallet + settlement layers; barriers are regulatory (both central banks must bless local-currency settlement) and network-effects (merchant + user two-sided adoption). "Why now": post-2022 sanctions cut Russian cards (Visa/Mastercard) off internationally, so Russians abroad rely on the crypto→local-currency workaround the note describes — the corridor's explicit pitch is to remove that crypto middle-step. Tailwind: Russia is pursuing BRICS-aligned de-dollarised, national-currency settlement — Deputy Trade Minister Alexey Gruzdev has said Moscow's interest in a rupiah/ruble local-currency settlement deal "remained unchanged" (per Jakarta Globe, no target date). Headwind: in 2026 Indonesia is enforcing a rupiah-only mandate for tourist transactions on Bali (per nomadlawyer.org) — supportive of local-currency rails in principle but tightens what unlicensed intermediaries may do. Bali targets 6.6mn international visitors in 2026 (per multiple tourism trackers) `(analysis)`.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: TPV, take rate, FX spread, per-corridor active users, on-boarding friction. Basis of competition: distribution (wallet installed base) + regulatory access + FX pricing, not brand. Peers/precedents pursuing the same "link two domestic rails / tourist corridor" playbook, but from a position of scale and central-bank sponsorship that Manna lacks: NPCI International × BENEFIT linking India's UPI to Bahrain's Fawri+ under RBI/CBB supervision (2025-11-10) [[NPCI International and BENEFIT link UPI for India-Bahrain payments]]; Idram × TenPay/WeChat Pay opening Armenia to Chinese-wallet tourists across 25,000 merchants (2025-11) [[Idram partners with WeChat Pay in Armenia]]; Bank Indonesia's own QRIS cross-border linkages (to Saudi Arabia, India) [[Bank Indonesia expands QRIS to Saudi Arabia and India]]. Protagonist position: NICHE / earliest-stage. PT Manna Indonesia Group is a self-styled trade/tourism/investment/culture holding — not a bank or licensed PSP — and by its own admission the system is not launched and still needs a payment partner. Its only concrete asset is the front-end (LinkAja for in-Indonesia spend); the rails, licensing and Russian-side rouble sourcing are all unowned. Moat: none demonstrated `(analysis)` — it is a distribution-and-narrative play dependent on someone else's rails.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation, round, TPV or take-rate disclosed — Manna is private and the note carries no financials, so **all multiples = no data / `[UNSOURCED]`**; distribution not computed. Qualitative-only comparison. Internal comps as directional precedents (execution / sponsorship benchmark, not valuation): [[NPCI International and BENEFIT link UPI for India-Bahrain payments]] (state-backed, live-path), [[Idram partners with WeChat Pay in Armenia]] (wallet-to-wallet tourist corridor, live at 25k merchants), [[Bank Indonesia expands QRIS to Saudi Arabia and India]] (central-bank-led). Against these, Manna is the outlier on credibility: announced-only, no central-bank sponsor named, non-bank promoter.

**Risk flags.**
1. Vaporware / execution risk — the system is explicitly not launched and lacks a payment partner; "announced" ≠ "live." Second-order: an unlicensed holding may never obtain Bank Indonesia PSP authorisation, in which case the corridor stays a press line.
2. Regulatory / sanctions risk — a ruble↔rupiah tourist rail sits squarely in secondary-sanctions territory; any bank/PSP partner risks OFAC exposure, which is why credible partners may stay away. Compounded by Indonesia's 2026 rupiah-only tourist-payment tightening, which raises the bar on intermediaries.
3. Tiny economics + policy fragility — the IDR 40mn/month (~RUB 170k) cap plus a required local SIM caps TPV and per-user value, while Bali authorities have periodically floated ending visa-on-arrival for Russians over conduct issues (per RFE/RL) — demand for the corridor is itself politically contingent.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** If it ever launches, it is a small, sanctions-driven point solution replacing a crypto workaround for one tourist flow — not a re-rating or a threat to incumbent rails; the structural signal is Russia's persistent push for national-currency corridors into BRICS/Global-South markets `(analysis)`. Falsifiable thesis: this stays an announcement — trigger that would prove it real = a named, licensed Bank-Indonesia-regulated payment partner and a live LinkAja rouble-funding flow; absent that within ~12 months, treat as narrative only.
