Stripe, Advent offer USD 53 bln for PayPal, sources say
Paula Albu
15 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Payments company Stripe and private equity firm Advent International have made a joint offer to acquire PayPal Holdings for USD 60.50 per share, in a deal that would value the company at more than USD 53 billion, according to two people familiar with the matter who declined to be named due to the confidential nature of the discussions.
Details of the proposal
The offer, submitted earlier in July 2026, is backed by approximately USD 50 billion in committed bank financing, according to one of the sources. The proposed price represents around a 28% premium to PayPal's recent closing share price. Under the proposal, Stripe and Advent would jointly own PayPal, each holding an equal stake, rather than dividing the company between them.
The proposal follows an initial approach made in early April 2026. Stripe and Advent have not received a response from PayPal and are said to be seeking to advance discussions in the coming weeks. PayPal, Stripe, and Advent International declined to comment.
Market and industry context
PayPal has faced increasing competition in recent years as consumers have adopted alternative payment methods, including Apple Pay and Google Pay, contributing to a decline in the company's market value from a 2021 peak of approximately USD 360 billion to as low as roughly USD 36 billion in 2026. Enrique Lores, who became chief executive officer in March 2026, has since led a restructuring of the company's operations into three units covering checkout, Venmo, and payments and crypto, alongside broader efficiency measures.
A potential deal of this scale would add to a wider period of consolidation in the global payments sector, following transactions including Global Payments' USD 24.25 billion acquisition of Worldpay in 2025 and Nuvei's USD 2.75 billion acquisition of Payoneer Global, as companies pursue scale and exposure to faster-growing payment segments.
What happens next
As of the time of reporting, PayPal had not issued a public response to the proposal, and discussions remain confidential. According to the sources, Stripe and Advent are seeking to advance discussions in the coming weeks, though no formal agreement or rejection has been confirmed by any of the parties involved, and there is no certainty the approach will result in a transaction.

The prior corpus note [[Stripe explores acquisition of all or part of PayPal]] (2026-02-24) was an EARLY-STAGE exploration rumor — Bloomberg said Stripe "considering... very early stages... may not happen," and Semafor reported PayPal was "not currently in talks" and merely defensively prepping bankers, with NO price, NO financing, NO second bidder. The 15 Jul 2026 event is a FORMAL, PRICED, FINANCED joint offer: **$60.50/share, >$53bn EV, ~$50bn committed bank financing, ~28% premium, 50/50 Stripe/Advent structure**, with Advent International added as co-equal bidder and architect. New terms + new party + new deal stage (exploration → submitted offer) = a new event. Cite the Feb note as the precedent, not a duplicate. ## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Reuters (exclusive, 15 Jul 2026) reports Stripe + Advent International submitted a joint offer to acquire PayPal at **$60.50/share, ~$53.4bn EV**, backed by **~$50bn committed bank financing**, ~28% premium to the 14 Jul close, **50/50 ownership** (no plan to break PayPal up). Timeline: exploration surfaced Feb 2026 ([[Stripe explores acquisition of all or part of PayPal]]) → renewed approach reportedly April 2026 → formal offer "earlier in July" → leaked 15 Jul. **De-PR'd core: this is an UNSOLICITED, PRELIMINARY approach PayPal has NOT accepted or publicly acknowledged.** PayPal, Stripe, Advent all declined to comment. No signed agreement, no filing, no board response. Entirely "sources say." Reported plan: ~20% workforce cut, ~$1.5bn cost reduction over 2–3 years (TechCrunch).

**Why structured this way & what it reveals.** The ~$50bn is DEBT, not equity — the tell that this is a leveraged take-private, not a strategic merger. Stripe cannot pay in shares (private, illiquid, tender-set $159bn valuation), so it needs a PE partner (Advent) with balance-sheet + take-private playbook AND rock-solid debt commitments to overcome a "stiff-arm" — exactly the constraint Semafor flagged in Feb ([[Stripe explores acquisition of all or part of PayPal]], citing the Dell/EMC tracking-stock precedent). The 50/50 split prices risk: Stripe gets strategic control of PayPal's ~430m consumer accounts + Venmo + PYUSD without funding the whole check; Advent gets the capstone consumer asset for its payments roll-up. `[UNVERIFIED]`: one aggregator claimed "Block + $17bn equity" and a "20 July board meeting" — NOT corroborated by Reuters/CNBC/TechCrunch; treat as erroneous. The stock trading BELOW $60.50 post-leak is the market's own vote that this closes far from certain.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Combining **two of the largest online payment processors** — Stripe (~$1.9tn TPV 2025, [[Connecting the Dots Stripe processed $1.9 trillion in 2025]]) + PayPal (~$1.8tn) ≈ ~$4tn combined — is the core antitrust flag. Merchant-acquiring rivals: Adyen, Checkout.com, Worldpay/Global Payments; consumer-wallet rivals: Apple Pay, Google Pay (the very forces that halved PayPal's share). Precedent M&A: **Global Payments–Worldpay $24.25bn** (agreed Apr 2025, closed Jan 2026) — a Stripe/PayPal deal would be ~2x it and the largest fintech takeover attempt in years. **Why the landscape is this way (2nd order):** PayPal's decline is not idiosyncratic mismanagement alone — it's structural disintermediation of the standalone wallet by OS-level wallets (Apple/Google) and networks moving into checkout. So the premium here is NAME-specific opportunism (cheap distressed target), not a sector re-rating. Advent's angle differs from Stripe's: Advent is running the PE consolidation pattern seen across payments (cf. [[Nuvei to acquire Payoneer for $2.75 billion]], [[Elliott Management builds stake in Global Payments]] context) — buy commoditizing take-rate assets at scale on leverage.

## [2] Company history / fit
**PayPal:** market cap fell from ~$360bn peak (2021) to ~$36bn low (2026), briefly below former parent eBay. Enrique Lores (ex-HP) became CEO 1 Mar 2026 ([[PayPal names Enrique Lores as new CEO]]), then restructured into three units — checkout, Venmo, payments & crypto ([[PayPal reorganizes into three business units with leadership changes]], [[PayPal makes Venmo a standalone business unit]]) — plus job cuts ([[PayPal plans job cuts under new CEO turnaround plan]]) and a strategic review that wound down PayPal Ventures ([[PayPal winds down PayPal Ventures amid strategic review]]). It had ALREADY been prepping bankers against a hostile bid since early 2026 ([[PayPal attracts takeover interest after stock slump]]). **Advent:** took Nuvei private 2024 (~$6.3bn, $34/share), bought Paya earlier, and via Nuvei is acquiring Payoneer ($2.75bn, [[Nuvei to acquire Payoneer for $2.75 billion]]) — an 18-month pay-in + pay-out cross-border stack ([[Linas Newsletter Nuvei-Payoneer completes Advent's payments empire]]). PayPal would be the consumer-side capstone. **Stripe:** ~$159bn tender valuation ([[Stripe reaches $159 billion valuation in employee tender offer]]), largest-ever deal to date only $1.1bn (Bridge). **Why they act this way:** Stripe is merchant-side and wants PayPal's consumer graph + Venmo + PYUSD to combine with its Bridge/Tempo stablecoin stack; Advent needs a consumer front-end for its acquiring/payout empire. Structural pressure: commoditizing take-rate → buy a distressed brand with 430m accounts cheaply rather than build consumer reach.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**As an EVENT: genuinely new** — the deal moved from Feb "exploration" to a priced, financed, joint formal offer with a named co-bidder. That is real news, not a reprint. **But the VALUE-ADD (would the combination work?) is unproven:** (a) Stripe has zero large-M&A track record — a private, non-IPO'd company underwriting a ~$53bn take-private on ~$50bn debt against a declining-EBITDA target is aggressive; (b) the "$4tn combined TPV" headline likely double-counts marketplace/Braintree flows and is not a synergy number; (c) the strategic prize — consolidating Bridge + Tempo + PYUSD stablecoin stacks — is real but is ALSO the sharpest antitrust flag. **Who captures the margin (2nd order):** even if the deal closes, the same OS-wallet and network disintermediation that gutted PayPal keeps pulling checkout margin toward Apple/Google/Visa/Mastercard — so buying PayPal buys distribution + a stablecoin float, not a defensible take-rate. Traction on the DEAL itself: zero — no board engagement, PYPL trades below offer.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
PYPL jumped ~14–16% on the leak (largest daily gain in years) but stayed below $60.50 — market prices meaningful deal risk. Next binding gates: (1) PayPal board response (none yet; a 4-month-old CEO mid-turnaround may argue the plan is worth more than 28%); (2) financing bank disclosure + conditions on the ~$50bn debt (unnamed); (3) antitrust — combining two top online processors invites 18–24mo US/EU review, with analyst speculation of Venmo/Braintree divestiture remedies (`(speculation)` — no regulator has commented). Elliott Management held a ~$2bn stake (2022); current position unconfirmed. **Why the market may stay skeptical (counterintuitive 2nd order):** a leaked unsolicited bid is a classic PE tactic to pressure a silent board in public; the leak itself signals the private approach stalled. And the very scale/leverage that makes this a landmark also makes it fragile — long clearance + committed debt against a target still shrinking on checkout is a live financing/break-risk, not a safe megadeal.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Payments M&A is in a consolidation super-cycle: this $53bn take-private bid would be the largest fintech takeover attempt in years, following Global Payments' $24.25bn purchase of Worldpay (2025, 8.5x adj. EBITDA net of synergies, per bscapitalmarkets/SEC 8-K) and Nuvei's $2.75bn take-out of Payoneer (2026). Structure: mature acquiring/checkout is a **consolidating, scale-driven** business — value concentrates in distribution and processing scale, while take rates compress structurally (PayPal's transaction take rate fell 6bps YoY to 1.62% in Q1'26, per earnings release). Barriers = capital, regulatory licensing, and two-sided network effects. **Why now:** (1) PayPal de-rated from a ~$360bn 2021 peak to ~$36-42bn in 2026 as Apple Pay/Google Pay eroded branded checkout — cheap enough for a leveraged take-private; (2) Advent has spent ~18 months assembling a full-stack payments platform (Nuvei + Payoneer) and PayPal would be the capstone; (3) ~$50bn committed bank financing signals debt markets are open for mega-LBOs again.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: **TPV/GPV, take rate, transaction-margin dollars, branded-checkout TPV growth.** Key players: incumbents (PayPal, Global Payments/Worldpay, Fiserv, Adyen), scaled disruptor **Stripe** (private, ~$1.9tn 2025 TPV +34%, ~$5.84bn est. net revenue, $159bn Feb-2026 tender valuation — per PYMNTS/Sacra), and Advent's Nuvei+Payoneer stack. Basis of competition: distribution + product breadth + platform scale, not price. Recent moves: Stripe tender at $159bn (Feb-2026); Nuvei/Payoneer close (2026); PayPal CEO Enrique Lores (from Mar-2026) restructured into Checkout / Venmo / Payments-and-Crypto units. **PayPal's position: incumbent losing branded-checkout share but with re-accelerating adjacencies** — branded checkout only +2% cn TPV, while Venmo (+34% Pay-with-Venmo TPV) and BNPL (+23%) outgrow the market. Moat = brand + two-sided consumer/merchant network + Venmo; eroding at the checkout button (disintermediation by wallets).

**Comps & multiples.** Offer: $60.50/share, >$53bn equity value, ~28% premium to recent close.
- **PayPal (target):** Q1'26 net revenue $8.35bn → annualized ~$33.4bn; TPV $464bn/qtr (~$1.86tn ann.). Offer EV/Revenue ≈ `$53bn / ~$33.4bn ≈ 1.6x` (equity proxy; true EV higher w/ debt but PayPal holds large customer-cash/receivables — treat as **~1.6x sales**). GAAP operating margin 17.8%, non-GAAP op income ~$1.54bn/qtr (~$6.2bn ann.) → offer ≈ 8.5x that op-income run-rate. **In-line-to-cheap** vs the sector: mid/low-single-digit growth (rev +7%) at ~1.6x sales is not demanding — the discount reflects checkout share loss, not a growth premium.
- **Worldpay (2025):** 8.5x adj. EBITDA net of synergies — a scaled, slower-growth processor; PayPal's ~8.5x op-income run-rate screens broadly consistent for a mature payments asset.
- **Stripe (2026 private):** $159bn on ~$5.84bn est. net revenue → ~27x sales — not a comp (hyper-growth private multiple), but frames why Stripe as *acquirer* buying a $1.6x-sales incumbent is accretive on multiple.
- Internal comps (base): [[Nuvei to acquire Payoneer for $2.75 billion]], [[Nuvei in advanced talks to acquire Payoneer for $2.7B]], [[Linas Newsletter Nuvei-Payoneer completes Advent's payments empire]]. Clean EV/EBITDA and forward multiples → `[UNSOURCED]` (no free consensus).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Deal uncertainty / no counterparty engagement** — as of 15-Jul PayPal has not responded to an offer first floated in early April; "no certainty the approach results in a transaction." A public leak with no board sign-off is often a pressure tactic; downside if talks collapse back toward ~$42bn.
2. **Antitrust / concentration** — Stripe (largest scaled acquirer, ~$1.9tn TPV) + PayPal (branded checkout + Venmo) is a horizontal combination in US/global checkout; regulatory review could be prolonged or block it, especially layered on Advent's Nuvei/Payoneer roll-up.
3. **Leverage / rates** — ~$50bn bank financing on a business with *compressing take rate* (-6bps) and only +3% transaction-margin-dollar growth; debt service against a decelerating branded-checkout core is the core execution risk. Second-order: forces aggressive cost-out that could accelerate merchant/user churn.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** If consummated, this marks the shift from *organic decline* to *financial-sponsor consolidation* of scaled Western payments incumbents — watch for a PayPal board response/poison-pill or a competing/higher bid as the trigger. Falsifiable thesis (analysis): the 28% premium anchors PayPal's floor near ~$60; thesis breaks if no formal engagement emerges within weeks and shares round-trip to pre-leak levels, signaling the board sees standalone value above the bid. Read-through: re-rates other de-rated payments incumbents (Global Payments, Fiserv) as LBO/strategic candidates.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
**Bid vs fundamentals (headline read).** This is an M&A bid, not an earnings print — grounding the $60.50/share (~$53B) offer in PayPal's OWN latest reported financials (Q1 2026, period ended 2026-03-31, released 2026-05-05). Verdict: the bid is a **~28% premium to the undisturbed share price but only a modest premium to fundamentals** — it re-rates PayPal from a depressed ~8.9x forward P/E to ~11.4x FY2025 non-GAAP EPS, i.e. still a discount to the ~10–13x large-cap payments peer range on a growth-adjusted basis, given PayPal's re-accelerating volumes but flat/declining transaction margin dollars.

**PayPal's latest reported figures (Q1 2026, non-GAAP unless noted; YoY).**
- Net revenue **$8.35bn, +7% YoY** (beat public consensus ~$8.05bn by ~$0.30bn / ~+3.8%).
- Transaction margin dollars **$3.81bn, +3% YoY** — the key value line, growing well below revenue: margin compression is the core thesis flag (ex-interest-on-customer-balances TMD roughly flat).
- Total payment volume (TPV) **$463.96bn, +11% YoY** — volumes re-accelerating, but monetization lagging (take-rate/TMD growth < TPV growth).
- Non-GAAP EPS **$1.34 (+1% YoY)**, beat consensus ~$1.27 by ~5.5%; GAAP EPS **$1.21 (−6% YoY)** — GAAP declining, buyback-flattered (~34m shares / ~$1.5bn repurchased in the quarter).
- Active accounts **225m, +1% YoY** — near-stagnant user base; growth is per-user engagement, not acquisition.
- Net profit margin ~15%; quarterly dividend $0.14/sh.

**FY2026 guidance (reaffirmed at Q1).** Management reiterated FY guide: transaction margin dollars **slight decline** (ex-interest TMD roughly flat); non-GAAP EPS from a **low-single-digit decline to slightly positive** vs FY2025's **$5.31**; adjusted free cash flow **at least $6bn**; non-transaction opex +~3%. Tone: cautious, "complex and dynamic operating environment" — no upgrade despite the top-line beat, which is why the stock fell post-print on cost/margin concerns.

**Bid math vs these fundamentals.**
- Offer $60.50/sh × ~882m shares ≈ **~$53.4bn equity value**; ~$50bn committed bank financing.
- Implied P/E ≈ **$60.50 / $5.31 = ~11.4x** FY2025 non-GAAP EPS, vs the pre-bid **~8.9x forward P/E** (undisturbed price ~$47.37, mkt cap ~$40bn; EV ~$41bn). So the buyers are paying a ~28% headline premium (to $47.37) but only stepping the multiple to ~11.4x — roughly the Credit-Services peer median (~10.3x) and far below PayPal's own 2021 peak (~$360bn mkt cap → ~$36bn 2026 trough per the note). On ≥$6bn adjusted FCF, the bid is ~**8.9x equity/FCF** — cheap for a business still compounding TPV at +11%.
- Read: **premium to a beaten-down tape, discount to a healthy-fundamentals valuation.** The bid capitalizes on (a) stalled account growth and (b) declining TMD — i.e. it prices PayPal as an ex-growth cash cow, not as a re-accelerating volume franchise. LBO logic (50/50 Stripe/Advent, $50bn debt) works precisely because ≥$6bn FCF services the leverage.

**Thesis-flags (why-ladder).**
1. *TMD +3% vs TPV +11%* → monetization/take-rate erosion → the value line is stagnating while volume grows → a buyer can argue the public market is right to discount it, justifying a "low" 11.4x; conversely a strategic (Stripe) could re-monetize checkout → the premium is arguably too thin. Second-order: PayPal board has room to reject/push for a higher bid.
2. *Reaffirmed (not raised) guidance despite the revenue beat* → management signals the beat isn't sustainable margin → weak negotiating hand for the target, strong for the acquirer.
3. *Active accounts +1%* → growth is engagement, not TAM expansion → limits standalone re-rating → makes the take-private premium look adequate to some holders but light vs 2021 highs.
4. *~$50bn debt financing on ≥$6bn FCF* → ~8x leverage-to-FCF → deal is FCF-driven, so any further TMD decline (the guided "slight decline") directly pressures debt serviceability → financing risk is the real gating factor, not price.
