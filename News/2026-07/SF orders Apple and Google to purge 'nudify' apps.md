Apple and Google ordered to purge ‘nudify’ apps from App Stores
The city of San Francisco has ordered Apple and Google to remove dozens of “nudify” apps — — software programs that can digitally alter pictures to unclothe the people in them — from their app stores.
California law criminalizes any activity that “knowingly facilitates” or “recklessly aids or abets” the creation of non-consensual deepfake pornography. In 2025, California also passed a law that allows victims to pursue civil actions against third-party facilitators of such material. The city says that, despite these well-known regulations, both tech companies have continued to host and make money from such programs.
“Apple and Google are profiting off apps that exploit women and girls by generating nonconsensual intimate deepfakes,” San Francisco City Attorney David Chiu said in an emailed statement to TechCrunch. “While the companies cut ties with some problematic apps, Apple and Google have a responsibility to be proactive and vigilant to prevent sexual abuse.”
Letters sent to Google and Apple by Chiu’s office, which were viewed by TechCrunch, note that the companies have “been on notice” for their role in “processing payments for illegal purchases for almost a year” but have, nevertheless, continued to do so. 
According to the letters, both companies have been repeatedly warned that they are hosting these apps. In January and again in April, the Tech Transparency Project issued reports and sent letters to both companies noting that there were “dozens of apps” within their app stores that “sold deepfake NCII [non-consensual intimate images] in exchange for payments” from processed by the firms.
TTP’s report from April said that Google and Apple had intentionally “steered” users towards such apps and called both companies “key participants in the spread of AI tools that can turn real people into sexualized images.”
Additionally, Chiu told Wired that both companies had likely made “millions of dollars in fees” from apps that offered such services.
The letters from Chiu’s office warn that Apple and Google could face civil penalties for violating the law and request that they contact the city within 28 days.
When reached for comment by TechCrunch, an Apple spokesperson said that nudify apps were forbidden from appearing in its App Store, further noting: “We have removed three of the apps in question and are in the process of terminating their developer accounts from our program. We are in contact with four others that need to address policy violations or risk being removed as well.” 
A Google spokesperson claimed that all five Play apps referenced in Chiu’s letter had been suspended from Google Play. “When violations are reported to us, we investigate and take swift action, which in the case of these apps has included suspending hundreds of violating apps and restricting related search terms like ‘nudify’ on our store,” the spokesperson added. 
Deepfake pornography has largely been a problem for female celebrities, although nudify apps make it possible for anyone with a publicly available photo to be targeted.

# Context-enrichment: SF orders Apple and Google to purge 'nudify' apps
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 2026-07-17, SF City Attorney David Chiu sent **demand/cease-and-desist letters** (not a filed lawsuit) to Apple and Google, targeting **13 specific "nudify" apps** — 8 on Apple's App Store, 5 on Google Play — that generate non-consensual intimate deepfakes ("undressing" real photos). This is a **letter with a 28-day response window**, warning of possible **civil penalties**, not yet an enforcement action or fine.

The precise legal hook is what makes this novel: Chiu targets the **platforms as facilitators**, not the app developers. The theory rests on (1) California's criminal statute against activity that "knowingly facilitates" or "recklessly aids or abets" non-consensual deepfake porn, and (2) a **2025 California law** letting victims pursue **civil actions against third-party facilitators**. The money angle is the lever: because Apple/Google **process in-app payments and take their ~30% cut**, Chiu argues they "profit off" the abuse — Chiu told Wired the two "likely made millions of dollars in fees." The letters cite that both were "on notice" for ~a year, after **Tech Transparency Project** reports in **January and April 2026** named dozens of such apps and accused the stores of "steering" users toward them.

**Why framed this way:** the payment-cut framing converts a content-moderation problem (where platforms usually hide behind Section 230 immunity) into a **commercial-participation / aiding-and-abetting** problem. That is the whole game — it sidesteps 230 by casting Apple/Google as paid distributors, not neutral hosts.

**Live vs announced:** partial compliance is already real. Apple: removed 3 of the named apps, terminating those developer accounts, "in contact with four others." Google: says all 5 named Play apps suspended, plus it restricted the search term "nudify." So the stores are moving reactively — but note their own claims (Apple "forbidden," Google "swift action") sit awkwardly against a full year of TTP notice.

## [1] Competitors / peers
This is a **regulator-vs-platform** item; the "peers" are other enforcement actions and the platforms' other regulatory fights:
- **Chiu's own Aug 2024 lawsuit** against operators of 16 (later expanded to ~22) "nudify" **websites** — the landmark first-mover action (sites visited 200M+ times in H1 2024). The 2026 letters are the **app-store sequel**: having gone after the sites, he now goes after the distribution/payment layer.
- **EU antitrust pressure on the same gatekeepers** — see [[EU forces Google to give Gemini rivals equal Android access]]: regulators globally are prying open Apple/Google's control of app distribution, and [[Apple opens iOS to new competitors in Brazil]]. Nudify enforcement is a different vector (content/liability vs competition), but same second-order theme: **the app-store chokepoint is becoming a locus of legal responsibility, not just gatekeeping power.**
- **Deepfake-detection / integrity** peers in-corpus: [[Google deepfake detector used to debunk McConnell hoax image]] — Google publicly leans into anti-deepfake tooling while simultaneously hosting deepfake-generation apps, a tension this letter exploits.

**Why the lay of the land is this way:** SF/California is deliberately first-mover on deepfake law (Chiu wants to "sound the alarm" and pressure others to follow). The platforms' position is **catching up reactively** — they only remove after a named report, which is exactly the "not proactive/vigilant" failure Chiu alleges.

## [2] Company history / fit
Chiu has built a **multi-year deepfake enforcement franchise**: Aug 2024 website lawsuit → 2025 CA civil-facilitator law → Jan/Apr 2026 TTP notice → Jul 2026 app-store letters. Logically consistent escalation up the distribution stack: content creators → hosting sites → the two app stores + payment rails that monetize them.

For Apple/Google, the fit is uncomfortable. Both **already ban this content by policy**, so every named app that persisted for a year is a **policy-enforcement gap**, not a stated-policy gap. Structural driver: app-store scale (~millions of apps) makes proactive detection genuinely hard, but the **~30% payment cut** removes any "we're just a neutral host" defense — they materially profit from every transaction. That is the pressure point Chiu found.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**What's genuinely new:** not the anti-nudify cause (Chiu did that in 2024), but the **legal target**. This is one of the first high-profile attempts to hold **app stores themselves civilly liable as payment-processing facilitators** of illegal content — an explicit end-run around Section 230, enabled by the specific 2025 CA facilitator statute. If it sticks, it reframes platform liability from "who created the content" to "who took a cut of the sale."

**Traction so far (real, modest):** 8 apps removed/suspended (Apple 3, Google 5) within days; Google restricted the "nudify" search term. But this is **whack-a-mole** — nudify apps rebrand as "face-swap" / "photo editor" tools whose real function unlocks post-install, so removals don't durably solve discovery. No fine, no filed suit, no court ruling yet — so the **precedent value is unproven**.

**Who captures what / fintech relevance:** the durable question is the **payments rail**, not the app. If liability attaches to whoever processes the payment, the exposure lands on Apple/Google's IAP system and, upstream, card networks/PSPs. That is why this is a fintech-adjacent story: it's an early test of **payment-processor complicity liability** for illegal digital goods.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
28-day clock → either voluntary compliance (likely the path of least resistance; both are already removing apps) or escalation to a filed suit / civil penalties. Given both companies moved within 48h, **litigation is not the base case**; expect negotiated compliance + tightened review. Regulatory backdrop is a tailwind for Chiu: CA is stacking deepfake statutes and other states/AGs watch SF as the template.

**Second-order:** the real risk to Apple/Google is not these 13 apps — it's the **liability theory** becoming portable. If "you took a payment cut, you facilitated" holds, it extends to any illegal in-app commerce and pressures the entire IAP model (already under antitrust attack per [[EU forces Google to give Gemini rivals equal Android access]]). Counterintuitively, the 30% cut — historically pure margin — becomes a **compliance liability multiplier**. For fintech, watch whether card networks / payment processors get pulled into the same facilitator framing next.

**Freshness verdict: FRESH.** No prior corpus note covers this event; the closest ([[Google deepfake detector used to debunk McConnell hoax image]], the EU/Brazil app-store items) are different events. This is a new regulatory action (new target = the app stores, new letters, 2026 dates), not a re-run of Chiu's 2024 website suit — it is the next step up that same escalation ladder.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a platform-liability / content-moderation risk story, not a payments deal — the "market" is the app-store gatekeeping economics of Apple and Google and the tightening regulatory net around AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII). Scale of what is at stake for Apple: **Services** (which contains the App Store) hit an all-time-high **$30.98bn in Q2 FY2026** (quarter ended 2026-03-28), +16% YoY, out of **$111.2bn total revenue** — App Store share = 30.98/111.2 = ~28% of quarterly revenue [IR: Apple 8-K/press release, 2026-04-30]. Full FY2025 Services was **$109.1bn of $416.1bn** total (~26%) [per Visible Alpha/FT, via IBTimes]. App Store commissions specifically are only a slice of Services: Apple disclosed **$1.4tn** of 2025 App-Store billings/sales but says **~90% carried no commission** (physical goods + $1.1tn commerce vs ~$149bn commissioned digital goods) [Apple, via TechCrunch 2026-06-04]; Asymco pegs commissions at **~$30bn/yr**. So the direct commission exposure is real but a minority of Services. Why now: US federal **TAKE IT DOWN Act** (signed 2025-05-19) took effect against online services **2026-05-19**, carving a Section-230 exemption and a 48-hour takedown duty; FTC sent its first warning letters to 12 nudify sites 2026-05-20. This SF City Attorney action layers **California state law** (criminalizing facilitation of NCII + 2025 civil-action statute) on top — regulators are moving up the stack from the app makers to the **distribution/payment gatekeepers**.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for the gatekeepers: App Store/Play commission take (15–30%), Services revenue & margin, and — newly — content-moderation/enforcement cost and legal exposure. Key players: **Apple** and **Alphabet/Google Play** are the only two mobile gatekeepers; both are DMA-designated gatekeepers (Apple iOS/App Store 2023-09; Alphabet Play 2023-09). Basis of competition here is not price but **control of the funnel** — the same choke-point that lets them take a cut also makes them the enforcement target. Recent moves with dates: Apple says it removed 3 apps and is terminating developer accounts, 4 more on notice (2026-07-17); Google says it suspended all 5 named Play apps plus "hundreds" and restricted the search term "nudify" (2026-07-17); Tech Transparency Project reports flagged the apps in Jan and Apr 2026. Protagonist position: Apple is on the back foot (reactive, "on notice for almost a year" per Chiu) but its curation-first App Store model is a **structurally better moat** on content risk than Google's more open Play/sideload posture — `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** No transaction/valuation in this news, so trading multiples are not the point — the comparable is **regulatory-pressure precedent on the same gatekeepers**. Internal comps from the base (`grep company/apple`, `type/regulation`): [[EU forces Google to give Gemini rivals equal Android access]] and [[Klarna wins $1.97bn PriceRunner antitrust award against Google]] (gatekeeper-funnel liability), [[Apple opens iOS to new competitors in Brazil]] and [[Apple nears CADE deal to allow contactless Pix on iPhone in Brazil]] (App-Store gatekeeping unbundled by regulators), [[EU orders Meta to make Instagram, Facebook less addictive]] and [[UK to ban teenagers from social media at night]] (platform content-duty trend), [[FTC fines Amazon $2.25M over Fair Credit Reporting Act breach]] (platform-penalty scale). Valuation math not applicable — "no data" on a deal multiple. Sizing the tail risk instead: California civil penalties + the small removed-app commission stream are **immaterial** vs ~$31bn quarterly Services; the exposure is reputational and precedent-setting, not P&L — `(analysis)`.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Section-230 erosion / rising platform duty of care** — TAKE IT DOWN Act's 230 carve-out (effective 2026-05-19) plus CA facilitation law shifts liability from app developers onto the store + payment layer; second-order effect: the store's curation function becomes a legal cost center and a template other states/harm-categories (CSAM, fraud, addiction) will copy.
2. **"Processing payments for illegal purchases"** — Chiu explicitly names Apple/Google's role as payment processors (~"millions in fees"); this attacks the same in-app-payment rails already under DMA/antitrust fire, so content liability and commission-model liability converge on one choke-point.
3. **Reputational / gatekeeper-legitimacy risk** — being labeled a "key participant in the spread" of abuse imagery undercuts the "safe, curated store" justification Apple uses to defend its 15–30% take; if the curation narrative weakens, the commission economics are harder to defend before regulators.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** The regulatory theatre has moved from "regulate the AI app" to "hold the distribution + payment gatekeeper liable" — the app store is being redefined as an accountable publisher/processor, not a neutral pipe `(analysis)`. Watch: whether SF actually files (28-day clock from 2026-07-17), whether the FTC/other state AGs pile on, and whether Apple/Google respond with proactive AI-abuse scanning at ingest (a new compliance cost). Falsifiable thesis: this is precedent-building, not a P&L event — it would be wrong if a settlement or statute imposed a per-transaction fine or a broad take-down-or-be-liable regime that raises Apple's App-Store operating cost or forces commission concessions.
