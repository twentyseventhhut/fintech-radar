## Агрегированный текст (из дайджестов)

[a16zcrypto] Eddy Lazzarin, Miles Jennings, and Scott Duke Kominers

[a16zcrypto] In our token taxonomy, we walk through seven types of tokens, including network tokens, collectible tokens, and memecoins. Of these, the least explored and most underappreciated is the arcade token: a token with a relatively stable value within a specific software or product ecosystem, often managed by an issuer (like a company).

[a16zcrypto] While companies have used assets like these for decades, almost all previous instances have existed on centralized databases, limiting ownership, transferability, and user choice. Arcade tokens on public blockchains differ in that they are open, interoperable, and composable, which offers a range of new market-design benefits.

[a16zcrypto] So, where does the term “arcade token” come from? Irrespective of whether you’ve visited an arcade, you’re probably familiar with the concept: You go inside; you trade cash for tokens, typically physical ones; then you use those tokens to play a few rounds of Galaga, Gator Panic, or whatever else you’re into. The tokens let you participate in the arcade’s economy.

[a16zcrypto] The metaphor of an arcade makes it clear how these tokens work: Arcade tokens have a relatively stable value within the context of the economic system of which they are a part — whether that’s within a single service or across a variety of services. ## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **2026-07-11** a16z crypto's Substack pushed an essay by **Eddy Lazzarin, Miles Jennings, Scott Duke Kominers** (canonical byline also lists Tim Roughgarden) titled "The most underrated token type," arguing that **"arcade tokens"** — issuer-controlled tokens with *relatively stable value inside one product/ecosystem* (like physical arcade coins, airline miles, in-game gold) — are the least-explored slot in a16z's seven-type token taxonomy. The pitch: putting these onchain makes them open, interoperable, composable vs. today's centralized loyalty ledgers.
- **This is a re-circulation, not a new idea.** The same essay was first published on a16zcrypto.com on **2025-11-13** ("Arcade tokens: The most underappreciated token type"); the "arcade token" label itself dates to a16z's **"Defining tokens" taxonomy of 2025-03-05**. So the concept is ~16 months old and the deep-dive ~8 months old — the July 2026 event is a newsletter re-share, not a fresh announcement. **Why it matters:** stripped of PR, there is no new product, deal, or number here — it is a thought-leadership/policy essay being re-surfaced.
- **Why framed this way (the real tell):** author **Miles Jennings is a16z crypto's General Counsel / head of policy.** The essay's load-bearing claim is *legal*: arcade tokens — uncapped supply sold at prevailing price, spend-only, no promised financial return — arguably sit **outside U.S. securities law** (leaning on the Quarters no-action letter) AND outside the GENIUS Act's stablecoin regime (they aren't fiat-pegged/redeemable payment instruments). That carves a **third regulatory lane** between "security" and "stablecoin." The framing is as much regulatory positioning as investment thesis. Related corpus: [[a16z crypto crypto's mainstream moment after GENIUS Act and market-structure rules]].

## [1] Competitors / peers
Onchain issuer-controlled / stable-value loyalty models and their fate:
- **Blackbird $FLY** (the essay's flagship live example) — restaurant loyalty token, ~500–600 restaurants, **242M $FLY distributed to 107K wallets in 2024**; the only meaningfully-live case, and small.
- **Starbucks Odyssey** (Polygon NFT loyalty) — beta Dec 2022, **shut down 2024-03-31**. Dead.
- **Reddit Community Points** (MOONs/BRICKs) — **shut down Nov 2023**. Dead.
- **Rally** (creator/social tokens) — wound down 2022–23.
- **Working analogues stay off-chain:** airline frequent-flyer miles, Starbucks Stars, credit-card points, in-game currencies — all massive, decades-proven, all centralized. **Why the landscape looks this way:** the *loyalty-points economics* work enormously well precisely because issuers control redemption; the onchain versions add composability but repeatedly failed to graduate from beta (Odyssey, Reddit). Second-order: the value of a loyalty token comes from the *issuer's promise*, not the ledger — so "put it on a blockchain" solves a problem (interoperability) most issuers don't want solved, because closed loops are the point.

## [2] Company history / fit
a16z crypto has been systematically building a **token-taxonomy → policy → portfolio** stack: "Defining tokens" (Mar 2025) → dedicated arcade-token essay (Nov 2025) → this re-share (Jul 2026). It fits a clear pattern of a16z crypto publishing frameworks that legitimize categories it is invested in (cf. [[a16z crypto closes fifth fund at $2.2 billion]], [[a16z crypto leads $10m seed in Better Money Company]] — a stablecoin clearinghouse). **Structural driver:** with GENIUS locking down stablecoins and CLARITY defining securities-vs-commodities, a16z needs a *named, defensible* category for the issuer-controlled utility tokens its portfolio ships — before the rules harden.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Real value-add (modest):** the *taxonomic distinction* — spend-only, issuer-controlled, stable-value ≠ speculative token ≠ fiat stablecoin — is a genuinely useful design/legal clarification. That is the durable contribution.
- **Novelty is low:** "arcade token" = decades-old loyalty points / miles / in-game currency with an onchain wrapper. Not new economics.
- **Traction is thin and conflicted:** the sole live exemplar, **Blackbird $FLY, is an a16z portfolio company** (a16z crypto led its **Oct 2023 $24M Series A** and re-invested in the **Apr 2025 $50M Series B** led by Spark). So the essay is simultaneously a thesis and a **legitimization of its own book** — a real conflict worth flagging. **Who captures value:** in this model the issuer captures the float and redemption spread; the blockchain captures little; a16z captures the narrative + equity. What breaks it: if regulators fold arcade tokens into stablecoin or securities regimes, the "third lane" thesis collapses.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Regulatory backdrop:** GENIUS Act signed **2025-07-18** (payment stablecoins); CLARITY Act passed House **2025-07-17**, still stuck in Senate (278-page Senate Banking draft **2026-01-12**, which notably *allows activity-linked rewards*). The arcade-token framing is a bid to claim the gap these leave. (analysis)
- **Sentiment:** this is a policy trial balloon with a small live example, not a market-moving event. Expect a16z to keep pushing the category into CLARITY-era rulemaking. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the harder GENIUS/CLARITY squeeze stablecoins and securities, the *more* attractive a lightly-regulated "arcade token" lane becomes for issuers — which is exactly why regulators may eventually close it. Risk: no-action letters (Quarters, 2019) are fact-specific, non-binding, revocable — a thin foundation for a whole category.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a thesis piece, not a deal — so "size" is the loyalty + closed-loop-token adjacency, not a company valuation. Global loyalty-management software is ~$13.3bn (2024) growing to a projected ~$41.2bn by 2032 (per industry research cited via Photon/enable3, as of late 2025 — secondary/vendor sources, treat as directional, not audited). The specific "arcade token" (stable-value, issuer-controlled, on-chain closed-loop) sub-segment has **no credible standalone TAM** — vendor blogs conflate it with all blockchain-loyalty; [UNSOURCED]. Structure: highly fragmented on the issuer side (every brand its own points economy), but the a16z pitch is that public-chain "arcade tokens" become interoperable/composable — i.e. value migrates from siloed points ledgers to shared rails. Drivers / "why now": (1) regulatory tailwind — the July 2019 SEC no-action letter to **Pocketful of Quarters** ($0.0025 fixed-value "Quarters," usable only across participating games) is the a16z essay's anchor precedent that a fixed-value, consumption-only utility token is *not* a security (per Pillsbury/McCarter, 2019); (2) a live flagship, Blackbird's $FLY, giving the abstraction a concrete example. Counter-signal on timing: **Starbucks Odyssey**, the highest-profile brand web3-loyalty experiment, shut down **31 Mar 2025** as NFT demand collapsed (per Decrypt/Entrepreneur) — the reference case for "brand tried tokenized loyalty and retreated."

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: for a token-loyalty issuer the real ones are wallets holding the token, redemption/velocity (txns per wallet), merchant count, and % of rewards actually redeemed vs breakage. Blackbird/$FLY (the essay's example): **>100,000 wallets** holding $FLY, avg **6.2 txns/wallet**, ~**600–1,000 restaurants** across NYC/SF/Charleston; **$85m raised** total (incl. a **$50m** round Apr 2025), backers a16z, Coinbase, Spark, American Express (per TechCrunch/Fortune, Apr 2025). Blackbird runs its own L3 ("Flynet") on Coinbase's Base. Basis of competition: distribution (merchant density in a city) and rewards economics, not token tech. Adjacent players in the base compete for the same "rewards" budget without the token wrapper: [[HeyMax raises $11M for loyalty and rewards platform]] (interoperable points), [[Revolut launches RevPoints rewards scheme in New Zealand]], [[Bilt revamps rewards program and caps card interest at 10%]], [[Agoda and Mastercard collaborate on flexible travel rewards]] (real-time point redemption), and tokenization infra like [[WisdomTree expands tokenization ecosystem to Solana]]. Protagonist ($FLY) position: **niche but the clearest live proof-point** for the arcade-token category (analysis) — moat is merchant network effects within a metro, which is exactly the moat traditional closed-loop programs already have; the on-chain layer adds transferability, not obviously a new moat (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round in this news item (it's an essay), so **no multiples to compute — "no data."** Reference points only: Blackbird private, ~$85m raised cumulatively; last round $50m (Apr 2025), post-money not disclosed → [UNSOURCED]. Internal comps are all *strategy* comps, not trading comps: [[HeyMax raises $11M for loyalty and rewards platform]] and [[Bilt revamps rewards program and caps card interest at 10%]] (loyalty economics without a token), [[WisdomTree expands tokenization ecosystem to Solana]] (tokenization rails). Distribution not computed — qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
- **Regulatory (securities) — the whole thesis rides on one 2019 no-action letter.** The Pocketful-of-Quarters letter is narrow, fact-specific, non-precedential, and pre-dates most of today's token market; the moment an "arcade token" becomes freely tradable / has price upside (the essay's "composable, interoperable" selling point), it drifts toward Howey and loses that shelter. Bloomberg Law (2024) explicitly frames crypto "loyalty points" as a potential **regulatory dodge** under scrutiny. Second-order: the interoperability that makes arcade tokens interesting is the same feature that undermines the not-a-security argument.
- **Money-transmission / stored-value.** The closed-loop exemption from state MTLs holds only while the token is redeemable **just** with the issuer/affiliates. Interoperability across many unaffiliated merchants (Blackbird's cross-restaurant "Blackbird Club" points) can push it out of closed-loop into regulated money transmission (per Cooley/Goodwin, 2024–25; MA and PA tightened stored-value/virtual-currency rules in 2025). Second-order: 50-state licensing burden kills the "just launch a token" simplicity.
- **Demand / adoption risk (Odyssey precedent).** Tokenized loyalty has a graveyard: Starbucks Odyssey wound down Mar 2025; Crypto.com's Cronos rewards cut triggered a 22% token drop (2022). Consumer pull for on-chain rewards is unproven at brand scale; $FLY's 6.2 txns/wallet is early and metro-bound. Second-order: if breakage/velocity disappoints, the "stable value" promise (issuer must honor redemptions) becomes an unredeemed-liability overhang on the issuer's balance sheet.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) If the "arcade token" framing sticks, it's a **repositioning play**: recasting stablecoins' unglamorous cousin — closed-loop utility tokens — as a distinct, regulation-friendly category to keep consumer-crypto issuance alive after the NFT-loyalty bust. Falsifiable thesis: arcade tokens are viable only where they stay narrowly closed-loop (Quarters-style), and any push toward cross-brand tradability re-triggers securities + money-transmission risk. Watch triggers: (1) any *new* SEC/CFTC guidance or enforcement touching utility/loyalty tokens post-2025; (2) whether Blackbird Club's cross-restaurant points cross the closed-loop line; (3) a second non-crypto-native brand shipping (not just announcing) an on-chain loyalty token — absent that, the category stays a single-example thesis.
