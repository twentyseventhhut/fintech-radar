# Revolut rebrands as Revolut Bank in Ireland

## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
### [0] What happened
Revolut has rebranded its Irish consumer proposition to **"Revolut Bank"** in Ireland (announced ~2026-07-15, via Connecting the Dots in Fintech). The stated aim is to encourage users to **deposit their salaries** with Revolut, removing "ambiguity" and assuring customers that funds and savings balances carry deposit-insurance protection comparable to legacy banks. This is a marketing/positioning move sitting on top of Revolut's existing EU banking licence — not a new licence grant.

### [1] Internal context (prior notes)
- [[Revolut passes 1.5 million Irish customers after 100,000 H1 additions]] (2026-06) — scale: ~3.4M Irish customers per the digest text (the ">1.5M" title likely refers to a sub-segment); Ireland is one of Revolut's deepest-penetrated markets, so a "become-your-main-bank" push has large addressable upside.
- [[Revolut launches first Irish brand campaign with Graham Norton]] (2026-06) — the "Welcome To My Bank" campaign is the immediate marketing predecessor; the rebrand is the productisation of that same "we are your bank" message. The two are complementary, NOT duplicates.
- [[Revolut's Irish mortgage launch slips into 2026]] (2025-11) — Revolut is building a full retail-banking stack in Ireland (mortgages, savings, investments) to challenge the incumbent trio (AIB, Bank of Ireland, PTSB) that hold ~92% of new mortgages; the salary-capture rebrand is the deposit-funding leg of that strategy.
- Related Revolut structural notes: [[Revolut appoints Kuba Fast CEO of Revolut Bank UAB]] (2026-07), [[Revolut strengthens AML and fraud controls in Lithuania]] (2026-07) — reinforce that the Irish entity is a **branch of Revolut Bank UAB (Lithuania)**, which is central to the deposit-guarantee caveat below.

### [2] External timeline / prior art
- 2025-11: Irish mortgage launch slips into 2026; ~3M+ Irish customers cited.
- 2026-06: First major Irish brand campaign ("Welcome To My Bank", Graham Norton); +100k H1 Irish additions.
- 2026-07: Rebrand to "Revolut Bank" in Ireland + Irish (IE) IBAN migration rollout — accounts of Irish-resident customers migrate to the Irish branch of Revolut Bank UAB, enabling salary receipt, direct debits and utility payments on a local IBAN. Migration occurs ≥2 months after eligibility notification.
- The Irish branch is on the Central Bank of Ireland register (firm ref C494274). Revolut Bank UAB is ECB/Bank-of-Lithuania supervised, passported EU-wide.

### [3] Why it matters
- **Deposit funding / main-bank status**: capturing salary inflows converts Revolut from a "second card" into a primary account, unlocking cheaper deposit funding, higher LTV, and cross-sell (savings, mortgage, investments) — the strategic prize in a market where AIB/BoI/PTSB dominate.
- **Competitive**: direct assault on Irish incumbents at a moment when Revolut already has deep penetration and a running brand campaign.
- **Deposit-protection nuance (key caveat)**: the note's "European Deposit Guarantee Scheme" phrasing is a simplification. Because the Irish entity is a **branch of Revolut Bank UAB**, eligible deposits are protected up to €100,000 under the **Lithuanian** deposit-insurance scheme (implementing the EU DGS Directive), **not** the Irish Deposit Guarantee Scheme. Same €100k ceiling and EU-harmonised, but a different national backstop — a live consumer-communication and trust risk.

### [4] Open questions
- Exact scope: is "Revolut Bank" a formal legal/product rebrand or purely marketing naming? (Legal entity remains Revolut Bank UAB, Irish branch.)
- Does the rebrand come with any new Ireland-specific product (local deposit protection, Irish mortgage GA, credit)? Not evident yet.
- Timeline for full IE IBAN migration of the whole ~2–3.4M Irish base.
- Regulatory/marketing-standards risk from branding as "Bank" while deposits sit under the Lithuanian scheme.

1. **Is this a licence event or just marketing?** — Marketing/positioning. Revolut already holds an EU banking licence via Revolut Bank UAB; the "Revolut Bank" name and salary-capture push sit on top of it. No new authorisation. (answered)

2. **Is the note's "European Deposit Guarantee Scheme" claim accurate?** — Partly. Deposits are covered up to €100k under the EU DGS Directive, but via the **Lithuanian** scheme (Revolut Bank UAB), not the Irish DGS. The digest wording glosses over the national backstop. (answered — material caveat)

3. **Is this a duplicate of the June "Welcome To My Bank" campaign note?** — No. That was a brand-advertising launch; this is the product/entity rebrand + IE IBAN migration enabling salary deposits. Sequential, complementary. (answered)

4. **How real is "deposit your salary" today vs announced?** — Live-ish: IE IBAN migration is actively rolling out (≥2 months post-eligibility notice), enabling salary/direct-debit locally. Full-base migration is phased, not complete. (answered)

5. **Does the caveat undermine the "same protection as legacy banks" claim?** — Somewhat. The €100k ceiling matches, but recourse is to a Lithuanian fund, not Irish — a trust/communication and potential advertising-standards exposure. (open — regulatory reaction unknown)

6. **Is there quantified deposit or salary-capture data?** — No hard numbers in the source; strategic intent only. Prior notes give ~3.4M Irish customers as the addressable base. (open)

7. **Does this change Revolut's funding profile materially?** — Directionally yes if salary inflows convert; no disclosed deposit-balance figures for Ireland to size it. Spain comparator (€4.64bn deposits, 2026-06) shows the mechanism works elsewhere. (open)

8. **Competitive response risk from AIB/BoI/PTSB?** — Incumbents still hold ~92% of new mortgages and primary-account inertia is strong; salary-switching friction is Revolut's main obstacle. No incumbent counter reported. (open)

9. **Regulatory/ECB overhang?** — The ECB has recently curbed some Revolut EU product launches ([[ECB curbs Revolut's new product launches in Europe]], 2026-06); a "Bank" rebrand invites scrutiny but no Ireland-specific block reported. (partly open)

10. **Naming-as-"Bank" legality in Ireland?** — Permitted because it operates as an authorised branch of an EU credit institution on the CBI register (firm ref C494274). (answered)

11. **Is savings/investment adoption real?** — Prior note flags "growing adoption of savings and investment products" but no figures; supports the cross-sell thesis qualitatively. (open)

12. **Second-order: does salary capture accelerate mortgage push?** — Likely — primary-account data and payroll history strengthen credit underwriting once the delayed Irish mortgage launches. (open)

13. **Is the €100k/Lithuanian-scheme detail a reputational time-bomb?** — Possible if a stress event ever tested cross-border DGS payout speed; academically a known EU-passporting friction. (open)

14. **Any evidence of user-facing confusion / IBAN migration complaints?** — Not surfaced; Revolut's help pages describe a phased, notified migration. (open)

15. **Does "Revolut Bank Ireland" imply a standalone Irish entity/subsidiary?** — No. It remains a branch of the Lithuanian entity; Revolut's separate second-EU-bank ambitions (Paris licence, 2026-05) are distinct. (answered)

**Importance: 3/5** — Meaningful strategic step (primary-account/deposit-funding play in a top Revolut market, direct challenge to Irish incumbents) and a notable accuracy caveat on deposit protection, but it is a marketing/positioning rebrand on an existing licence with no new numbers or products, and it extends an already-known Ireland expansion narrative rather than opening a new front.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** European neobanking / daily-banking. Ireland is Revolut's most-penetrated market: ~3.4m customers, >80% of the adult population (per Irish Times/Startup Fortune, as of Jun 2026). The rebrand from "Revolut" to "Revolut Bank" in Ireland is a positioning move up the value chain — from a secondary spending/FX app toward the *primary* salaried account, where the deep deposit relationship (and cheap funding) lives. **Why now:** (1) Revolut completed migration to Irish (IE) IBANs in 2026, removing the "IBAN-discrimination" friction that had capped its use as a salary/direct-debit account; (2) the incumbent counter-attack has begun — AIB, Bank of Ireland and PTSB launched shared payments rail **Zippay** in Mar 2026 (~5m reachable users, instant transfers by mobile number) (per Business Post / RTE). Structure: Irish retail banking is a consolidated oligopoly (AIB, BoI, PTSB "Big Three" post-Ulster/KBC exits) — Revolut is the disruptor attacking a concentrated incumbent base, which is exactly why the incumbents can coordinate on Zippay.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: primary-account share / salary-deposit share, customer balances (funding), retail customers, PBT margin. Revolut Group (parent) 2025: revenue **$6.0bn (+46%)**, PBT **$2.3bn (+57%)**, PBT margin **38%**, retail customers **68.3m (+30%)**, customer balances **$67.5bn (+66%)** — 5th straight profitable year (Revolut FY25 Annual Report, 24 Mar 2026). Position in Ireland: **ahead on reach, catching up on depth** — ubiquitous as a spend/FX app but "not yet the main account where financial life sits for most Irish customers" (Settle.ie, 2026). Moat: network effects (P2P ubiquity) + scale/cost advantage in FX and onboarding; the rebrand attacks the incumbents' one remaining moat — the trust/protection perception of a "real bank." De-PR flag: Irish deposits are protected under the **Lithuanian** DGS (via Revolut Bank UAB), not the Irish Deposit Guarantee Scheme — the note's aggregated text says "European Deposit Guarantee Scheme," which blurs *which* national scheme actually backs the funds.

**Comps & multiples.** Private, so round-valuation not market cap. Revolut last primary/secondary marks: **$75bn** valuation (see [[Revolut completes share sale at $75B valuation]], [[Revolut share sale draws NVentures, a16z, Franklin Templeton]], Nov 2025). On FY25 revenue of $6.0bn → P/S = `$75bn / $6.0bn ≈ 12.5x` — rich in absolute terms but paired with +46% revenue growth and a 38% PBT margin, i.e. growth-justified rather than an anomaly. External neobank comps (WebSearch, Sacra/Sifted 2025-26): Monzo ~$5.9bn (Oct-24 secondary), targeting ~£6–7bn IPO, ~15.2m customers, £25bn+ deposits, small PBT; N26 ~$6bn (2023, reportedly seeking a *down* round in 2025); Starling profitable since 2021, ~2.9m active accounts. Vs peers Revolut is the outlier on scale (68m vs 3–15m) and profitability ($2.3bn PBT), which anchors the multiple premium. Internal neobank comps in-base: [[Monzo surpasses 14 million customers]], [[Starling's rebrand and new website may cost $5 million]] (rebrand cost precedent), [[Revolut's Irish mortgage launch slips into 2026]] (Ireland depth/execution). Ireland-specific revenue/deposit split for Revolut: **not disclosed** → no data.

**Risk flags.**
1. **DGS/protection ambiguity → regulatory & trust risk.** The rebrand's whole pitch is "same protection as legacy banks," yet Irish deposits sit under the Lithuanian, not Irish, DGS. If Irish regulators/media press this, the trust message can backfire — the exact perception Revolut is trying to win.
2. **Incumbent coordination (Zippay, Mar 2026).** AIB/BoI/PTSB now share a mobile-payments rail across ~5m users — attacks Revolut's P2P network-effect moat where it is strongest (Ireland). Second-order: raises the cost of converting "spend app" users into salaried primary accounts.
3. **Primary-account conversion is unproven.** 80% reach ≠ 80% salary share; most Irish users still keep a Big-Three account. Rebrand is *announced*; salary-deposit migration is the metric that isn't yet in evidence.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) This is a *depth* play, not a new market entry: Revolut converting distribution (reach) into balance-sheet primacy (salaries, deposits, cross-sell into the delayed Irish mortgage). Falsifiable thesis: if the rebrand works, watch for disclosed growth in Irish customer *balances* / salary direct-debits over the next 2–4 quarters. Trigger/what breaks it: an Irish regulator or major outlet publicly contesting the "same protection" framing (DGS-scheme confusion), or Zippay achieving meaningful P2P share — either would blunt the conversion.

## Earnings Review

<!-- enrichment:earnings_review -->
> [!note] Earnings layer (added on top of [0]–[4]). The news itself is a **rebrand/product** announcement (no figures). But Revolut is IR-covered and its own **FY2025 annual report** (Revolut Group Holdings Ltd, year ended 31 Dec 2025; filed 2026-04-29, results announced 2026-03-24) is fresh and materially relevant to the deposit-gathering thesis behind the Ireland rebrand. Figures below are from Revolut's own filing/release (PRIMARY), not consensus.

**Verdict (headline read).** BEAT / record year (self-reported; Revolut is private — no sell-side consensus to beat, so "beat" = vs prior year) · revenue **$6.0bn (£4.5bn), +46% YoY** · PBT **$2.3bn (£1.7bn), +57% YoY, 38% margin** (up from 35%) · net profit **$1.7bn (£1.3bn)** · fifth consecutive year of net profitability. Driver: broad-based growth across all streams + strong deposit/balance inflows. No forward guidance issued (private co.).

**Key figures (with growth, FY2025 vs FY2024).**
- Revenue **$6.0bn / £4.5bn, +46% YoY** (from $4.0bn / £3.1bn).
- Profit before tax **$2.3bn / £1.7bn, +57% YoY**; PBT margin **38%** (vs 35% in 2024).
- Net profit **$1.7bn / £1.3bn**.
- Total customer balances (deposits) **$67.5bn / £50.2bn, +66% YoY** — the single most thesis-relevant metric for the Ireland rebrand.
- Retail customers **68.3m, +30%**; business customers **767K, +33%** (~70m total).
- Lending portfolio **£2.2bn ($2.9bn), +120% YoY** — the fastest-growing line; needs cheap deposit funding.
- Balance-sheet posture: ~90% of assets held in cash/cash-equivalents and Treasury investments (conservative).

**By segment / driver.** Revenue is now broad and diversified — 11 product lines each cleared ~$135m (£100m):
- Card payments/interchange **£1.0bn ($1.3bn), +45%** (~22% of revenue — largest stream).
- Interest income **£974m ($1.3bn), +23%** (~22% of revenue).
- Subscriptions **£708m ($936m), +67%** (fastest-growing major stream).
- Wealth (incl. crypto) **£663m ($876m), +31%**; stablecoin volume ~$10.5bn, +156%.
- FX **£606m ($800m), +43%**.
- Fee-based revenue = **76% of turnover (+4.2pp YoY)** — distinguishes Revolut from deposit-spread-reliant legacy banks. Interest income growth (+23%) lagged the group (+46%), so the mix stayed fee-led despite balance growth.

**vs expectations / prior period.** No public sell-side consensus (private). Qualitatively a clean beat vs prior year on every line: revenue +46%, PBT +57% (margin expansion +3pp), deposits +66%, customers +30%. Momentum is accelerating on deposits and lending vs the group rate. Growth here decelerated slightly off the very high prior-year base but remains ~46% — still hyper-growth for a business at $6bn scale. No prior earnings note on Revolut in the corpus to `[[wikilink]]`.

**Guidance / forward.** None issued — Revolut is private and gives no formal guidance. (analysis) The Ireland "Revolut Bank" rebrand is a deposit-funding play: with the lending book +120% and balances already +66% to $67.5bn, converting light "e-money" balances into sticky, guaranteed salary deposits (European Deposit Guarantee Scheme) lowers funding cost and supports net-interest-income growth. Management emphasis in the news — "remove ambiguity," "same protection as legacy banks" — points to deposit primacy / salary switching as the next growth vector.

**Thesis-flags.**
1. **Deposit gathering is the strategic pivot.** Fact: balances +66% to $67.5bn while lending +120%. Why: a fast-growing loan book needs cheap, stable funding. Why it matters: the Ireland bank rebrand (and prior UK/EU licence build-out) is precisely about converting balances into insured salary deposits. Second-order: rising net interest income could shift the 76% fee-led mix over time and lift durable margin.
2. **Margin expansion at scale.** PBT margin 38% (+3pp) at $6bn revenue with +46% growth is rare — signals operating leverage, not just top-line. Second-order: strengthens the case for the widely-reported ~$45bn+ private valuation and a future listing.
3. **Diversification lowers single-stream risk.** 11 lines >$135m; largest (cards) only ~22%. Cushions any one stream (e.g. crypto/stablecoin volatility, or interchange regulation) — a de-risking positive for the thesis.
4. **What's not disclosed / watch:** cost of risk on the +120% lending book is not highlighted in the release — as Revolut leans into credit and deposits, credit-loss provisions and NIM become the metrics to watch; a private co. with light disclosure warrants scrutiny here.
