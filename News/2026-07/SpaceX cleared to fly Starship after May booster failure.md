SpaceX cleared to fly Starship again after booster failure in May
The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) has cleared SpaceX to fly Starship prototypes again, after the company identified the probable cause of the failure of the rocket system’s booster stage during a flight in May. 
SpaceX said over the weekend that the next flight of Starship could happen as soon as this Thursday, July 16. It would be the second-ever launch of the third version, or V3, of Starship. SpaceX also said that this Starship will carry the first third-generation Starlink satellites to space. Previously, Starship had only carried dummy versions of the larger, more powerful internet satellites.
This is SpaceX’s second test flight of its Starship system, and its first as a public company, testing the market’s appetite for the company’s “fly, fail, fix” approach to rocket development that often ends in fireballs — or, as CEO Elon Musk calls the explosions: “rapid unscheduled disassembly.” SpaceX completed its IPO and publicly listed on the Nasdaq Stock Exchange on June 12, making it one of the 10 most valuable companies in the world and raising nearly $86 billion, a record.
SpaceX’s first test launch of the V3 Starship on May 22 was largely successful. The company’s Super Heavy booster lifted the 407-foot rocket into space before the upper stage section separated and deployed 20 satellite simulators along with two modified Starlinks that recorded footage of the Starship exterior.
The new third-generation booster was supposed to return to Earth and perform a simulated landing in the Gulf of Mexico. But its engines didn’t properly re-ignite, and it instead plummeted into the water below. 
The problem happened at that moment of booster separation, according to SpaceX and the FAA. SpaceX said in a post published over the weekend that “slight differences in engine startup on the ship” caused the Booster to turn 90 degrees in the wrong direction. SpaceX said it has modified this engine startup sequence to allow the booster to “more reliably flip in the desired direction” and that the booster has been modified to “improve re-light reliability.”
The FAA said in a statement Monday that the most probable root causes of the Super Heavy booster failure were “heat effects on propulsion system components during the [rocket’s] ascent and erroneous engine alarm system settings.” SpaceX said in its post that it has made changes to Starship’s engine alarm and abort systems that should reduce the chance of a similar failure in the future.
While the first upper stage of Starship V3 was able to successfully deploy its test payload in May and simulate a landing in the Gulf — a milestone SpaceX had struggled to reach before — it also did so while losing one of the three Raptor engines that are meant to be used in the vacuum of space. SpaceX said over the weekend that it has made “[s]everal hardware and operational modifications” to prevent this from happening again.
This next Starship test flight will see the company launch the first of its V3 Starlink satellites to space, which are supposed to increase the satellite network’s capacity and user speeds. SpaceX is planning to deploy 20 of these new satellites during the launch. They are designed to connect with the larger Starlink constellation “via high-capacity lasers” and then burn up in the atmosphere roughly 20 minutes after they are deployed, according to SpaceX. Six of them will be equipped with cameras to photograph the exterior of Starship.
The V3 versions of both Starship and Starlink are crucial to SpaceX’s future. Starlink was the only profitable part of SpaceX’s business in the run-up to its IPO, and SpaceX needs Starship to become a fully reusable rocket system to even attempt its galaxy-brained plans for space-based data centers and interplanetary travel.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is a commercial-space / satellite-connectivity event, off-core-fintech — but the fintech-relevant thread is Starlink Direct-to-Cell as payments/financial-inclusion rails (see landscape below), which is why the launch payload matters more than the rocket. Facts: Starlink is the majority of SpaceX revenue — ~$11.4bn in 2025 (~61% of SpaceX total) and its only profitable segment (~$4.4bn operating income), tracking to ~$15.5bn in 2026 (per valueaddvc / Yahoo Finance, 2026). Subscribers passed 10m in Feb 2026, ~10.3m across 155 countries by 2026-03-31, with analysts (Quilty Space) modelling ~16.8m by year-end 2026. Structure: satellite-internet is a capital-intensive, capacity-constrained, near-oligopoly — the moat is launch access, spectrum and constellation scale, not software. Starlink's edge is vertical integration: it flies its own rockets, so cheaper launch = cheaper capacity. Why now / why this event: Flight 13 (Thu 2026-07-16) carries the first V3 Starlink satellites, which raise network capacity and user speeds; the whole V3 Starship+Starlink stack is what underwrites the ~$15bn revenue ramp. Launch economics: Falcon 9 already delivers ~$1,500/kg reused at ~3 launches/week (per orbital-intel / NextBigFuture, 2026); Starship *targets* $100–200/kg fully reusable with 100t+ payload (Musk, May 2026) — but that is a design target, not achieved: only 1–2 flights/quarter as of mid-2026 (per beyondtmrw / spaceorbitals, 2026). The gap between promise and cadence is the whole story.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: constellation size (sats in orbit), subscribers + blended ARPU, launch cadence and $/kg, and — for the fintech angle — direct-to-cell MNO partnerships / covered subscribers. Key players and basis of competition:
- **Starlink (SpaceX):** incumbent, ~10.3m subs, thousands of sats, in-house launch. Dominant in both broadband and direct-to-cell; V3 launch extends the capacity lead.
- **Amazon Leo (ex-Kuiper):** the credible LEO-broadband challenger — >390 sats in orbit by Jul 2026 (578 needed for Phase 1), "initial service" beta later in 2026 in US/UK/FR/DE/CA, broad availability realistically 2027; Amazon also agreed to buy Globalstar for >$11bn to bolster direct-to-cell (per CNBC 2026-07-02, GeekWire, SatNews 2026-07). Structurally behind on both sats-in-orbit and its own launch capability (buys launches, incl. from rivals).
- **AST SpaceMobile (Nasdaq: ASTS):** the direct-to-cell pure-play — ~60 MNO partners covering >3bn subscribers, FCC direct-to-cell grant, planning 45–60 next-gen sats by end-2026 for US 5G data (per SatNews / SEC filings 2026). Competes specifically on the phone-connectivity layer, not broadband.
- **Blue Origin:** launch-side rival, capital-hungry — [[Bezos space firm Blue Origin seeks $10B new financing]].
Protagonist position: Starlink is clearly ahead on scale, cadence and revenue; its moat is the vertically-integrated launch + spectrum + installed base. But direct-to-cell — the fintech-adjacent layer — is where competition is real (AST on phones, Amazon/Globalstar closing in), and where Starlink's claimed TAM is most contested. SpaceX is also weighing an owned US mobile service ([[SpaceX weighs launching Starlink mobile service for US consumers]]), pushing further down the connectivity stack.

**Comps & multiples.** Revenue/EBITDA multiples for SpaceX are "no data" — SpaceX does not disclose clean consolidated financials, runs large group losses, and its ~$1.77tn valuation is narrative-driven (see the sibling IPO note for the equity/aftermarket comps set). What IS computable here is the connectivity economics, not a trading multiple:
- Starlink revenue-per-sub (blended, analysis): `~$15.5bn / ~13.5m avg 2026 subs ≈ ~$1,150/sub/yr` — order-of-magnitude only, mix-distorted by high-ARPU maritime/aviation/enterprise/direct-to-cell vs $10–30/mo emerging-market residential (per valueaddvc, 2026). Treat as illustrative, not a disclosed metric `[UNSOURCED]` on the exact denominator.
- Direct-to-cell TAM: IPO prospectus claims **$740bn** TAM for Starlink Mobile; GSMA / independent equity research put true addressable revenue nearer **~$15bn/yr** — a ~50x gap that is itself a valuation risk flag (per SatNews / valueaddvc, 2026).
- Internal connectivity/mobile-money comps as read-through, not valuation peers: [[Airtel targets $2bn London IPO for mobile money unit]] (Airtel Africa has agreed Starlink Direct-to-Cell across all 14 markets — the customer side of this trade), [[Bezos space firm Blue Origin seeks $10B new financing]] (capital intensity of the sector). Distribution not computed — qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Execution / cadence gap (Starship).** The $100–200/kg economics that justify the valuation require Starship to be *fully reusable at high cadence*; at 1–2 flights/quarter and a "fly, fail, fix" method that literally ends in intentional splashdown-explosions, that is unproven. A visible Flight-13 failure (first flight as a public company, into the Sept insider unlock) feeds the "sobering up" narrative — second-order: it de-rates the whole Starship-dependent revenue ramp, not just the launch.
2. **Direct-to-cell TAM overstatement.** A $740bn prospectus TAM vs ~$15bn GSMA-grounded reality means the fintech-relevant growth leg is the most likely to disappoint; if direct-to-cell revenue undershoots, the "connectivity → payments" thesis and the premium valuation both weaken.
3. **Competitive encroachment on the fintech layer.** Amazon Leo + Globalstar ($11bn) and AST SpaceMobile (>3bn covered subs via MNOs) are targeting exactly the direct-to-cell / underserved-markets segment that carries the payments angle. Starlink's lead is real but not a monopoly — second-order: MNO partners (Airtel, Kyivstar) can multi-home, capping Starlink's pricing power on the very rails that matter for financial inclusion.
4. **Single-key-man / narrative dependence.** Valuation and cadence both hinge on Musk's promises; post-IPO bonds already trading weak (per sibling note) signals informed money pricing execution risk.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** For fintech, the load-bearing angle is Starlink Direct-to-Cell as resilient payment rails: Mastercard + Kyivstar have already tested Starlink-Direct-to-Cell 4G POS terminals (Mar 2026), and Airtel Africa is deploying Direct-to-Cell across 14 markets from 2026 — extending mobile-money/POS reach to ~3.5bn under-connected people where towers don't pencil out (a cited ~$300bn digital-inclusion opportunity) (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if V3 Starlink capacity + Direct-to-Cell scale on schedule, satellite becomes a genuine last-mile rail for emerging-market payments/POS acceptance (trigger: live Airtel/Kyivstar commercial rollouts, not pilots). What breaks it: repeated Starship failures throttling capacity, or AST/Amazon fragmenting the direct-to-cell market so no single provider gets the scale to serve payments cheaply.
