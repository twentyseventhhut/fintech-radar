Episode Six, Decisionly team up on card dispute automation
Sinziana Albu
09 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Episode Six and Decisionly have partnered to combine card issuing infrastructure with AI-powered dispute automation for issuers.
The two companies have announced a partnership aimed at giving card issuers access to a combined offering covering both card infrastructure and end-to-end dispute management. Episode Six provides enterprise-grade card issuing technology, while Decisionly operates an AI-powered platform for dispute automation. Under the agreement, Episode Six's issuing clients will be able to connect to Decisionly's dispute management platform alongside their existing infrastructure.
Addressing dispute management complexity
According to the companies, the partnership responds to demand from issuers operating across multiple regions, currencies, and product lines, many of which have relied on dispute and chargeback tools not designed for the scale and complexity of current card programmes. John Mitchell, CEO and Co-Founder at Episode Six, said that the initiative was built to address every layer of the card programme stack, and that disputes represent an operational function within payments that is increasingly moving towards purpose-built technology.
Decisionly's platform automates the dispute lifecycle, incorporating regulatory requirements, payment network rules, and programme-specific configurations. The companies stated that the platform achieves automation rates above 95% from the outset of implementation and reduces manual dispute-handling work by more than 80%. Both platforms are built on an API-first architecture, which the companies said allows data to be exchanged between the two systems.
Episode Six describes itself as an API-first, cloud-native card issuing provider operating in more than 50 countries, serving banks and fintechs that use its infrastructure either alongside existing systems or as the basis for new deployments. Decisionly provides API-first dispute infrastructure for fintechs, banks, and processors. Its founding team previously built Chargehound, a dispute automation company later acquired by PayPal, where it processed more than USD 1 billion in disputes for large payments and technology companies.
 Pallavi Kuppa-Apte , CEO and Co-Founder at Decisonly, said the partnership provides access to a base of banks and fintechs already operating on modern card infrastructure, which the company views as positioned to adopt dispute automation. The two companies said their platforms serve overlapping markets across the US, Canada, and Europe.
Implications for issuers
For issuers, the partnership signals a shift in how dispute management is positioned within card programmes: rather than being treated purely as an operational cost, the companies suggest disputes handling can also factor into the cardholder experience. As card issuing infrastructure continues to modernise, dispute automation is emerging as a distinct area of technology investment for banks, fintechs, and processors managing increasingly complex, multi-region card portfolios.

# Context-enrichment: Episode Six, Decisionly team up on card dispute automation
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

**FRESHNESS: fresh.** Internal retrieval shows no prior note on this tie-up: only this note carries `company/decisionly`, and the two existing `company/episode-six` notes are unrelated ([[Trustly and Episode Six partner on US pay-by-bank]], [[Fireblocks and Episode Six unify fiat and digital payments]], plus [[Monee and Episode Six launch ShopeePay card in Thailand]]). This is a genuinely new (small) partner-ecosystem announcement, not a re-run. But note the frame: it is a **distribution/referral partnership, not equity/M&A and not a product with named live customers.**

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- Episode Six (E6, enterprise card-issuing infrastructure) and Decisionly (AI dispute-automation for issuers) announced a **partnership** on **7 Jul 2026** (press release; The Paypers carried it 9 Jul). Decisionly becomes "the disputes partner within Episode Six's partner ecosystem" — i.e. E6 issuing clients *can* connect to Decisionly's dispute platform via API alongside their existing infra.
- **De-PR'd:** this is a **channel/referral/co-sell arrangement**, not a merger, not an equity investment, not a joint product build. Both stacks are already API-first; the "integration" is that data *can* be exchanged. Language is future-oriented ("can now offer", "will be able to connect") — **announced, not a live named deployment.** No customer names, no committed GMV/volume, no revenue-share disclosed.
- Claimed metrics are **Decisionly's own product claims, not partnership results**: ">95% automation from day one" and ">80% reduction in manual dispute work." No independent validation; these predate and are unrelated to the E6 tie-up.
- **Why structured this way:** E6 sells the "rails" (issuing) but disputes are a painful adjacent operational function it doesn't want to build. Rather than build or buy, it slots a specialist into its partner marketplace — cheap, non-exclusive, low-commitment. For Decisionly (a ~2-year-old, ~6-person startup) the value is pure **distribution**: instant access to E6's book of banks/fintechs already on modern rails. Neither side is betting the company; that is *why* there are no numbers and no exclusivity — the deal is deliberately lightweight.

## [1] Competitors / peers
The dispute/chargeback-automation space is crowded and hotter than this note suggests:
- **Casap** — $25M Series A (Aug 2025, Emergence + Lightspeed; $33.5M total), same pitch: AI dispute automation for banks/issuers, first-party-fraud deflection. Closest direct competitor to Decisionly by segment (issuer-side) and pedigree. [[Casap raises $25 million Series A for card-dispute AI]]
- **Chargeflow** — $35M Series A (Nov 2025; $49M total), 15,000 merchants, but **merchant-side** (friendly-fraud, representments) — adjacent, not the same buyer. [[Chargeflow raises $35M for chargeback automation]]
- **Chargebacks911 / Fi911** — launched a "Disputes-as-a-Service" **Unified Dispute Management System** (Oct 2025) spanning issuers, acquirers, merchants — an overlay incumbent directly targeting the same "unify fragmented dispute data" pitch. [[Chargebacks911 launches Unified Dispute Management System]]
- **Visa** — six AI dispute-resolution tools (Apr 2026); processed 106M disputes in 2025 (+35% since 2019). The **network itself** is moving into dispute tooling for issuers/acquirers. [[Visa unveils dispute resolution services and subscription manager]] / [[Visa unveils services to modernize dispute resolution]]
- **Verifi (Visa) / Ethoca (Mastercard)** — network-owned pre-chargeback alert rails; the scheme layer everyone else must integrate with.
- **Ant / Antom Copilot 2.0** — merchant-side chargeback AI (Jul 2025). [[Ant International expands Antom Copilot 2.0 with chargeback AI]]
- Also in-corpus: [[Guzco raises €1 million to automate e-commerce chargeback disputes]], [[Chargebacks911 partners cside to boost fraud prevention]].
- **Position:** Decisionly is a **late, sub-scale entrant** (est. Feb 2024, ~6 FTE, seed-stage, backed by Fika Ventures + Vesey Ventures) whose edge is the founders' Chargehound track record (built the first merchant dispute-automation tool, sold to PayPal 2021, processed >$1B disputes). **Why the landscape is this way:** disputes are exploding in volume and regulatory complexity (Reg E/Z, network rules, multi-currency) while incumbent tooling is manual — so both **networks (Visa/MC top-down)** and **startups (Casap/Decisionly bottom-up)** are converging on it. Second-order: the networks (Visa dispute tools + Verifi/Ethoca alerts) can compress the startup TAM from above; the durable question is whether an issuer-side point solution survives when the scheme offers "good enough" native tooling.

## [2] Company history / fit
- **Episode Six:** API-first, cloud-native issuing/ledger platform, >50 countries, ~70+ enterprise customers (banks + fintechs), used alongside or in place of legacy cores. Recent corpus activity is a string of **partnership/rails deals**: pay-by-bank with Trustly (Sep 2025), fiat+digital with Fireblocks (Nov 2025), ShopeePay card in Thailand (Jan 2026). **Why:** E6's model is to be neutral infrastructure and grow via an ecosystem of best-of-breed partners rather than a monolith — this disputes tie-up is textbook same-playbook, extending the "full card-programme stack" narrative (CEO John Mitchell's framing).
- **Decisionly:** founded Feb 2024 by ex-Chargehound leadership (CEO Pallavi Kuppa-Apte, ex-COO Chargehound; team incl. Malcolm McDonald, JJ Weber). Debuted at FinovateFall 2025; seed-backed (Fika Ventures, Vesey Ventures; angels reportedly from Stripe/Plaid/Brex). **Fit is logical:** the founders' pattern is "automate disputes for merchants → this time for *issuers*." A distribution deal with an issuer-platform is the fastest GTM for a tiny team — it needs channels, E6 needs an off-the-shelf disputes answer. Clean mutual fit, low stakes.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Novelty: low.** AI dispute automation is not new (Chargehound did it ~2014; Casap/Chargeflow/Chargebacks911/Visa all shipping in 2025–26). The only differentiator is **issuer-side, full-lifecycle, embedded in an issuing platform** — a positioning nuance, not a technical breakthrough.
- **Traction: unproven for this deal.** The 95%/80% figures are vendor claims, not E6-integration results. No named client is live on the combined offering; no volumes. It is an **announcement**, not adoption.
- **Where the margin sits (analysis):** value in disputes is captured by whoever owns (a) the network alert rails (Verifi/Ethoca — Visa/MC) and (b) the issuer relationship. Decisionly owns neither; it is a **thin automation layer** riding on scheme rules and E6's distribution. If Visa's native dispute tools + Verifi/Ethoca cover "enough," a standalone issuer-side automator risks commoditization and being disintermediated by the network from above and by issuing platforms (which could build/acquire, as PayPal did to Chargehound) from the side. **The real question is not "is dispute automation valuable" (yes) but "can an independent point solution keep the margin, or does it get squeezed between the networks and the platforms?"**

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Backdrop is a genuine tailwind:** Visa 106M disputes in 2025 (+35% since 2019); Mastercard projects +24% chargeback volume by 2028 (~324M/yr), ~80% "friendly fraud"; PYMNTS: 42% of issuers say fraud/disputes are driving up costs. Dispute management is explicitly shifting "from back-office to strategic priority." So the category has clear demand and is well-funded (Casap, Chargeflow raises).
- **What to watch:** whether this partnership converts to a **named, live issuer deployment with volumes** (the tell for real vs. PR); whether Decisionly raises a priced Series A (would validate it beyond seed); and whether E6 keeps it non-exclusive (it will — ecosystem model). **Counterintuitive second-order:** the hotter the category and the more Visa/MC build native dispute tooling, the *worse* the odds for a sub-scale independent — network entry can shrink the startup's addressable margin even as headline demand grows. Most likely outcome for THIS item: a modest channel deal that helps Decisionly's pipeline; low near-term systemic significance.

## Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Is this equity, M&A, or just a channel deal?** — Just a **distribution/partner-ecosystem (referral/co-sell) deal.** Decisionly is added to E6's partner marketplace; no equity, no acquisition, no revenue-share disclosed. (fintech.global, Finovate)

2. **Live deployment or announcement?** — **Announcement only.** Language is "can now offer" / "will be able to connect." No named issuer is live on the combined stack; no volumes. Open on any real go-live.

3. **Are the 95%/80% figures partnership results?** — **No.** They are Decisionly's standalone product marketing claims (">95% automation from day one," ">80% manual reduction"), independent of and predating the E6 tie-up. No third-party validation.

4. **Is dispute automation actually novel?** — **No.** Chargehound did merchant dispute automation ~2014; Casap, Chargeflow, Chargebacks911, Ant, and Visa all shipping in 2025–26. Novelty here is only positioning: issuer-side, embedded in an issuing platform.

5. **Who is the closest competitor and is Decisionly ahead or behind?** — **Casap** (issuer-side AI dispute automation, $25M Series A Aug 2025, $33.5M total). Decisionly is behind on capital/scale (seed-stage, ~6 FTE) but comparable on founder pedigree. Behind on traction.

6. **What is Decisionly's real edge?** — Founders built **Chargehound** (first merchant dispute automation, sold to PayPal 2021, >$1B disputes processed). Domain credibility + the E6 distribution channel. Not a tech moat.

7. **Who captures the margin in the dispute stack?** — The **networks** own the alert rails (Verifi/Visa, Ethoca/Mastercard) and are building native issuer dispute tools (Visa's 6 tools, Apr 2026). Decisionly owns neither rails nor the issuer relationship — it's a thin automation layer, structurally squeezable. (analysis)

8. **Downside trigger for Decisionly?** — Visa/MC native tooling becoming "good enough" for issuers (top-down disintermediation), or an issuing platform building/acquiring the capability in-house (as PayPal did to Chargehound). (analysis/hypothesis)

9. **Is the deal exclusive?** — Effectively no; E6's model is a non-exclusive best-of-breed partner ecosystem. Decisionly gets a channel, not a lock. Open on formal terms (undisclosed).

10. **How big is the underlying market / is demand real?** — **Real and growing:** Visa 106M disputes in 2025 (+35% since 2019); Mastercard projects +24% chargebacks by 2028 (~324M/yr, ~80% friendly fraud); 42% of issuers cite fraud/disputes as rising cost (PYMNTS 2026). Tailwind is genuine.

11. **Has Decisionly raised a priced round?** — Only **seed** confirmed (Fika Ventures, Vesey Ventures; angels reportedly Stripe/Plaid/Brex). No disclosed Series A or amount found. Open (PitchBook gated).

12. **Does this fit E6's pattern?** — Yes — E6 has stacked partnership/rails deals: Trustly (Sep 2025), Fireblocks (Nov 2025), ShopeePay/Monee (Jan 2026). This is the same ecosystem playbook. Low incremental signal.

13. **Is it a duplicate of a prior note?** — **No.** No prior note covers this tie-up; only unrelated E6 partnerships exist. Fresh.

14. **Why does the PR anchor to E6's "50+ countries / 70+ customers"?** — To imply distribution scale for a ~6-person startup; but access ≠ conversion. The flattering base is E6's, not proof of Decisionly adoption. (analysis)

15. **What would move importance up?** — A named enterprise issuer live with disclosed dispute volumes, a priced Series A, or exclusivity. None present today.

**Importance: 2/5 — rationale:** Fresh but low-weight. A lightweight, non-exclusive channel partnership between neutral infrastructure (E6) and a sub-scale, seed-stage specialist (Decisionly); no equity, no named live customer, no partnership-specific numbers — the headline metrics are vendor claims. Real category tailwind (disputes exploding; well-funded peers Casap/Chargeflow; Visa/MC entering), but this specific event is an announcement in a crowded, potentially network-squeezed niche. ## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The news sits in **issuer-side card dispute / chargeback automation** — distinct from the merchant-side chargeback space (Chargeflow, Guzco). Dispute volume is the secular driver: Mastercard/Datos project ~261m chargebacks in 2025 rising to ~324m by 2028 (+24%), with ~80% attributed to "friendly"/first-party fraud (per Mastercard, via chargeflow.io, 2025). Visa says it processed >106m disputes globally in 2025, +35% since 2019 (per Visa, via cnbc.com, Apr 2026). Global chargeback cost is cited at ~$33.8bn (2025) → ~$41.7bn (2028) (per chargeback.io, 2026 — secondary aggregator, treat as directional). **Why now:** (1) network rule tightening — Visa's excessive-chargeback threshold drops from 1.5% to 0.9% effective 1 Apr 2026 (per Visa Acquirer Monitoring docs, via paymentnerds.com), raising the compliance cost of poor dispute handling; (2) issuers face hard regulatory clocks (Reg E for debit resolution windows, Reg Z), a compliance burden merchant-side tools never carried; (3) the networks themselves are automating disputes (Visa AI dispute tools, Apr 2026), pulling the whole category toward "purpose-built" tech. Structure: **fragmented and early** — a cluster of sub-scale startups (Decisionly, Casap, Efficio) versus legacy back-office/BPO workflows inside banks. Barrier to entry is regulatory/network-rule depth plus issuer distribution, not capital.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs the players run on: **automation rate** (Decisionly claims >95% from day one), **manual-work reduction** (>80% claimed), **win rate**, and **regulatory SLA adherence**. De-PR note: 95%/80% are vendor-stated, unaudited, and undefined as to denominator `[UNSOURCED]`. Basis of competition = product depth (network-rule + Reg E coverage) and distribution into issuer books, not price. Key players / recent moves: **Casap** — $25m Series A at a **$105m** post-money (Emergence, Lightspeed, SoFi; per pymnts.com / forbes.com, 2025), 15+ customers incl. banks/credit unions; **Decisionly** — founded by the ex-**Chargehound** team (Pallavi Kuppa-Apte, ex-COO; Chargehound acquired by PayPal May 2021), backed by Fika Ventures, Vesey Ventures, angels from Stripe/Plaid/Brex (per medium.com/fika-ventures, 2026); **Efficio** — named as a third issuer-side entrant. Protagonist positioning: **Episode Six** is the card-issuing rail (API-first, cloud-native, 50+ countries, ~$98–105m raised, founded 2015; per Tracxn/CBInsights) — here it is the *distribution partner*, not the dispute IP. Decisionly is the disputes specialist, **catching up** to Casap on funded scale but with a stronger pedigree moat (Chargehound/PayPal track record, >$1bn disputes processed). Episode Six's moat is switching costs / embedded rails; Decisionly's is domain intangibles. `(analysis)`

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round attached to *this* deal — it is a distribution partnership, so no deal multiple to compute. Reference comps (private, round valuations — NOT market caps):
- **Casap** — $105m post-money on $25m Series A (2025). Revenue undisclosed → EV/Rev = **no data**.
- **Decisionly** — round size/valuation undisclosed `[UNSOURCED]`; only investor list known.
- **Episode Six** — ~$98–105m total raised; latest a corporate minority (Mar 2025); post-money undisclosed → **no data**.
- Internal comps (in-base): [[Chargeflow raises $35M for chargeback automation]] ($35m Series A incl. $10m debt, 15k merchants, ~$49m total — **merchant-side**, a contrast not a peer); [[Guzco raises €1 million to automate e-commerce chargeback disputes]] (€1m seed, **merchant-side**). Episode Six precedent partnerships: [[Fireblocks and Episode Six unify fiat and digital payments]], [[Trustly and Episode Six partner on US pay-by-bank]], [[Monee and Episode Six launch ShopeePay card in Thailand]].
- Distribution not computed: <3 comparable revenue figures. Qualitative read: category is **early-stage / pre-multiple**; Casap's ~$105m private mark is the only anchor and rich only if measured against still-tiny revenue — normal for seed/Series A SaaS, not a flag on its own.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Unaudited efficacy metrics** — the >95% automation / >80% manual-reduction figures are vendor PR with no disclosed denominator or independent validation; issuer procurement (regulated buyers) will discount them, so real attach may lag the announcement. Second-order: "announced" partnership ≠ "live/adopted" clients.
2. **Disintermediation by the networks** — Visa (AI dispute tools, Apr 2026) and Mastercard are building dispute automation into the rails themselves; a thin third-party layer risks having its value captured by the network stack, compressing Decisionly's addressable margin.
3. **Distribution dependence / channel-not-lock-in** — Decisionly is one entry in Episode Six's "partner ecosystem," not exclusive; conversely Episode Six's own issuer base is modest vs Marqeta/Galileo/Thredd, so the channel it opens may be narrow. Both sides depend on the other's book without committed economics (revenue split undisclosed — who's silent).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Signals that **dispute management is unbundling from the core issuer-processor stack** into a purpose-built layer, mirroring how fraud and KYC unbundled earlier `(analysis)`. Falsifiable thesis: if issuer-side dispute automation is a real category, expect ≥1 more funded round or an acquisition of an issuer-side player (Casap/Decisionly/Efficio) within ~12 months, and named live issuer logos (not just "partnership") from this tie-up. Thesis breaks if Visa/Mastercard native dispute AI commoditizes the workflow first, leaving startups as feature vendors — watch the Apr 2026 Visa threshold change and network tool rollout as the catalyst/kill-switch.
