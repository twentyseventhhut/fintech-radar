# Context-enrichment: Visa and M-Pesa test stablecoins in DRC for cross-border payments
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Per **one report (The East African, ~2026-07-02)**, echoed verbatim by the "Connecting the Dots in Fintech" newsletter, **Visa and M-Pesa are said to be "testing" stablecoins in the DRC** to settle M-Pesa wallet top-ups and cross-border payments "behind the scenes." That is the entire substance. **De-PR'd, almost nothing is disclosed:** no stablecoin named (USDC/EURC is a plausible guess given Visa's default, but **not stated — do not assume**), no chain, no volumes, no corridor, no launch date, no partner beyond "Visa and M-Pesa." The word **"test"** carries all the weight — there is **no evidence it is live**; treat as exploratory/pilot at best.
- **Why so vague → what it reveals:** No Visa or Vodacom press release, no second outlet, and the primary article is paywalled with no Wayback capture → this is a **single-source, numbers-free item**. The "behind the scenes to settle top-ups" phrasing describes a **settlement/pre-funding** application of Visa's *existing* stablecoin rails, not a new consumer product.
- **Entity correction (load-bearing):** In the DRC, M-Pesa is operated by **Vodacom Congo (RDC) S.A.**, **not Safaricom**. The brand sits in M-Pesa Africa (Safaricom + Vodacom JV, completed Apr 2020). The source's loose "M-Pesa" is imprecise; the counterparty is Vodacom.
- **Conflation hazard:** Secondary write-ups blend this with two *distinct, real* DRC/M-Pesa stories (see [1]/[2]). Those are **not** evidence for this specific Visa+M-Pesa+stablecoin+DRC test.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Africa's *live* stablecoin cross-border activity is concentrated in **Nigeria/Ghana/Kenya**, not the DRC:
- **Yellow Card** — Visa stablecoin partnership (2025-06-18); Circle Payments Network (2025-08); Mastercard EEMEA (2026-05). Covered internally: [[Visa expands stablecoin reach across CEMEA with Yellow Card]].
- **Onafriq (ex-MFS Africa)** — Circle USDC pilot (2025-04); and notably the DRC-specific [[Onafriq and Visa launch Visa Pay in DR Congo]] (2025-09, card↔mobile-money interop in **CDF and USD**, **no stablecoin**).
- **Circle Payments Network** (Apr 2025), **HoneyCoin** ([[HoneyCoin raises $4.9 million for stablecoin cross-border payments]], Aug 2025), Flutterwave, Chipper Cash/RLUSD, MoneyGram+Stellar USDC (Kenya).
- **Why this matters (analysis):** A Visa+M-Pesa **DRC** stablecoin settlement corridor, *if real*, would be **geographically novel** — the incumbents haven't prioritized DRC. But Visa itself already has the DRC covered non-stablecoin (Visa Pay/Onafriq) and via [[Visa and Central Bank of Congo sign payments-modernization agreement]] (2026-04), so this "test" is Visa **extending an existing settlement capability into a market it is already building**, not opening a new front. Position: **parity/incremental**, not a leap.

## [2] Company history / fit
**Visa's stablecoin settlement trajectory** (all confirmed): first USDC settlement w/ Crypto.com on Ethereum (2021-03); Solana + Worldpay/Nuvei (2023-09); VTAP tokenization platform (2024-10); [[Visa to pilot pre-funded stablecoins via Visa Direct]] / [[Visa launches stablecoin prefunding pilot via Visa Direct]] (Sibos 2025-09, "limited availability by Apr 2026"); USDC settlement to US banks (2025-12); network expanded to **9 blockchains, ~$7B annualized run-rate (+50% QoQ), 130+ stablecoin-linked card programs in 50+ countries** (2026-04-29).
- **Fit:** Tight. This DRC "test" is a logical **local instantiation of the Visa Direct pre-funding / settlement model** already announced globally, layered onto Visa's DRC push ([[Visa and Central Bank of Congo sign payments-modernization agreement]]; [[Visa powers M-Pesa tap-to-pay across Tanzania and Ethiopia]]).
- **Why Visa acts here:** DRC is **~90%+ dollarized** (IMF: ~90.9% of bank deposits USD, Jun 2024), cash-heavy, and a large mobile-money market (~29M active users, ~30% of population, Q4 2024). A USD-stablecoin settlement rail behind M-Pesa fits users who already prefer USD value → structural demand pull.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Novelty: low-to-moderate and unproven.** The *capability* (stablecoin settlement) is not new to Visa; only the *market* (DRC via M-Pesa/Vodacom) would be. **Traction: none disclosed** — no volumes, no live-client claim, no launch date. This is announcement-grade, not adoption-grade.
- **Why value-add is uncertain (deeper):** Everyone is **silent on economics** — fees, FX spread, CDF↔USD/stablecoin mechanics — which are exactly the load-bearing numbers in a dollarized economy. **Who captures the margin:** if real, Visa captures settlement/rail economics; Vodacom keeps the wallet/customer relationship; the stablecoin issuer (likely Circle) captures reserve yield. No party's cut is disclosed.
- The "top-up settlement behind the scenes" framing suggests **B2B/treasury plumbing**, invisible to consumers — so even if live, the user-facing delta may be marginal cost/speed improvement, not a new product.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Regulatory backdrop (the real gate):** DRC uses the **Congolese franc (CDF)** — it is **NOT** in the CFA/CEMAC zone (that's Congo-Brazzaville). The central bank (BCC) has issued **crypto warnings, not a framework** (communiqués 2018 & 2020: crypto "neither regulated nor authorized"), and since Jun 2024 mandates **CDF-configured terminals** as a de-dollarization push. A behind-the-scenes USD-stablecoin settlement rail sits in a **grey zone**, in tension with both the anti-crypto warnings and CDF-only mandate. No enabling licence is confirmed.
- **Why the market leans this way (analysis):** structural demand (dollarization + cheap/fast remittance need) pulls toward stablecoin settlement, but the **regulatory ceiling in DRC is the binding constraint** — which is likely why this surfaces as a quiet "test," not a launch. **Second-order:** a "behind-the-scenes settlement" framing is precisely how one operates in a grey zone without triggering the consumer-crypto prohibition.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Stablecoin cross-border settlement in Africa — used here as invisible "behind-the-scenes" rails to settle M-Pesa wallet top-ups and cross-border flows, not as a consumer-facing coin. Sector tailwinds are real and dated: Sub-Saharan Africa took in >$205bn of on-chain value Jul-2024→Jun-2025, +52% YoY (per Chainalysis, via web3enabler/FurtherAfrica, 2026-03); stablecoin true payment volume ≈$390bn in 2025, >2x vs 2024 (per McKinsey, 2026). The "why now": SSA remittance fees averaged ~8.78% in 2025 vs 6.49% global (per web3enabler citing World Bank), and stablecoin corridors settle in ~60s at ~1.5–2.5% all-in — a step-change over 3–5 day, 6–8% bank corridors (per opendue/tazapay, 2026). DRC specifically is a thin-banking, dollarized, high-remittance market where mobile money is the primary rail — a natural stablecoin proving ground. Structure: still fragmented and pre-consolidation — dozens of settlement-infra startups (NALA/Rafiki, Tempo, Noah, HoneyCoin, Yellow Card) competing with card networks and MMOs; the durable moat sits with whoever owns *distribution* (wallets, agent networks), not the chain. Barriers: FX/AML licensing per country, on/off-ramp liquidity, and regulatory posture (crypto rules across Africa still tightening in 2026, per Ripple).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: corridor TPV, settlement cost bps, settlement speed, on/off-ramp liquidity depth, take rate. Players split three ways — (a) card networks embedding stablecoins into existing rails (Visa; Mastercall analog); (b) MMOs (M-Pesa, MTN MoMo) owning the ~100m-customer distribution; (c) crypto-native settlement infra (Tempo, NALA, Noah, Ripple). Basis of competition = distribution + liquidity, not tech. Recent dated moves: Flutterwave–Tempo stablecoin settlement (2026-06-08); Ripple into Flutterwave Series E at $3.2bn (2026-06-16); M-Pesa–ADI Foundation dirham-stablecoin/ADI Chain deal across 8 markets incl. DRC (2026, per Business Daily); Flutterwave default-chain = Polygon (2025-10). Protagonist position: Visa is the incumbent-with-optionality — already live at scale (processed $3.7bn stablecoin payments volume across 200+ countries; ~$2.5bn annualized settlement run-rate; Visa Direct stablecoin prefunding pilot moving to limited availability ~Apr-2026, per Visa FY2025 annual report / ARS 2025-12-08). M-Pesa brings the distribution Visa lacks in DRC. This is a pilot ("test"), not a live product — de-PR: no disclosed volumes, corridor, or economics (who captures the FX spread is unstated) `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** Internal comps (same sector/geo, from base): [[Flutterwave partners with Tempo for stablecoin settlement]] (2026-06); [[Ripple invests in Flutterwave's Series E at $3.2 billion valuation]] (2026-06, $3.2bn round post-money — round valuation, not market cap); [[MoneyGram partners with NALA for stablecoin cross-border payouts]] (2026-04); [[Noah and Nala launch instant stablecoin settlement network]] (2026-01, targeting an $850bn emerging-markets pool); [[HoneyCoin raises $4.9 million for stablecoin cross-border payments]] (2025-08). Precise EV/Revenue/PE not computed: no free real-time EV/share-count in-base → `[UNSOURCED]` (Visa trades as a premium, low-double-digit-growth, ~70%+ operating-margin network; multiple is a function of that durability, not this pilot). **Risk flags.**
1. *Pilot ≠ adoption.* De-PR: "test"/"behind the scenes" with zero disclosed volume, corridor economics, or timeline — high risk it stays a demo. For Visa it's immaterial to the $11.2bn/qtr P&L; treat as optionality, not a driver.
2. *Regulatory/FX in DRC.* Dollarized, thin-banking, evolving crypto rules across Africa in 2026 (per Ripple); AML/sanctions exposure on DRC corridors and stablecoin de-peg/liquidity risk could stall a live rollout.
3. *Disintermediation of Visa's own rails.* Second-order: if stablecoin settlement scales in Africa, MMOs (M-Pesa) + crypto-native infra (Tempo/Ripple/NALA) can route cross-border value *around* card networks. Visa co-opting the tech (as here) is defensive — but the value-capture layer may migrate to whoever owns the wallet + liquidity, not the network.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is the incumbent adopting the disruptor's rails to defend its highest-growth segment (cross-border, +12%) — a "if you can't beat the chain, settle on it" move, not a re-rating event for Visa. Watch for: a live (not pilot) DRC corridor with disclosed volume, or M-Pesa naming its settlement stablecoin/chain publicly — that would signal the shift from experiment to product. Falsifiable thesis: stablecoins become the default cross-border settlement layer for African mobile money by 2027; it breaks if MMOs keep stablecoins as an internal treasury tool with no consumer-facing corridor and volumes stay undisclosed.
