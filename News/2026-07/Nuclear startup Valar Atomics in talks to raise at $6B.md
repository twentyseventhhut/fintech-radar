Nuclear startup Valar Atomics in talks to raise new funding at $6B valuation
Valar Atomics, a startup building small modular nuclear reactors (SMRs) — essentially miniaturized, factory-built power plants designed to be cheaper and faster to deploy than traditional reactors — is in talks to raise a new round of capital, according to three sources familiar with the company. The three-year-old company is seeking a valuation of about $6 billion, and Sequoia is expected to lead the deal, the people said.
The Information was the first to report the funding discussions, including that the El Segundo, California, startup is raising a $1 billion equity round.
Part of that capital has been raised previously at a lower valuation, the people told TechCrunch. Specifically, Valar has raised $450 million — including $340 million in equity and $110 million in debt — at a $2 billion valuation, per a Bloomberg report in March.  
Deals structured in multiple installments at varying valuations, occasionally executed at different times, are becoming increasingly common in today’s AI-fueled fundraising environment. These deals can create the perception that capital was invested at a single, uniform valuation. In reality, investors in the same round can end up paying different prices for the same company — a distinction that matters more than ever as outsiders try to benchmark red-hot startups against one another.
Sequoia and Valar Atomics declined to comment. 
Earlier this month, the company showed that its nuclear reactor provided a small amount of power to an Nvidia AI chip.  Concurrently with that proof-of-concept demonstration, Valar and Nvidia announced a partnership to explore the development of nuclear energy to power future AI data centers.
Valar’s rise is playing out against a broader demand crunch. Data center electricity needs are projected to grow sharply over the next several years, and utilities in many regions are years away from adding enough new capacity. That vacuum has turned nuclear power — long plagued by cost overruns and regulatory bottlenecks — into one of the more closely watched corners of the AI infrastructure boom. 
Valar counts Palmer Luckey, the Anduril founder, and Shyam Sankar, Palantir’s chief technology officer, among its backers. Others chasing the opportunity include Kairos Power and TerraPower (backed by Bill Gates), which are building next-generation reactors aimed at tech and industrial customers, and NuScale Power, the only SMR developer with U.S. regulatory design approval. (Last year, it won approval for an upgraded, higher-output reactor design.)
Valar’s technology is based on a helium-cooled, high-temperature gas reactor. The company says it eventually plans to build hundreds of SMRs to power data centers. But while SMRs are theoretically cheaper to manufacture than traditional reactors, the technology is still nascent and it’s not at all clear how long it will take to be deployed at industrial scale.
In the background, Valar has taken an aggressive legal stance toward its regulator. Last year, it joined several states and rival startups in suing the Nuclear Regulatory Commission , arguing the agency wrongly applies the same lengthy licensing process to small test reactors that it uses for full-size commercial plants. (The case hasn’t been unresolved, with both sides repeatedly pausing litigation, which suggests some kind of settlement is in the works.)
The company was founded by Isaiah Taylor, who dropped out of high school when he was 16. The now-27-year-old has said he launched two startups before Valar and proudly shared that his great-grandfather worked as a nuclear physicist on the Manhattan Project.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Valar Atomics (El Segundo, CA; SMR developer, founded 2023) is **in talks** — not closed — to raise a ~$1bn equity round at a **~$6bn** valuation, Sequoia expected to lead. The Information broke it; three sources familiar per TechCrunch. Both Sequoia and Valar declined to comment, so every figure is source-attributed, not company-confirmed.
- **The real story is the round STRUCTURE, not the headline number.** Part of the "$6bn round" capital was raised *earlier at a lower valuation*: Valar took $450m ($340m equity + $110m debt) at a **$2bn** valuation (Bloomberg, March 2026). So a single "round" spans entry prices from $2bn to $6bn. TechCrunch's own framing: multi-installment deals at varying valuations "mask true entry prices" — investors in the *same* round pay different prices.
- **Why structured this way (the anti-PR core):** in the AI-fueled market, stapling an earlier cheap tranche to a later expensive one lets a company (a) advertise a big top-line valuation while (b) the marquee lead effectively anchors a step-up, and (c) creates the *perception* of one uniform price. The "$6bn" is a marketing surface; the blended entry price is lower and unknowable from outside. That is precisely why the item exists — it is a case study in valuation opacity, not a nuclear-milestone story. (analysis)
- **What is a proof-of-concept vs. a business:** earlier in July, Valar powered an Nvidia Blackwell AI chip with a *small amount* of electricity from its Ward250 helium-cooled HTGR (TRISO fuel), and announced an Nvidia partnership to *explore* a ~30MW near-waterless data center in Utah. "Powered a chip" and "announced to explore" ≠ commercial deployment. No live customer, no grid-scale plant, no NRC design approval.

## [1] Competitors / peers
SMR/advanced-fission peers, with 2026 market signals:
- **Oklo** — public, ~$10–11bn market cap, *zero revenue*, no NRC-certified reactor expected until 2027–28; Meta 1.2GW Ohio deal (Jan 2026). The market's richest SMR name despite being pre-revenue.
- **NuScale** — ~$3.6bn cap; the **only US SMR developer with NRC design approval** (and an upgraded higher-output design). Most de-risked on regulation, lowest multiple — the market pays *up* for AI-narrative optionality (Oklo), not for regulatory maturity.
- **X-Energy** — ~$6.1bn (filing for IPO); HTGR/TRISO, i.e. the *same reactor family as Valar*, but far more mature and Amazon-backed.
- **Kairos Power** (fluoride-salt HT reactor; Google 500MW deal), **TerraPower** (Gates-backed sodium fast reactor, ~$1.7bn raised).
- **Position:** Valar at ~$6bn private would sit *above* NuScale and near X-Energy publicly — despite being the youngest (2023), pre-license, pre-commercial. **Why the land lies this way (2nd-order):** the premium is *narrative/name-specific* (Nvidia halo, Luckey/Sankar defense-tech network, a charismatic 27-yr-old founder), not fundamentals-specific. X-Energy proves you can build the same HTGR/TRISO tech with more maturity for a similar number — so Valar's edge is distribution/brand, not technology. The multiple gap vs NuScale closes only if the AI-power narrative deflates. (analysis)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **What's genuinely new here:** almost nothing operationally — the news is a *financing* event, and even that is "in talks." The only hard new datapoint is the ~3x valuation step-up in ~4 months.
- **Traction reality:** "powered an Nvidia chip with a small amount of power" is a proof-of-concept, not adoption; the Utah data center is "to explore." No live customers, no revenue, no license. SMRs are theoretically cheaper to build than large reactors but the tech is nascent and time-to-industrial-scale is unproven — TechCrunch says so explicitly.
- **Who captures the margin / what breaks it (2nd-order):** the value at $6bn rests entirely on (a) the AI-datacenter power shortage persisting and (b) Valar clearing NRC licensing years before rivals — neither is in hand. The same helium/TRISO HTGR is being built by X-Energy (Amazon) with more maturity, so Valar isn't technologically differentiated; its moat is the Nvidia/defense-network brand. If the power crunch eases (behind-the-meter gas, grid build-out, demand response) or licensing drags, the multiple collapses. Disintermediation risk: hyperscalers may back the *most-licensed* option (NuScale) over the *best-branded* (Valar). (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Immediate:** round is unclosed; watch for confirmation of the $1bn / $6bn terms and the blended entry price. Watch the NRC suit — a settlement would be a real catalyst.
- **Sentiment:** SMR-for-AI is a red-hot corner (Oklo ~$10bn pre-revenue; 10+ GW of nuclear committed by big tech), but 2026 public SMR names (Oklo, NuScale) have been *falling* — the public market is already re-pricing the froth even as private valuations climb. That divergence is the tell.
- **Regulatory backdrop:** DOE $17.5bn loan program ([[MTS US government backs nuclear power with $17.5B in loans]]) supports the *demand* side; NRC licensing remains the binding *supply* constraint for advanced designs.
- **Counterintuitive 2nd-order:** capital concentration at a $6bn paper mark makes Valar *more fragile*, not safer — it raises the bar the company must clear (a license, a live plant, real offtake) to justify the mark, and a multi-stage structure means later investors are underwater fastest if the AI-power narrative cools. The story's true weight is as a *market-structure* signal (valuation opacity in AI-era rounds), not a nuclear breakthrough. (analysis)
