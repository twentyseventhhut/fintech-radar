Ripple secures full MiCA CASP authorisation in Europe
Sinziana Albu
06 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Ripple has received full MiCA CASP authorisation from Luxembourg's CSSF, completing its EU regulatory framework.
The authorisation follows the preliminary approval Ripple announced in June 2026 and confirms the company's compliance with the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation. With this licence, Ripple's regulated crypto payments product becomes available to financial institutions, corporates and businesses across all 30 countries of the European Economic Area (EEA).
Regulatory background
MiCA, which came into force to harmonise crypto-asset regulation across the EU, introduced a licensing regime for companies offering crypto-asset services within the bloc. Firms operating in this space were required to move through a transitional period before securing full CASP authorisation from a national regulator. The CSSF, Luxembourg's financial supervisory authority, is one of the bodies responsible for issuing these licences. Ripple's progression from preliminary approval to full authorisation places the company among a limited number of digital asset firms holding complete MiCA CASP status, allowing it to operate under a single regulatory framework throughout the EEA rather than seeking approval on a country-by-country basis.
 Cassie Craddock , Managing Director, UK & Europe at Ripple, said the authorisation allows Ripple to enter the post-transitional MiCA period in a compliant position, adding that institutions across Europe seeking to develop digital asset services are looking for regulated partners.
Expanding regulatory footprint
The CASP authorisation adds to Ripple's existing EU Electronic Money Institution (EMI) licence, also issued in Luxembourg, which permits the company to provide payment and e-money services. Combined, the two licences give Ripple a regulatory basis for offering both payment infrastructure and crypto-asset services within the EU under a unified compliance structure. According to the company, the CASP authorisation forms part of a broader portfolio of more than 75 regulatory licences held by Ripple globally, reflecting a strategy of pursuing jurisdiction-specific approvals across multiple markets rather than relying on passporting from a single authorisation alone.
For financial institutions and corporates evaluating digital asset partners, regulatory status has become a central consideration under MiCA, as the regulation places compliance obligations on both crypto-asset service providers and the institutions that work with them. Full CASP authorisation may therefore factor into how banks and payment providers select partners for crypto-related services in the region. The development also reflects the wider process among crypto and blockchain firms operating in Europe to secure licensing ahead of, or shortly after, the close of MiCA's transitional arrangements, as national regulators continue to process authorisation applications from companies across the sector.

# Context-enrichment: Ripple secures full MiCA CASP authorisation in Europe
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
- On **2026-07-06** Ripple's preliminary CASP approval was **upgraded to a full, final MiCA CASP authorisation** by Luxembourg's **CSSF** (ripple.com press; CoinDesk headline is literal: "Luxembourg upgrades Ripple's preliminary crypto asset provider to fully compliant"). This is the **final grant** — the passportable licence — completing the "Green Light Letter" stage disclosed on 2026-06-23 ([[Ripple receives preliminary MiCA CASP licence in Luxembourg]]).
- **De-PR:** unlike the June disclosure (which the digest overstated as "enabling full-scale payments" when it was only conditional), this one is the real thing: EEA-wide passporting now activates. But note the mechanics — a MiCA licence passports across all 30 EEA states **only after a short host-regulator notification** per country (web); "available across all 30 EEA countries" is the legal reach, not proof of 30 live markets. Live-vs-announced: the **permission** is live; **client traction is still not disclosed** (no new named EEA banks, no RLUSD EEA volume, no GMV/TPV).
- **Why the timing:** regulatory, not commercial. MiCA's transitional grandfathering window **closed 1 July 2026, no extensions** ([[MiCA transitional window for crypto service providers closes 1 July]]). Ripple's preliminary letter (23 Jun) got it in under the wall; the full grant (6 Jul) lands days after the deadline, closing the continuity risk flagged in the June note. This is the "we cleared the bar, on schedule" capstone.
- **The stack it completes:** the CASP pairs with Ripple's **existing EU Electronic Money Institution (EMI) licence, also held in Luxembourg** (preliminary EMI clearance Jan 2026 per CoinDesk; the EMI covers e-money/payment services and is what lets RLUSD — an EMT, the only MiCA stablecoin category — be issued/redeemed). Combined: banks/fintechs/corporates get "collect → exchange → pay out" through **a single integration**. Ripple states the CASP is part of a portfolio of **>75 regulatory licences globally** (company PR figure, not independently verified).

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Circle** — the only clean stablecoin-issuer comp: USDC + EURC are the **only top-ten stablecoins fully MiCA-compliant** (web); Circle France got AMF CASP approval ([[Circle France gets MiCA approval for crypto-asset services]]). RLUSD is a small challenger EMT vs USDC's scale.
- **Coinbase** — first US exchange with full MiCA, **also via Luxembourg/CSSF, Jun 2025** ([[Coinbase secures MiCA license in Luxembourg]]; [[Coinbase secures MiCA licence to serve EU crypto customers]]). Ripple is ~13 months behind Coinbase on the same jurisdiction and stage.
- **Kraken** (Ireland + CSSF Luxembourg), **OKX/Crypto.com/Gemini/BVNK/Blockchain.com** (Malta hub), **Nuvei** ([[Nuvei secures EU CASP licence under MiCA]]), **Triple-A** ([[Triple-A secures MiCA CASP licence from France's AMF]]), **Bridge** (Stripe; MiCA+EMI, [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]]) — all authorised. By **6 Jul 2026 ~283 firms held full CASP** across 25 EU/EEA states (CASP Tracker/ESMA register, web).
- **Position: parity, not first.** The EMI+CASP dual-rail is strong but no longer novel (Bridge/Stripe and Fipto marketed the identical "single integration" pitch earlier, both with final licences). Ripple's edge is **scale + brand + the institutional stack** (Ripple Prime/Hidden Road, GTreasury, Rail, RLUSD), not regulatory firstness.
- **Why the landscape is this way:** the **1-July shakeout** — >80% of EU crypto firms missed the deadline (web) — turned licensing from differentiator into table stakes. Luxembourg CSSF is the passporting hub of choice (Coinbase, Bitstamp, Clearstream, Kraken, now Ripple) for proportionate framework + one-filing EEA reach. Competition has moved from "who gets licensed" to "who monetizes a full-stack rail across the EEA."

## [2] Company history / fit
- Trajectory: Luxembourg EU-ops launch **Jul 2025** ([[Ripple launches European operations in Luxembourg]], stated EMI-for-RLUSD + cross-border goal) → preliminary EMI clearance **Jan 2026** (CoinDesk) → **FCA EMI + Cryptoasset Registration (UK), Jan 2026** ([[Ripple receives FCA EMI license in UK]]) → **preliminary CASP, 23 Jun 2026** → **full CASP, 6 Jul 2026.** The full grant is the on-schedule capstone of a year-long, publicly telegraphed plan; it fits cleanly.
- Broader M&A context (2025, web): Hidden Road ~$1.25bn (now Ripple Prime), GTreasury ~$1bn, Rail ~$200m; ~$500m round at ~$40bn valuation (Citadel/Fortress, [[Ripple raises $500M at $40B valuation]]).
- **Why Ripple acts this way:** post-SEC it is rebuilding from "the XRP company" into a **vertically integrated institutional crypto-payments + custody + prime + stablecoin stack**. Selling that stack to banks needs a regulated EEA moat; EMI+CASP is the entry permit. Structural pressure: stablecoin issuance is commoditizing (USDC/EURC dominate MiCA-authorised supply), so Ripple must win on **distribution/integration breadth**, not the token.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Genuinely new vs the June note:** the licence is now **final and passportable**, not conditional — that is the substantive delta and the reason this is a fresh event, not a re-run. The continuity gap risk (final conditions slipping past 1 July) that the June enrichment flagged is now **resolved**.
- **Still NOT new / not proven:** the dual-licence "single integration" pitch predates Ripple (Bridge/Stripe, Fipto). And **zero new live traction is disclosed** at this milestone — no new EEA client names, no RLUSD EEA circulation, no GMV. "Institutions seeking regulated partners" is unquantified PR.
- **Who captures the margin (analysis):** durable value sits with the **client-integration + settlement rail**, not the EMT. USDC/EURC own the MiCA-authorised stablecoin supply; if EEA banks adopt Ripple's rail but route USDC/EURC over it, Ripple earns rail/FX fees and loses RLUSD issuer yield. The moat is real only if single-integration switching costs build before issuance commoditizes further.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Next: passport-notify across the 30 EEA states, then scale RLUSD issuance/redemption and rail volume via EEA institutional clients; the real proof is disclosed named integrations + TPV, not the licence. Watch the ESMA CASP register for the live entry.
- Sentiment: XRP-press framed both the preliminary and full grants as price catalysts (yahoo: "Ripple's MiCA Win Raises a Hard Question for XRP") — retail-narrative, not earnings-driven.
- Regulatory backdrop: the 1-July deadline crowned ~283 licensed survivors while >80% of firms fell out (web) — this is **consolidation**, and Ripple bought its ticket on time. USDT is shut out of EU-regulated markets ([[Tether abandons Europe as MiCA ban wipes USDT from exchanges]]).
- Risks / **counterintuitive second-order (analysis):** being 1 of ~283 CASPs is a scarcity premium **now**, but Craddock's own framing (institutions building digital-asset capability) implies banks may in-source; the licensed-rail premium compresses as the cohort grows and banks build in-house. The licence is a ticket to the game, not a durable lead.

6. **Is the EMI+CASP "single integration" novel?** No. Bridge/Stripe (MiCA+EMI, [[Bridge secures MiCA and EMI authorisations across EU]]) and Fipto (dual PI+CASP, Jan 2026) marketed the identical pitch earlier, with final licences. Ripple's edge is scale/brand, not firstness.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** MiCA became the single EU crypto-asset rulebook and its transitional period closed **1 July 2026 with no extensions** (per ESMA, via coinpaprika); from that date a firm cannot legally serve EU clients without a CASP authorisation. As of ~6 Jul 2026 the ESMA register held **283 authorised CASPs** (via casptracker.eu) — so a licence is table-stakes, not a moat; the differentiator is *which* passportable licences a firm stacks. The prize is EEA-wide passporting: one national authorisation (here Luxembourg's CSSF) covers all **30 EEA countries** rather than country-by-country approval (note text; IR 2026-06-23). Global stablecoin market cap is **~$290–323bn** with USDT alone ~$188bn (~65% share) and USDT+USDC ~92% (via CoinMarketCap/Motley Fool, Jun 2026) — a highly concentrated, dollar-token-dominated market that MiCA is explicitly trying to reshape toward euro/EU-supervised issuance. Why now: post-transition, banks and corporates face compliance obligations on *both* sides of a crypto relationship, so counterparties are actively filtering for licensed partners — turning regulatory status into a commercial sales lever.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for a regulated crypto-payments/stablecoin play: circulating supply / market cap of the token, transaction/settlement volume (TPV), number of passportable licences, and institutional counterparties onboarded. Basis of competition = **regulatory reach + distribution to institutions**, not retail price. Peers with full MiCA CASP status by mid-2026: **Coinbase** (Central Bank of Ireland), **Kraken** (CSSF Luxembourg + CBI), plus Bybit, OKX, Crypto.com, Bitpanda, and crypto-bank **[[AMINA secures MiCA license in Austria]]** (Austria FMA, Nov 2025). On the *token* side, **Circle's USDC/EURC are the only top-10 stablecoins with full MiCA EMT authorisation** (via eco.com/stableregistry) — a status Ripple's RLUSD does **not** yet hold. Recent Ripple moves: full EU EMI licence in Luxembourg (IR 2026-02-02); FCA permissions in the UK (IR 2026-01-09); preliminary MiCA CASP "green light" (IR 2026-06-23) → full CASP now. Position: **ahead of the pack on breadth** — Ripple claims >75 licences globally, and CASP+EMI in one jurisdiction gives a unified payments+crypto compliance base (analysis). Moat = regulatory/intangible-asset stacking + institutional switching costs, not network effects; it is replicable by any well-capitalised peer (Coinbase/Kraken already have it).

**Comps & multiples.** Ripple is **private**; last round **$40bn post-money** ($500M led by Fortress/Citadel, Nov 2025 — round valuation, not market cap; [[Ripple raises $500M at $40B valuation]]). No public revenue disclosed → **EV/Revenue, EV/EBITDA, P/E = no data**; multiples not computable. Token-level comp: **RLUSD market cap ≈ $1.58–1.64bn**, ranked ~#9 among stablecoins / ~#41–47 overall (CoinGecko/CoinMarketCap, 2026); ~82% of supply on Ethereum, ~18% on XRP Ledger (Apr 2026). Versus USDC's tens-of-billions and USDT's ~$188bn, RLUSD is **sub-1% of the stablecoin market** — a niche token, not a scale competitor. Public-peer valuation anchors: Kraken filed confidentially for a US IPO at **~$20bn** ([[Kraken files confidentially for US IPO at $20B]]); Coinbase Q3 revenue ~$1.9bn ([[Coinbase reports $1.9 billion Q3 revenue as Base turns profitable]]) — both listed/near-listed, so Ripple's $40bn private mark is **[UNSOURCED]** on an earnings basis and rests on strategic/token value. Distribution not computed (no comparable disclosed revenue set); qualitative: valuation looks rich vs disclosed fundamentals and leans on optionality.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Licence ≠ token approval.** The CASP clears Ripple's crypto payment *services*, **not RLUSD itself**; a USD-referenced stablecoin is an EMT under MiCA needing separate EMI-backed authorisation + notified white paper (via eco.com/digitaltoday). Ripple can passport services but **cannot yet freely offer RLUSD to EU users** — the PR framing ("full EU framework") overstates token access. Second-order: EU distribution still tilts to Circle's already-authorised USDC/EURC.
2. **Commoditised advantage.** With 283 CASPs and peers (Coinbase, Kraken) already fully authorised, the licence is necessary but non-differentiating; the moat is intangibles that competitors can and did replicate → limited pricing power.
3. **Regulatory reflexivity.** The EC opened **two MiCA-review consultations closing 31 Aug 2026** ([[European Commission launches two consultations on MiCA review]]), and France/Italy/Austria's FMA have pushed for tighter central EU control — the rulebook Ripple just complied with is itself in flux, and USD-stablecoin treatment is a live target (dual-licensing risk flagged by [[Circle warns EU stablecoin rules risk dual licensing by March 2026]]).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Not a re-rating event — it's a **defensive box-tick** that keeps Ripple in the EEA past the 1 July cutoff and strengthens its "regulated rails" pitch to institutions (analysis). The real catalyst to watch is a **separate RLUSD EMT authorisation** (or a Ripple EU stablecoin issuer): that would let RLUSD actually compete with EURC/USDC in Europe and is the falsifiable test — if no EMT approval lands in the next 2–3 quarters, the CASP win stays a services story and RLUSD remains a sub-1% niche token in the EU (thesis breaks if RLUSD scales EU distribution without a token authorisation).
