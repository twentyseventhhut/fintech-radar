Netflix could be planning ‘always-on’ live TV channels
As Netflix searches for new ways to keep viewers engaged amid signs of slowing engagement, the streaming giant appears to be exploring yet another avenue: always-on live TV channels.
According to The Wall Street Journal , Netflix is considering launching live channels that continuously stream content, giving subscribers something to tune into 24/7. Rather than committing to binge-worthy dramas like “Avatar: The Last Airbender” or the newest thriller “I Will Find You,” subscribers could leave a channel running in the background for hours of entertainment.
This move would put Netflix in more direct competition with free, ad-supported streaming services such as Pluto TV and Tubi. It could also provide a meaningful boost to Netflix’s ad business, since live programming typically doesn’t allow viewers to skip commercials.
The WSJ also reports that Netflix is exploring bundles, similar to offerings from Apple and Amazon. According to people familiar with the matter, Peacock is among the services being discussed as a potential partner.
Netflix didn’t immediately respond to our request for comment.
The reported plans come as Netflix has made other moves to increase viewer engagement. Recently, the company has experimented with short-form video , video podcasts , and a new gaming app for kids to drive viewership. 
Earlier this week, Bloomberg reported that the company has become increasingly concerned about audience declines between the first and second seasons of many of its original shows, raising questions about its ability to sustain long-term hits. Plus, Netflix’s share of total TV viewing has also slipped. According to Nielsen , the streamer accounted for 7.8% of TV viewing in April.
Additionally, Variety reported on Friday that Netflix is in talks to buy Letterboxd, the popular social platform for movie fans.

## Контекст

<!-- enrichment:context -->
### [0] What happened (dated)

- **2026-07-09/10** — Per a **Wall Street Journal** report (echoed by TechCrunch 2026-07-10), Netflix is **exploring "always-on" 24/7 linear TV channels** — continuously streaming tiles on the home page dedicated to a single show, a franchise, or a genre — giving subscribers something to leave running in the background rather than actively pick. No timeline, format, or content strategy has been confirmed; a formal reveal would most naturally land at Netflix's expected **Q4 2026 investor day**. ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/10/netflix-could-be-planning-always-on-live-tv-channels/), [thestreamable](https://thestreamable.com/netflix-considering-live-channels-peacock-bundle))
- Same WSJ report: Netflix is **also exploring service bundles** (Apple/Amazon-style aggregation), with **Peacock named as a candidate partner** — Netflix would resell third-party subscriptions through its app, à la Prime Video.
- **Trigger:** slowing engagement. Netflix's share of total US TV viewing slipped to **7.8% in April 2026** (Nielsen). Bloomberg reported growing concern about audience drop-off between S1 and S2 of originals.

### [1] Why it matters

- **Netflix is copying the cable/FAST format it long called obsolete.** Always-on channels solve the "paradox of choice" (decision fatigue) and lift passive watch-time — the metric that feeds the ad business.
- **Ad-tier accelerant.** Live/linear programming typically **can't be ad-skipped**, so it directly boosts ad load and CPMs. Netflix's ad tier now has **250M+ global MAUs** (May 2026, up from 94M a year earlier) and ad revenue is guided to **~double to ~$3B in 2026** (from >$1.5B in 2025). Linear feeds fresh, non-skippable inventory into that engine.
- **Bundle strategy** turns Netflix from a walled garden into an **aggregator** — a defensive move against Amazon/Apple storefront economics and a hedge on churn.

### [2] Landscape / competitive context

- **FAST market:** ~**$12.3B (2025) → ~$14.9B (2026)**, ~21% CAGR; **1,300+ US FAST channels**. Leaders by US viewership: **Roku Channel ~97.3M**, **Tubi ~92.5M**, **Pluto TV ~68.6M**; **Samsung TV Plus** growing (adding Amazon interactive video ads). Tubi/Pluto built exactly the linear model Netflix is now studying. ([eMarketer](https://www.emarketer.com/content/faq-on-fast--how-free-streaming-tv-reshaping-ad-market-2026), [adwave](https://adwave.com/resources/fast-channel-count))
- **Disney+ already ships "Streams"** — an always-on linear feature — and is reportedly weighing a free tier; so Netflix would be a **follower, not a pioneer**, here.
- **US streaming share:** Amazon Prime Video ~22%, Netflix ~21% (Motley Fool / Hudson Labs 2026).
- **Netflix's live muscle is real but event-based:** WWE Raw (weekly, 10-yr / ~$10B deal since Jan 2025), NFL Christmas games (through 2026), boxing (Canelo–Crawford, Fury 2026). Execs stress "big breakthrough events," not full live seasons — always-on channels are a **cheaper, library-fed** complement to those tentpoles.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
### Red-team / second-order questions

1. **Is this even confirmed, or WSJ-sourced speculation?** — Unconfirmed. It is a WSJ "considering/exploring" report; Netflix declined comment. Treat as a signal, not a launched product.
2. **Is it a fintech story at all?** — Marginal. The fintech-relevant hooks are the **ad-business / monetization** angle and **bundle-as-aggregator storefront** economics; the TV content itself is out of scope.
3. **How is this different from the Letterboxd pick?** — Fully distinct: Letterboxd is M&A of a social/discovery asset; this is a native product/format change. Same news cycle, different events. Not a dup.
4. **Does linear actually lift ad revenue, or cannibalize on-demand?** — Likely additive: non-skippable linear inventory raises effective ad load without new content spend. Risk = it dilutes premium on-demand CPMs if audiences drift to lean-back viewing. Open.
5. **Why would a paid subscriber want ad-laden linear channels?** — They wouldn't, on the ad-free tier. This only monetizes via the **ad tier** (250M+ MAUs, 60%+ of signups), so the upside is concentrated there. Ad-free subs get a lean-back convenience feature, no ads.
6. **Is Netflix late?** — Yes. Tubi/Pluto/Roku built FAST years ago; Disney+ already runs "Streams." Netflix is a fast-follower leveraging scale + a library, not a first-mover.
7. **What's the marginal cost?** — Near-zero incremental content cost (repackaging existing library into channels); mainly product/eng + ad-ops. That's what makes it attractive vs. live-sports tentpoles ($10B WWE).
8. **Could the Peacock bundle signal weakness?** — Possibly. Aggregating rivals hedges churn and mirrors Amazon/Apple, but also concedes Netflix can't hold engagement alone. Strategically defensive.
9. **Regulatory/rights friction?** — Some. Library titles may carry windowing/licensing terms that restrict linear "broadcast-style" use; likely manageable for owned originals, murkier for licensed catalog. Open.
10. **How does 7.8% TV-viewing share reframe the story?** — It's the real driver: engagement erosion + S1→S2 drop-off. Linear is a retention/watch-time tactic, not a growth story per se.
11. **Does this threaten the FAST incumbents (Roku/Tubi)?** — Only inside Netflix's walled garden; it doesn't attack free-ad platforms' reach. Competitive pressure is modest, not existential.
12. **Investor-day timing risk?** — A Q4 2026 investor-day reveal means quarters of "will they/won't they"; the trade here is ad-revenue guidance ($3B/2026), not this feature per se.
13. **Is there a data/targeting angle?** — Yes: linear channels + 4,000+ advertisers + new attributes (income, marital status, high-propensity segments) = better yield. Reinforces the ad thesis.
14. **What would make this a 4-5/5?** — A confirmed launch with disclosed channel economics, or the Peacock bundle closing (would reprice aggregator dynamics). Not there yet.
15. **Base-rate on "considering" reports shipping?** — Mixed; many Netflix trial balloons (short-form, podcasts) do ship in some form given low cost. Moderate probability it launches in 2026-2027.

**Importance: 2/5** — A credible but unconfirmed strategic signal in Netflix's engagement-defense/ad-monetization playbook, and a fintech-adjacent read on streaming ad economics + aggregator bundling. Low direct fintech relevance and no launched product cap its weight; the durable takeaway is the ad-tier trajectory ($3B/2026, 250M+ MAUs) and the aggregator pivot, not the channels themselves.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The target market is FAST (free ad-supported streaming TV) / linear-style channels inside on-demand apps. Sizing: US FAST/streaming ad-market sources put the FAST segment at ~$10.6bn (2025) → ~$12.2bn (2026), CAGR ~15.4% to the mid-2030s (per Global Growth Insights, via web, as of 2026-07); a higher variant cites ~$14.3bn 2026 at ~16.9% CAGR (Mordor Intelligence) — dispersion across secondary reports is wide, treat as order-of-magnitude, not precise. Adjacent: US CTV ad spend projected ~$30bn in 2026 vs ~$18.9bn 2025 (per eMarketer citations, via web) — the pool Netflix's ad tier competes for. FAST US reach ~131mn users, ~54% of CTV users in 2026 (eMarketer, via web). Structure: consolidating at the top — Tubi (Fox), The Roku Channel, Pluto TV (Paramount) lead (Parks Associates, via web); barriers are content-licensing cost + ad-sales scale + CTV distribution, not capital-light. "Why now": Netflix's US share of TV viewing slipped to 7.8% in April 2026 (Nielsen, via TechCrunch/note); engagement decay between S1→S2 of originals (Bloomberg, via note) pushes it toward always-on "lean-back" formats and non-skippable ad inventory.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: ad ARPU / CPM, minutes-of-engagement, ad-load (min/hr), share-of-viewing (Nielsen Gauge). Players & basis of competition: Tubi, Roku Channel, Pluto TV compete on FREE + breadth of themed linear channels + ad-load tolerance; Netflix would enter with a PAID base (325mn subs, per demandsage/web) plus a fast-growing ad tier (>250mn global MAU on the $8.99 plan by May 2026, up from ~190mn Nov 2025; ad revenue tracking ~$3bn in 2026, per web). Recent moves: Fox's Tubi hit its first profitable quarter on +27% revenue (Fox FQ1-2026, via TheWrap); Comcast Peacock revenue $2.1bn in Q1-2026 (+71% y/y), 46mn subs (Hollywood Reporter, via web) — and Peacock is named as a candidate Netflix bundle partner (WSJ, via note). Position: Netflix is a late entrant to the linear/FAST format but enters from a position of scale and a captive ad audience — catching-up on format, ahead on distribution `(analysis)`. Moat: scale + owned recommendation/engagement data + content library (intangibles), not a format-specific moat.

**Comps & multiples.** Netflix is the protagonist; peers are ad-supported/CTV names (imperfect comps — different scale/model, so qualitative).
- **Netflix:** mkt cap ~$448.7bn post-Q1-2026; FY2025 revenue $45.2bn → P/S = $448.7bn / $45.2bn = **9.9x** (web). P/E not computed — no clean free EPS figure `[UNSOURCED]`.
- **Roku:** mkt cap ~$19.9bn, TTM revenue ~$4.96bn → P/S = $19.9bn / $4.96bn = **4.0x**; reported EV/Revenue ~3.55x (GuruFocus, Jun 2026, web).
- **Fox (Tubi parent):** Tubi is embedded in Fox — no standalone multiple; Tubi revenue +27% y/y, first profitable quarter (web). Peacock (Comcast) similarly non-separable.
- Internal comps (grep fallback; semsearch 'notes' table unbuilt): [[Netflix expands revamped app across Asia, pushes kids' games]], [[Netflix in talks to acquire film-and-TV social network Letterboxd]] — same-name engagement-driving moves; no in-base valuation prints for FAST peers.
- Flag: Netflix P/S ~9.9x is ~2.5x Roku's ~4.0x — richness is a function of Netflix's paid-subscription profitability/growth, not directly comparable to ad-only Roku; distribution not computed (only 2 clean multiples), qualitative comparison only. Not flagged as over-valuation on this evidence.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Format is cannibalization-prone, not additive.** Always-on channels reshuffle existing catalog into linear windows; if they mostly shift where subscribers watch (vs adding ad-tier sign-ups), the ad-revenue uplift is marginal while licensing/programming cost rises `(analysis)`.
2. **Entering a low-margin, price-anchored category.** FAST is anchored at FREE (Tubi/Pluto); Netflix's edge is a $8.99 paid wall — the non-skippable-ad economics only pay off if the ad tier keeps compounding, and the FAST leaders are already break-even-or-better and scaling ad volume (Tubi upfront double-digit growth), i.e. competitive pressure on CPMs.
3. **Reported/exploratory, not committed.** Sourced to WSJ "considering/exploring" (via note) — Netflix declined to comment; execution, launch timing and the Peacock bundle are all unconfirmed. De-PR: "weighing," not "launching."

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` This is a defensive engagement play, not a re-rating catalyst: it signals Netflix conceding that pure on-demand is losing share-of-time to lean-back/linear, and moving to defend both minutes and ad inventory against Tubi/Roku/Pluto. Falsifiable thesis: if launched, watch the ad tier's MAU and ad-revenue trajectory (run-rate toward/above ~$3bn) plus Nielsen share-of-viewing — a re-acceleration validates it; flat share + no ad-ARPU lift breaks it. Trigger to watch: a formal announcement and whether the Peacock/bundle partnership materializes.
