Stripe, Visa and over 140 other businesses to launch stablecoin to rival Tether and Circle

 A consortium of financial giants are taking aim at the top two stablecoin issuers. On Tuesday, Open Standard, a company that says it's partnered with Stripe, Visa , BlackRock and over 140 other businesses, announced that it will launch a new stablecoin called Open USD, or OUSD. The two largest stablecoin issuers, Tether and Circle, are notably not part of the new consortium. 
 OUSD will launch later this year and is structured to return most reserve revenue, or interest earned on the assets backing it, minus a small management fee, to participants. Open Standard did not disclose on which blockchain the stablecoin will operate. 
 "It's a stablecoin built for the internet economy, designed by the businesses growing it," Zach Abrams, interim CEO of Open Standard, said in a statement. Abrams is also the cofounder of the stablecoin startup Bridge, which Stripe acquired for $1.1 billion in 2025. 
 The new initiative appears to be a serious challenge to Tether and Circle, which have long dominated the market for stablecoins, or cryptocurrencies pegged to real-world assets like the U.S. dollar. In April, Tether's stablecoin, USDT, accounted for about 62% of the stablecoin market, while Circle's USDC held roughly 25%, according to a report from the crypto analytics company CoinGecko. 
 Circle's stock slipped 13% after the announcement and now trades at $66 per share. 
 "We welcome continued innovation and competition in the space," Circle CEO Jeremy Allaire wrote on X following the announcement.  
 "Welcome OUSD. Player 2 has entered the game," wrote Tether CEO Paolo Ardoino. 
 Corporate stablecoin race 
 Open USD is the latest in a flood of new stablecoins after July 2025, when President Donald Trump signed into law the Genius Act, which establishes a regulatory framework for the tokens. Several corporations have also begun to experiment with stablecoins. In November, payments provider Klarna launched KlarnaUSD. Retail giants Amazon and Walmart have also expressed interest in issuing their own stablecoins. 
 Meanwhile, consortiums in crypto between large corporate players aren't novel. In November 2024, Singapore-based Paxos launched USDG, a stablecoin backed by the Global Dollar Network, a pool of major crypto, fintech, and traditional finance firms. Participants include Mastercard , Robinhood, and Kraken. 
 There have also been attempts to create shared blockchain networks. The fintech Stripe worked with the venture firm Paradigm, along with a number of design partners, to launch Tempo , a blockchain created to support stablecoin payments. 
 And major banks have also joined forces to launch a shared deposit network. Earlier this month, JPMorgan Chase , Bank of America , Citigroup , and others unveiled The Clearing House, a payments system that lets bank deposits move as digital tokens over blockchain-style rails.

Open Standard launches Open USD stablecoin for firms
Sinziana Albu
01 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
Open Standard has launched Open USD, a stablecoin backed by more than 140 companies including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, and BlackRock.
The stablecoin brings together card networks, banks, fintechs, and crypto firms under a shared governance structure. According to Open Standard, Open USD is intended to address recurring limitations in the existing stablecoin market, including high minting and redemption fees at scale, limited access for businesses to earnings generated on reserves, and dependence on the roadmaps of individual third-party issuers.
Open Standard has structured Open USD around three stated principles. First, businesses will be able to mint and redeem Open USD without cost and without volume limits, a feature the company describes as built for scale. Second, partners will receive the earnings generated on Open USD's reserves, less a management fee to cover operational costs. Third, the stablecoin will be governed collaboratively through a board composed of Open USD's partner companies, rather than being controlled by a single entity.
Open Standard stated that Open USD will go live later in 2026.
Backers span payments, banking, and crypto
The list of participating companies includes payment networks and processors such as Visa, Mastercard, American Express, Discover, Fiserv, and Adyen, fintechs including Stripe, Klarna, Affirm, and Ramp, as well as remittance and cross-border specialists such as Western Union, MoneyGram, Remitly, and Ria.
Banking participants include BlackRock, BNY, Standard Chartered, Commonwealth Bank of Australia, DBS Bank, U.S. Bank, and BBVA, among others, across Asia-Pacific, the Middle East, Latin America, and Africa. Technology and commerce platforms Google, Samsung Electronics, IBM, Shopify, Mercado Libre, and DoorDash are also listed as participants, alongside crypto and digital asset firms including Coinbase, Ripple, Solana, Base, Fireblocks, and Stellar.
Representatives from several partner companies commented on the initiative. BlackRock's Samara Cohen said stablecoins with neutral governance and shared economics could support further growth in digital assets, adding that the firm anticipates the broader stablecoin market could reach USD 1.5 trillion by 2030. Mastercard's Jorn Lambert said the goal was for infrastructure underpinning stablecoins to be open, interoperable, and broadly accessible, while Stripe's Will Gaybrick said Open USD would become the default stablecoin for businesses operating on Stripe's platform.
The announcement comes as stablecoin transaction volumes have been approaching levels comparable to the ACH network, according to Open Standard. The initiative reflects a broader trend of payment networks, banks, and technology platforms coordinating on shared stablecoin infrastructure rather than pursuing separate, proprietary approaches. Open Standard has not disclosed further technical or regulatory details ahead of Open USD's planned launch later this year.

# Context-enrichment: Visa, Stripe, 140 firms launch Open USD (OUSD) stablecoin
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On 30 Jun 2026, **Open Standard** — an independent consortium company — announced **Open USD (OUSD)**, a USD-pegged stablecoin backed by 140+ firms (Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, BlackRock, BNY, Coinbase, Ripple, Google, Shopify, DoorDash, Klarna, Affirm, Ramp, Western Union, MoneyGram, Standard Chartered, DBS, BBVA, and others). Interim CEO: **Zach Abrams**, co-founder of Bridge (the stablecoin infra firm Stripe bought for ~$1.1bn in late 2024/2025).

De-PR'd reality — this is an **announcement, not a live product**:
- **OUSD is not live.** It "will launch later in 2026." No token in circulation, no market cap, no on-chain settlement volume yet. Everything below is design intent, not adoption.
- **Chain not fully committed at launch PR.** Open Standard did not confirm chains in the core release; Solana's account said OUSD launches natively on Solana day one, with Stellar/Base/Polygon (and per some outlets Aptos) later. Treat as "planned," multi-source but not from Open Standard's own release.
- **The one real inversion (mechanism delta):** partners who drive adoption capture **nearly all reserve yield** (interest on the T-bill/cash backing), minus a small management fee — vs the incumbent model where the issuer (Circle/Tether) keeps the float income. Plus **zero-cost, uncapped mint/redeem** and a **partner-board governance** structure.
- **The tell:** the PR anchors on "who profits from the reserve yield," because that is the ONLY structural novelty. The tech (fiat-backed token on public chains) is commodity. → The whole pitch is a **distribution + economics play**, not a technology play: assemble everyone who moves dollars and bribe them with the float.

**Why structured this way:** yield-sharing is precisely the wedge against Circle. Circle's entire P&L is reserve income (it keeps the float; even pays Coinbase a rev-share to keep 100% of on-exchange interest). A consortium that hands the float back to distributors directly attacks the incumbent's revenue model — hence Circle's ($CRCL) stock fell ~15-17% on the day (to ~$62-66). Note the regulatory subtlety: under the GENIUS Act (effective Jul 2025) issuers are **prohibited from paying yield to token holders** — OUSD routes yield to **partner businesses** (distributors), not holders, which is the legally survivable path.

## [1] Competitors / peers
- **Circle (USDC)** — ~$75-77bn cap, ~25% share; the direct target. Model = keep reserve income → most exposed. Coinbase rev-share renews Aug 2026, adding pressure.
- **Tether (USDT)** — ~$186-190bn, ~62% share; less threatened near-term (offshore/liquidity moat). CEO Ardoino: "Player 2 has entered the game."
- **Global Dollar Network / USDG (Paxos)** — the true precedent: a **100+ partner consortium built on the SAME yield-sharing pitch** (Mastercard, Robinhood, Kraken, Nuvei, Worldpay). Launched Nov 2024. Result after ~18 months: only ~$1-3bn cap — i.e. **the consortium-yield model has already been tried and has NOT dented USDC/USDT** (>95% combined share). This is the single most important comparator: same idea, weak traction. [[Visa and Mastercard accelerate stablecoin and crypto payments]]
- **Bank consortium (The Clearing House / tokenized deposits)** — JPMorgan, BofA, Citi moving deposits as tokens; adjacent competition for the same B2B settlement flows.
- **Stripe Tempo** (with Paradigm) and **Circle Arc** — proprietary stablecoin-payment chains; note Stripe is now backing BOTH Tempo and OUSD.

**Why the landscape is this way + second order:** distribution, not tech, decides stablecoin winners; that's why incumbents' moats are network liquidity (USDT) and regulatory/institutional trust (USDC). A consortium can in theory out-distribute both — but USDG proves that **shared governance dilutes urgency**: 140 co-owners each have a side bet (Visa/Mastercard have their own settlement rails; Stripe has Tempo/Bridge; Coinbase has Base/USDC rev-share). The multi-homing that makes the roster impressive also makes commitment shallow.

## [2] Company history / fit
Direct continuation of the ecosystem's 18-month march into stablecoins, captured in the corpus:
- Stripe bought **Bridge** (~$1.1bn) and launched USD stablecoin payments on Ethereum/Base/Polygon (Dec 2025). [[Stripe launches USD stablecoin payments on three chains]]
- Visa expanded stablecoin settlement to 9 chains, hit a **$7bn run-rate** (2026). [[Visa expands stablecoin settlement network to $7bn run rate]] [[Visa expands stablecoin settlement support]]

**Why they act this way:** card networks (Visa/Mastercard) earn on interchange, not float, so they lose nothing by giving yield away — and gain a hedge if stablecoins disintermediate cards. Stripe, via Bridge/Abrams, is positioning to be the **default settlement layer** ("OUSD will be the default stablecoin for businesses on Stripe" — Will Gaybrick). For them OUSD is cheap optionality on a rail they don't yet control.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Genuinely new:** the scale of the coalition (140+ incl. BOTH card networks + BlackRock + a big-tech/bank spread) and the yield-back economics at this scale. That combination has not existed before.
- **Not new:** the model itself. USDG did yield-sharing consortium in 2024 and stalled at ~$1-3bn. The delta OUSD needs is that Visa+Mastercard+Stripe+Coinbase together command far more real-world payment distribution than USDG's roster.
- **Traction = ZERO today.** No token, no volume, no market cap. Claimed context: Open Standard says stablecoin volumes are "approaching ACH levels" and BlackRock's Samara Cohen cites a $1.5T stablecoin market by 2030 — both are market-size framings, not OUSD adoption.

**Why the value-add is real or not (2 levels deep):** the value is real ONLY if distributors actually route volume through OUSD. But (analysis) each major partner captures margin elsewhere and multi-homes: Visa/Mastercard keep their own settlement + interchange; Stripe has Tempo + Bridge; Coinbase keeps its USDC float rev-share (renews Aug 2026) and Base. So the party that would most need OUSD to win — Open Standard itself — is the one that keeps the *least* (only the "small management fee"). → **The central question shifts** from "can OUSD beat Circle's economics?" (yes, on paper) to **"will 140 co-owners with better margins elsewhere actually push volume into a coin they collectively but weakly own?"** USDG's history says: probably slowly.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **Near-term:** launch "later in 2026," Solana-first per Solana. Watch (a) which chains at genesis, (b) whether Visa/Mastercard actually route settlement through OUSD vs their existing multi-stablecoin support, (c) Coinbase's Aug-2026 USDC rev-share renewal — a real tell of commitment.
- **Sentiment:** Circle -15-17% on the news = market prices this as a credible threat to Circle's yield-based P&L specifically; Tether shrugged. Bullish framing (Forbes/Catalini: "why an open standard will win"); skeptics note it's an announcement with no product and the USDG precedent.
- **Regulatory:** GENIUS Act + OCC proposed rules (Mar 2026) shape who can issue and bar yield-to-holders; OUSD's yield-to-distributors design is built around that constraint but is untested against final rules.
- **Risks:** vaporware/slow launch; governance gridlock among 140 co-owners; partner multi-homing; incumbents (esp. Circle) cutting fees or sharing yield in response, collapsing OUSD's only edge.

**Why the market may go this way + counterintuitive second order:** the biggest risk to OUSD isn't Circle out-competing it — it's that the same 140-partner breadth that makes it look inevitable is what makes it inert (no single owner with skin to force adoption). And the biggest risk to *Circle* isn't OUSD's launch — it's that OUSD has already **repriced the whole thesis that issuers get to keep the float**, so even if OUSD flops, incumbents may be forced into yield-sharing, compressing Circle's margins regardless. The announcement did damage before the product exists.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

### Challenge / red-team (second-order)

1. **Is OUSD actually launched, or just announced?** Announced only (30 Jun 2026). No live token, no market cap, no on-chain volume. "Launch later in 2026." Everything is design intent. → weight discounted for vaporware risk.

2. **Is this a duplicate of the 3 Jun 2026 note?** No — **fresh.** [[Stripe, Visa and Mastercard near joint stablecoin platform]] was the *reported/rumored* stage ("said to be among backers"). This is the named reveal with new material facts: brand (OUSD/Open Standard), 140 firms, Zach Abrams as CEO, yield-back terms, Circle stock reaction. Genuine "talks → announced" follow-up.

3. **What already did exactly this?** Global Dollar Network / USDG (Paxos), launched Nov 2024 — same yield-sharing consortium pitch, 100+ partners incl. Mastercard/Robinhood/Kraken. Result: ~$1-3bn cap after ~18 months, negligible dent in USDT/USDC (>95% combined). Strong prior-art evidence the model underperforms.

4. **Did the previous version (USDG) fly?** No. Answered above — it stalled. This is the single biggest bear signal for OUSD.

5. **Why did Circle drop but Tether didn't?** Circle's P&L IS reserve income (keeps the float) → directly attacked by yield-sharing. Tether has liquidity/offshore moat and diversified income. Market correctly priced who is structurally exposed.

6. **Can OUSD legally share yield under the GENIUS Act?** Yield to *holders* is prohibited (OCC rule). OUSD routes yield to *partner businesses/distributors*, not holders — a distinct, likely-survivable path, but untested against final rules. Open.

7. **Who actually issues OUSD and holds the reserves?** Not disclosed in the launch PR (no named permitted issuer, no chain confirmed by Open Standard itself). Material gap — a GENIUS-Act stablecoin needs a permitted issuer. Open.

8. **Do the 140 partners have real commitment or is it a logo wall?** Mostly logos + optionality. Most partners multi-home (Visa/MC own rails; Stripe has Tempo+Bridge; Coinbase has Base+USDC rev-share). Only soft quotes ("default stablecoin on Stripe" — Gaybrick), no volume commitments disclosed. → shallow commitment likely.

9. **Who captures the margin if OUSD wins?** Distributors capture the float; Open Standard keeps only a "small management fee." The entity most needing OUSD to succeed keeps the least → weak central incentive to force adoption. This is the core structural flaw.

10. **What's the precise mechanism delta vs Circle in one sentence?** Same fiat-backed token, but reserve yield flows to adoption-driving partners (minus small fee) instead of to the issuer, plus zero-cost uncapped mint/redeem. Tech is commodity; only economics + governance differ.

11. **Why is Stripe backing both Tempo AND OUSD?** Hedge/optionality: Tempo = proprietary chain; OUSD = industry-wide coin via Bridge/Abrams. Stripe wants to own the settlement layer regardless of which model wins. Not a contradiction — a straddle.

12. **What could kill OUSD even if it launches?** (a) governance gridlock among 140 co-owners; (b) partner multi-homing / no volume; (c) incumbents pre-empting by cutting fees or sharing yield; (d) regulatory issuer/chain gaps; (e) slow/vaporware launch. Multiple independent failure modes.

13. **Does OUSD damage Circle even if it flops?** Yes (analysis) — it has already repriced the market's belief that issuers keep the float. Incumbents may be forced into yield-sharing regardless, compressing Circle's margins. Announcement did damage before product exists.

14. **Are the traction stats real OUSD numbers?** No. "Volumes approaching ACH" and "$1.5T by 2030" (BlackRock/Cohen) are total-market framings, not OUSD adoption. No OUSD-specific metric exists yet.

15. **What's the one metric that would prove commitment?** Whether Visa/Mastercard route actual settlement through OUSD (vs their existing multi-stablecoin support) and Coinbase's Aug-2026 USDC rev-share renewal decision. Until then, treat as aspiration. Open.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Stablecoins are a fast-growing settlement/payments rail: total supply ~$323bn in May 2026 with USDT ~59% and USDC ~24% share — the two incumbents ~93% of the category combined (per DefiLlama/CoinLaw & Bitcoin.com aggregation, as of May 2026). BlackRock's Samara Cohen cited a potential $1.5tn market by 2030 (per The Paypers, 2026-07-01; treat as sponsor projection, not neutral TAM). "Why now": the GENIUS Act (signed 2025-07-18, implementation rules due 2026-07-18) gave dollar stablecoins a US legal framework, triggering a wave of corporate entrants (KlarnaUSD Nov 2025; Amazon/Walmart exploring). Structure — issuance is concentrated (Tether/Circle duopoly) but *distribution* is fragmenting: card networks, banks, fintechs and crypto firms are forming consortia (USDG/Global Dollar Network via Paxos, Nov 2024; The Clearing House bank-deposit-token network, 2026). Value historically accrued to the *issuer* via reserve yield (Circle earns interest on ~$77bn USDC reserves); OUSD's inversion is to redistribute that yield to the ~140 distribution partners minus a management fee. Entry barriers are regulatory (reserve/licensing) + network effects (acceptance) — exactly the assets the consortium members already own.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: circulating supply / reserves, reserve yield (≈ short-Treasury rate × float), mint/redeem fees, chain coverage, and merchant/wallet acceptance. Key players: incumbents Tether (USDT ~$190bn) and Circle (USDC ~$77bn); consortium challengers USDG (Paxos/Mastercard/Robinhood/Kraken); and now Open USD, backed by Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Discover, Stripe, Klarna, Affirm, Ramp, BlackRock, BNY, Coinbase, Ripple, Solana, Shopify, DoorDash et al., led by Zach Abrams (Bridge co-founder; Bridge acquired by Stripe for $1.1bn in 2025). Basis of competition shifts from *issuer brand* to *distribution + economics + governance* (zero-fee mint/redeem, shared yield, partner-board governance). Recent moves: OUSD announced 2026-06-30, targeting multi-chain launch (Solana/Stellar/Base/Polygon) "later in 2026"; Stripe's Will Gaybrick said OUSD becomes the default stablecoin on Stripe's platform. Protagonist positioning: Visa/Stripe are *catching up* as issuers but enter from a position of strength on the moat that matters — Visa already processes stablecoin-linked card volume ($3.7bn+ across 200+ countries, per Visa FY2025 CEO letter) and Stripe owns Bridge's issuance rails; their moat is distribution/acceptance (network effects), not the token itself. De-PR: "announced/planned," not live — no chain, no reserve custodian economics, no launch date disclosed.

**Comps & multiples.** IR-grounded (PRIMARY): Visa FY Q2 2026 (ended Mar-31, reported 2026-04-28) — net revenue **$11.2bn, +17% (+16% cc)**; non-GAAP net income $6.3bn, $3.31/sh (per Visa earnings release, drive: https://drive.google.com/file/d/1X9YkQAcH51_U9lg1phhxEHmDvWhOE2Bu/view). FY2025 net revenue $40.0bn, +11% (Q4 release 2025-10-28, https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KAwJMvzVrjq8djc0mVZL_7DoN40UcQdN/view). Visa's stablecoin stance is offensive-defensive: FY2026 CEO letter frames tokenization/stablecoins as an "inflection point," with existing redeem/convert/transfer integrations (https://drive.google.com/file/d/1pUbzBtAM3aCo6Ug0ci-vdGZQE7Wy0xLW/view, https://drive.google.com/file/d/1AiNabeS-GVyuQIxcOW1Rf_M38JL-8wk6/view). Stripe is private (Bridge bought for $1.1bn, 2025) — no public multiple, [UNSOURCED]. Pure-play read-through — Circle (CRCL): market cap ~$15.6bn on ~$63.79/sh (2026-07-01, per stockanalysis/CoinMarketCap), down ~14-18% on the day of the OUSD news, ~-39% for the month — the market priced OUSD as a direct threat to Circle's reserve-yield economics. Circle EV/Revenue not computed — Circle's "revenue" is largely reserve interest and its float ($77bn USDC) is the denominator that matters; a clean multiple needs both a verifiable revenue line and EV, not available free → "no data." Internal comps (base): [[BBVA joins euro stablecoin bank consortium]], [[Euro stablecoin consortium Qivalis expands to 37 banks]], [[Blockchain firms form consortium to standardize stablecoin payments]], [[Banking Circle launches stablecoin settlement after CASP license]] — same "bank/network consortium → shared stablecoin" pattern, mostly euro-side; OUSD is the largest US-dollar analogue. Distribution not computed — qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.** (1) **Execution/vaporware risk** — 140 logos, but no chain, no reserve custodian, no live token and no firm date ("later in 2026"); a partner list is not adoption. Second-order: if launch slips or partners treat it as optionality, the "Circle-killer" narrative unwinds and CRCL re-rates back up. (2) **Governance/economics ambiguity** — "partner board" governing a stablecoin with 140+ competing members (Visa vs Mastercard vs Amex all inside) is untested; who sets the management-fee split, who bears fraud/redemption liability, and how a neutral network avoids antitrust scrutiny are undisclosed — classic "who's silent about the economics." (3) **Disintermediation of the issuer model** — the whole design moves reserve yield from issuer to distributor; if it works, it compresses the economics of *any* standalone issuer (Circle) and commoditizes issuance — but it also means OUSD itself earns only a thin management fee, so the network's own P&L is unproven. (4) **Regulatory** — GENIUS Act reserve/licensing rules (due 2026-07-18) apply; a 140-party shared issuer is a novel structure for regulators to bless.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** OUSD reframes the stablecoin fight from *issuer vs issuer* to *ecosystem vs ecosystem* — distribution and shared economics, not the token brand, become the moat (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: if OUSD ships on-chain with real merchant volume by end-2026 and partners route flow to it, Circle's reserve-yield margin structurally compresses and it re-rates down; conversely, if it stays a press release through H2-2026, Circle recovers and this is a defensive signaling move by Visa/Stripe to avoid being disintermediated by their own partners. Trigger to watch: named chain + reserve custodian + first live mint/redeem volume, and whether Visa/Stripe route real transactions vs merely lending logos.
