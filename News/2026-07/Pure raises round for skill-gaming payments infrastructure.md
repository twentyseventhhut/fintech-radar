# Context-enrichment: Pure raises round for skill-gaming payments infrastructure
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Pure — a ~2-year-old San Francisco startup — raised an **$8M seed** led by **Hidden Capital**, with MaC Venture Capital, Ludlow Ventures, Goodwater Capital, SignalFire, Z Fellows, angel Jake Brooks, and **Alex Pall (The Chainsmokers)** participating. Confirmed by pulse2, finsmes, MaC VC's portfolio page and PitchBook (announced ~2026-07-15). **No valuation, GMV, user count, or revenue disclosed.**

The de-PR'd reality: "Pure" is **Pure Poker** — a consumer **rake-free, real-money, peer-to-peer online poker app** (iOS "Pure Poker: Real Money Games"), NOT B2B "payments infrastructure for gaming." The StrictlyVC framing ("payments, identity verification, wallet infrastructure, and community tools for P2P skill games") describes *features of its own consumer app*, assembled from **third-party vendors** — payments via Checkout.com / Finix / Plaid / Aeropay / Gr4vy, KYC via CLEAR / ComplyAdvantage / Stripe Radar. There is **no proprietary payments or identity tech** and **no external game-operator "clients"**; Pure *is* the operator.

**Why framed this way:** calling itself "compliance-first payments/identity infrastructure" (a) borrows a defensible-fintech-infra multiple instead of the legally-exposed-online-poker discount, and (b) softens the core fact — it runs **real-money poker in ~38 states that don't license online poker**, resting entirely on a "poker is skill + we take no rake → not gambling" theory. "Rake-free" is a positioning choice, not an absence of monetization: revenue comes from premium features, cosmetics and **instant-transfer fees** on cash-outs (i.e. a payments take by another name). Live only as a NY/CA/TX beta with a ~4,650-person waitlist — **early, not scaled**.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Two different competitive sets — and Pure is mis-shelved against the wrong one:
- **Real-money-gaming (RMG) payments/wallet incumbents (B2B, licensed, at scale):** Worldpay gaming (~$2.15T volume), **Nuvei** (regulated-jurisdiction acquiring + alt rails; note [[Nuvei to acquire Payoneer for $2.75 billion]]), Paysafe (Skrill/Neteller wallets), Sightline (Play+ stored value), PayNearMe (cash-to-digital), Trustly ("Pay N Play" open banking). Identity/geo: **GeoComply** (the US RMG geolocation standard), Jumio/GBG for KYC.
- **Pure's actual peers:** other gray/unlicensed real-money "home game" poker apps (PokerBros, ClubGG, PPPoker-style club apps) — offshore/gray operators, not regulated incumbents.

**Why this matters (analysis):** Pure does not compete with Nuvei/Worldpay/GeoComply — it **consumes** their categories via off-the-shelf vendors. Its only differentiator is a **legal/product wrapper** (rake-free social skill poker), not technology. Regionally, the corpus shows the legal *inverse* strategy winning: in Brazil, [[Z.ro Bank acquires payments fintech Paag for iGaming]] and [[Yaspa appoints Justin Fears as US sales director]] both build gaming-payments franchises **inside regulated markets**. Pure instead monetizes the gap *outside* the licensed perimeter — higher optionality, far higher tail risk.

## [2] Company history / fit
Founded ~2023-24 by Columbia squash captains **Jacob Bulbulia** (CEO) and **Harold Castiaux** (CTO), with **Maximo Jalife** and **Fernando Notari** (Columbia + ex-Goldman Sachs). Origin: Bulbulia's COVID-era poker habit; founders cite a claimed **"2022 regulatory change allowing real-money social gambling across state lines"** as the opening — a claim I **could not independently verify** (likely founder spin; interstate poker compacts like MSIGA apply only to *licensed* states).

**Why it acts this way (analysis):** licensed US online poker exists in only ~6-10 states, a tiny TAM. To sell a venture-scale story the team must claim the ~38-state gray zone via the skill-game exemption — hence the aggressive legal posture *and* the "infrastructure/compliance-first" language that dresses an unlicensed operator as a neutral rails provider.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Little that is genuinely new.** Rake-free P2P poker and skill-game legal theories long predate Pure; the vendor stack is commodity. **Traction is undisclosed**: no GMV, no cash-in/out volume, no revenue, no headcount — only a ~4,650 waitlist and a NY/CA/TX beta. An $8M seed with **zero disclosed metrics** is above the 2026 median (~$2.5-3.5M) and reads as **priced on pedigree (Columbia/Goldman) and a celebrity angel, not on numbers**.

**Where the margin/risk sits (analysis):** the hard part of this business is *payments*, and that is exactly Pure's structural weakness, not its moat. Gambling MCC **7995** carries 30-40% decline rates; Mastercard now requires ≥$1M volume + registration; **Visa VAMP** cut the chargeback ceiling to **150bps effective 2026-04-01**. Either Pure codes as 7995 (declines + 5-10% reserves, 90-180-day holds) or it presents to processors as non-gambling P2P transfers — which, if that representation is wrong, invites card-network fines and MATCH/TMF listing. Selling itself as "payments infrastructure" while its own acquiring is chronically fragile is the central irony.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Plans: expand from the NY/CA/TX beta toward "50%+ of US population." **The regulatory backdrop is the whole thesis.** The 2025-26 state crackdown that is shrinking the *sweepstakes* casino market (~-10% to ~$3.6B net in 2026; CA AB 831 eff. 2026-01-01; 50-AG letter to DOJ; C&Ds naming **processors, banks and platform providers**) is the **same enforcement machinery** now applicable to real-money gray-market poker. A "rake-free skill poker" label is arguably *weaker* cover than the sweepstakes dual-currency dodge, because the product is literally real-money poker.

**Second-order (analysis):** unlike a diversified B2B processor, Pure is a **single self-operated app in ~3 states** — 2-3 more state C&Ds could remove most of its addressable base (concentration risk). Being live in CA/NY/TX — three aggressive-AG states — is either bold or reckless. The upside case (a genuine legal wedge into a huge underserved RMG demand pool) exists, but it is a binary regulatory bet, not a compounding infrastructure story.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Pure sits in payments/identity/wallet infrastructure for real-money peer-to-peer skill-based games — an "arms-dealer" position under the skill-gaming vertical (fantasy sports, esports, head-to-head cash tournaments), distinct from casino iGaming/sports-betting. The underlying activity market is large but disputed by definition: real-money skill games ≈$25.3bn in 2026 → $81.7bn by 2035 at ~13.9% CAGR (per Business Research Insights); broader "skill gaming" put at $52.7bn 2026 (Fortune) or $40.9bn 2024 (Grand View) — no single agreed TAM, treat the $25–53bn range as an order-of-magnitude, not a point estimate. The *payments-infrastructure slice* of this (the actual addressable market for Pure) is not separately sized in any free source — **no data**. Structure: highly fragmented on the merchant-acquiring side (many high-risk PSPs: PayKings, Centrobill, WebPays, Basis Theory tooling), with the real barrier being not technology but *high-risk banking access + state-by-state legality*. "Why now": real-money skill games route around the sports-betting/iGaming licensing regime via the "predominance of skill" test, and a wave of P2P skill apps needs compliant rails; Pure's pitch is explicitly "compliance-first" — packaging KYC/identity + payments + community into one stack so game developers don't each have to solve high-risk onboarding.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: TPV/deposit volume, take rate on deposits/withdrawals, chargeback/fraud rate, and *approval/onboarding rate* (how many high-risk merchants can actually get banked). Players by layer: (a) full-stack real-money skill *platforms* that build their own rails — [[Skillz raises...|Skillz]] (public SKLZ), Papaya, AviaGames; (b) high-risk PSPs/gateways — PayKings, Centrobill, Nuvei's gaming vertical, Trustly (Pay-by-Bank withdrawals), Worldpay; (c) US bill-pay/cash-in specialists — [[PayNearMe raises $50M Series E for expansion|PayNearMe]] (renamed PayXM, deep in regulated-gaming payments). Basis of competition: not price but *risk appetite + compliance depth + banking relationships*. Recent moves: PayNearMe $50M Series E (Sep-2025) to expand its gaming/non-commerce payments platform; Paysafe launched crypto-funded iGaming deposits via MoonPay (Apr-2026); Z.ro Bank acquired iGaming PSP Paag in Brazil (May-2026); Nuvei publishing gamer-payment research through 2025. Pure's position: *sub-scale new entrant* at seed stage — its differentiator is the bundled identity+payments+community stack aimed at P2P *skill* (not casino), a narrower, less-served niche than PayNearMe/Nuvei's regulated-betting focus (analysis). Moat at this stage is thin: any edge is intangible (compliance know-how) and relationship-based (bank sponsorship), not yet network effects or scale.

**Comps & multiples.** Round: **$8M seed**, led by Hidden Capital (MaC VC, Ludlow, Goodwater, SignalFire, Z Fellows, angels incl. Alex Pall). Post-money valuation **not disclosed → [UNSOURCED]**; no revenue/TPV disclosed → deal/revenue multiples **not computable**.

That ~1x sales on a shrinking-then-stabilizing real-money skill platform signals the *end-market* is valued cheaply by public markets — a caution for the whole vertical, though Skillz is an operator, not infra.

- Distribution not computed (only 1 comparable multiple). Verdict: an $8M seed is *in-line* for pre-scale US payments/identity infra; the flag is on the end-market's cheap public valuation, not on Pure's price.

**Risk flags.**
1. **State-by-state legality is the core existential risk.** "Real-money skill" is legal only where it passes the predominance-of-skill test; ~12 states restrict/ban it, and the PA Supreme Court ruled (Jun-2026) that "skill games" are slot machines under state gambling law. A single adverse re-classification shrinks Pure's addressable geography *and* its merchants' viability simultaneously — its own note flags "supported U.S. markets" only. Second-order: legal ambiguity is exactly why banks price this as high-risk.
2. **High-risk banking access = single point of failure.** MCC 5816 gaming merchants face elevated chargebacks and card-network scrutiny; Pure depends on sponsor-bank/acquirer relationships it does not own. If a sponsor de-risks (a recurring pattern in gaming/crypto), the rails go dark regardless of product quality — dependence on someone else's balance sheet and someone else's risk tolerance.
3. **Fraud/chargeback + collusion liability.** P2P real-money play invites collusion, bonus abuse and money-laundering exposure; who bears chargeback and AML liability (Pure vs. the game operator) is not disclosed — a silence worth noting, since fraud is the largest driver of gaming chargebacks.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) Pure is a *thesis bet that P2P skill-gaming needs a horizontal compliance-first payments+identity layer* the way iGaming got PayNearMe/Nuvei — a "picks-and-shovels" entry, not consolidation. Falsifiable thesis: if real-money skill survives the current wave of state challenges (PA ruling being the key stress test), specialist compliant rails re-rate; the trigger to watch is regulatory (a favorable/adverse skill-vs-chance ruling in a large state) far more than product traction. What breaks it: further courts adopting PA's "skill = slot machine" logic, which would collapse the merchant base and route deposits back to licensed sportsbook/casino stacks. Public-market comp (Skillz at ~1.3x sales) argues the market is not yet paying up for this end-demand.
