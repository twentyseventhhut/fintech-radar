# Context-enrichment: FCA tells UK banks to improve basic account access
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **7 July 2026** the FCA published a "good and poor practice" report on **basic bank accounts (BBAs)** plus a press release, "Banks told to improve access to basic accounts." This is **NOT a new rule, consultation or Dear-CEO letter** — it is a mystery-shopping review + **voluntary industry commitments brokered via UK Finance**, layered on the pre-existing legal duty (Payment Accounts Regulations 2015) and Consumer Duty.

Method: **298 mystery-shop interactions** (272 branch visits + 26 phone calls) using two "unbanked" personas with vulnerability traits. Findings:
- **34% of interactions rated poor/very poor** (20% poor + 14% very poor); 28% good, 38% fair.
- In **29%** staff didn't mention BBAs until prompted; **15%** never mentioned them even after prompting.
- For no-standard-ID / no-fixed-address applicants: staff failed to explain alternative ID in 18%; **51%** left uncertain about alternatives.
- **~70%** of interactions failed to properly acknowledge vulnerability indicators; vulnerable customers were pushed to unsuitable online-only journeys.

Banks agreed **three voluntary commitments**: (1) "right account, first time"; (2) clear/consistent non-standard-ID processes; (3) spot vulnerability early + offer alternatives to online-only. Follow-up: **6-month** UK Finance sector review, **12-month** FCA outcomes review, "further action as appropriate."

**→ Why framed this way / what it reveals (analysis):** the underlying obligation to offer a fee-free BBA to eligible applicants is genuinely binding (PARs 2015); the July 2026 package is **conduct enforcement by moral suasion**, not new law. The FCA chose the softest instrument — a good/poor-practice report + industry pledge — because the hard duty already exists and the failure is at the *branch-staff execution* layer, not the rulebook. The teeth are the pre-existing PARs + Consumer Duty, not a new deadline. The central question is therefore not "what did the FCA mandate" but "will voluntary commitments fix a front-line-behaviour problem that a 10-year-old legal duty hasn't."

## [1] Competitors / peers
The **nine designated firms** legally required to offer BBAs all signed improvement plans: Barclays UK, The Co-operative Bank, HSBC UK, Lloyds (Halifax/BoS), Nationwide, NatWest (RBS/Ulster), Santander UK, TSB, Virgin Money UK.

Neobanks (**Monzo, Starling, Revolut**) are **not PARs-designated** and offer no statutory BBA; their photo-ID + selfie onboarding reproduces exactly the barrier the FCA flagged for no-fixed-address applicants (Revolut UK deposits also sit under the Lithuanian scheme, not FSCS). Peer regulatory action: [[RBI mandates free mobile, internet banking for basic accounts]] shows India tightening its basic-account (BSBD) framework in Dec 2025 — the same global regulator instinct to force banks to make no-frills accounts genuinely usable.

**→ Second-order (analysis):** the "fintechs solve the unbanked problem" narrative is overstated. The genuinely excluded (no fixed address, no standard ID, low digital capability) are precisely who app-only KYC filters out. The gap is being patched by charity partnerships — see [[Five UK banks join Shelter to bank homeless people]] (HSBC's "No Fixed Address" service, 7,000+ helped, expanded to Lloyds/NatWest/Barclays/Nationwide/Santander from Spring 2026), not by neobank onboarding.

## [2] Company history / fit
FCA's 2026 trajectory is heavily inclusion/conduct-tilted: [[FCA sets out vision for open finance]] (Apr 2026, framing open finance as inclusion + SME-credit access) and [[FCA confirms BNPL protections under Consumer Duty from July 2026]] (Feb 2026) share the same Consumer-Duty spine now applied to basic accounts. The BBA review operationalizes the **banking pillar of HM Treasury's Financial Inclusion Strategy (5 Nov 2025)**.

Framework: **PARs 2015** implement the EU Payment Accounts Directive; BBA provisions live since Sep 2016 — fee-free, no overdraft, no cheque book. **HMT annual stats (report 5 Nov 2025): ~7.04m BBAs open** across the nine as of 30 Jun 2024 (HMT notes figures unverified).

**→ Why the FCA acts now (analysis):** this is not a spontaneous review — it is the FCA delivering its assigned slice of the Nov 2025 government strategy. The structural pressure is political: financial inclusion became a cross-government priority, and the FCA needed a visible, evidenced deliverable (hence the mystery shop) to show the strategy is being enforced, not just announced.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Modest novelty. Value-add is the **evidence**, not the instrument: the FCA's first BBA-specific mystery-shop audit converts a vague "access problem" into quantified front-line failure (34% poor, 29% not offering BBAs unprompted). Traction signal = all nine designated firms signed individual plans + a collective UK Finance commitment with 6/12-month checkpoints.

**→ Why the value-add may not stick (analysis):** a good/poor-practice report + voluntary pledge has weak enforcement gravity. The failure mode is dispersed branch-staff behaviour across thousands of sites — hard to fix with a commitment and no hard deadline. Worse, structural drift works against it: continued branch closures shrink the in-person channel on which no-standard-ID applicants depend, while the industry's cost incentive is to push everyone digital. Real-world impact is **open/unconfirmed** until the 2027 reviews report.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- **~early 2027:** UK Finance 6-month sector progress review; **12-month:** FCA broader outcomes review; escalation "as appropriate."
- **Financial Inclusion Strategy delivery:** Bank Account Access Pilot (Shelter expansion, Spring 2026); Identity & Verification Working Group (convened end-2025) to solve non-standard-ID onboarding.
- **Adjacent theme — de-banking:** the Farage/Coutts saga (2023) and the FCA's Sept 2023 review (found no bank closed accounts primarily over political views) sit at the *closure/exit* end; FSMA 2023 lifted closure notice to 90 days. Access (this item) and debanking are the two ends of the same "right to a bank account" lifecycle.

**→ Counterintuitive second-order (analysis):** the soft-touch route may be deliberate leverage-building: by documenting failure and extracting public commitments, the FCA creates a paper trail that lets it escalate to enforcement under PARs/Consumer Duty in 2027 without needing new rules. The risk is the opposite — no deadline dilutes urgency and the 2027 review quietly notes "progress" while the ~0.9m unbanked (FCA Financial Lives 2024, down from 1.1m in 2022; 97% of adults now hold a current account) barely moves.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

**Importance: 3/5 — rationale:** A real, dated regulatory action (FCA, 7 Jul 2026) with quantified findings and all nine designated banks committing — genuine substance and a clean anchor for the UK financial-inclusion / debanking theme. But it is a soft-touch instrument (good/poor-practice report + voluntary pledge, no new rule, no hard deadline), incremental over the existing PARs 2015 duty, and confined to the UK. Not a market-mover; a solid mid-tier inclusion/conduct story.

2. Freshness — does a prior note cover the same FCA action? **No.** Prior FCA notes cover crypto, open finance, BNPL, AI, scams — none is this July 2026 BBA review. Fresh.

4. Is "a third poor" accurate? **Yes** — 20% poor + 14% very poor = 34%.

6. Latest count of open BBAs? **~7.04m as of 30 Jun 2024** (HMT report Nov 2025, figures unverified by HMT); FY2024–25 figures not yet published — open.

11. What are the enforcement teeth? **PARs 2015 + Consumer Duty (in force 31 Jul 2023)** — no new deadline; escalation "as appropriate" after 6/12-month reviews.
12. Why now? Delivers the **banking pillar of HMT's Financial Inclusion Strategy (5 Nov 2025)** — a political deliverable, not a spontaneous review.
13. Biggest structural risk to the fix? **Branch closures + digital-only push** shrink the in-person channel that no-standard-ID / low-capability applicants depend on — cost incentives run against the commitments.
14. What would prove real traction? The **2027 outcomes review** showing measurable drops in "not offered / refused / left uncertain" rates and unbanked numbers — not just "progress" language.
15. Second-order read: is the soft touch weakness or strategy? **(analysis)** Possibly deliberate — documented failure + public pledges build a paper trail to escalate to PARs/Consumer-Duty enforcement in 2027 without new rules; downside is no deadline dilutes urgency.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is UK retail-banking regulation / financial inclusion, not a company deal — no valuation, round or multiples attach, so comps collapse to "no data". Market structure is a legal near-oligopoly: only nine designated firms (Barclays, Co-operative Bank, HSBC UK, Lloyds Banking Group inc. Halifax/BoS, Nationwide inc. Virgin Money, NatWest inc. RBS/Ulster, Santander UK, TSB) are *legally required* to offer fee-free, no-overdraft basic bank accounts (BBAs) (per FCA/UK Finance, via fintechfutures.com/RBI, 2026-07-08). ~4.3m UK adults hold a BBA; the unbanked population fell from 1.1m (2022) to ~0.9m (2024), so >97% of adults have a current account (per FCA financial-lives data, via Statista/Yahoo, as of 2024). "Why now": the July-2026 action lands under the same Consumer Duty regime that just absorbed BNPL (see internal comp below), and follows the Nov-2025 Treasury Financial Inclusion Strategy — regulatory momentum is compounding, not a one-off.

**Competitive landscape.** The "KPIs" here are regulatory, not commercial: (1) BBA offer/decline rate, (2) mystery-shop quality score, (3) onboarding friction for no-ID / no-fixed-address customers. FCA's mystery shop (298 shops) rated 28% good/very good, 38% fair, 20% poor, 14% very poor — i.e. ~34% poor-or-worse (per FCA, via Yahoo/RBI, 2026-07-08). Core failing: banks often didn't mention BBAs at all and pushed vulnerable customers to online-only journeys. Separately, 10% of the 1.3m adults who applied for a BBA in the two years to May 2024 were declined (per FCA report, via Statista). Recent moves: Nov-2025 five UK banks partnered with Shelter to bank homeless people alongside the Treasury inclusion strategy — see [[Five UK banks join Shelter to bank homeless people]]. There is no protagonist "company" to position; the FCA is regulator, the nine banks are the regulated. Note: this is a "collective commitment" secured via UK Finance plus individual improvement plans — soft supervisory pressure, not a rulebook change or enforcement action (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** No data — regulatory news, no financials, valuation or peer trading multiples to compute. Internal regulatory comps in-base: [[FCA confirms BNPL protections under Consumer Duty from July 2026]] (Consumer Duty extension, same July-2026 window), [[Five UK banks join Shelter to bank homeless people]] (inclusion strategy), and the de-banking politics thread [[JPMorgan and BofA disclose debanking inquiries in SEC filings]] / [[US Attorney Pirro subpoenas JPMorgan and Bank of America]] (US mirror of the access-vs-exclusion debate).

**Risk flags.**
1. *Compliance cost with no revenue offset.* BBAs are fee-free and overdraft-free by mandate; improving onboarding for no-ID/no-address customers raises per-account cost against zero yield, so the nine designated banks absorb the full economics while non-designated challengers/neobanks (Monzo, Starling, Revolut) carry no BBA obligation — asymmetric burden that could widen with each supervisory ratchet.
2. *De-banking / access politics.* The action sits inside a politically charged access-vs-exclusion fight (UK 2023–24 de-banking row; US debanking subpoenas above). Banks face contradictory pressure: onboard vulnerable/thin-file customers *and* meet AML/KYC and fraud-liability standards — the FCA's own finding that firms pushed no-ID customers to online journeys is partly a symptom of that tension, not just poor practice.
3. *Enforcement ambiguity.* A "collective commitment" is not binding rules; if voluntary plans underdeliver, the next step is Consumer Duty enforcement or formal remedies — near-term teeth are limited, so headline risk exceeds immediate P&L risk (analysis).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** Marginal, not structural: this is regulatory tightening on incumbents' cost of serving the bottom of the market, with the durable read-across being that Consumer Duty is now the FCA's general-purpose lever for inclusion (BNPL, then BBAs). Falsifiable thesis: if the next annual BBA report or a follow-up mystery shop still shows >30% poor-or-worse, expect escalation to formal Consumer Duty action against named banks — that would be the trigger to watch; absence of it would confirm this stays a soft-supervision matter (analysis).
