# Context-enrichment: Webull wins EU approval to offer crypto custody
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **2026-07-13**, Webull's Dutch subsidiary **Webull Securities (Europe) B.V.** said it received **MiCAR (MiCA) CASP authorization from the AFM** (Dutch national competent authority). What the "wins EU approval" headline flattens:
- **Approval, not live service.** Webull plans to launch crypto in the EU in **late 2026** — nothing is live today. "Approved" ≠ "adopted".
- **Custody + order transmission, NOT own execution.** Users transmit orders; assets are custodied by Webull EU; **execution is outsourced to Coinbase Luxembourg S.A.** Same Coinbase-dependent pattern as Webull's US ([[Webull relaunches crypto trading in the US]]) and Brazil ([[Webull re-enters the crypto market through Brazil launch]]) launches.
- **Netherlands only.** EU-wide **passporting is pending, not granted**. The PR frame "across the European Union" is aspirational — only NL permission exists.
- Webull EU already held an AFM investment-firm license (equities/ETFs/options/fractional; authorized Sep 2024, brokerage live Sep 2025 — [[Webull launches retail investment platform in Netherlands]]). This is a bolt-on CASP wrapper on that entity.

**Why structured this way / what it reveals:** The value is regulatory *access*, not a product edge. By renting execution from Coinbase, Webull holds only the CASP/custody wrapper — it gets a compliance checkbox and a distribution funnel, not an economic moat. The "EU approval" language does heavy lifting for a NL-only, not-yet-live, execution-outsourced permission. (analysis)

## [1] Competitors / peers
MiCA passports from one member state EU/EEA-wide. As of **2026-07-12 ~280 CASPs are authorized** (BaFin/Germany leads ~25%); [[EU's MiCA marks first year with 102 approved CASPs]] shows the pace a year earlier. Webull is a **latecomer** — peers licensed 12+ months before and at vastly larger scale:
- **Bitpanda** — BaFin+FMA custody+trading, **2025-01-23**; heading to a Frankfurt IPO ([[Bitpanda gears up for Frankfurt IPO in H1 2026]]).
- **Trade Republic** — BaFin, May 2025, EEA-wide custody+execution; **~10M customers / ~€150bn assets**, €12.5bn valuation ([[Trade Republic raises EUR1.2B secondary at EUR12.5B valuation]]).
- **Coinbase** — CSSF Luxembourg, **2025-06-20** (and Webull EU's execution partner).
- **Revolut** — CySEC Cyprus, effective **2025-11-25**, 70M+ customers ([[Revolut hires Coinbase risk chief Michael Schroeder for crypto push]]).
- Kraken (CSSF), Robinhood Europe (Bank of Lithuania, owns Bitstamp), eToro (CySEC), plus non-crypto-native entrants ([[Nuvei secures EU CASP licence under MiCA]], [[StoneX adds crypto offering under MiCA license]]).

**Position:** Late, Netherlands-only, undifferentiated on execution (Coinbase), competing against firms that already bundle crypto into a brokerage EU-wide at 10-70M-user scale. **Why:** custody is now table stakes, not a wedge; the retail-broker angle is exactly what Trade Republic/Revolut/Robinhood already own. The real contest is distribution and funded accounts, where Webull is far behind in Europe. (analysis)

## [2] Company history / fit
- Founded **2016** (Anthony Wang, ex-Alibaba/Xiaomi); parent lineage ties to Hunan Fumi (China).
- **SPAC merger** (SK Growth Opportunities) closed; Nasdaq **BULL** from **2025-04-11** — spiked ~375% intraday then round-tripped to **~$6-8 / ~$3.5bn cap** by mid-2026.
- **$1bn standby equity agreement** (Yorkville) announced **2025-07-03** ([[Newly public Webull secures $1bn standby equity agreement]]); drew $173.2M, then **terminated 2026-04-06**.
- EU/crypto build-out is deliberate geographic + asset-class expansion (the stated use of the $1bn facility). **Why:** a commodity retail-brokerage take-rate needs new lines and geographies to justify a growth multiple; crypto custody is a cheap, Coinbase-powered way to extend the app's asset menu without building an exchange. (analysis)

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
- **Traction is zero so far** — the service launches "late 2026". This is an authorization, not adoption.
- Company-wide: Q1 2026 revenue **$159.9M (+36% YoY)**, adjusted EPS just turned positive (~+$0.03) but **GAAP still loss-making**; **27.6M registered users**, customer assets **$24bn (+90% YoY)**. Funded-account count not disclosed (unconfirmed).
- **Where the margin sits:** with Coinbase executing, Webull earns a custody/spread share, not exchange economics — a competitor sits in its value chain across US, Brazil and now the EU. The custody wrapper is real but thin; disintermediation risk is structural, not hypothetical. (analysis)

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
- Launch slated **late 2026** (slippage risk); passporting to a second member state pending.
- **China-ownership overhang is the material risk:** US House Select Committee on the CCP letter over "deep ties to the PRC," a multistate AG data-privacy inquiry, and Tennessee banning Webull from state devices. For a *custody* business — holding EU customer crypto under a China-linked parent — this invites MiCA fit-and-proper / beneficial-ownership scrutiny and trust erosion. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the very thing being licensed (holding keys) is where the ownership overhang bites hardest. (analysis)
- Dilution overhang eased after the Yorkville termination, but a GAAP-loss-making firm funding an EU build-out has finite runway.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** EU retail crypto brokerage under MiCA. The regime hard-gated on 1 July 2026: pre-MiCA ~3,000+ national-registered crypto providers collapsed to ~244 firms in the ESMA CASP register (<8% survived); roughly 80% of the >1,200 formerly-registered firms failed to secure a CASP licence before the transitional window closed (per LeapRate/FinanceMagnates, via articles below, as of Jul 2026). Structure — consolidating fast: regulation is now the primary entry barrier (a passportable CASP licence = an EU-wide moat), and Binance, the largest global exchange, holds no valid MiCA licence and exits the EU from 1 Jul 2026 — leaving retail share up for grabs. Why now: Webull's approval (from the Dutch AFM, initial scope Netherlands, EU passporting pending) lands exactly in this reshuffle; crypto operations planned to launch late 2026 (per PRNewswire, as of Jul 2026).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs for a retail crypto-broker: funded accounts, customer assets/AUC, DARTs (daily average revenue trades), and crypto take rate/spread. Key licensed players already live as CASPs: Coinbase (hub Luxembourg), Kraken, eToro (CySEC licence, trading+custody), Bitpanda (EU-passported), Revolut Digital Assets (CySEC), Trade Republic (BaFin CASP, >4mn customers), Bitvavo, Bitstamp, Crypto.com. Basis of competition: distribution (embedded in a broker/neobank app) + price/spread + trust/regulation. In-base peers moving on the same vector: [[Bitpanda partners Ribbon to launch UK digital asset offering]], [[eToro to acquire crypto wallet Zengo in 70M deal]], [[Trade Republic confirms €12.5bn valuation in secondary]], [[Revolut share sale draws NVentures, a16z, Franklin Templeton]]. Webull's own EU build-out: [[Webull launches retail investment platform in Netherlands]], [[Webull EU partners with Upvest on fractional investing]], [[Webull UK scraps US and Hong Kong stock trading fees]]. Protagonist position: catching up, not leading — it enters after incumbents are already licensed and live, and only with Netherlands approval so far. Moat is thin on crypto specifically: execution is outsourced to Coinbase Luxembourg S.A., so Webull custodies but does not run its own matching engine (analysis). Its edge is distribution — an existing multi-asset brokerage funnel (equities/options) into which crypto plugs (analysis).

**Comps & multiples.** Webull is public (NASDAQ: BULL). Market cap ~$3.82bn as of 1 Jul 2026; TTM revenue $606.89mn → P/S ≈ $3.82bn / $606.89mn = 6.3x (macrotrends/stockanalysis, as of Jul 2026). One source cited enterprise value ~$1.11bn vs ~$2.99–3.82bn cap — the large net-cash gap implies EV/Rev ≈ $1.11bn / $0.607bn ≈ 1.8x, far cheaper than the P/S, so treat P/S as the cleaner headline; exact reconciliation not verifiable here [UNSOURCED]. Q1 2026: revenue $159.9mn (+36% YoY), customer assets ~$24.6bn (nearly doubled), DARTs 1.312mn, but GAAP net loss $21.7mn on $162.3mn opex (StockTitan/PRNewswire, as of Q1 2026). EU crypto-native peers are mostly private: Trade Republic ~€12.5bn secondary valuation ([[Trade Republic confirms €12.5bn valuation in secondary]], round valuation not market cap); Bitpanda and Revolut private. eToro is public but P/S not pulled here — "no data". With only one clean public multiple and disparate business mixes, distribution not computed; qualitative read: ~6.3x P/S is not extreme for a 36%-growth broker, but the GAAP loss and the -49.8% 52-week stock move say the market is skeptical of the growth's quality.

**Risk flags.**
1. Revenue concentration on PFOF — payment-for-order-flow was $304.1mn in 2025, 53.3% of total revenue (up from 50.5% in 2024) (StockTitan 20-F). Over half of revenue rides on a rebate model that is politically/regulatorily fragile (EU has moved to restrict PFOF); crypto custody diversifies the story but is not yet a material earner. Second-order: any PFOF curb hits the core P&L, not just the new EU line.
2. Disintermediation of the crypto stack — execution runs through Coinbase Luxembourg S.A., so the trade-economics (spread/matching) sit with Coinbase; Webull earns custody + order-transmission, the thinner slice. If Coinbase or another CASP goes direct-to-retail in the same markets, Webull is a reseller with limited pricing power (analysis).
3. Late + narrow entry / execution risk — approval is Netherlands-only with EU passporting still pending and launch only "late 2026," against incumbents (Coinbase, Revolut, Trade Republic, Bitpanda, eToro) already licensed and live EU-wide. Land-grab window from Binance's exit may largely close before Webull scales.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** This is an incremental EU-passport optionality play, not a re-rating catalyst on its own (analysis). Falsifiable thesis: Webull's EU crypto adds a durable revenue line only if it (a) wins the passport and (b) moves crypto from "Others" into a disclosed, growing take-rate segment — watch for a broken-out crypto revenue line in FY2026 filings and confirmation of EU-wide passporting; absence of both by the FY2026 20-F would falsify the "meaningful EU crypto" thesis. Trigger to watch: passporting approval beyond the Netherlands and any signal Webull moves execution in-house rather than via Coinbase.
