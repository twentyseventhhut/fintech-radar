## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Two things are happening in one event: a UK wealthtech (savings/investing app) crossing $1bn, and a first-of-kind use of PISCES market infrastructure. (1) *UK wealthtech / D2C digital wealth.* Structurally fragmented and split by ownership: incumbent-owned robos (Nutmeg → JPMorgan; Wealthify → Aviva) vs independent challengers (Moneybox, Moneyfarm, Plum, Chip). Monetisation runs on AUM management fees + subscription tiers; Moneybox's edge is a savings-first funnel (round-ups, Cash ISA, LISA, pensions) that converts cash into higher-fee wealth/investing balances (per moneytothemasses / growjo peer data, as of 2026). "Why now": rate-cut cycle pushing savers off cash into invested products, plus a UK retail-participation policy push (targeted support / advice-guidance boundary reform — see [[Zopa first UK bank cleared to roll out targeted support]]). (2) *PISCES* is the more novel layer — the FCA's new Private Intermittent Securities and Capital Exchange System, a regulated, intermittent (auction-window) secondary market for *private* company shares, with LSEG's Private Securities Market the first FCA-approved operator (FCA approval 26 Aug 2025; per FCA / LSEG press releases). JP Jenkins also holds a PISCES-adjacent licence ([[JP Jenkins wins FCA licence for PISCES private market]]); Crowdcube is FCA-authorised as a Public Offer Platform / operator ([[Crowdcube authorized by FCA as Public Offer Platform]]) and is running the Moneybox employee-sell / investor-buy process. The point of PISCES: give employees and early investors liquidity without a full IPO/private round — a "stepping stone" to flotation ([[Revolut, Octopus, OakNorth join LSE Pisces private market]]).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: AUA/AUM, net inflows, customer count, revenue-on-AUA (monetisation rate), profitability. Moneybox positioning vs peers is strong on scale and — unusually — on profit:
- FY2025 (per Moneybox / Finextra, May 2026): gross revenue £115.6m (+23% YoY vs £93.3m FY24), PBT £14.9m — *third consecutive profitable year*; AUA >£19bn at Dec-2025 (+62% YoY), now stated >£23bn with >1.9m customers; H1-2026 net inflows £3.5bn, +390k new customers YTD.
- Prior trajectory ([[Moneybox trebles net inflows to record GBP5.8bn]]): net inflows tripled to £5.8bn (+241%), AUM doubled to £11.7bn — a genuine step-change, not a one-off.
- Peers: Nutmeg (JPMorgan-owned, no standalone valuation), Wealthify (Aviva-owned, ~$27.6m revenue per growjo — a fraction of Moneybox), Plum/Chip (subscription round-up models). Basis of competition: distribution + product breadth (ISA/LISA/pension bundle) + fees. Moneybox is *ahead on independent scale and the only one printing profit* (analysis). Moat: switching costs from tax-wrapper stickiness (ISA/pension balances don't move easily) + a savings-to-invest funnel that lowers CAC (analysis; CAC/LTV not disclosed → [UNSOURCED]).

**Comps & multiples.** Deal: £45m / $60.2m of *secondary* shares (employees selling existing stock — no primary capital raised) at ~£800m / $1.1bn, transaction slated 22 Jul 2026.
- **P/S (revenue multiple), arithmetic:** £800m / £115.6m = **~6.9x** trailing P/S. For a +23%-growth, *profitable* consumer wealthtech this is inside the normal sanity range (P/S ~0.5–20x) and reasonable, not stretched — growth-adjusted it looks fair rather than rich (analysis).
- **P/E, arithmetic:** £800m / £14.9m PBT = **~54x** pre-tax. High in absolute terms but PBT is thin/early-stage; the market is paying for the AUA growth curve, not current earnings (analysis).
- **Valuation step-up:** ~£800m is stated as **~+45% vs the 2024 mark** (per Sifted/TFN) — a genuine up-round, consistent with the AUA/revenue inflection.
- Internal comps (private wealthtech rounds in base): [[Farther raises $150 million Series D for wealth management]], [[Sahi raises $33 million Series B at $200 million valuation]], [[Moment raises $78 million for AI investment management platform]] — different geos/models, so qualitative only; distribution not computed (<3 comparable UK D2C figures). Verdict: **in-line / mildly favourable** vs the sector given profitability is rare here.

**Risk flags.**
1. *Price discovery / thin-market risk on PISCES itself.* PISCES is an intermittent auction market with almost no public volume or depth data to date (per The TRADE / Humphreys Law, 2026); the £800m is a *negotiated employee-secondary* clearing price in a nascent venue, not a broad market-tested valuation — treat it as an indicative, not a liquid, mark.
2. *Secondary ≠ growth capital.* No primary money enters the business; this is employee liquidity. It signals maturity and retention pressure (long-serving staff cashing out), not fresh runway — don't read it as a funding round.
3. *Rate-cycle / inflow sensitivity.* The AUA and monetisation story leans on savers moving off cash; a reversal or return of high deposit rates would slow the savings-to-invest funnel that drives the revenue line.
4. *Regulatory / advice-boundary execution.* Push into regulated advice (Aurora AI guidance) sits on the shifting advice-guidance boundary; mis-execution here is a conduct-risk and margin-timing risk.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The bigger signal is market-structure, not Moneybox: a name-brand fintech using PISCES gives the venue its first credibility test. Falsifiable thesis — if the 22 Jul 2026 auction clears with real investor demand and a defensible price, PISCES becomes the default UK path for pre-IPO employee liquidity and a soft de-risking of London listings; watch whether Revolut/OakNorth/Octopus (already circling) follow. What breaks it: a thin, dominated-by-insiders clearing with no external price validation would brand PISCES a niche cap-table tool rather than a real secondary market. For Moneybox specifically, the trigger to watch is a *primary* raise or IPO filing — the secondary is a rehearsal, not the event.
