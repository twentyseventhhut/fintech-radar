ACE Money Transfer and Visa announce payments collaboration
Sinziana Albu
13 Jul 2026 / 5 Min Read
ACE Money Transfer has partnered with Visa to support Account Funding Transactions for card-funded international transfers. 
The companies said the initiative is intended to support faster processing and a more secure and efficient transfer experience for customers sending money internationally.
According to the official press release, the collaboration centres on strengthening the infrastructure that supports card-funded transactions on ACE Money Transfer's platform. AFTs are used within card payment networks to move funds from a sender's card into a payment service provider's system, forming part of the process for cross-border money transfers funded by debit or credit cards.
 Rehan Ashraf , Head of Payments and Banking Infrastructure at ACE Money Transfer, said the AFT capability strengthens the infrastructure underpinning card-funded transfers on the company's platform, adding that the work with Visa is intended to support the corridors the company serves.
 Olga Ovchinnikova , VP Head of Visa Direct Europe, said the collaboration forms part of Visa's broader work through Visa Direct, its platform for direct-to-account payment capabilities, and that the aim is to support secure and reliable money movement for customers.
Market context
Demand for digital cross-border payment services has continued to grow, driven in part by migrant populations relying on remittance corridors to send money to family members abroad. Payment networks such as Visa have expanded their direct-to-account infrastructure in recent years to support money transfer operators, banks, and payment service providers in processing card-funded and account-funded transactions across borders.
Moreover, for remittance providers, access to card network infrastructure such as AFTs can affect transaction processing times and the reliability of fund collection at the point of send, particularly for corridors where card funding is a common method for initiating transfers.
The companies said the collaboration reflects a shared focus on the adoption of digital payment methods for international money movement. No financial terms of the collaboration, specific corridors affected, or implementation timeline were disclosed in the announcement.
The partnership adds to Visa Direct's existing network of remittance and payment service provider relationships, while giving ACE Money Transfer additional infrastructure support as it continues to process card-funded transfers across its international corridors.

# Context-enrichment: ACE Money Transfer x Visa — Account Funding Transactions (AFT) collaboration
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
ACE Money Transfer (UK remittance MTO) announced a "collaboration" with Visa to support **Account Funding Transactions (AFTs)** for card-funded international transfers (13 Jul 2026, The Paypers). De-PR'd, an AFT is only the **pull/pay-in leg**: it debits the sender's Visa card into ACE's account. The payout to the recipient is a separate **OCT (Original Credit Transaction)** — not clearly part of this announcement. So this is about *how senders pay in*, framed as a strategic tie-up.

**Nothing quantitative disclosed**: no corridors, no economics, no exclusivity, no go-live date. Language is qualitative ("faster, more secure, efficient"). Visa's own quote (Olga Ovchinnikova, VP Head of Visa Direct Europe) frames it as work "through Visa Direct" — i.e. **an extension of an existing Visa relationship, not a first contact**.

**Why structured this way → what it reveals:** AFT adoption is partly **mandated**, not purely elective. Visa has been rolling out AFT-classification requirements for money-transfer operators (MCC 4829), with cross-border AFT/OCT processing-rule changes (new sender data fields — DOB, ID, purpose) effective ~17 Apr 2026 (analysis, external research). So a meaningful part of this "collaboration" is plausibly **ACE completing a required compliance/migration to Visa's AFT framework**, re-dressed as a partnership. That shifts the central question from "new capability?" to "was there any choice?".

## [1] Competitors / peers
Card-funded pay-in + Visa Direct payout is **table-stakes for MTOs by 2026, not a differentiator**:
- **Remitly x Visa Direct** since Mar 2019 (expanded 2021 + Visa equity) — ~7 years ahead.
- Zepz/WorldRemit, Azimo, TransferGo — long-standing Visa Direct adopters.
- Recent direct parallels in our own corpus: [[PayAngel expands Visa partnership to boost cross-border remittances]] (Jun 2026), [[9Pay becomes direct Visa partner for cross-border payments]] (Jun 2026), [[Qik launches inbound remittances via Visa Direct]] (Feb 2026), [[Afriex and Visa bring real-time payments to 160+ markets]] (Nov 2025). Visa is signing a steady stream of near-identical MTO/PSP deals.
- Cross-network rivals route via TerraPay/Thunes (Western Union x Thunes Aug 2025); MoneyGram on Mastercard Move.
- **ACE's own Mastercard Move deal** (Feb 2025, external) already did the Mastercard-side of card funding — so this is simply the Visa counterpart of something ACE shipped ~17 months earlier.

**Why this lay of the land → second order:** Visa/Mastercard are commoditizing card-rail access for MTOs as a bank-rail alternative; being on both networks is now hygiene. ACE is a **late/parity mover**, so there is no competitive edge here — the value accrues to Visa (volume + compliance capture), not to ACE as a differentiator.

## [2] Company history / fit
ACE Money Transfer (founded 2002 as Aftab Currency Exchange, Bolton UK; FCA-authorised; ~1.3M customers; pays into 100+ countries via 350,000+ payout points; CEO Rashid Ashraf — note the release quotes a different exec, **Rehan Ashraf**, Head of Payments & Banking Infrastructure). Core corridors: UK/EU/Australia/Canada → South Asia (Pakistan/India/Bangladesh), Philippines, Africa. Not VC-backed — an established regulated MTO, not a growth-fintech. Recent moves: Mastercard Move (Feb 2025), Nuvei (processing), Philippine National Bank (Jun 2026).

**Why ACE acts this way:** a legacy MTO must run multi-rail (Visa + Mastercard + bank) to keep pay-in conversion and stay compliant across networks. The Visa AFT step is a **defensive/keep-up** move, structurally consistent with the Mastercard Move deal — filling out the second card network rather than opening new ground.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Low novelty, no disclosed traction.** No numbers, no live-client claim, no corridor. It is announcement-stage, not a demonstrated GA capability. The mechanism (AFT pay-in) predates this by years across peers.

**Why the value-add is thin (deeper):** card funding can *raise* pay-in cost (interchange) vs a bank debit, so the customer benefit ("faster/cheaper") is unproven and possibly negative on economics. In the stack, **Visa captures the margin** (interchange + AFT/OCT fees + volume + regulatory-classification lock-in); ACE captures marginal conversion/UX at best. What breaks the "partnership" narrative: this is largely mandated compliance + a parity feature, so there is no durable moat created for ACE.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Watch for a **go-live / corridor-specific follow-up** (would upgrade this from PR to substance) and whether ACE brings its **OCT payout** onto Visa (full card-to-card). Backdrop: global remittances ~$905B (2024); Visa Direct claims ~12B endpoints, 195+ countries (FY2025 10-K) and keeps onboarding MTOs. Regulatory driver: cross-border AFT/OCT data rules (2025–Apr 2026).

**Why the market goes this way / counterintuitive second order:** as Visa mandates AFT classification and enriched data, MTOs' card rails become **more standardized and more surveilled** — good for Visa's compliance/data position, neutral-to-negative for MTO differentiation. The "Visa +2.5% on the day" framing seen in some coverage is coincidental: a single mid-size MTO AFT deal cannot move a ~$600B-cap company — discount it entirely.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** Global remittance flows reached ~$857bn in 2025 (World Bank, via worldbank.org / statista.com); flows to low- and middle-income countries ~$685–690bn in 2025, larger than FDI and ODA combined — a structural, migrant-driven demand base, not a cyclical one. The economics that matter: digital providers now send $200 at ~3.65% average cost vs ~9.50% for banks (World Bank Q1 2025 data), the wedge that lets digital MTOs take share from legacy cash/agent networks. "Why now": card networks have spent the last several years wiring direct-to-account rails (Visa Direct, Mastercard Send) specifically to sell infrastructure — AFTs, push-to-card, account payouts — to MTOs, banks and PSPs rather than compete with them. This ACE deal is one more MTO plugging into that rail. Structure: the value chain splits into (a) consumer-facing MTOs competing on price/UX/corridors (fragmented, low switching costs) and (b) the money-movement rails underneath (concentrating around Visa/Mastercard + a few payout aggregators like TerraPay/Nium) — value and margin durability sit increasingly in layer (b).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: send/cross-border volume, take rate (revenue/volume), active customers, cost-to-send, corridor mix. Basis of competition at the MTO layer = price + corridor coverage + funding/payout speed; at the rail layer = network reach (endpoints) + reliability. Recent peer moves: Zepz acquired Pomelo (Jan 2026) to push beyond remittances into cards/credit ([[Zepz acquires Pomelo to expand into credit and cards]]); Remitly posted record Q4'25 — send volume $20.8bn (+35% YoY), 9.3m active customers, first full year of GAAP profitability ([[Remitly reports Q4 and full year 2025 results above outlook]]); Felix + Mastercard on US–LatAm ([[Felix and Mastercard accelerate US-to-LatAm remittances]]); Western Union, Nuvei, Wirex, Qik and others already sit on Visa Direct ([[Nuvei expands payout offering with Visa Direct]], [[Qik launches inbound remittances via Visa Direct]]). ACE Money Transfer's position: a niche/regional MTO (private, UK-based, focused on Asia/Africa corridors from Europe), a rail-consumer not a rail-owner. The announced work is card-funded AFT infrastructure on the send side — genuinely useful for funding reliability, but it is a plumbing upgrade, not a moat. Moat = low `(analysis)`; ACE's edge is corridor-level distribution/compliance, not proprietary technology.

**Comps & multiples.** No financial terms, corridors or timeline disclosed for the ACE–Visa deal, and ACE is private → no ACE multiple ("no data"). Listed peers (via stockanalysis.com / macrotrends / multiples.vc / gurufocus, as of ~Jun 2026):
- Remitly (RELY): market cap ~$4.71bn, TTM revenue ~$1.73bn → P/S = $4.71bn / $1.73bn ≈ 2.7x (reported P/S 2.57x, ~12% below its 10-yr median 2.91x). Growing send volume +37% YoY (Q1'26), Adj. EBITDA margin ~22%.
- Wise (WISE.L): market cap ~$11–11.9bn, revenue ~$3bn → EV/Revenue ≈ 3.4x, EV/EBITDA ≈ 12.3x, P/E ~20.8x; FY26 cross-border volume $243bn (+31% YoY).
- Zepz: private; Pomelo deal terms undisclosed → round/deal multiple "no data".
- MoneyGram: taken private by Madison Dearborn (2023) → no live market multiple; leaning heavily into stablecoin payouts (Stellar/Solana) as an alternative rail.
Read: MTOs trade at ~2.5–3.4x revenue — modest for the growth (30%+ volume), i.e. the market prices persistent take-rate compression and thin steady-state margins, NOT hyper-growth premiums. In-line, not rich. ACE, as a smaller private niche MTO, would rationally sit at or below the low end. Visa itself (the counterparty) is the scale story here: Q2 FY2026 net revenue $11.2bn (+17% YoY), total cross-border volume +12%, Visa Direct transactions +23% to 3.7bn with >18bn endpoints (Visa Q2 FY26 release, IR-grounded).

**Risk flags.**
1. **Take-rate compression / margin squeeze** — the whole digital-remittance thesis is falling cost-to-send (3.65% and dropping). Adding a card-network AFT layer inserts Visa's interchange/scheme economics into ACE's cost stack; for a small MTO that is fee leakage to the rail owner, pressuring already-thin unit economics — the classic "who captures the margin" problem, and it is the party silent about economics here.
2. **Dependence on someone else's rails / disintermediation** — ACE is a rail-consumer; the strategic value (endpoints, +23% Visa Direct growth) accrues to Visa, which serves ACE and every competitor equally. No exclusivity was announced, so this is table-stakes infrastructure, not differentiation — and it deepens ACE's dependence on a counterparty that could tighten terms.
3. **Corridor concentration + regulatory/AML** — ACE's book is concentrated in specific Asia/Africa remittance corridors (higher-risk from an AML/sanctions standpoint); card-funded flows also carry fraud-liability and chargeback exposure that the announcement is silent on. A single-corridor regulatory shock or a fraud/chargeback spike hits a concentrated MTO disproportionately.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Marginal for the sector, confirmatory for the thesis: Visa Direct is winning the "arms dealer to MTOs" land-grab, monetizing remittance growth without corridor or FX risk — bullish for Visa's new-flows narrative (+23% Visa Direct tx), neutral-to-slightly-negative for sub-scale MTOs that pay to rent rails they can't own. Watch/falsify: if disclosed later, whether ACE gets any exclusivity or preferential pricing (would upgrade it from table-stakes to edge); trigger to revisit = ACE releasing corridor coverage or volume metrics, or a Visa Direct pricing change. Thesis breaks if stablecoin payout rails (MoneyGram/Western Union/NALA) undercut card-network AFT economics fast enough to make the Visa layer uncompetitive.

**Thesis-flags (ACE / Visa Direct partnership fit).**
1. **The deal sits in Visa's single fastest-growing volume line.** ACE adopting Visa Account Funding Transactions (AFTs) to fund card-to-account cross-border transfers feeds Visa Direct (+23% txns) and cross-border (+11–12% cc) simultaneously → why it matters: it is incremental "new-flows" volume in exactly the buckets Visa is guiding investors toward; second-order → each MTO like ACE that standardizes on Visa Direct deepens Visa's remittance-rail network effect (>18bn endpoints) and raises switching costs vs Mastercard Move / A2A rails.
2. **Take-rate mix is the subtle read.** New-flows/Visa Direct money-movement typically carries a different (often thinner headline, but incremental) yield than consumer card interchange → why it matters: partnerships like ACE grow volume and VAS/processing revenue but Visa stayed silent on take-rate/margin impact — watch whether new-flows scale dilutes blended yield even as absolute revenue climbs.
3. **De-PR — announced vs live.** The ACE release disclosed no financial terms, no specific corridors, and no timeline (per the note); it is infrastructure enablement, not a reported revenue event. Treat as a pipeline/adoption signal for Visa Direct's remittance land-grab, not a P&L mover — the material fact is Visa's reported +23% Visa Direct growth that this partnership is designed to compound.
4. **Corridor resilience.** Remittance flows are counter-cyclical/defensive vs discretionary travel; ACE-type deals diversify Visa's cross-border mix away from the travel softness (CEMEA/Middle East) that management flagged as the quarter's main headwind.
