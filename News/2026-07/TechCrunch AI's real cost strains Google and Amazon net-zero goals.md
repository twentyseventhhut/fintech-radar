A warning sign about AI’s real cost, courtesy of Google and Amazon
It’s no secret that AI is a hog, consuming energy and water like no digital technology before it. Now we know just how much Big Tech’s pursuit of AI is costing the environment.
Both Google and Amazon released their sustainability reports this week, and the numbers aren’t pretty. Each company has pledged to zero-out its carbon emissions in the coming years, but AI has made those goals a lot harder to hit. Google’s total carbon emissions are up 25% since last year , Amazon’s are up 16%.
A close reading of the reports suggests that both Amazon and Google will have to make some serious, and potentially costly, adjustments to their businesses if they’re going to achieve their net-zero targets.
Neither company comes out and blames AI directly for the rising emissions, but there’s plenty of indirect evidence.
AI at the center of it all
Both Amazon and Google acknowledge their energy use has increased significantly in the last year as use of AI has risen. Both talk about carbon intensity — essentially, how much pollution a company generates for every dollar of revenue it brings in — a metric China has used over the last several years when negotiating climate treaties even as its emissions were skyrocketing. And both devote several pages touting how AI can benefit the environment, a case of “protesting too much,” to borrow some Shakespeare.
The picture gets clearer the deeper you dig into the data. Both companies are actually doing OK when it comes to carbon pollution from energy purchases. Years of buying renewable power have helped keep a lid on things, though that may change in the near future as tech companies, including Google , have begun to invest heavily in natural gas power plants to keep pace with AI’s power demands.
Rather, most of Amazon’s and Google’s growing carbon footprint comes from so-called Scope 3 emissions — a catch-all category covering pollution a company doesn’t directly control, like the goods and services it buys or the products it sells. For companies like Amazon and Google, Scope 3 includes things like GPU purchases and the use of a company’s products, like phones and tablets.
Google lumps together two categories of Scope 3 emissions — capital goods and use of sold products —though it admits the latter is small enough to not be material. (Most of Google’s hardware products are small devices that don’t consume a lot of electricity.) That likely leaves data centers as the main driver. Last year, Google’s Scope 3 emissions increased by 2.1 million metric tons, which means they’re now double what they were in 2019, the year Google uses as its baseline when assessing its performance.
Amazon’s rising Scope 3 emissions mostly come from capital goods and fuel and energy. The former can include data centers and warehouses, which can help explain why Amazon’s Scope 3 emissions spiked higher than Google’s. Still, a good chunk is probably data centers. “To meet strong customer demand, in 2025 we added more data center capacity globally than any other company, including more than 1.2 gigawatt (GW) in Q4 alone,” Amazon wrote in the report.
Hitting a wall
That kind of spending helps explain why decarbonization is suddenly getting so much harder. For years, the biggest contributor to their carbon footprints was energy for offices and more modestly sized data centers. That could easily be canceled out buying renewable power. 
AI has upended that approach. While tech companies could still use renewables plus batteries to power their data centers, they’re starting to fall back on fossil fuels. It’s a trend that will make their net-zero pledges that much harder to deliver, but it’s not irreversible.
The more pernicious emissions come from the construction and outfitting of data centers themselves. The steel and cement industries are both heavy polluters, and while startups are working on low-to-zero carbon approaches, they’re still not ready to deliver at the scale that tech companies need. 
Then there are the GPUs and memory chips powering the AI boom. Semiconductor manufacturing uses lots of energy, and many of the world’s leading-edge chip factories are located in Asia, where the electrical grids remain dominated by fossil fuels. Making matters worse, many of the chemicals used in those factories are also potent greenhouse gases, capable of warming the atmosphere thousands of times more than an equivalent amount of CO 2 . The bingeing on chips has probably inflated both Amazon’s and Google’s carbon footprints.
None of these problems are intractable, though Amazon, Google, and their peers have their work cut out for them. To deliver on their net-zero pledges, they’ll need to ramp up their renewable energy purchases, invest heavily in advanced steel and cement manufacturing, and buy many millions of tons of carbon removal credits. It’s still possible, but their embrace of AI hasn’t made it any easier.

# Context-enrichment: TechCrunch — "AI's real cost strains Google and Amazon net-zero goals"
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 3/5._

**Freshness: FRESH.** This is a first-order write-up of the just-released (late Jun / early Jul 2026) Google 2026 Environmental Report and Amazon 2025 Sustainability Report. The internal corpus contains an adjacent but distinct item — [[Datacentres use 6% of UK and US electricity, says IDCA]] (2026-05, industry-wide electricity share) — which is a different event (macro grid-share research, not company disclosures), so this is not a duplicate.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
Two big-tech sustainability reports landed in the same week and both show emissions climbing on AI buildout, contradicting their net-zero pledges:
- **Google:** total carbon emissions up ~25% YoY. Confirmed by the report: **Scope 3 rose 25%** and now makes up **~80% of Google's footprint** (esgdive, carboncredits). Data-center **construction** alone added ~2.3 Mt CO2e (the TechCrunch text cites a slightly different +2.1 Mt Scope 3 delta and says Scope 3 is now double the 2019 baseline — both point the same way). Electricity consumption jumped **37% YoY**, Google's largest single-year rise ever, cumulatively **>250% since 2019**. Operational (Scope 1+2) emissions actually **fell ~2%** on renewables — the problem is embodied/supply-chain carbon, not purchased power.
- **Amazon:** total emissions up **16%** to ~**80.9 Mt CO2e** (Bloomberg/geekwire). Electricity-related emissions +34% YoY (largest ever). **Carbon intensity rose for the first time since 2019** (109.5 → 112.8 g CO2e per $ revenue). Capex hit **$131.8B in FY2025** (+59%), with **$200B guided for 2026** — so emissions growth is baked in. Amazon still claims 100% renewable matching for the 3rd year and holds its **2040** net-zero date; Google's target is **2030** (halve Scope 1/2/3 vs 2019 + 24/7 CFE).

**Why framed this way:** Neither firm names AI as the culprit; both pivot to "carbon intensity" (a per-dollar metric that flatters a fast-growing top line — the article rightly flags this as the same rhetorical move China used) and to "AI helps the environment" pages ("protesting too much"). The real signal is that the growth is in **Scope 3 (capital goods, chips, steel/cement, construction)** — the category hardest to buy your way out of with renewable PPAs.

## [1] Competitors / peers
Same dynamic across all hyperscalers. Microsoft and Meta report comparable AI-driven emissions increases (Insurance Journal: "Big Tech's carbon emissions spike"). Peers are turning into energy companies: **Microsoft** — 835 MW / $16B 20-yr PPA to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1, plus a 20-yr Chevron natural-gas deal for a ~2.67 GW West Texas site; **Amazon** — $700M into X-energy SMRs and a Talen Energy nuclear offtake expanded to **1.92 GW through 2042** (Susquehanna, PA); **Google** — 500 MW from Kairos Power (SMR) and a **1 GW / 1.5 TWh solar PPA with TotalEnergies** (Feb 2026). **Position:** parity — everyone faces the same wall; differentiation is who secures firm 24/7 baseload (nuclear) fastest vs. who falls back on gas.

**Second-order:** The scramble for nuclear/gas is the tell that renewables+batteries can no longer keep pace with AI load. Falling back on natural gas (Google is now investing in gas plants) directly undermines the "operational emissions are fine" story going forward.

## [2] Company history / fit
For a decade both companies used renewable PPAs to zero out office+datacenter power — a cheap, clean story. AI broke the model: the footprint migrated from **purchased electricity (Scope 2, buyable-away) to embodied carbon (Scope 3, structural)** — GPUs/memory (Asian fabs on fossil grids, plus fluorinated process gases thousands of times worse than CO2), steel and cement for buildout. **Why they act this way:** demand for capacity is non-negotiable competitively (Amazon: "added more data center capacity globally than any other company… >1.2 GW in Q4 alone"), so the sustainability target flexes, not the capex.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
News value is the **hard numbers**, not novelty of the trend (the AI-energy strain was already covered internally: [[Datacentres use 6% of UK and US electricity, says IDCA]]). What's genuinely new/traction-bearing: (a) carbon **intensity rising for the first time since 2019** at Amazon — a break in a 6-year improving line; (b) Google's Scope 3 now **~80% of total** and **double its 2019 baseline**, making the 2030 halving target arithmetically very hard; (c) the tacit admission that offsets/removal credits (Google plans to count removals only from 2030; has contracted ~1.38 Mt removals + >1 Mt superpollutants) are becoming load-bearing. **Who captures the "margin" here:** the burden shifts onto carbon-removal sellers, advanced steel/cement startups, and nuclear/SMR developers — none yet at scale.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Expect the gap between pledge and trajectory to widen through 2026 (Amazon's $200B capex guide guarantees it). Likely responses: more nuclear/SMR + gas PPAs, heavy purchases of carbon-removal credits, and pressure on 24/7 CFE claims. **Counterintuitive second-order:** the more these firms match 100% renewables on paper, the more scrutiny falls on Scope 3 and on RECs/matching accounting (techtimes: "renewable certificates cannot cover supply-chain carbon"). Regulatory/community backdrop is hardening — the same IDCA piece flagged grid strain and local opposition, and the Guardian reported Google developers understated UK datacenter emissions. Net-zero dates (Google 2030, Amazon 2040) are increasingly at risk of quiet redefinition.

**Sector & drivers.** This is data-center energy / AI-infrastructure sustainability, not a company deal — no valuation is in play, so comps below are qualitative. Size/growth: global data-center electricity ~doubles to ~945 TWh by 2030 (~3% of world electricity), growing ~15%/yr; AI-focused DC load triples, with the US + China ~80% of the growth (per IEA Energy & AI, Apr 2025, via iea.org). AI-attributable DC power demand projected near ~1,000 TWh by 2026 in some tallies (secondary, treat as high-end). Structure: capital-intensive, consolidating around hyperscalers (Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta) whose combined 2026 AI capex is put at ~$725bn, up ~77% from ~$410bn in 2025 (secondary analyst aggregates, via valueaddvc/futurum — unaudited, directional). Entry barriers are capital + grid interconnect + power access, and power (not chips) is increasingly the binding constraint. Why now: the emissions inflection is fresh — Google's 2026 environmental report (covering 2025) shows total footprint +18% YoY and ~81% above its 2019 baseline, DC electricity +27% YoY (blog.google, via ESG Dive/Axios, Jun 30 2026); the TechCrunch note's "+25%" figure does not match Google's reported +18% total footprint and looks like a different cut or an error — flagged, not adopted.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: contracted clean-energy GW, carbon-free-energy % (CFE), PUE/WUE, and carbon intensity per $ revenue (the metric the note rightly de-PRs as a way to show "progress" while absolute emissions rise). Key players & basis of competition = who can secure firm, low-carbon power fastest: Google signed a record ~12 GW of new clean-energy agreements in 2025 (blog.google); Microsoft has firm nuclear PPAs incl. the Three Mile Island restart; Meta signed a nuclear PPA with Vistra (Comanche Peak); Amazon says it "added more data center capacity globally than any other company... more than 1.2 GW in Q4 [2025] alone" (per its own sustainability report, quoted in note). Protagonists' position: Amazon and Google are both scaling faster than their decarbonization tooling — ahead on renewable *purchasing*, behind on Scope 3 (capital goods: GPUs, steel, cement). Moat here is not sustainability but power-procurement scale + balance-sheet (intangibles/scale) `(analysis)`.

**Comps & multiples.** No transaction/valuation in the news → no EV/Rev or P/E to compute; "distribution not computed, qualitative comparison." IR-grounded scale anchors (Amazon, real reported figures): FY2025 revenue $717bn (+12% YoY), AWS $91bn→$107bn+ (+19–20%), net income $77.7bn, operating cash flow $139.5bn TTM, but free cash flow fell to ~$11.2bn TTM driven by capex (Amazon 8-K/earnings release 2026-02-05, drive: https://drive.google.com/file/d/1z1hH-8_MMB91Q28RddlFMz8K-4L4tkh4/view; ARS 2026-04-09, drive: https://drive.google.com/file/d/1ZRXSDzNtDIrzI1RlzboZllpH-b78iP6N/view). Capex is the load-bearing number: PP&E ~$131.8bn in FY2025 (+59% vs ~$83bn in 2024), guided to ~$200bn in FY2026, "predominantly AWS/AI" (Amazon, via CNBC/DCD, Feb 2026). Free-cash-flow drain: OCF $139.5bn − ~$132bn capex ≈ the ~$11.2bn FCF the filing reports — i.e. capex is now ~92% of operating cash flow (arithmetic on reported figures). Amazon's own ARS notes AI chip "annual run rate would be ~$50bn" if sold to third parties, signaling the Scope-3 chip footprint scale. Internal base comps (sector precedent, not valuation peers): [[Datacentres use 6% of UK and US electricity, says IDCA]], [[Scala Data Centers secures $328 million for Chile expansion]], [[Mastercard invests €250 million in three France data centres]], [[Bitzero raises $25 million for clean blockchain mining]].

**Risk flags.**
1. **Capex → FCF compression, not a sustainability line-item.** At ~$132bn FY2025 PP&E rising to a guided ~$200bn FY2026 vs ~$139.5bn OCF, capex already ~equals operating cash flow; FCF fell to ~$11.2bn. The net-zero "cost" is second-order: the same buildout that pressures FCF also pushes Scope 3 up — decarbonizing it (advanced steel/cement, carbon-removal credits) adds cost on top of an already stretched cash profile.
2. **Scope 3 is largely outside direct control** (GPUs, memory, fab chemicals, steel/cement). Low-carbon steel/cement and durable carbon removal are not available at hyperscaler scale — so pledges depend on supplier decarbonization and a credits market that may not clear at required volume/price. Execution + market-availability risk, not a policy switch.
3. **Fossil back-slide + reporting optics.** The pivot to gas turbines and nuclear PPAs to get firm power risks re-carbonizing Scope 2 just as renewables had capped it; meanwhile "carbon intensity per revenue" can improve while absolute emissions rise (the note's China-treaty analogy) — a disclosure-quality/greenwashing-scrutiny risk, reinforced by an active Amazon shareholder proposal on the financial impact of its climate commitments (Amazon DEF 14A 2026-04-09, drive: https://drive.google.com/file/d/1qpcVpiPzzFVZ56jc5649GHivEN1tewLd/view).

**What this changes (idea-lens).** `(analysis)` Net-zero for hyperscalers has flipped from a marketing asset to a *cost and procurement* problem: the durable read is that firm-power access (nuclear PPAs, on-site gas, grid interconnect) — not chips or ESG scores — is the new competitive bottleneck, which re-rates power/utility/nuclear counterparties and carbon-removal suppliers into the AI-infra value chain. Falsifiable thesis: absolute Scope-3 emissions keep rising through 2027 despite pledges; it breaks if a hyperscaler shows an absolute (not intensity-adjusted) Scope-3 decline in its next report. Watch: 2026 sustainability reports, further nuclear/gas PPA announcements, and any hyperscaler quietly resetting its net-zero target year.

**Thesis-flags (capex/energy-cost lens).**
1. **Capex is now structural, not cyclical.** $43.2bn/quarter and ~$200bn/year, reaffirmed even into a beat → why it matters: this is the physical demand behind the TechCrunch net-zero strain — gigawatts of new data-center load that Amazon's own filing frames as "installed in future years." Second-order: the energy/emissions bill lands over 2026-2028, lagging the spend, so the net-zero pressure is only beginning.
2. **Free cash flow gutted (~$25.9bn → ~$1.2bn TTM).** The buildout consumes nearly all operating cash flow → the market is funding AI infrastructure out of FCF today for a payoff management admits is years out. Any slip in AWS demand and the return-on-capex math (and the energy spend) turns hostile fast.
3. **AWS margin *expanded* (~37.8%) despite the buildout** — the bull rebuttal to the cost thesis: custom Trainium silicon (>$20bn run rate, ~30% better price/perf) is Amazon's tool to hold margins and blunt per-unit power cost. Whether that offsets rising energy/memory input costs is the open question.
4. **New input-cost stack beyond power:** memory-price spike + fuel inflation flagged into Q2 → the AI-cost story is broadening from electricity to the whole component/logistics chain; net-zero goals collide not just with power sourcing but with the sheer scale of hardware being installed.
