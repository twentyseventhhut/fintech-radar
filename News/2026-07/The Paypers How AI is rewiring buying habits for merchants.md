# Context-enrichment: The Paypers — "The Shortlist Economy: How AI Is Rewiring Buying Habits (and How Merchants Can Respond)"
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 2/5._

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
This is **not an event** — it is a **webinar promo + teaser for a not-yet-released consumer-research report**. The Paypers, PSE Consulting, Mastercard, Layla.ai and Glenbrook are running a panel on **28 Jul 2026** built around "exclusive new consumer research" — a survey of **4,250 AI-shopping users across UK/US/FR/DE**. The blurb (author Alexandra Rusu, 14 Jul 2026) frames a thesis: consumers increasingly discover/compare/decide via AI shopping assistants, and as "agentic commerce moves from emerging trend to commercial reality," merchants/agents/PSPs face strategic questions on **customer acquisition, trust, discovery, and commercial models for AI shopping assistants**.

**Thesis + who argues it:** the "Shortlist Economy" idea — AI agents collapse consideration into a short, agent-curated shortlist, so a product not on the shortlist "effectively doesn't exist" — is being pushed here by a **payments-consultancy + card-network (Mastercard) + AI-shopping-startup (Layla.ai) coalition**, i.e. parties commercially interested in merchants preparing (and paying) for agentic rails. The survey numbers are **not disclosed yet** in this teaser; only the framing is. So treat all quantitative claims as **announced, not evidenced** until the report drops.

**Why structured this way:** the panel is stacked toward the *sell side* of agentic infrastructure (Mastercard = rails/TAP-adjacent; PSE/Glenbrook = payments consulting; Layla.ai = an AI shopping agent). No merchant, retailer, or independent consumer-advocacy voice on the panel — so the "what merchants should do now" agenda is inherently vendor-shaped.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
**Novelty of the commentary: low.** The "Shortlist Economy / AI collapses the funnel to a curated shortlist / merchants must optimize product data for agent discovery" thesis is now a widely-circulated 2026 framing (commercetools, Microsoft Ads, multiple 2026 guides all say the same thing). The *potential* value-add is the **unreleased 4,250-shopper 4-country survey** — that would be genuinely new *data* if/when published. As of this teaser, **no numbers, no traction, no report** — only an agenda.
**Where the margin/second-order question sits:** if agents own discovery, the funnel's top shifts from **paid-search/SEO (merchant-controlled) to agent shortlisting (agent/network-controlled)** → merchants lose attribution and pricing power over discovery, and the entities defining "trusted agent" identity (Visa TAP, Mastercard Agent Pay) capture a new toll booth. The real question isn't "should merchants prepare" but **"who owns the shortlist ranking and the agent-identity rail — and what rent do they extract."** This teaser asks the first, softer question.

## [4] What's next / sentiment
Watch for the actual report (post-28 Jul 2026) — only then is there citable data. Regulatory backdrop: EU SCA compliance is already the gating constraint on live agentic txns (see Visa Payment Passkeys note). Risk to the thesis: the persistent **trust/fraud gap** (Worldpay, Checkout.com) means "commercial reality" may lag the hype; adoption is issuer/merchant-cooperation-contingent. Counter-intuitive second-order: heavy standardization around a few agent-identity protocols could **concentrate** discovery power in 2-3 networks/AI platforms, making individual merchants *more* fragile, not less — the opposite of the "empowerment" framing.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** The piece is a webinar-teaser on *agentic commerce* — AI shopping assistants that discover, shortlist and transact on the shopper's behalf. Market sizing is wide and definition-dependent (treat as directional, third-party PR/analyst data): global agentic-commerce is pegged near ~$7.7bn in 2026 rising toward multi-trillion GMV by 2030; McKinsey estimates agent-orchestrated US retail could reach ~$1trn by 2030 and $3–5trn globally, Bain ~$300–500bn US by 2030 (per Grand View / McKinsey / Bain via secondary citations, as of 2026 — no single audited TAM, do not over-index). Structure: the *checkout/protocol* layer is consolidating fast around a handful of standards — OpenAI/Stripe's Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP), Google-led AP2/UCP coalition (PayPal, Amex, Coinbase), Visa Intelligent Commerce + Trusted Agent Protocol (TAP), Mastercard Agent Pay — while the *merchant/discovery* layer stays fragmented. Why now: the survey underpinning the webinar covers 4,250 AI-shopping users across UK/US/FR/DE (per note), and Visa cites >70% of LatAm consumers already using AI to shop (per revistaeyn, Jan 2026) — a genuine consumer-behaviour shift, not just PR.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs to watch: agent-attributed GMV / share of online transactions agent-mediated, agent-authenticated traffic share, conversion & basket size via agents, take-rate on the new checkout layer. Players: (a) *AI front-ends* — OpenAI/ChatGPT (~900m weekly users), Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot; (b) *rails/tokenization* — Visa (Intelligent Commerce, TAP, Agent Directory; live in Europe with >30 issuers + lastminute.com/Frasers/Cleverbridge, per [[Visa expands agentic commerce access for merchants in Europe]]), Mastercard Agent Pay ([[Mastercard agentic commerce raises new consent and trust questions]]), PayPal (ACP + [[PayPal acquires Cymbio to accelerate agentic commerce]]); (c) *PSP/checkout* — Stripe (ACP co-author; [[Stripe launches tools for German businesses to sell via AI agents]], [[Cross River expands Stripe partnership for agentic commerce]]), Adyen, Worldline, FIS, Revolut Pay (AP2). Basis of competition = standards adoption + distribution (which agent front-ends you plug into), not price. The Paypers here is a *media/research* protagonist (webinar + consumer survey with PSE Consulting, Mastercard, Layla.ai, Glenbrook) — niche/influence role, not a rails player; no moat beyond audience.

**Comps & multiples.** No valuation/round/metrics in this commentary → company multiples = **no data** (The Paypers is a private trade publisher; not comparable to the rails names). Internal comps for the theme (adoption/rails moves, not financials): [[Visa expands agentic commerce access for merchants in Europe]], [[Mastercard is helping set the rules for agentic commerce, partnering with Google]], [[PayPal acquires Cymbio to accelerate agentic commerce]], [[Worldpay report consumers wary of agentic AI shopping fraud]], [[Thredd joins Visa Agentic Ready Programme starting with Zilch]]. Peer trading multiples for the ecosystem (V/MA/PayPal) not computed — no clean per-name financials tied to agentic revenue disclosed → `[UNSOURCED]`; distribution not computed, qualitative comparison only.

**Risk flags.**
1. *Merchant disintermediation / commoditisation.* If agents own discovery and the "shortlist," merchants risk being reduced to price-and-fulfilment inventory suppliers, losing the direct customer relationship, first-party data and brand loyalty (per McKinsey/Bain/Riskified). Second-order: margin and demand-shaping power migrate to the agent layer.
2. *Standards fragmentation → stranded integrations.* ACP vs AP2/UCP vs Visa TAP vs Mastercard Agent Pay are still competing; the note's own subject (OpenAI reportedly pivoting ACP away from in-chat checkout, per web reporting) shows the layer is unsettled — merchants/PSPs betting on the wrong protocol face rework.
3. *Fraud & trust ceiling.* Consumers flag identity theft (55%), unauthorized purchases (55%), fraud (53%) per Worldpay ([[Worldpay report consumers wary of agentic AI shopping fraud]]); agent traffic also trips existing anti-fraud models (odd hours/geo/velocity, per Mastercard), risking false declines that cap adoption.
4. *Vendor/rails dependence.* Merchants and PSPs become dependent on someone else's agent directory and tokenization consent policies — control over how agents access listings and pricing shifts to Visa/MA/OpenAI.

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) The battleground is discovery, not payment: whoever owns the shopper's "shortlist" captures the demand-shaping rent, and card networks are re-fighting the contactless playbook to stay in the flow rather than be disintermediated. Falsifiable thesis: within 12–18 months, agent-attributed GMV should show up in merchant/Shopify/PSP disclosures — if it doesn't (adoption stays a survey/webinar story), the "rewiring" is premature. Watch: first hard agent-attributed conversion/GMV numbers from a large acquirer or platform, and protocol consolidation (does one of ACP/AP2 win, or do they interop via Visa TAP).
