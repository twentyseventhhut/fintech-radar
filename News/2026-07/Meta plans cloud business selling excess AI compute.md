Meta, like SpaceX, looks to turn excess AI compute into cash
Meta has spent billions of dollars developing AI and building out data centers to support it. But now, the company may be preparing to put those data centers to a more immediately profitable purpose.
On Wednesday, Bloomberg reported that Meta is developing plans for a cloud infrastructure business, selling access to both AI compute power and models. The move would pit it against the big cloud providers like Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure. 
Meta’s decision to sell off excess compute comes weeks after SpaceX, via xAI, announced similar plans . In early May, SpaceX signed a deal with Anthropic to buy out all of the compute capacity at SpaceX’s Colossus 1 data center. SpaceX has signed similar leases since with Google and Reflection AI. The fact that Meta is doing the same is a signal that the winners of the AI race may not be the ones providing the best models and services, but rather the ones who own the data centers.
That is, if the demand for compute continues to hold, and if data centers retain their value. Some skeptics have warned the race to build out AI infrastructure is creating a bubble that leans heavily on rapidly depreciating chips . Others have questioned whether AI companies can generate enough end-user revenue to justify the trillion-dollar bets. 
Those concerns haven’t stopped Meta from investing heavily in infrastructure for AI compute. As of the end of the first quarter, Meta had committed to spending $182.9 billion on AI infrastructure in the coming years, including massive ongoing projects in Louisiana and Ohio . The Ohio project, which Zuckerberg said would be the size of Manhattan , is expected to come online this year.
Unlike Google and OpenAI, Meta hasn’t seen significant demand for its own AI models and services. Meta doesn’t break out its revenue from Meta AI or from Llama, its open-weight AI model family, in its earnings, and executives have mostly emphasized the internal corporate uses of AI in public statements. That could mean that Meta’s AI endeavors don’t yet represent a material standalone revenue line. 
To get a return on some of its own colossal spend, Meta may copy CoreWeave’s business model and sell access to “raw” compute capacity, according to Bloomberg. The outlet also reported Meta is considering following AWS’s lead and selling access to various AI models — including its recently launched closed-weight model, Muse Spark — hosted on its AI infrastructure.
The new business line will be part of a new initiative reportedly dubbed Meta Compute, which is led by head of infrastructure Santosh Janardhan, Meta Superintelligence Labs leader Daniel Gross, and president Dina Powell McCormick.
The report confirms Zuckerberg’s May statements that a Meta cloud computing business is “definitely on the table” as a way to get a return on some of the massive investment into its strategy to develop AI “superintelligence.”
TechCrunch has reached out to Meta for comment.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
On **Wed July 1, 2026, Bloomberg** reported Meta is *developing plans* for a cloud-infrastructure business selling (a) raw AI compute (CoreWeave-style) and (b) access to models incl. its closed-weight **Muse Spark** (AWS-Bedrock-style). Internal org **"Meta Compute"**, led by Santosh Janardhan (infra), Daniel Gross (Superintelligence Labs), Dina Powell McCormick (president). **This is a report of internal plans — NOT a product, no customers, no revenue, no launch date, no dollar target. Meta unconfirmed.** Earliest first-party signal: Zuckerberg at the ~May 28 shareholder meeting called it "definitely on the table" / "an option if we overbuild" — hedge language, not a commitment.
**→ Why framed this way:** Meta invokes the "sell excess capacity" story to defray record capex while claiming optionality, not obligation. That framing leans on the **AWS-origin myth** (AWS was purpose-built, NOT repurposed retail excess — per Vogels/Black) — a yellow flag: durable cloud is purpose-built, not idle-resale.

## [1] Competitors / peers (cloud run-rates, ~2026)
- **AWS** ~$124B/yr; **Azure** ~$75B; **GCP** ~$50B — full enterprise stacks, sales orgs, model marketplaces (Bedrock).
- **Oracle OCI** ~$18.1B FY26 IaaS (+77%), total cloud $34B — closest analog (big GPU-cluster contracts to AI labs).
- **CoreWeave** 2026 guide $12–13B, backlog $99.4B; **Nebius** guide $3–3.4B. Pure-play neoclouds — **direct competitors**, yet **Meta is their customer** (CoreWeave $21B thru 2032; Nebius ~$27B).
**→ Second-order:** On the July 1 report neoclouds sold off hard (Nebius −15%, CoreWeave −15%, IREN −6.5%) — market treats Meta as a credible supply-side threat. But Meta becoming a *seller* competes with the suppliers it currently *buys* from → vendor-flip friction.

## [2] Company history / fit
Meta has been a compute **buyer/renter** (multi-$B neocloud leases), not a seller — this reverses direction. Only adjacent revenue: **Llama license/rev-share** with hyperscaler hosts (revealed Mar 2025 filing) — real but **never disclosed as material**; Meta doesn't break out Meta AI/Llama revenue.
**→ Why now:** 2026 capex **$125–145B** (~2× 2025's $72.2B actual; ~$182.9B committed for coming years) against **~98% of revenue from ads** → investors want proof the buildout isn't a black hole. A cloud line is the narrative answer + a way to monetize the utilization gap before superintelligence workloads arrive.

## [3] Novelty / value-add / traction
Genuinely new for Meta at raw-compute scale; the "models" side is a modest extension of existing Llama rev-share. **Traction = zero disclosed** (plans, not product). Nearest live analog: SpaceX/xAI monetizing excess/co-located compute (Anthropic, Google, Reflection AI deals from May 2026) — proves a market exists, but as bespoke bulk deals, not a productized cloud.
**→ Who captures the margin:** cloud/infra gross margins are far below Meta's ~80%+ ad margins (CNBC July 2: Wall Street must "prepare for lower margins"). Meta lacks the unglamorous 80% of a cloud business — **sales org, enterprise tooling, SLAs, billing, compliance** — a multi-year build. Channel conflict: enterprises/AI labs may not rent from a rival to their ad business.

## [4] What's next / market sentiment
Stock popped ~+9–10% on July 1–2 — **largely narrative relief** against capex anxiety, with zero disclosed economics. Forbes framed the neocloud drop as "a reset, not the end." Key infra backing it is real: **Prometheus** (Ohio, ~1 GW, online 2026), **Hyperion** (Louisiana Richland Parish, up to 5 GW, >$200B, ramp 2028, $27B off-balance-sheet financing), MTIA custom silicon (explicitly NOT an Nvidia replacement).
**→ Counterintuitive:** the same buildout that lets Meta claim a cloud option also makes it fragile — capex concentration + low-margin diversification could dilute its premium ad multiple.

**Sector & drivers.** AI cloud / GPU-compute rental ("neocloud" + hyperscaler IaaS). Live market data is heavily concentrated: AWS ~28%, Azure ~21%, Google Cloud ~14% of global cloud share, the Big Three ~67% of enterprise cloud spend (per sqmagazine/Statista-cited comps, Q1 2026). Growth is re-accelerating on AI: Q1 2026 AWS +19-28% to ~$37.6bn, Azure ~+40%, Google Cloud +63% to ~$20.0bn with backlog reported ~$460bn (per company results via heygotrade/mindstudio, Apr 2026). Structure = capital-intensive oligopoly at the top, with a fast-growing "neocloud" fringe (CoreWeave, plus SpaceX/xAI-style excess-capacity resellers) renting raw GPU capacity. Entry barrier = capital + power + chip allocation, not software. Why now: hyperscaler capex has ballooned (Meta alone raised 2026 capex to $125-145bn at Q1, up from $115-135bn; ~$107bn of new multi-year infra commitments signed in Q1 2026 per Fortune/Yahoo, 2026-04-29) — owners of underutilized data centers now have a strong incentive to monetize idle compute rather than let depreciating chips sit. Second-order: this is Meta trying to convert a cost center (capex-heavy AI buildout with no material standalone AI revenue line yet) into a revenue line.

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: committed backlog / take-or-pay contracts, utilization of installed GPU fleet, EV/Revenue vs growth, adj-EBITDA margin, and (for owners) capex-to-revenue. Key players: incumbents AWS / Azure / Google Cloud (full-stack, sticky enterprise distribution); neoclouds CoreWeave (pure-play GPU rental, the model Bloomberg says Meta would copy) and Nebius/others; new excess-capacity entrants — SpaceX/xAI (Colossus leases to Anthropic, Google, Reflection AI) and now Meta. Basis of competition: price-per-GPU-hour and chip availability at the raw-compute layer; distribution + managed services + model catalog at the hyperscaler layer. Recent moves: [[SpaceX signs $150M month compute deal with Reflection AI]] (Jun 2026) and prior Colossus leases; [[Amazon raises AWS GPU reservation prices about 20% amid chip shortage]] (Jun 2026, signals tight supply — supportive of a reseller move); [[This Week in Fintech Semafor on the rise of an AI futures market]] (Jun 2026) notes hyperscalers "benefit from opaque, bundled pricing." Meta's position: **new entrant, catching up (analysis)** — no cloud sales org, no enterprise IaaS track record, and per the note it has *not* seen material external demand for its own models (Meta AI / Llama / Muse Spark revenue not broken out). Moat if any = scale of owned data centers + captive chip supply (intangible/scale), not switching costs or network effects, which it lacks in cloud.

The beat is 100% ads — the AI spend is still a cost, not yet a product. "Excess compute to sell" also implicitly concedes some of the $125–145bn buildout is ahead of internal demand.
3. **Margin optics vs mix shift.** 41% operating margin held only because ad revenue (+33%) outran opex (+35%). A low-margin, capital-heavy compute-resale business would **dilute** consolidated margins even if it adds revenue — the opposite of Meta's historical software-margin profile.
4. **Net-income quality.** The +61% net-income / GAAP-EPS $10.44 headline is inflated by an $8.03bn one-off tax benefit; underlying earnings growth (~+14% EPS, +30% operating) is the real base against which any compute-business investment must be judged.
