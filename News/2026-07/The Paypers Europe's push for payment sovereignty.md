Europe's payment sovereignty ambition: breaking free from transatlantic dependency?
Estera Sava
01 Jul 2026 / 8 Min Read
 Robrecht Vandormael , Senior Managing Director and Head of Financial Services at FTI Consulting , analyses Europe’s approach to payments sovereignty. 
For decades, European consumers and businesses have conducted most payments transactions through the two so-called US card networks: Visa and Mastercard. By processing a significant amount of payments across the EU, they are setting rules and, critically, sitting at the centre of the financial infrastructure. Considering the sharpening geopolitical tensions between the EU and the US, European politicians have identified dependency as more than a commercial inconvenience, now seeing it as a strategic vulnerability, an assessment that has been fuelled by the current US administration’s stance towards Europe.
Europe’s political ambition to reduce dependence on the US is real; however, this framing needs nuance in payments. Both networks are genuinely global, deeply embedded in European financial infrastructure, and broadly aligned with European regulatory standards and values around consumer protection and data privacy. Domestic schemes, like France's Cartes Bancaires, Germany's Girocard, and Belgium's Bancontact, have long coexisted with them, and both Visa and Mastercard have invested substantially in localising operations, partnering with European institutions, and meeting EU compliance requirements. Although they are not adversaries, the concentration of critical payment infrastructure in the hands of two foreign-headquartered entities has raised questions — both about geopolitics and the nature of resilience itself.
What does European payments resilience look like?
The case for European payments sovereignty is sometimes framed in narrow geopolitical terms: what if Washington weaponised financial networks against European interests? That scenario, while not implausible given the use of financial infrastructure as statecraft in the Russia-Ukraine context, deserves additional context, and resilience is more complex.
In payments, resilience is multidimensional, and technological matters just as much as geopolitical resilience. We’re looking at a complex picture, in which Visa and Mastercard are, by many measures, leaders in cybersecurity, fraud detection, and hybrid-threat response. Their investment in AI-driven risk systems, tokenisation, and real-time transaction monitoring is substantial. A European payments ecosystem creating fragmentation without matching that technological depth could inadvertently reduce systemic security. Yes, Europe’s political ambition is outspoken. Dependency, even on trusted partners, carries risk in today’s world: in innovation roadmaps, in crisis scenarios, and in the ability to set standards rather than follow them.
The instant payments foundation
The EU's most notable move toward payments autonomy is the Instant Payments Regulation (IPR) , which entered into force in 2024. It mandates that all eurozone payment providers offer real-time credit transfers (settled within seconds, 24/7) at no premium over standard transfers, with cross-border interoperability required across the bloc.
The structural relevance of this move lies in the fact that account-to-account (A2A) instant payments can bypass card rails. The regulation is therefore a deliberate infrastructure move, helping to build the rails on which European alternatives can credibly run.
Wero: the European market-led payments challenger
The market response is Wero, the consumer wallet launched by the European Payments Initiative (EPI), a consortium of major banks across France, Germany, Belgium, the Netherlands, and beyond.
Built on the regulation-mandated instant payments rails, it benefits from bank-level trust, broad distribution through existing banking apps, and a deliberate design to keep value in European hands.  Wero enables real-time person-to-person (P2P) and person-to-merchant (P2M) payments, with cross-border rollout expanding progressively.
The ambition is an interoperable, bank-backed wallet that functions seamlessly from Lisbon to Warsaw — a European alternative to Visa and Mastercard. 
The digital euro: public infrastructure
Alongside these private-sector efforts, the European Central Bank is advancing the digital euro — a retail central bank digital currency (CBDC) designed to be a universally accessible public infrastructure. Legal tender, operable offline, and insulated from commercial motives, the digital euro would provide a monetary anchor in a space increasingly shaped by private intermediaries. A legislative framework is expected by the end of the year or soon after.
Its proponents (mainly central banks) frame it as the only instrument capable of guaranteeing truly sovereign, public digital payments, independent of any private network, European or otherwise.
Are Wero and the digital euro complementary or competing?
The visions for Wero and the digital euro sit in uncomfortable proximity. Both target everyday digital payments and seek wallet space on the same consumer devices. European central bankers present them as complementary, with the digital euro acting as a foundation, and Wero as a private layer built on top. Supposing the digital euro mandates broad acceptance and rides regulatory tailwinds, it could undercut the commercial model sustaining Wero. Conversely, if Wero reaches dominant scale first, the public option may arrive too late to matter. How Europe manages this tension will shape its payments architecture for a generation. 
Stablecoins – another contender to cross-border sovereign infrastructure
One other pressure point deserves attention: USD stablecoins (backed by Washington's increasingly supportive legislative stance) are extending dollar dominance into digital and cross-border payment flows, including corridors into Europe. No EUR-denominated stablecoin has achieved comparable scale. If USD stablecoins become the default medium for digital cross-border settlement, Europe risks ceding yet another monetary layer to foreign infrastructure: dollar hegemony for the digital age. This adds urgency to an already crowded agenda, which Europe has noticed, and is now looking to review its pioneer crypto asset regulation (MiCA).
Conclusion
European payments sovereignty is a real political ambition. However, it needs nuance as it should not be a case against the US or transatlantic cooperation, but for strategic depth: the capacity to innovate independently, respond to crises without foreign permission, and set standards rather than adopt them. Building that capacity through regulation, public infrastructure, and private initiative is one of the defining economic policy ambitions of this decade.

# Context-enrichment: The Paypers — Europe's push for payment sovereignty
_Analytical notes (not a post). Importance: 4/5._

Analysis/thesis piece by Robrecht Vandormael (FTI Consulting, Brussels; member of the EU Commission Payment Systems Expert Group), published in The Paypers 01 Jul 2026. Not a news event — a framing essay. So the enrichment job is to fact-check the thesis against dated reality and separate the argument's substance from the op-ed gloss.

## [0] What exactly happened (de-PR'd)
An FTI Consulting director argues Europe's "payment sovereignty" ambition is real but needs nuance: Visa/Mastercard are not adversaries (globally embedded, EU-compliant), so the case is "for strategic depth" not "against the US". Piece maps the four levers: Instant Payments Regulation (IPR), Wero/EPI, the digital euro, and — as a threat vector — USD stablecoins.

**De-PR'd reality check (the essay is directionally correct but softens the delivery gap):**
- The essay leans on ambition; the actual scorecard is thin. As of Jul 2026 only **one** sovereignty instrument is broadly live: **Wero P2P** (DE 02 Jul 2024, FR 30 Sep 2024, BE 19 Nov 2024, LU 01 Jun 2026; NL via iDEAL co-branding Jan 2026). Wero e-commerce is only weeks-to-months old (DE ~Nov 2025, BE 03 Mar 2026) and merchant-limited; **in-store POS is not live** (QR aspirational end-2026, NFC 2027).
- IPR = Reg (EU) 2024/886, in force 08 Apr 2024. Euro-area obligations are now LIVE: **receive + charge-parity 09 Jan 2025**, **send + Verification-of-Payee 09 Oct 2025**. This is the essay's strongest claim — the A2A rails that can bypass card networks genuinely exist. But pre-deadline only ~33% of PSPs were "prepared" (EY survey); VoP had data-quality problems at go-live.
- The essay's dependency premise is real and quantified (which it omits): ECB "Report on card schemes and processors" (26 Feb 2025) — **~61% of euro-area card payments run on international schemes (Visa/Mastercard, 2022); 13 euro-area countries have no national card scheme at all.** That is the load-bearing fact behind the whole thesis, and the op-ed leaves it as vibes.
- **Why framed this way:** the author is a Brussels payments-policy advisor whose clients include Visa/Mastercard-adjacent firms. The "not adversaries / need nuance / don't over-fragment" framing is exactly the incumbent-friendly line — it defends the international schemes' technological superiority (fraud/AI/tokenisation) while conceding the political point. That is a genuine analytical caveat AND a positioning stance; treat as interested-but-informed.

## [1] Competitors / peers (the four levers vs each other)
- **Wero (EPI)** — market-led, bank-backed. ~43.5M registered users and €7.5B transferred in first year (mid-2025, company-reported); >50M registered Feb 2026. Caveat: **registered ≠ active** (often auto-provisioned in bank apps, no MAU published); German adoption weak (~5% of volume per Crédit Agricole deputy CEO, Jun 2025). Momentum: Revolut (Jun 2025), ING/N26/Deutsche Bank (Dec 2025), Commerzbank rejoined 11 Feb 2026. cf. [[SBS adds Wero wallet support to open banking platform]].
- **Digital euro (ECB)** — public CBDC. The essay says a "legislative framework is expected by the end of the year or soon after" — **overstated**. Commission proposal is from 28 Jun 2023; Council only reached a general negotiating position 19 Dec 2025; Parliament ECON committee vote scheduled 23 Jun 2026; trilogue only starting. Earliest issuance ~2029, all conditional. Banks resist over deposit-flight — cf. [[Italy's banks back digital euro but want costs phased]] (ABI: €880M IT cost for Italian banks alone).
- **USD stablecoins** — the essay flags them as a contender; reality is starker. US GENIUS Act signed **18 Jul 2025** structurally reinforces USD stablecoins; **EUR stablecoins are <€350M vs a ~$320B market (~0.3–1%)**; Lagarde (08 May 2026, Madrid): ~98% dollar-pegged, warns of "digital dollarisation". EU responses are still aspirational: bank consortium Qivalis euro stablecoin targeting H2 2026 launch; cf. [[Italy's Bancomat announces EUR.bank stablecoin]] (EUR.bank).
- **Why the landscape is this way:** the essay's key tension — Wero vs digital euro "in uncomfortable proximity" — is real second-order dynamics: both fight for the same wallet space; a mandated-acceptance digital euro could undercut Wero's commercial model, while a dominant Wero could make the public option "arrive too late." That is the sharpest point in the piece.

## [2] Author / source fit
The Paypers routinely runs "Expert views" op-eds; this fits its house style (thought-leadership from named industry principals). Vandormael sits on the Commission Payment Systems Expert Group — genuinely close to the file. But FTI is a communications/advisory firm and the piece is unmistakably a positioning artefact: pro-transatlantic-cooperation, skeptical of fragmentation, defends incumbent tech depth. **Why he argues this way:** advisory firms profit from clients navigating (not upending) the existing rails; the "strategic depth, not decoupling" thesis protects incumbents' freedom to operate while ceding the political headline.

## [3] Novelty / value-add
Little factual novelty — it's a synthesis essay, no new data, deals or numbers. Value-add is the **framing correction**: (a) reframing "sovereignty" from anti-US to "strategic depth"; (b) surfacing the Wero-vs-digital-euro cannibalisation tension; (c) flagging USD stablecoins as the newest and least-addressed leak in the sovereignty bucket. The **traction gap is the honest read**: rhetoric (mostly ECB speeches — Cipollone "no true sovereignty without sovereign money", 15 May 2025; "kindness of strangers") vastly outruns delivery. Who captures the value: today, still Visa/Mastercard (~61% share, superior fraud/AI stack) and increasingly USD stablecoins — the sovereignty stack is aspirational.

## [4] What's next / sentiment
- Digital euro: ECON vote 23 Jun 2026 → trilogue → (if adopted 2026) pilot mid-2027, issuance ~2029. High political friction (~1,500 amendments, rapporteur turnover, bank disintermediation fears).
- Wero: POS/in-store the real 2026-27 test; e-commerce scaling across FR/LU/NL.
- Stablecoins: MiCA review consultation open to 31 Aug 2026 — the Commission is questioning whether its own rules suppressed a competitive EUR market (MiCA pushed USDT out of EU retail rather than seeding EUR alternatives — counterintuitive backfire).
- **Second-order:** the gap is *widening*, not closing — GENIUS Act + EUR-stablecoin near-absence mean the newest monetary layer is being ceded even as the card-rail layer slowly diversifies. The essay's own logic (add urgency) understates how far behind the digital-cross-border front is.

## Челлендж / ред-тим

<!-- enrichment:challenge -->
### Red-team / challenge questions

1. **Is the essay's "framework by end of year" claim accurate?** No — overstated. Council reached only a general negotiating position 19 Dec 2025; ECON committee vote scheduled 23 Jun 2026; trilogue only starting; earliest issuance ~2029 (conditional). The op-ed compresses a multi-year political fight.
2. **What is the actual, cited dependency figure the essay omits?** ECB (26 Feb 2025): ~61% of euro-area card payments run on international schemes; 13 euro-area countries have no national scheme. This is the load-bearing fact left as vibes.
3. **How much of Wero is real vs sign-ups?** >50M "registered" (Feb 2026), €7.5B first-year — but registered ≠ active (auto-provisioned in bank apps, no MAU published); German adoption ~5% of volume. Soft numbers.
4. **Is Wero actually a Visa/Mastercard alternative today?** No — P2P live in 4 countries + limited e-commerce; in-store POS not live until end-2026/2027. It does not yet contest the card use case.
5. **Author bias?** FTI Consulting advisory principal on the Commission expert group — informed but interested; the "not adversaries / avoid fragmentation" line is incumbent-friendly positioning. Flag as such.
6. **Is the Wero-vs-digital-euro cannibalisation risk real or rhetorical?** Real and the piece's sharpest point: both compete for the same wallet; mandated digital-euro acceptance could undercut Wero's commercial model. Open which wins.
7. **Did the EPI predecessor fly?** No — EPI abandoned its card scheme in early 2022; ~20 banks withdrew; shareholders fell 30→16. Wero is the salvaged pivot, not a fresh success. Essay omits the failure history.
8. **Is the stablecoin threat quantified?** Yes and it's severe: EUR stablecoins <€350M vs ~$320B market (~0.3–1%); GENIUS Act (18 Jul 2025) reinforces USD. The gap is widening. Essay underplays magnitude.
9. **Did MiCA help or hurt EUR competitiveness?** Counterintuitively may have backfired — pushed USDT out of EU retail rather than seeding a competitive EUR market; Commission reviewing (consultation to 31 Aug 2026).
10. **Is IPR genuinely the sovereignty foundation the essay claims?** Largely yes — Reg (EU) 2024/886 in force, euro-area receive/send live (Jan/Oct 2025), A2A can bypass card rails. But readiness patchy (~33% PSPs prepared) and VoP data issues at go-live.
11. **Where does the value actually accrue today?** Still Visa/Mastercard (~61%, superior fraud/AI/tokenisation stack) and increasingly USD stablecoins — the sovereign stack captures little value yet.
12. **What is the strongest counter-thesis?** Bruegel/Reichlin argue EU restrictions on euro stablecoins could *worsen* dollarisation by pushing demand offshore — i.e., sovereignty measures may be self-defeating. Genuine expert disagreement.
13. **Is this note a duplicate of prior corpus items?** No — prior notes are discrete Nov-2025 events (Wero integration, ABI cost, Bancomat stablecoin). This is a fresh, standalone thesis synthesis. Fresh.
14. **What would change the verdict?** Digital-euro legislation actually adopted in 2026, or Wero POS launching — either would move sovereignty from aspiration to delivery. Open.

**Importance: 4/5** — a well-sourced, timely synthesis of the single most strategically consequential EU payments theme (US-network + USD-stablecoin dependence), from an insider close to the file; downgraded from 5 because it is an op-ed with no new data, softens the delivery/traction gap, and carries a visible incumbent-friendly bias. High value as an anchor/context piece for the whole European payments-sovereignty cluster.

## Market Research

<!-- enrichment:market_research -->
**Sector & drivers.** This is the European retail-payments-infrastructure market — an autonomy/sovereignty theme, not a single company. Size/structure (sourced): Visa + Mastercard processed **47% of eurozone card-payment value in 2025**, and in **13 of 19 countries the two carry ≥96%** of card-transaction value (ECB Payments statistics H1 2025, via search; consistent with Cipollone's "two-thirds of euro-area card transactions governed by non-European firms" cited in [[Cashless US-EU digital money split into two incompatible standards]]). Cash use in the euro area fell 68%→40% (2019→2025) (ECB, same note), forcing the payments-autonomy question. **Structure:** the international-scheme layer is a **duopoly** (Visa/Mastercard) sitting on top of a **fragmented** patchwork of domestic schemes (Cartes Bancaires, Girocard, Bancontact, BLIK, iDEAL, Bizum) that historically don't interoperate cross-border — the exact gap Wero targets. Value in the value chain sits at the **network/scheme layer** (rule-setting, fraud/risk, tokenisation) and in **distribution** (bank apps); barriers are regulation, network effects, and fraud/cyber capability (the note concedes Visa/MA lead here). **Why now / drivers:** (1) **Instant Payments Regulation (IPR, in force 2024)** mandates free real-time A2A credit transfers eurozone-wide — the rails on which card-bypassing alternatives can run; (2) sharpening EU–US geopolitics reframing dependency as "strategic vulnerability"; (3) **USD stablecoins** (GENIUS Act, signed Jul 18 2025) extending dollar reach into EU corridors while no euro coin has scale (~99% of >$280bn stablecoin supply is USD — see [[Cashless US-EU digital money split into two incompatible standards]]).

**Competitive landscape.** Sector KPIs: transaction volume/value, registered users, merchant acceptance (P2P → P2M → e-com → POS), cross-border interoperability, and per-transaction cost. **International schemes (incumbents):** Visa 51% of the European *credit-card* market 2025 (Mordor via search); Visa+Mastercard 47% of eurozone card *value*. **Market-led challenger — Wero (EPI):** **43.5m registered users** and **>€7.5bn transferred** in its first ~12 months (EPI Company, via search); live in FR/DE/BE, adding Luxembourg (Jun 2026) and Netherlands via iDEAL migration; e-commerce acceptance began Nov 2025, POS/NFC rollout scheduled 2026–2027. Recent peer/ecosystem moves (dated, from corpus): [[Dutch iDEAL to rebrand as iDEAL Wero in 2026]]; [[Van Lanschot Kempen becomes Principal Member of EPI, adds Wero]] (2026-06); [[EPI's Wero adds Erste and Raiffeisen as shareholders in Austria]]; [[Airwallex becomes EPI Principal Member to bring Wero]]; [[Poland's BLIK joins pan-European cashless payments push]] (2026-05); [[BLIK processes 756 million transactions in Q1 2026]]. **Public layer — digital euro (ECB):** EU Parliament ECON/plenary votes 2026 ([[Digital Euro clears key hurdle in EU parliament]]); Council approved Dec 2025; if the Regulation is adopted in 2026, 12-month pilot H2 2027, **first issuance targeted 2029**; **~€3,000–4,000 holding limit** per person (search: ECB/Wikipedia/adesso). **Position:** Wero is *catching up* — real scale (43.5m users) but still overwhelmingly P2P, not yet a card substitute at POS; the digital euro is *pre-shipping* (rhetoric, not live). Moat basis = bank-level trust + distribution through existing banking apps (network effects among consortium banks); the note itself concedes Wero lacks Visa/MA's fraud/cyber depth.

**Comps & multiples.** No single covered public company → **IR grounding skipped, no equity multiples**. Reference points: Visa/Mastercard are the only listed pure-play scheme comps but are global (not EU-specific) — cross-market cap comparison not meaningful here → "distribution not computed, qualitative comparison." Wero/EPI is a private bank consortium (no valuation disclosed); digital euro is public infrastructure (**€1.3bn build / €320m-per-year run**, per [[Cashless US-EU digital money split into two incompatible standards]]) — a cost line, not a valuation. Internal comps confirm a broad, accelerating rollout ([[PAYONE rolls out Wero wallet nationwide for German merchants]], [[Nuvei and EPI launch Wero payments for European merchants]], [[ING enables Wero online shopping payments in Germany]]) but disclose no unit economics → CAC/take-rate/margin **[UNSOURCED]**.

**Risk flags.**
1. **Wero–digital-euro cannibalisation (structural).** Both target the same wallet space on the same devices; the note flags the tension explicitly. If the digital euro rides a legal-tender acceptance mandate, it could undercut the commercial model funding Wero — so Europe's public and private sovereignty bets may **compete rather than stack**, wasting the €1.3bn public spend or stranding bank investment in Wero.
2. **Technological-resilience gap vs the schemes.** Fragmenting away from Visa/Mastercard *without* matching their AI-fraud/tokenisation/real-time-monitoring depth could **reduce** systemic security — sovereignty bought at the cost of resilience (the author's own caveat; a rare de-PR from a sovereignty piece).
3. **USD-stablecoin flanking (monetary layer).** Even if card rails are re-shored, dollar stablecoins (GENIUS tailwind) could capture cross-border *settlement* — a second sovereignty leak the card-focused agenda doesn't address, pushing the EU to reopen MiCA. Adoption still lags: Wero is 43.5m users but P2P-heavy, not yet a POS card substitute — "announced" ≠ "live at checkout."

**What this changes (idea-lens).** (analysis) Payments sovereignty is real *policy ambition* but the execution is a **coordination problem, not a technology problem**: the winning outcome needs Wero (private, live, scaling) and the digital euro (public, 2029) to be genuinely complementary rather than mutually undercutting, plus a euro-stablecoin answer on cross-border. Falsifiable thesis: Wero crosses from P2P into **material POS/e-com acceptance in 2026–2027** OR it stalls as a P2P niche while cards keep the checkout. Triggers to watch: (a) the 2026 EU vote adopting the digital-euro Regulation (pilot 2027, issuance 2029) and whether it mandates acceptance in a way that squeezes Wero; (b) Wero NFC/POS go-live and cross-border expansion beyond FR/DE/BE/LU/NL; (c) any EU move to promote a euro stablecoin under a revised MiCA. If the digital euro arrives with a broad acceptance mandate before Wero reaches POS scale, the private layer's economics break.
