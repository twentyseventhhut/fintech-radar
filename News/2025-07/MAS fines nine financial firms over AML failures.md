# MAS penalises CS, UOB, UBS, Citi, JB and LGT for AML failings

## The combined penalties of S$27.45m ($21.54m) related to AML breaches in Singapore's S$3bn money laundering scandal that rocked the city in August 2023, and involve individual bankers, as well as financial institutions.

ByPriyanka Boghani,James Phillipps

TwitterFacebookLinkedinWhatsAppprint;)

TwitterFacebookLinkedinWhatsAppprint;)

Singapore’s regulator, the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) has hit nine financial institutions, including leading banks in the region, with penalties totalling S$27.45m ($21.54m), and penalised several relationship managers, over the money laundering scandal that rocked the country.

The individual bank penalties include S$5.8m for Credit Suisse; S$5.6m for UOB; S$3m for UBS; S$2.6 for Citibank; S$2.4m for Julius Baer and S$1m for LGT.

MAS said it completed its supervisory examinations of the roles of financial institutions and individuals in the S$3bn ($2.3bn) money laundering case of August 2023. The case resulted in the jailing of 10 foreign nationals and the seizure of assets including properties, gold bars and luxury watches.

Several other capital market services license holders were penalised, including UOB Kay Hian Private Limited (UOBKH), which was hit with S$2.85m. Asset manager Blue Ocean Invest and Trident Trust Company (Singapore) were slapped with S$2.4m and S$1.8m penalties.

The watchdog said the breaches took place between early 2023 and early 2025, and arose out of ‘poor or inconsistent implementation’ of AML/CFT policies and controls.

Julius Baer, Blue Ocean Invest, Citi, Credit Suisse and UOB Kay Hian all failed to implement adequate customer risk assessments which led to the mid-rating of money laundering risks. On transaction monitoring, Julius Baer, Citi, Credit Suisse, LGT, UOB, UOB Kay Hian, Trident and UBS all failed to meet MAS’ standards, showing inconsistencies.

Both UOB and UOB Kay Hian did not follow up in a timely manner with a post-suspicious transaction report.

All nine financial institutions did not detect or adequately follow up on significant discrepancies or red flags in information and documents that should have cast doubt on some customers’ purported source of wealth and which indicated increased risk of money laundering.

MAS said the institutions have embarked on remediation of the deficiencies and MAS will monitor their progress closely.

## Private bankers penalised

The MAS also took action against a series of individuals, including censuring two senior former UOB bankers for failings around the money laundering scandal. Ang Sze Hee, Alvin, and Tan Sheng Rong, both ex-team head of group retail privilege banking at the Singaporean bank, received reprimands for ‘failures to conduct or ensure proper due diligence’.

The censures related to not adequately scrutinising persons of interest and failures regarding post-suspicious transaction report follow-ups.

A further nine relationship managers and RM supervisors, whose employers were not disclosed, were privately reprimanded for ‘more limited’ lapses, MAS said.

‘MAS may take action against a few remaining individuals, after the conclusion of ongoing court proceedings or investigations,’ the regulator said.

MAS issued prohibition orders ranging from three to six years against four Blue Ocean Invest staff. The prohibition orders them prevent them from carrying out any regulated activities for the duration of their term.

A UOB spokesperson said: ‘We have conducted a thorough assessment of the facts and circumstances surrounding the issues and staff involved, and taken appropriate actions to address accountability and discipline.’

## Banks respond

The fined banks have all said they accept MAS’s findings and have acted to improve their systems and controls.

A spokesperson from Citi said the bank is ‘committed to ensuring the highest standard of governance and controls to detect and prevent money laundering’. It has also strengthened its client onboarding and monitoring processes, the spokesperson added.

Noting the announcement, Julius Baer said: ‘We have taken concrete steps to strengthen our processes and anti-money laundering framework and remain firmly committed to upholding the high standards of the Singapore financial centre.

In response to the penalties, a spokesperson from UOB said: ‘We acknowledge and accept MAS’ findings with regard to the identified areas for improvement.’

UOB added that over the past two years, it has invested in technology and implemented ‘prompt remedial actions to address the deficiencies identified after a comprehensive internal review, including stepping up on our transaction monitoring and customer due diligence processes’.

Meanwhile, a spokesperson from LGT said it acknowledges the MAS’ findings and the financial penalty imposed.

‘We remain strongly committed to the fight against money laundering and safeguarding the integrity of Singapore’s financial system,’ the spokesperson said.

While UBS said it has ‘fully cooperated with the authorities to resolve this issue.’

Citywire Asia also reached out to Credit Suisse for a comment.

_This article has been amended on 5 July to add comments from LGT, Julius Baer and UBS._
