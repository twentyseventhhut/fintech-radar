# Selar-Mainstack billboard feud erupts at Lagos conference

**On one of the days of Moment, a three-day gathering of digital creators in Lagos, the atmosphere carried the quiet confidence of an ecosystem coming into its own. Convened by Mainstack, an all-in-one platform for global creators and entrepreneurs, the event drew creators, funders, and builders from within and outside Nigeria into a shared space defined by ambition and possibility. It was not just another tech convening. It was a statement about where the creator economy is headed and who intends to shape it.**

But somewhere within that carefully curated experience, a different kind of statement appeared, uninvited and unmistakably deliberate.

Banners belonging to Selar, a competitor that markets itself as an all-in-one e-commerce store, were placed within the event venue without the organisers’ approval. By the following day, they had been removed by Mainstack.

What could have remained a quiet assertion of control quickly spilled into public discourse, with social media splitting, as it often does, between admiration for boldness and concern for professionalism.

Selar did not retreat from the moment. In its official response, the company framed the action as intentional and even necessary.

“We put up a few billboards around Landmark yesterday. The idea was simple: creators are gathering in one place, and we wanted to meet them where they are. A little bit of playful competition never hurt anyone,” the statement reads.

The company went further to position the act within a broader philosophy: “Competition is healthy, and creators win when platforms keep pushing each other to build better tools, better communities, and better opportunities”.

It is a compelling argument on the surface. Competition, after all, is the lifeblood of innovation. The presence of multiple platforms vying for creators’ attention has undoubtedly accelerated the growth of Nigeria’s digital economy. Tools have improved, access has widened, and creators now have more options than ever before.

Yet, beneath the language of playfulness and progress lies a more complicated reality.

**WHEN COMPETITION CROSSES INTO CONTROL**

There is a fundamental difference between meeting creators where they are and inserting oneself into a space deliberately built by a competitor. Events like Moment are not accidental gatherings. They are investments. They require capital, planning, partnerships, and a clear strategic vision. Every element within that space, from the branding to the programming, is part of a narrative the organiser has paid to construct.

To enter that space without invitation and place competing brand material within it is to benefit from that investment without participating in its cost. It is not simply clever positioning. It is, at its core, an appropriation of controlled space.

In more mature ecosystems, such actions would likely be classified as ambush marketing, a tactic that has long occupied a grey area in global brand strategy but is increasingly scrutinised for precisely this reason.

While often defended as creative disruption, ambush marketing becomes contentious when it undermines the economic structure that sustains organised events. If competitors can freely insert themselves into curated spaces, the incentive to invest in those spaces begins to erode.

Nigeria’s tech and creator ecosystem is still defining its boundaries, which is why this moment has generated as much debate as it has.

There is, within the local startup culture, a strong admiration for audacity. Founders are encouraged to move quickly, think differently, and capture attention by any means necessary. In that environment, actions that test the limits of convention are often celebrated rather than questioned.

This cultural instinct helps explain why a segment of observers viewed Selar’s move as impressive rather than inappropriate. It fits neatly into a familiar narrative of the bold challenger refusing to be invisible. It speaks to a generation that values ingenuity and rewards visibility.

But ecosystems cannot be built on audacity alone. They require structure, and structure depends on shared norms.

Without those norms, competition risks becoming extractive. Companies begin to prioritise visibility over value, presence over principle. The result is not a vibrant marketplace but a chaotic one, where the lines between innovation and intrusion are constantly blurred.

Mainstack’s response, in this context, was both predictable and necessary. Removing the banners was not an act of insecurity; it was an assertion of ownership. To have allowed them to remain would have signalled that its event space was open for appropriation, a precedent that could quickly spiral into loss of control over its own brand environment.

Yet, the reaction to that decision also reveals an important truth about building in public. In today’s ecosystem, perception travels faster than intention. While Mainstack acted within its rights, the absence of a strong, framing narrative left room for misinterpretation. Some saw the move as lacking sportsmanship, others as an overreaction to what was framed as harmless competition.

**AN ECOSYSTEM STILL WRITING ITS RULES**

This is where the incident becomes instructive, not just for the companies involved but for the ecosystem at large. It highlights the dual responsibility that emerging platforms must carry. They must not only enforce boundaries but also communicate them in ways that reinforce confidence rather than invite doubt.

At the same time, Selar’s statement, while measured and forward-looking, does not fully resolve the ethical tension at the heart of the issue. The framing of the act as “playful competition” softens its edges but does not change its nature. Playfulness implies mutual consent. In this case, consent was absent.

There is also a subtle but important distinction between competition that creates value and competition that borrows visibility. The former pushes platforms to build better products, improve user experience, and deepen community engagement. The latter risks reducing competition to moments of spectacle, where attention is captured not through innovation but through proximity.

The irony is that both Mainstack and Selar ultimately depend on the same constituency. Their success is tied to the growth and trust of digital creators. In that sense, they are not just rivals; they are co-architects of an emerging economic space.

This is precisely why the incident matters beyond the immediate drama. It forces a broader reflection on what kind of ecosystem is being built. One where competition sharpens value, or one where it stretches boundaries until they become meaningless.

If the creator economy in Nigeria is to mature, it must develop a clearer sense of what is acceptable and what is not. This does not require rigid regulation or the stifling of creativity. It requires a shared understanding that certain lines, once crossed, weaken the collective.

Events like Moment play a crucial role in ecosystem building. They are not just marketing exercises; they are trust-building platforms. Creators attend because they believe in the space, in the conversations, and in the integrity of the environment. When that environment becomes contested in ways that feel unstructured or intrusive, it risks undermining that trust.

It is also worth considering the long-term implications for collaboration. As the ecosystem grows, partnerships between platforms, creators, and investors will become more important. Trust will be the currency that enables those partnerships. Incidents that introduce friction or uncertainty, even if framed as harmless, can have ripple effects that extend beyond a single event.

None of this is to suggest that competition should be sanitised or stripped of personality. On the contrary, the energy of rivalry can be a powerful driver of innovation. But that energy must be anchored in respect for boundaries. Without that anchor, it becomes destabilising rather than productive.

What the Mainstack–Selar moment ultimately reveals is an ecosystem in transition. It is moving from informality to structure, from experimentation to expectation. In that transition, tensions are inevitable. Companies will test limits. Communities will debate. Norms will be challenged and, eventually, defined.

The question is not whether such incidents will occur again. They will. The more important question is how they will be interpreted and what precedents they will set.

If actions like this are consistently framed as clever without acknowledging their implications, the ecosystem may drift towards a culture where visibility is pursued at any cost. If, on the other hand, they prompt reflection and recalibration, they can contribute to a more balanced and sustainable environment.

Selar’s closing line in its statement is, perhaps unintentionally, the most resonant: “This is your moment”. It speaks to creators, to opportunity, to the sense that something significant is unfolding in Nigeria’s digital space.

But moments, by their nature, are fleeting. What endures are the structures built within them.

For Nigeria’s $30 billion creator economy, the task now is to ensure that those structures are not only innovative but also principled. That competition remains healthy without becoming intrusive. That boldness is encouraged without eroding respect. And that, as the ecosystem grows, it does so on foundations strong enough to support the weight of its own ambition.

Because in the end, the real measure of maturity is not how loudly an ecosystem competes, but how well it defines the rules that make that competition meaningful.

Review 
 Africa’s creator-enabler wars: between Selar and Mainstack on the Moment conference 
In the heart of Lagos, Landmark Event Centre, the Moment 2026 conference, hosted by Mainstack from March 13 to 15, 2026, promised inspiration, networking, and growth for thousands of African creators. 
Instead, it delivered one of the juiciest public feuds in the Nigerian tech scene this year, pitting two leading creator monetisation platforms against each other: Selar and Mainstack.
Selar, positioning itself as Africa’s largest creator platform with over 2 million registered users and more than $26 million paid out to creators, executed a classic ambush. They secured ad space at the venue for eye-catching billboards sporting motivational taglines like “Create with intention” and “Create what sells”, prominently featuring the Selar logo. 
Selar CEO Douglas Kendyson later explained they had proactively disclosed the competitive nature to venue operators, who approved the placements after vetting. It was bold, calculated guerrilla marketing, inserting your brand into the rival’s spotlight without saying a word directly.
Milton Tutu, Selar’s former CMO unveils as Mainstack’s new CMO
But Mainstack, having invested heavily in curating panels, workshops, and a premium experience for attendees, saw red. They lodged complaints, and the billboards were swiftly removed. 
What could have ended as a cheeky anecdote exploded on X when former Selar Chief Marketing Officer Milton Tutu was unveiled as Mainstack’s new CMO right at the event.
Tutu, who spent four years at Selar scaling it from modest numbers to a continental force – growing to 2.2 million users across 13 countries, as detailed in his October 2025 Medium farewell post titled “Leaving Selar, Not the Mission” – had publicly framed his departure as amicable. 
Yet Kendyson, in a viral thread on X today, dropped a bombshell: “He was fired. I gave my blessings about his new position in December 2025 when they called me. It’s unfortunate that they’re stooping to making a cheap coordinated PR attack on our brand with scripted tweets when it’s been nothing but love from me. I’m not Michelle Obama.”
Accompanying screenshots showed a wave of near-identical tweets from various users: “So, I guess I am moving with Milton Tutu to Mainstack #moment2026” and “#Momentbymainstack just announced that Milton is now the CMO! Milton moves from stellar to moment!” 
Kendyson labelled them scripted and targeted, implying Mainstack orchestrated a subtle or not-so-subtle dig at Selar by framing Tutu as the star product being “transferred”.
The timeline added fuel. Tutu’s exit announcement came months earlier, with no public bad blood until this moment. Mainstack’s announcement photo of Tutu smiling confidently circulated widely, and while observers noted the irony, Tutu had still spoken positively about Selar partnerships as recently as late 2025.
X reactions captured the chaos perfectly. Queens, that cut through the noise: “‘What Selar did was not fair.’ ‘Mainstack invested so much into that event.’ Honey, this is competition. Like the Burger King v McDonald’s, Coke v Pepsi style. It’s done all over the world. It’s not some ‘friendly Kumbaya, let’s all get along’ vibe lol.” 
Her post garnered strong support, framing Selar’s move as standard big-brand tactics rather than betrayal.
Others weren’t as charitable. TheiAmEmmanuel amplified the drama by alleging Mainstack hadn’t paid staff salaries for February, questioning priorities amid their splashy event. Zegbua, a respected growth voice, quoted Kendyson’s thread to urge de-escalation: the “scripted” tweets didn’t overtly trash Selar, and airing the “fired” detail felt unnecessary. 
He noted Milton had never spoken ill of Selar publicly and had even pushed collaborations recently.
This clash highlights a maturing but still raw African creator economy. Platforms like Selar and Mainstack empower millions to monetise digital products, courses, and services amid rising internet access and creator ambition. 
Yet rapid growth breeds intense rivalry: talent poaching, event hijacking, and social media mudslinging become weapons when differentiation is tough.
For attendees at Moment 2026, the drama likely overshadowed some sessions, but it also underscored the stakes. Healthy competition drives innovation, yet the personal barbs risk fracturing a community still building itself. As one observer put it, this isn’t kumbaya; it’s business. Whether it fuels better products or just more tweets remains to be seen.
