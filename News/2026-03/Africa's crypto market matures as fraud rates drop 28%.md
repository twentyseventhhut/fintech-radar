 Africa's crypto market matures, fraud rate drops 28% 
A new report from cryptocurrency verification platform Sumsub has found that African crypto platforms are improving identity verification and fraud prevention as crypto adoption accelerates on the continent.
March 20, 2026
 Cryptocurrency platforms across Africa are rapidly improving identity verification performance as the industry moves toward stricter regulation and stronger fraud prevention. 
 That is a key takeaway from the State of the Crypto Industry 2026 report from crypto verification platform Sumsub.  
 Sumsub's fourth annual report reveals how crypto companies are balancing fraud prevention, regulatory pressure and user experience as they scale in 2026.  
 The report draws on Sumsub's internal data from 2024-2025 and insights from 300 crypto companies outside of Sumsub's customer base. 
 Globally, 74% of crypto providers now prioritize verification accuracy over onboarding speed (39%), signaling a shift away from the "growth at all costs" model that defined the sector's early expansion. 
 The report said the crypto industry is entering a phase of "regulated maturity," where compliance, fraud prevention and user experience are increasingly integrated into core product design rather than treated as separate operational layers.

 The improvement reflects Africa's mobile-first fintech ecosystem, where onboarding processes are increasingly optimized for smartphone users and digital identity tools. 
 "Africa's crypto ecosystem is entering a phase where operational discipline matters more than momentum," said Hannes Bezuidenhout, Sumsub's VP of sales for Africa. 
 "As platforms scale, the focus is shifting from how fast they can grow to how effectively they can operate under increasing regulatory scrutiny. Compliance, fraud resilience, and onboarding efficiency are becoming interconnected challenges that must be addressed together," Bezuidenhout added.  
 The trend is particularly visible in major crypto adoption hubs, including South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, Ghana and Mauritius. 
 The report's authors said the findings highlight how Africa's fast-growing digital finance ecosystem is adapting to a more regulated environment while maintaining strong user onboarding performance. 
Fraud dynamics vary across African crypto markets
 While fraud remains a global challenge, the report shows evolving patterns across African markets as crypto adoption accelerates. 
 Across the continent, average fraud rates rose from 1.7% in 2023 to 3.6% in 2024, before declining to 2.6% in 2025, representing a 28% year-on-year drop.  
 Sumsub said the decline suggests that earlier investments in stronger know-your-customer (KYC) processes, mobile‑first verification and better transaction‑monitoring controls are starting to pay off.  
 However, fraud dynamics still vary across key markets, with Ghana recording a fraud rate of 4.6%, South Africa 3.1%, Nigeria 2.6% and Kenya 2.5%. 
 Related: Identity fraud spikes across Africa - Sumsub 
 The report points out that the continent remains a target for scams and "mule-account recruitment," particularly as crypto usage expands alongside mobile payments and digital banking platforms. 
 Mule-account recruitment is a form of financial fraud where criminals recruit individuals to use their personal bank accounts to receive, move or withdraw illicit funds – often disguised as legitimate work-from-home jobs. 
 In some parts of the continent, fraud rates were extremely high in 2025, exceeding 5% of all verification attempts in Mali, Tanzania, Chad, Uganda and Cameroon, while reaching the regional maximum of 6.4% in Senegal, despite the overall improvement in regional fraud rates.

How AI is reshaping both fraud and fraud prevention
 The report said that artificial intelligence (AI) is becoming a defining factor in the next stage of crypto security. 
 Globally, 57% of those surveyed cited AI-powered fraud detection as their top security priority. 
 "Attackers are using automation and generative tools to scale fraud attempts faster than ever," Bezuidenhout added. 
 Around 55% of crypto platforms reported fraud incidents in 2025, while 15% were unsure whether fraud had occurred, highlighting detection challenges.

 "Defenders need systems that can respond in real-time across identity, behavior, and transaction data," Bezuidenhout added. 
 According to the report, the strongest competitive advantage for crypto platforms in 2026 and beyond will come from integrated systems that continuously learn across the entire user lifecycle. 
Regulatory interventions across Africa 
 The report also noted that implementation of the Travel Rule – which requires crypto platforms to share sender and receiver information for transactions – remains uneven globally, with only 23% of providers reporting full compliance. 
 At the same time, several African governments are introducing new regulatory frameworks aimed at bringing digital assets under formal financial oversight. 
 Key regulatory developments include: 
 South Africa implemented the Travel Rule directive in 2025 and introduced the Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF) tax reporting regulations from March 2026.  
 Nigeria passed the Investments and Securities Act 2025, formally recognizing virtual assets as securities under the supervision of the Securities and Exchange Commission. 
 Kenya's Virtual Asset Service Providers Act establishes licensing requirements under the Capital Markets Authority and the Central Bank. 
 Ghana is preparing Virtual Asset Service Providers legislation, expected to introduce oversight by the Bank of Ghana and the Securities and Exchange Commission. 
 Mauritius continues to strengthen its digital asset framework as part of its strategy to position itself as a regulated global financial center. 
 "These regulatory developments signal a broader shift toward formal oversight of digital assets across Africa, as governments seek to balance innovation with stronger financial crime controls," the report said. 
Verification speeds improve even as regulatory expectations rise
 Average verification time in Africa decreased from 25 seconds in 2024, to 18 seconds in 2025, a 28% year‑on‑year improvement. 
 The report's authors said this shows that verification can become significantly faster even as regulatory expectations rise.  
 "Mobile‑first user journeys, optimized for low bandwidth and diverse ID formats, reduce re‑submissions and manual interventions, especially in markets where most users rely on smartphones, supporting broader digital‑economy goals around inclusion and formalization," Sumsub said.   
 "This is in line with digital‑economy guidance that mobile‑native onboarding and context‑aware KYC are key to bringing under‑documented users into regulated ecosystems," the company added. 
Document-based verification pass rates in African countries. (Source: Sumsub's State of the Crypto Industry 2026 report)
 Another notable trend is user experience merging with compliance, increasing popularity in "non-doc" and "reusable identity" solutions among future-oriented crypto platforms. 
 Non-doc refers to document-free user onboarding, and reusable identity allows for verifying clients across multiple platforms without repetitive document uploads. 
 Across Africa, the highest user pass rates in 2025 referred to the non-doc verification method: 92% in Nigeria, 93% in Kenya and 94% in South Africa.  
Non-document-based verification pass rates in South Africa, Kenya and Nigeria. (Source: Sumsub's State of the Crypto Industry 2026 report)
 In 2026, high pass rates are expected to be maintained alongside compliance requirements in increasingly complex cross-border onboarding scenarios, the report predicted. 
 It said these figures reflect both rapid crypto adoption and growing investments in verification infrastructure across Africa's leading digital finance markets. 
The next phase of crypto growth 
 Sustainable expansion of the crypto sector will depend on embedding compliance directly into product architecture, according to the report. 
 Sumsub believes that crypto "has entered a regulated maturity era" and that growth in 2026 will be defined by regulatory scrutiny, sophisticated fraud pressure, and competition to deliver compliant, low-friction onboarding at scale. 
 "Regulated maturity means building better systems, not just adding more rules. The platforms that win will be those that embed verification into their product DNA and wrap automation in strong controls, transparency, and accountability," explained Ilya Brovin, chief growth officer at Sumsub.  
 Brovin said that in the era of AI agents, the central dilemma is how AI-powered verification vendors continuously outmaneuver AI-driven fraud without eroding user experience or auditability.
