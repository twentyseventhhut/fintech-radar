# Santander Brasil names Gilson Finkelsztain as new CEO

Gilson Finkelsztain , chief executive of B3 , will become the new CEO of Banco Santander Brasil , replacing Mario Leão, who has held the position for nearly five years. The transition is expected to take place by July. Finkelsztain will take the helm of Brazil’s third-largest private bank at a complex moment, as the institution continues to recover from a deterioration in delinquency levels and seeks to reinvent itself amid growing competition from fintech companies. 
 Finkelsztain’s departure from B3 had been previously reported by Valor . 
 Leão informed staff of his departure during a meeting on Thursday afternoon, saying he had completed a cycle and requested to step down. The request was accepted by Ana Botín , executive chair of the Spanish banking group. 
 Leão was announced in July 2021 and officially took over in January 2022, succeeding Sérgio Rial . From a traditional family of lawyers, he has been with Santander for ten years, having previously worked at Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, and Morgan Stanley. 
 In a statement, Botín said Finkelsztain ’s experience and recognition in Brazil’s financial sector “make him well qualified to lead the next phase of growth in this highly relevant market, as we continue executing our strategy and delivering profitable growth.” She also said Leão played a key role in transforming the bank and strengthening its position in Brazil. “I would like to sincerely thank him for his leadership, dedication, and the positive impact he has had on the organization and our teams, and wish him all the best for the future.” 
 In his own statement, Leão said Santander has reached a level of maturity that allows for a structured and well-planned succession process at an appropriate moment. “This will ensure continuity in executing our strategy through a team I am proud to have led. I am very pleased with the cycle I experienced here, with everything we have achieved, and with what lies ahead,” he said. “I wish Gilson every success and am confident he will do an excellent job leading the bank, building on what we have developed over the years.” 
 Finkelsztain said he is pleased to return to Santander and confident in the bank’s strategy and team. “Our focus will be on turning a solid foundation into meaningful outcomes for clients, shareholders, and society. Brazil is a market of great opportunities, and I am excited about what we can build in the coming years,” he said. 
 The executive, who has led B3 for nearly nine years, previously worked at Santander between 2011 and 2013. He holds a degree in civil engineering from PUC-Rio and has also worked at JPMorgan , Bank of America , and Cetip . 
 Finkelsztain takes over the bank at a time of recovery and deep transformation in the sector. Like its major incumbents, Santander has been closing branches to reduce servicing costs and compete with fintechs. The bank is developing a “ super app ” and leveraging the group’s global technology platforms to modernize customer service. Like Leão, however, he does not have a background in retail banking, at a time when the formula for making low-income segments profitable remains unclear. 
 Finkelsztain had been nominated in September to join Santander’s board of directors, fueling market expectations that he would eventually take over as CEO. Three months later, however, he withdrew from the nomination, reportedly to avoid potential conflicts of interest with B3. 
 He leaves B3 at a time when the exchange is preparing for the entry of new competitors into Brazil’s capital markets, which could intensify competition. Still, he will not be entirely removed from the topic. One project in development is CSD BR, backed by Santander, BTG Pactual, and Cboe Global Markets. 
 Leão faced a challenging mandate when he became CEO nearly five years ago. His predecessor, Rial, had significantly expanded the bank’s franchise, but the institution was heavily reliant on credit. When the cycle turned after the pandemic, Leão implemented a broad adjustment. During his tenure, he replaced most senior executives, invested in technology and business diversification, and simplified the group’s structure in Brazil. 
 In the latest earnings release, Leão said the bank was making progress in reducing risk across its portfolios. “The de-risking process is long, but this year marks material progress in this management,” he said. 
 Since taking office, he had been questioned by the market about when return on equity (ROE) would return to 20%. “The bank is moving toward a 20% ROE, but that is not the final goal. After that, we will aim for 22%, 23%,” he said last month. Profitability reached 17.6% in the fourth quarter. 
 There had already been signs that Leão was nearing the end of his tenure. In December, it was announced that chief financial officer Gustavo Alejo would be replaced by Spaniard Carlos Muñiz. Last month, Santander also announced plans to move its Brazilian headquarters to a new building . 
 Also in February, during a triennial investor meeting, Botín outlined the bank’s outlook and made it clear that Brazil was not among the group’s top priorities. She highlighted growth prospects in the United States and the United Kingdom, noting that the share of revenue in strong currencies is expected to increase, suggesting emerging markets may lose prominence. In previous years, Brazil had been Santander’s largest market, surpassing both the UK and Spain. 
 At the meeting, the Spanish group set a target to raise the profitability of its Brazilian operation to 20% by 2028. “Itaú Unibanco has 22% [profitability]. We should aim for more than 20% in the future, but for now, that is what we feel comfortable disclosing,” Botín said. She added that of the bank’s goal to add 30 million customers over three years, around 15 million are expected to come from Brazil and Mexico. 
 Sell-side analysts consulted by Valor said Leão’s departure is not exactly a surprise, as Santander has struggled to gain traction in its recovery. The situation differs from that of Bradesco, which replaced its CEO at the end of 2023 and, under Marcelo Noronha, has managed to convince the market of its restructuring plan. 
 At the same time, Finkelsztain’s appointment may ease speculation that the parent company was considering taking its Brazilian unit private. “They would not hire a new CEO to delist the company. To do that, they would have appointed someone from Spain,” one analyst noted.
