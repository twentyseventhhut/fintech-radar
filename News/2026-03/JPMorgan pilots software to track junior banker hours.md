JPMorganChase is reportedly using computer tracking systems to monitor the workloads of junior investment bankers. 
 Under a pilot programme, IT systems will monitor junior employees’ video calls, desktop keystrokes and scheduled meetings to estimate their weekly workloads, according to the Financial Times (FT). 
 Employees will then be sent reports that compare the computer-generated estimates against their self-reported time sheets.

 JPMorgan said the system was designed to address issues with overwork. In a statement to the FT , it explained: “Much like the weekly screen time summaries on a smartphone, this tool is about awareness – not enforcement. It’s designed to support transparency and wellbeing and encourage open conversations about workload.” 
 The US bank has sought to address issues around unsustainable working hours after an internal poll of 13 first-year investment banking analysts at Goldman Sachs was leaked in 2021 .  
 In their responses, the junior bankers claimed they faced “inhumane” working conditions, “abuse” from colleagues and sleep deprivation, with their average hours worked in the past week exceeding 100.  
 In 2024, JPMorgan created a new role to oversee the wellbeing of junior bankers and capped their working week at 80 hours.  
 Can employee monitoring reduce overworking? 
 While tracking employees’ hours may help organisations spot patterns of overwork, it is unlikely to change the organisational culture, according to Dannielle Haig, business psychologist and founder of DH Consulting.  
 “If employees feel watched rather than supported, it may increase distrust and do little to address the root causes of overwork,” she said.  
 Organisations have increasingly deployed so-called ‘bossware’ to track the online activity of remote workers. A third of all UK organisations are now using bossware technology to monitor staff, according to a Chartered Management Institute survey published last year.  
 Excessive overtime is often driven by unrealistic workloads, pressure from management, understaffing and reward systems that encourage long hours, Haig said, adding: “If employers genuinely want to reduce overwork, they need to go beyond surveillance and address job design, management capability and culture.” 
 The additional monitoring of employees can also create a culture that prioritises presenteeism over output, according to Chris Preston, founder and director of consultancy The Culture Builders. 
 “What we’re not seeing with the JPMorgan statement is the intent to use this tool to reduce the amount of time that people are working,” he said, “Strong organisations understand the difference between great work and volume of work.” 
 The problem of overwork is not confined to the banking sector – only 20 per cent of workers feel able to stick to their core office hours, with 66 per cent finding their workload too demanding to log off on time, a Robert Walters survey revealed.  
 The government had sought to address the issue through legislation and had included the right to disconnect in initial drafts of the employment rights bill, but this was ultimately dropped.  
 Employers that are experiencing issues of overwork should review staff workloads, ensure leaders model healthy work-life balance and ensure reward structures do not encourage overtime. 
 “ Data can provide a warning signal but culture change comes from changing what the organisation rewards, tolerates and expects,” added Haig.
