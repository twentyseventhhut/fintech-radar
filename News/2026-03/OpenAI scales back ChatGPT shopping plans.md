 OpenAI is killing Instant Checkout. Don't dance on the grave of agentic shopping yet
 

 By 
 Kiri Masters, 
 
 
 Commerce media columnist
 
 

 March 6, 2026 | 9 min read
 

 
 Listen to article
 
 4 min 
 
 An undercooked business model died this week. But the shopping behavior it was trying to capture is alive and well – just ask Alibaba. 
Shortly after OpenAI launched Instant Checkout last year, I made a fully agentic purchase through ChatGPT. A lavender candle. The experience was... fine. Not bad. Not broken. Just not meaningfully better than what I already do on Amazon or Target. I never went back.
If I – someone who covers this space for a living and genuinely wants AI to handle more of my shopping – couldn’t be bothered to return, imagine how the average consumer felt. So when the news broke this week that OpenAI is killing Instant Checkout and routing purchases back through retailer apps, I wasn’t exactly shocked.
I also wasn’t shocked by the victory laps everywhere. “Agentic commerce is dead.” “We told you so.” The naysayers are having a field day.
I reckon they’re celebrating the wrong thing.
What actually died
According to The Information, OpenAI is killing off Instant Checkout , the feature that lets users buy products without leaving ChatGPT. Going forward, if you want to actually purchase something ChatGPT recommends, you’ll either use a third-party app built inside ChatGPT (like Instacart or Expedia) or get bounced to the retailer’s own site to complete the transaction there.
As it turns out, product data across the internet is too messy, unstandardized, and fragmented to allow an AI agent to reliably automate checkouts without risking financial errors or inventory disputes. As of this week, OpenAI had only integrated a dozen out of millions of Shopify merchants. It hadn’t built sales tax collection. The Agentic Commerce Protocol survives in narrower form, but the in-chat checkout layer is gone.
Advertisement
Marc Massar at Aura Labs put it well in his essay this week: what died is a specific business model where OpenAI tried to be both the pipe and the toll booth, inserting itself as a new checkout intermediary and taxing every transaction. Users rejected the toll booth. The market was right to kill it.
But the commentary has made a category error. It’s treating one failed implementation as proof that the entire behavioral shift is a mirage.
Because while OpenAI was fumbling checkout, agentic shopping was already working in another market. Alibaba’s Qwen app completes food orders, travel bookings, and product purchases inside a single conversational interface – right now, at scale. It works because Alibaba owns the AI model, the marketplace, the payment rails, and the logistics. As Massar points out, OpenAI tried to replicate this without owning the stack. That’s what failed, not the concept.
So we know the behavior can work. The question is how and when it arrives in the West.
Stalls in adoption happen
Now, before the Qwen comparison gets anyone too excited, not every behavior that works in China catches on in the US, or at least not on any predictable schedule.
Want to go deeper? Ask The Drum
In China, livestream shopping emerged around 2016 and grew into a market worth roughly $807bn by 2024. In the US, the first wave flopped. Meta shut down Facebook Live Shopping in 2022. Amazon launched its TikTok-like short-form video and photo shopping feed ‘Inspire’ in 2022 and killed it by 2025. The reasonable conclusion at the time: American consumers don’t want to shop this way.
The stall lasted years. And then TikTok Shop launched in September 2023 and found traction by embedding commerce inside entertainment, creator trust, and native checkout. It worked once it stopped behaving like “QVC on the internet” and started feeling like a natural extension of an app people already loved.
There’s a two-sided lesson here. For the naysayers: a stall doesn’t mean the behavior is dead. For the enthusiasts (myself included): just because Qwen proves the model works in China doesn’t mean it’ll translate directly to Western markets on any predictable schedule.
Consumers don’t adopt new technology because it’s theoretically useful. They adopt it when the product experience is natural, worthwhile, and safe. ChatGPT’s version of agentic checkout ran headfirst into the harsh operational reality that retail fulfillment, payment processing, tax compliance, and fraud prevention are extremely thorny problems. That’s an execution failure, not a conceptual one.
The threats to retailers that persist
I’ve spent the last few months arguing that AI-enabled commerce poses a real threat to the $60bn+ retail media industry – that when discovery moves upstream into AI assistants, the on-site advertising surfaces where retailers earn 70-80% margins start losing their audience. I’ve laid out that case in detail in previous columns here and here . 
Nothing about this week changes that thesis. Three things are worth flagging:
Advertisement
Discovery has already moved upstream. Consumers are using AI assistants for product research, comparison, and narrowing their consideration sets right now. This doesn’t require checkout to work inside ChatGPT. Capgemini research found that 53% of US consumers have made a purchase based on AI recommendations. That discovery shift is already eroding the value of retailer-owned surfaces – and it’s accelerating.
Amazon isn’t pulling back. It has everything needed to make agentic shopping work: user context, the product graph, payment rails, fulfillment infrastructure, and Rufus getting more capable by the quarter. Massar makes an apt comparison to Alibaba’s Qwen app, which already completes purchases inside a conversational interface – because Alibaba owns the AI model, the marketplace, the payments, and the logistics. Amazon is the closest Western equivalent. And it's openly signaling interest in enabling purchases from other retailers through their AI tools. That’s the real competitive threat.
Google isn’t sitting still either. It has the shopping graph, Merchant Center, and is pushing Gemini and AI Mode aggressively. Google has fumbled in commerce before – but the pace of recent development suggests a willingness to disrupt itself that we haven’t seen from it in years.
Russ Dieringer of the retail research firm Stratably posed an interesting question in a LinkedIn thread this week: Did the $15bn Amazon investment in OpenAI have something to do with this pullback? That’s speculation, but it’s the kind of question worth sitting with. If OpenAI is now financially entangled with Amazon, its incentive to build competing commerce infrastructure looks rather different.
The last 10% is harder than the first 90%
The bar for meaningful change in consumer behavior is high. My own experience confirms that: One fully agentic purchase, never to return.
Advertisement
But here’s what’s happened since that first underwhelming checkout experience: AI has become indispensable to every other stage of my buying journey. I use it constantly for product research, comparison, reviews synthesis, finding deals, and figuring out what I need in the first place. That first 90% of the shopping journey has already been rewired, at least for me. And the consumer usage data suggests I’m not alone.
The last 10% – the actual transaction – is the hard part. There are many technical hurdles to overcome, including order management, product feeds, payment security, etc. It also needs to be appealing to consumers: loyalty program integration, handling returns and refunds, smarter replenishment than subscribe & save, budget optimization across my household spending. Cracking that code will take time.
But consider how Amazon’s own 1-Click ordering played out. When it launched in 1997, buying something with a single button click felt risky to a lot of people. No confirmation page? No review step? It worked because eventually, shoppers trusted the platform enough to let go of a familiar friction point. Amazon patented it, defended it aggressively, and it became one of the most consequential UX innovations in e-commerce history.
The “buy now” button of the agentic future will need that same combination: a trusted platform, a solved operational backend, and an experience that makes the old way feel unnecessarily clunky. By the time someone cracks it, we’ll all be so embedded in AI-assisted shopping at every other stage that the final step will feel like the obvious missing piece rather than a leap of faith.
The skeptics got the short-term call right. This particular version of agentic commerce was oversold and underbuilt. But are they right about the destination, or the timeline?
The grave-dancing feels premature. I’d keep your running shoes on.
