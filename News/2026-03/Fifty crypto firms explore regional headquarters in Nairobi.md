# Fifty crypto firms explore regional headquarters in Nairobi

More than 50 digital currency firms, including the world’s largest cryptocurrency exchange Binance, are in talks to set up regional headquarters in Nairobi, attracted by growing adoption and tax incentives as Kenya seeks to position itself as East Africa’s leading financial hub.

The Nairobi International Finance Centre (NIFC) revealed that it is engaging the firms as part of a push to attract investment and create jobs.

“We have about 50 companies, specifically virtual asset companies, both local and international, that we are discussing with to set up regional headquarters in Kenya through the NIFC,” the NIFC’s chief executive officer, Daniel Mainda, told the _Business Daily_ in an interview.

A cryptocurrency is a digital form of money that is not issued or controlled by any monetary authority and is traded online via digital exchanges and marketplaces. It is typically based on blockchain technology to enable peer-to-peer transactions without the need for a central authority such as a bank.

Binance confirmed that it is among the firms considering Nairobi as a regional hub through the NIFC incentives, but said its entry will depend on the favourability of the final crypto regulations published by the government.

“I can confirm that we are going to be part of that cohort and we will be number one among the 50 companies, but the anchor that is dependent for us entering a market is balanced, fair, robust regulations,” said Larry Cooke, Binance’s head of legal for Africa.

Crypto firms setting base in Kenya via the NIFC would enjoy reduced corporate income tax rates of up to 15 percent for the first 10 years and 20 percent for the next decade, compared with the standard 30 percent.

Kenya is one of Africa’s largest crypto markets, with an estimated 733,300 people in the country currently owning digital assets. Crypto research firm Chainalysis ranks Kenya third in Africa in crypto adoption, after Nigeria and South Africa.

Usage and adoption have also been rising, with a recent survey by the International Monetary Fund (IMF) showing that many companies in Kenya turn to digital assets to pay for shipments during periods of dollar shortages.

A market survey commissioned by the IMF team and conducted by a technical working group comprising officials from the Capital Markets Authority (CMA) and the Central Bank of Kenya (CBK) found that the typical cryptocurrency user in Kenya is aged below 40 years, and primarily invests in Bitcoin, Ethereum, and USDT, which are the top cryptos globally.

Mr Cooke spoke ahead of the publication of the final regulations governing virtual asset service providers in the country, which are expected to pave the way for the licensing of crypto companies.

The Treasury last week published the final regulations, assigning licensing of crypto exchange service providers to the CMA and crypto payment services to the CBK.

Discussions between the NIFC and the digital currency firms gained momentum following the signing of the Virtual Asset Service Providers Act by President William Ruto in October 2025, which paved the way for the introduction of the regulations.

The law created a dual-regulator model where the CBK licenses virtual assets like stablecoins, while the CMA supervises exchanges, brokers, and trading platforms. The Kenyan law has borrowed from established practices from other countries like the US and the UK.

Under the arrangement, the CBK and the CMA will oversee crypto wallet providers, exchanges, payment processors, brokers, investment advisors, asset managers, initial coin offering providers, administrators, and miners, all of which will be needed to secure a licence from the regulator.

The NIFC introduced additional incentives last year to attract more international companies, boost foreign exchange inflows, and create jobs. For regional headquarters, firms will pay 15 percent corporate tax for 10 years and 20 percent for the next 10, provided they invest at least Sh3 billion and ensure Kenyans make up at least 60 percent of senior management.

Holding companies qualify for similar incentives if they invest Sh3 billion locally and have at least 70 percent Kenyan representation in senior management.

Startups -- defined as businesses in operation for less than 10 years -- will enjoy a 15 percent tax rate for the first four years and 20 percent for the next three, without a minimum investment requirement.

Mr Mainda said the NIFC aims to increase the number of admitted firms to about 150 by the end of the year, up from 28 currently, with crypto companies expected to play a key role in that growth.

The NIFC has struggled to attract firms since its operationalisation in 2022, listing only three companies by June last year. However, new incentives, including lowering the minimum investment threshold from Sh5 billion to Sh3 billion, have boosted uptake, raising the number of admitted firms to 28 within a year.
