BlackFin Tech Weekly — March 23rd, 2026
Every Week, we publish a short digest which sums up last week’s Fintech activity
Hello FinTech Friends,
Welcome to another week of fintech insights. Let’s explore the news and trends shaping the industry!
 Over the last week, there were seven fintech deals in Europe, totaling €119.6m in disclosed funding, including three transactions in France, two in the UK, one in Sweden and one in Germany. 
Congratulations to the three largest rounds announced last week:
 Upvest , a Germany-based investment infrastructure provider, has raised €77.5m in an equity round led by Sapphire Ventures and Tencent . 
 Steward , a US- and UK-based AI-driven AML/KYC compliance platform, has raised €4.6m in a funding round led by Motive Partners . 
 Homaio , a France-based platform enabling retail access to carbon markets, has raised €3.6m in a seed round led by RAISE Ventures . 
 Let’s dive! 
 Upvest 
 Upvest , a Germany-based fintech providing API-first investment infrastructure for financial institutions, secured €107.7m in financing, including a €77.5m equity round led by Sapphire Ventures and Tencent , with participation from existing investors Bessemer Venture Partners and BlackRock , alongside a €30.2m debt facility. The company offers a modular, API-based platform enabling banks, brokers, and wealth managers to deliver trading, custody, and back-office investment services, helping modernize legacy systems and scale retail investment offerings. Processing over 100 million annual orders for more than 30 financial institutions, Upvest will use the funding to expand across Europe and the UK, develop localized pension products, and roll out AI-driven investment solutions, positioning itself as a key infrastructure provider powering the next generation of personalized investing.

 Steward 
 Steward , a US- and UK-based AI-driven RegTech platform focused on AML/KYC compliance , raised €4.3m in a funding round led by Motive Partners , with participation from Outward VC , Cooley , and a group of angel investors. The company provides an AI-powered platform that automates investor onboarding by integrating document collection, screening, risk assessment, and ongoing compliance into a single workflow , enabling firms to onboard complex investors such as fund-of-funds, family offices, and offshore structures significantly faster. The funding will support product development and team expansion , as Steward aims to become core infrastructure for investor lifecycle management , addressing growing demand for scalable, automated compliance solutions in financial services.

 Homaio 
 Homaio , a France-based fintech enabling retail access to carbon allowance markets, raised €3.6m in a seed round led by RAISE Ventures , with participation from Groupe Eren , XAnge , and Redstone . The company offers a platform allowing individuals to invest directly in European carbon allowances through securities backed by physical EUAs, expanding access to a market traditionally reserved for institutional players. The funding will support product expansion beyond carbon markets into broader energy transition assets, as Homaio aims to build infrastructure channeling private capital into decarbonization and industrial transformation.

 In addition to this week’s fundraising activity, here is the European M&A activity for the week: 
 Mastercard , a US-based global payments network and technology company, has agreed to acquire BVNK , a UK-based provider of stablecoin infrastructure, for up to €1.7bn (including contingent payments). The acquisition will strengthen Mastercard’s capabilities in digital assets by enabling interoperability between fiat payment rails and blockchain-based systems, combining its global payments network with BVNK’s infrastructure for stablecoins and tokenized assets. The transaction supports Mastercard’s strategy to expand its end-to-end digital asset offering and address new use cases such as cross-border payments, remittances, and B2B transactions, while enabling financial institutions and fintechs to integrate compliant, scalable on-chain payment solutions.

 And finally, we bring you four news stories that caught our eye last week: 
 The European Commission unveiled its EU Inc. rules on 18 March 2026, a “28th regime” designed to make Europe more competitive against China and the US by allowing individuals to register a company in any of the 27 member states in under 48 hours, fully online and for less than €100. The optional framework will not replace national company laws but adds a new cross-border structure open to all firms. Commission President von der Leyen noted businesses face “more than 60 national company forms,” while the IMF estimates barriers equate to a 110% tariff on services. The proposal now moves to the European Parliament and Council , with approval sought by end of 2026 as part of the “One Europe, One Market” reform goal.

 Visa launched Visa Agentic Ready , a global programme preparing the payments ecosystem for AI agent-initiated transactions, rolling out first in Europe including the UK as an extension of Visa Intelligent Commerce . The initial phase focuses on issuer readiness through structured testing in production-grade environments, leveraging Visa ’s trust layer combining tokenisation, biometric authentication, and identity verification to keep agent payments securely tied to consumers. Over 20 issuers have enrolled, including Barclays , HSBC UK , Revolut , and Banco Santander , with expansion planned across North America, Asia Pacific, and Latin America.

 The SEC approved Nasdaq ’s framework to trade certain tokenised stocks and ETFs on blockchain rails alongside traditional shares, with clearing and settlement handled by the DTCC . The move opens the door to near-instant settlement and 24/7 global trading, with Kraken distributing stock tokens globally. However, shares will still trade through brokers and settle via DTCC . Critics note the system keeps trading within a permissioned, intermediary-heavy structure. Jurisdictions like Switzerland and the UAE have moved faster with blockchain-native settlement, leaving the U.S. a step behind despite overseeing a $62 trillion equity market.

 Klarna announced its card reached 5 million active customers across 16 countries on 19 March. Full year 2025 results showed GMV of $127.9 billion and revenue of $3.5 billion, both up over 20% year on year. However, Bloomberg reported the same day that four senior executives have departed since January, including the head of investor relations and M&A and the global head of litigation. Klarna attributed the exits to natural attrition across its 3,000-strong workforce. The departures come as Klarna ’s stock trades approximately 66% below its September 2025 IPO price on the NYSE , one of the sharpest post-IPO declines among major fintechs in the current cycle.
