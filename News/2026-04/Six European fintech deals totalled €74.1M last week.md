BlackFin Tech Weekly — April 13th, 2026
Every Week, we publish a short digest which sums up last week’s Fintech activity.
Hello FinTech Friends,
Welcome to another week of fintech insights. Let’s explore the news and trends shaping the industry!
 Over the last week, there were six fintech deals in Europe, totaling €74.1m in disclosed funding, including two transactions in the UK, one in Spain, one in Poland and one in Czechia. 
Congratulations to the three largest rounds announced last week:
 MillTech , a UK-based company, has raised €55.2m in a growth funding round from Apax Partners . 
 AccuQuant , a UK-based company, has raised €17.1m in a funding round led by investors from the digital asset and fintech sectors. 
 INXY , a Poland and UAE-based company, has raised €3.4m in a new seed round led by Flashpoint . 
 Let’s dive! 
 MillTech 
 MillTech , a UK-based fintech providing FX risk management and treasury solutions, raised €55.2m in a growth funding round from Apax Partners through its Apax Digital Funds, valuing the company at €299.0m . The company offers a platform that automates FX hedging and cash investment workflows, integrating trade execution, settlement, reporting, and analytics into a single solution for fund managers and corporates. The funding will support international expansion, particularly in North America, and further product development , including AI-enabled treasury tools, as MillTech aims to modernize and optimize FX and cash management operations for institutional clients.

 AccuQuant 
 AccuQuant , a UK-based company, raised €17.1m in a funding round led by investors from the digital asset and fintech sectors. The company provides AI-driven financial infrastructure supporting advanced analytics, automated execution, and risk management systems. The funding will support the development of its AI capabilities, system architecture, and infrastructure stack, as AccuQuant aims to enhance scalability, efficiency, and automation across financial services. 
 Read more 
 INXY 
 INXY , a UK and UAE-based company, raised €3.7m in a new seed round led by Flashpoint , bringing its total seed funding to €6m. The company provides infrastructure bridging digital assets and traditional payment rails, enabling businesses to process stablecoin payments, convert funds, and execute cross-border payouts. The funding will support product expansion, regulatory licensing, and international growth, as INXY aims to scale its global payment infrastructure.

 In addition to this week’s fundraising activity, here is the European M&A activity for the week: 
 Experian , an Ireland-based global data and technology company, has acquired Konfir , a UK-based provider of digital income and employment verification solutions. The acquisition strengthens Experian’s verification capabilities by integrating Konfir’s technology combining open banking, payroll, and tax data to deliver faster and more reliable checks. The transaction supports Experian’s strategy to enhance its digital verification offering, improve customer journeys, and expand its permissions-based data ecosystem across financial services, employment, and housing markets.

 And finally, we bring you four news stories that caught our eye last week: 
 Revolut has signed a ten-year lease for its Western Europe headquarters in Paris , located in the historic Bourse district. The office, set to open in early 2027, will support operations across France, Spain, Italy, Germany, Ireland and Portugal. The move is part of a €1bn investment in the region, including a French banking licence application and plans to hire 1,500+ staff for the new bank. Western Europe is now Revolut’s largest and fastest-growing region. Separately, the neobank launched AIR (AI by Revolut) , an in-app assistant rolling out in the UK. The tool offers spending insights, investment tracking, subscription management and travel assistance through conversation. 
 Read more 
 Bolt , the fintech once valued at $11 billion , has cut a third of its workforce amid growing reliance on AI and financial struggles. CEO Ryan Breslow called the layoffs “unavoidable,” stating the company will operate as a leaner, “AI-centric” organisation. The firm has also terminated most contractors and reportedly struggled to pay vendors including AWS . Founded in 2014, Bolt raised around $1bn before Breslow stepped down over a controversial $30m personal loan. Breslow returned as CEO last year and launched a “SuperApp” combining crypto and everyday payments. Bolt joins Block , Solaris , and Crypto.com in citing AI when announcing cuts. Block recently axed 40% of its workforce.

 No businesses applied for a UK banking licence in 2025, down from six in 2024, raising questions over the government’s ambition to make Britain a fintech hub. The figures emerged from a freedom of information request by consultancy Pathlight Associates . Experts warn that if perceptions of the framework’s complexity are not addressed, investment and enterprise could flow to other regions. Meanwhile, the US has capitalised on a boom in enthusiasm for banking charters with an influx of applications. In response, finance minister Rachel Reeves announced a Scaleup Unit offering bespoke regulatory support for fintechs, giving companies a clear point of contact with UK watchdogs. 
 Read more 
 UK financial regulators are holding urgent talks with the National Cyber Security Centre to assess vulnerabilities exposed by Anthropic ‘s Claude Mythos Preview , released only to select customers last week. Leading banks, insurers and exchanges will be warned within the next fortnight. The move follows a similar summons by US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent to Wall Street leaders. Anthropic said Mythos had already uncovered thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities across every major operating system and browser, warning the fallout “could be severe”. The risk is on the agenda of the UK’s Cross Market Operational Resilience Group , while the government weighs standardised AI testing for all UK lenders.
