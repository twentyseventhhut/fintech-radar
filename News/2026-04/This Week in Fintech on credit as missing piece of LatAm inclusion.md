From digital wallets to real leverage: why credit is still the missing piece of inclusion in Latin America 
Many have accounts, few have credit: the new frontier of inclusion
Latin America has solved much of the access problem in financial inclusion, but not the incentives, regulation, or trust issues. This becomes even clearer when the conversation shifts to credit. By 2024, 70% of adults in the region had a financial account, up from 39% in 2011, according to the World Bank’s Global Findex 2025 data. Yet most low-income households and micro-entrepreneurs still face a brutal reality: they can receive digital payments into a wallet, but cannot get a reasonably priced loan to smooth shocks, invest, or grow. The region has built rails, but not enough ramps.
The challenge has moved from access to use and quality. A significant share of new account holders only use their accounts to receive wages or social transfers and then immediately cash out. Without meaningful access to credit (and without the literacy to use it well), that is closer to cosmetic inclusion than to economic transformation.
Who gets credit, and who doesn't? 
Household-level evidence shows how uneven formal credit remains across the region. A multi-country survey on financial services for the poor finds that only 4.5% of poor households reported using formal credit in the previous year, versus 8.3% of non-poor households. Rural households are even more constrained: about 3.8% of rural households had used formal credit, compared with 7.4% of urban households.
Formality is one of the sharpest dividing lines. The Latin American Economic Outlook 2024 shows that in Mexico, close to 15% of formal households accessed housing loans in 2020, compared to just 2.3% of informal households. In Costa Rica, more than 12% of formal households obtained housing loans in 2021 versus only 4.2% of informal households. Even in an era of instant payments and digital wallets, being informal still largely means being un-lendable.

A Mastercard regional study adds another dimension: 58% of Latin Americans have a credit card, but only around 30% access other formal credit-linked products such as personal loans, insurance, or investments. That means the relationship with the financial system remains shallow for many users: useful for transacting, but still too weak for building resilience or wealth.
 Why is credit harder than payments? 
Payments and accounts were easier to scale because they are relatively low-risk and often supported by public infrastructure. Credit is different: it depends on information, collateral, legal enforceability, and macro stability.
Informality remains a key barrier. Many workers still earn irregular cash income, which leaves them with thin or invisible credit files even if they use a digital wallet. On top of that, weak collateral frameworks and slow judicial processes make lenders cautious, especially when dealing with small firms and first-time borrowers.
Fintech credit: progress, but still limited 
After the credit tightening of 2022, several Latin American fintech lenders entered 2024 expecting a stronger lending environment as policy rates declined and funding markets improved. Brazil illustrates this pattern clearly: the policy rate had risen from 2% during the pandemic to nearly 14% before beginning to ease, improving the economics of consumer and SME lending.
Still, fintech remains small compared with incumbent banks. Industry estimates cited in regional analysis suggest fintechs account for roughly 6% of the Brazilian credit market, while traditional banks still hold about 85%. At the same time, fintech attracted around 61% of all venture capital invested in Latin America, according to a 2025 sector assessment, which shows strong investor belief but only gradual conversion into scale in core lending.
 By 2026, the regional conversation has shifted toward a new phase powered by artificial intelligence, better underwriting, and deeper integration with payment and banking infrastructure. That is promising, but it also raises questions about pricing, transparency, and over-indebtedness if innovation outpaces consumer protection. 
Legacy banks, concentration, and why innovation still matters 
Legacy banks still sit at the center of Latin America’s credit system. Their balance-sheet capacity, branch networks, regulatory relationships, and deposit bases shape who gets funded, at what price, and on what terms. That dominance comes with high concentration: on average, the three largest banks in the region held 69% of total banking assets as of 2021, up from 59% in 2000, according to IDB research—a trend that shows no signs of reverting.
This matters for inclusion because concentrated, legacy-heavy systems tend to serve salaried, information-transparent clients well while leaving smaller firms and informal borrowers underserved. Old underwriting models, expensive branch-based operations, and rigid product design make it structurally harder for incumbents to move down-market. The answer is not to displace these banks, but to build layers around them—open finance to reduce information asymmetries, embedded finance to reach clients where they already transact, and crucially, credit guarantees that allow lenders to extend further down the risk curve without destabilizing their balance sheets.
Credit guarantees and the plumbing of inclusive finance 
Where lenders (whether banks, fintechs, or non-bank intermediaries) run into risk limits, guarantees become essential. A credit guarantee scheme covers part of the loss when a borrower defaults, changing the economics of the loan: the lender can approve credits that would otherwise fall below its risk threshold and offer better terms. In short, guarantees do not just cushion losses—they reshape the conditions under which credit is viable for underserved segments.
The guarantee ecosystem in Latin America is growing, though it remains small relative to the region’s financing needs. REGAR’s Statistical Observatory reports that in 2022, the stock of outstanding guarantees reached roughly 45 billion dollars, supporting over 48 billion dollars in credit and benefiting more than five million MSMEs. These are meaningful numbers, but they pale against the estimated 1.2 trillion dollar MSME financing gap in the region. To put this in perspective, outstanding guarantees in Latin America represent approximately 0.7 percent of regional GDP, compared with nearly 2 percent in Europe and upwards of 6 percent in Japan and South Korea (economies where guarantee systems have become a core pillar of SME finance). Several markets saw rebounds in outstanding volumes that year, while others recorded notable growth in the number of MSMEs served, signs that the system is expanding, but far from sufficient.
Private, technology-driven players now complement public schemes. Anzi Finance, for example, issues digital credit guarantees (¨fianzas¨) that absorb part of the default risk faced by lenders serving underserved segments. By sitting between the borrower and the financial institution, Anzi allows credit to flow where it otherwise would not, without the lender having to assume the full loss exposure. Critically, the use of technology to automate both the issuance of guarantees and the management of claims is what makes the model scalable: it allows the product to reach lower-ticket segments that traditional guarantee providers cannot serve economically, while managing tens of thousands of guarantees simultaneously. With operations in Colombia and expansion underway in Uruguay and Peru, the company illustrates how a dedicated, private guarantee layer can work alongside public funds to deepen the plumbing of inclusive finance.
Ultimately, inclusive credit is unlikely to scale on any single mechanism. It will require a layered architecture—traditional banks, public guarantee funds, and private innovators each absorbing a slice of the risk—working as a coordinated system rather than in parallel silos.
Women, minorities, and the inclusion gap within the inclusion gap 
Exclusion is not evenly distributed. In 2024, 66% of women in Latin America and the Caribbean had a financial account compared with 74% of men, but the credit gap is sharper still: OECD analysis notes that only 25% of women borrow from financial institutions, 11 percentage points below men, and less than 5% of women who borrow use those funds for business purposes. Ethnicity and race intensify the pattern. IDB evidence shows that in Mexico, only 21% of Indigenous peoples and 21% of African descendants use banking services, roughly half the rate of the broader population. In Brazil, 37% of African descendants report loan denials—significantly above the rates for whites and mestizos—and when they do obtain loans, they often receive smaller amounts and face higher interest rates.
Migrants face their own version of exclusion. AFI reports an average 10-percentage-point gap in credit-product ownership between non-migrants and migrants across the region. Documentation hurdles, weak portability of credit histories across borders, and fragmented financial lives mean that even economically active migrants who regularly send or receive remittances can remain invisible to lenders. Better digital identity solutions, interoperable payment histories, and cross-border data portability are not niche fixes—they are core infrastructure for the next phase of inclusion in a region defined by mobility.
These gaps will not close with better apps alone. AFI’s Gender Inclusive Finance Roadmap and a 2024 CAF-OAS policy discussion both argue for structural reforms: movable collateral frameworks, gender-disaggregated data collection, national inclusion policies with explicit gender and ethnicity lenses, and stronger consumer protection. On the innovation side, alternative credit scoring—using transaction behavior, cash-flow regularity, merchant data, or digital payment usage instead of traditional collateral—can make thin-file borrowers legible to lenders. Women’s World Banking reports that in Mexico, more than one million entrepreneurs, 52% of them women, expanded their clientele through Banco Azteca’s redesigned interface tailored to women’s needs. The lesson is consistent: innovation fosters inclusion only when it changes the decision model, not just the channel. Alternative scoring, gender-sensitive product design, and guarantee structures specifically designed to cover the risk profiles of women-led businesses, minority entrepreneurs, and migrant borrowers can widen access—but only if institutions are willing to redesign risk assessment rather than digitize existing bias. In this sense, the credit guarantee layer described in the previous section is not just a general-purpose tool for SME finance: it is potentially one of the most direct mechanisms to unlock credit for the populations that traditional systems consistently leave behind.
 DeFi, stablecoins, and the emerging edges of credit 
Stablecoins have become practical financial tools across Latin America, increasingly used for remittances, treasury management, and cross-border payments—and, in economies with capital controls or high inflation, as a vehicle to preserve value in dollar terms. Their direct impact on credit is still limited, but they can improve the financial footprint of users and businesses by generating transaction histories, stabilizing balances, and making cash flows easier to document—data trails that can eventually support underwriting for SMEs, freelancers, and gig workers poorly served by traditional credit files.
On the DeFi side, platforms like Credix have begun connecting institutional capital with SME and consumer loan portfolios in the region, originating tens of millions of dollars in real-economy credit through blockchain rails. These are early but concrete examples of decentralized infrastructure being used for lending rather than speculation alone. At the retail level, crypto-backed borrowing products are also emerging, allowing digital asset holders to unlock liquidity without selling their positions.
The limitations, however, are real. Most DeFi credit remains overcollateralized, which means it does not solve the classic problem of lending to people without financial assets to pledge. Regulatory frameworks remain uneven: Brazil’s 2026 rules brought digital asset firms under central bank oversight, but genuinely decentralized protocols still sit in a legal gray zone across most of the region. Until regulation catches up—applying the same rules to the same activities regardless of the technology—DeFi will remain an experimental complement to the region’s credit architecture rather than a mass channel for inclusion.
Regional initiatives and 2026 outlook 
Regional policy initiatives are moving in the same direction. The Financial Inclusion Initiative for Latin America and the Caribbean, hosted by the Alliance for Financial Inclusion, brings together regulators and central banks from across the region to design policies for more inclusive and sustainable growth, including responsible access to credit.
The broader macro backdrop for 2026 is modest but supportive. The World Bank’s January 2026 Global Economic Prospects projects regional growth of around 2.2% in 2025 with some improvement thereafter, while a later update estimated growth of roughly 2.5% in 2026. Fitch’s 2026 credit outlook for Latin America is broadly stable, and separate 2026 commentary suggests refinancing risk for non-bank financial institutions has remained relatively contained after active market access in 2024 and 2025.
At the same time, digital commerce and electronic payments are expected to keep growing faster than GDP, according to Visa’s 2026 regional outlook and payments-sector commentary.
