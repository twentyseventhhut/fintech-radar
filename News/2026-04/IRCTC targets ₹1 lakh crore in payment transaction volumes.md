Indian Railway Catering and Tourism Corporation (IRCTC) is aiming to scale its digital payments business to nearly Rs 1 trillion in annual transaction value, as it accelerates plans to become a full-fledged payment aggregator across ticketing and government transactions.
The company, through its wholly owned subsidiary IRCTC Payments, is seeking to expand its role beyond railway bookings by capturing the entire ticketing gross merchandise value (GMV), estimated to exceed Rs 70,000 crore.
Currently, IRCTC’s in-house iPay gateway processes around 20 per cent of its platform GMV, or roughly Rs 13,000 crore, indicating significant headroom for growth as it moves to internalise a larger share of transactions.
The push follows the Reserve Bank of India’s in-principle approval granted to IRCTC Payments to operate as a payment aggregator, enabling the company to build its own payments stack and reduce reliance on third-party processors.
Beyond ticketing, IRCTC is also targeting government-related transactions, including payments to public sector departments, utilities and treasuries. This diversification is expected to position the company as a key payments platform for government clients, leveraging its extensive user base and integration with railway services.
The company already handles over 1.5 million ticket bookings daily, providing a strong foundation to scale its payments infrastructure. However, it continues to work with multiple payment partners even as it develops its in-house aggregation capabilities.
Industry participants note that while IRCTC’s scale and government linkage offer a competitive advantage, the payments arm faces stiff competition from established players such as BillDesk, Razorpay, and PhonePe. Pricing competitiveness, technology capability and execution will be critical factors in determining its success in the segment.
The move underscores IRCTC’s broader strategy to diversify revenue streams and deepen its presence in India’s rapidly expanding digital payments ecosystem, even as regulatory approvals and market competition remain key challenges.
