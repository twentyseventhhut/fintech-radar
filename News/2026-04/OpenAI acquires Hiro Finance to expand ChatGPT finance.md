OpenAI has acquired Hiro Finance , a 2023-founded AI personal finance startup, in what appears to be an acquihire. Hiro will cease operations on April 20 and delete all user data by May 13 , while its team, led by founder Ethan Bloch , joins OpenAI. Hiro built an AI-driven financial planning tool that asked users for salary, debts, savings, and expenses, then generated scenario simulations and included explicit arithmetic verification to improve numeric accuracy. The startup was backed by Ribbit Capital , General Catalyst , and Restive Ventures . Terms were not disclosed. The deal signals OpenAI is investing in deeper money-management capabilities for ChatGPT , but it raises operational questions about integration, regulatory exposure for financial advice, and how user financial data will be handled going forward.
 What happened
OpenAI has acquired Hiro Finance , a small AI personal finance startup founded in 2023 , and will bring Hiro employees onto its team. Hiro announced it will shut down its consumer app on April 20 and delete stored user data by May 13 . Financial terms were not disclosed, and the transaction is widely treated as an acquihire because Hiro never disclosed funding totals and its team is small, roughly 10 people by public listings.
 Technical details
 Hiro Finance built an AI financial planning product that ingested user inputs like salary, debts, savings, and monthly expenses to generate what-if scenario simulations and plans. The system emphasized numeric rigor, offering a user-facing verification option to validate computed cash flows and amortizations. That focus matters because large language models have historically struggled with reliable arithmetic and deterministic financial calculations, even as frontier models have improved. Hiro was backed by Ribbit Capital , General Catalyst , and Restive Ventures , and its founder Ethan Bloch previously founded Digit , which exited for over $200M .
 • Hiro's capabilities: scenario simulation, debt and savings modeling, financial math verification, user-driven what-if planning 
 • Implementation signals: likely combination of specialized fine-tuning, deterministic calculation modules, and UX for verification 
 • Transition timeline: app shutdown April 20 , data deletion May 13 , staff moving to OpenAI 
 Context and significance
This acquisition fits a broader trend of LLM vendors embedding verticalized, high-trust functionality into conversational assistants. Adding a small team expert in financial modeling accelerates OpenAI's ability to ship ChatGPT features that must balance natural language fluency with deterministic math and regulatory constraints. Financial planning is not just a UX play, it demands auditability, clear provenance, reproducible calculations, and strong privacy controls. That creates engineering workstreams beyond model prompts: secure data handling, role-based access, explainability for computed recommendations, and legal review to avoid unauthorized financial advice.
Operationally, the deal is low-cost and high-velocity. An acquihire of a compact team shortens the time to production by importing domain knowledge and product patterns for numerical verification. It also raises near-term integration questions: will these capabilities ship as a ChatGPT core feature, a paid professional tier, or as a sandboxed plugin style integration? There is also competitive context: other major AI providers are similarly exploring finance-focused assistants, so product differentiation will depend on trust, accuracy, and compliant workflows more than raw conversational polish.
 What to watch
Track how OpenAI implements numeric verification into ChatGPT , whether the company surfaces clear disclaimers and compliance guardrails, and if any of Hiro's engineers publish design notes on integrating deterministic calculation modules into LLM-driven flows. Also watch regulatory and partner responses, especially from banks and fintech platforms that may need to connect user accounts securely before any meaningful money management features are viable.
Scoring Rationale
This is a notable acquihire that meaningfully accelerates OpenAI's capability in a regulated vertical, but the target is small and terms are undisclosed. Impact is tactical rather than paradigm shifting.

_This is an as-told-to conversation with__Ethan Bloch__, the founder and CEO of Digit. He has just launched his 15th project, a financial planning platform called Hiro. It has been condensed and edited for clarity._

I didn't even hear the word or know what entrepreneurship meant until I went to college, but I started my first real business at the age of 13.

You could call it a project.

I imported electronics from China, specifically PlayStation controllers and memory cards. I created a website called Blochardware, after my last name. In 1998, it was an early e-commerce store, before people commonly paid online with credit cards, and actually, it was a real pain.

We sold everything on a cash-on-delivery basis, which meant a mail carrier would show up at a customer's house with the product, collect a money order, and then mail the money order back to me.

Even before that, though, I was that kid who thought finance software was fun. At 10 years old, I was playing with Quicken for entertainment and writing pretend checks.

## The entrepreneurial bug

My dad is an immigrant from the Soviet Union. He came to America in the '70s. My mom's family has been here longer, also from Eastern Europe. My dad was an entrepreneur. He ran a secondhand clothing business, and my mom later started her own company. That was just the way things were in our household.

Growing up around that kind of independence had an influence on me.

I've always had this innate problem — or maybe gift — that I don't like being told what to do. I want to be the one telling people what to do. That made me a real pain as a student, but when I started businesses and projects, it was like the world opened up. I could create whatever I wanted and help people however I wanted. It was a natural fit.

Hiro is my 15th endeavor. I don't want to say dream, but it's been an idea I wanted to materialize for years.

## What is Hiro?

In Orson Scott Card's science fiction series, "Ender's Game," there's a character named Jane — an AI — who helps the main character, Ender, file his taxes. Ender is someone who's saved the world, but he can't figure out how to do something as mundane as that, and Jane helps him.

When I first read the series, I thought, "Everyone should have a Jane."

That was years and years ago, but the idea stuck with me. I didn't know how it would happen.

My previous company, Digit, was an attempt at making that happen. We built it to create a financial guide that would give everyone access to the kind of help usually reserved for the wealthy. What we ultimately unlocked was automated savings — and it worked. We helped millions of people save billions of dollars, but the conversational, guiding element we envisioned just wasn't possible yet.

And then ChatGPT came out.

This kind of technology had never existed before, and suddenly it could do things that seemed almost magical. The pairing of a large language model — which happens to be exceptionally good at financial math — with a rigorous, verifiable financial planning engine can unlock truly sophisticated financial planning for anyone who wants to think seriously about their financial future.

Hiro is geared toward the mass affluent customer, who is earning somewhere in the top 30% of their geographical area.

When a user signs up, they go through a short conversation that builds their financial plan. You can test scenarios — like, "What if my income changes?" — and Hiro instantly shows you how that affects your net worth and financial independence timeline. You can open the math, see exactly what's happening, and verify it.

Under the surface, Hiro has a full financial-planning and tax engine. You can dig into the details if you want, but most people just talk to it — ask questions, try different scenarios, and explore. We've also added starter prompts for things like financial independence, life changes, and home buying.

## Trying and failing and trying again are key to success

When you're an entrepreneur, the most important way to win is to get in the reps. The notion that this is going to be the one, especially when it's your first one, is not the type of pressure you should put on yourself.

Do things because you like to, because you're interested, because you're curious. My first 13 companies all went to zero. Then I sold Flowtown for $4.5 million. And I just sold Digit for over $230 million.
