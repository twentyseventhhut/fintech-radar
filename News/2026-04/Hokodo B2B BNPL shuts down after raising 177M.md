London-founded B2B payments startup Hokodo has shut down. After eight years, €500 million in financed invoices, over 100,000 business buyers served across ten countries, and more than €50 million raised in equity, the company processed its final transaction in November 2025. A skeleton crew stayed on until March 2026 to handle collections and wind things down. The doors are now closed.

Richard Thornton, one of Hokodo's co-founders, put it plainly on LinkedIn: _"Closing Hokodo, a company we spent 8 years building, was definitely one of the toughest things I've ever had to do."_

Hokodo was built to solve something real. When two businesses trade with each other, the seller usually has to wait, sometimes 30, 60, or 90 days, before getting paid. That's trade credit, and it's been broken for decades, particularly for small and mid-sized businesses that don't have the balance sheet to absorb the wait. Hokodo's answer was B2B buy now, pay later, the merchant gets paid upfront, the buyer gets flexible payment terms, and Hokodo takes on the credit risk in between. It was essentially what Klarna does for consumers, rebuilt from scratch for business-to-business trade.

Founded in London in 2018 by Louis Carbonnier, Richard Thornton, and Sami Ben Hatit, the company raised approximately $177 million across equity and debt over its lifetime. Backers included Anthemis Group, Notion Capital, Korelya Capital, and Mosaic Ventures. BNP Paribas and Citi were among its commercial partners. It became the first B2B BNPL provider in Europe to hold a full EU payments licence. By any early measure, it looked like the kind of UK fintech success story worth watching.

So what went wrong? Hokodo said it themselves. In their official post-mortem published at hokodo.co, the company acknowledged taking too long to narrow their focus, scaling before they'd truly earned it, and building too much product complexity along the way. They tried to serve everyone, small marketplaces, large enterprise merchants, multiple geographies, and the product sprawl slowed them down at the precise moment the funding environment stopped being forgiving.

That moment matters. Hokodo raised its Series B in June 2022, right as interest rates began climbing sharply and VC appetite for fintech cooled just as quickly. The timing was brutal. UK fintech funding fell 54% between 2023 and 2024, from $15.3 billion to $7 billion, according to data from Fintech Global. Deal volume dropped 61% in the same period. By 2025, KPMG's Pulse of Fintech report recorded a further 21% decline in UK fintech investment, bringing it to roughly $10.96 billion, the lowest level since 2020. All of this while global fintech funding grew 21% over the same year. The UK was moving in the opposite direction from the rest of the world.

For everyday people, the Hokodo news might feel distant, a fintech startup shutting down doesn't land the same way as a high street bank branch closing. But the ripple matters. When startups that are building the financial infrastructure for small businesses disappear, those small businesses often go back to slower, more expensive, more manual ways of managing cash flow. The suppliers, retailers, and independent traders that used Hokodo's payment terms to buy stock and sell faster now need to find alternatives or revert to chasing invoices. The knock-on is quiet but real.

It also lands in the context of a uk tech sector that recently crossed the one trillion dollar valuation mark, a significant milestone that makes the loss of promising infrastructure-layer companies like Hokodo harder to ignore. The headline number is impressive; the finer detail tells a more complicated story about which parts of that ecosystem are actually getting the oxygen they need to survive.

There's also an AI subplot here that Hokodo themselves flagged, and it's worth paying attention to. In their post-mortem, they wrote that the advantages a specialist trade credit provider once held, automated credit decisioning, fraud detection, agentic collections, can now be built inside enterprises directly, using AI, at a fraction of the historical cost. The moat that once justified a standalone fintech in this space is narrowing fast. That doesn't mean the problem Hokodo was solving has gone away; it means the shape of who solves it is changing.

The founders aren't stepping away from the space. They've launched Liquidity Lab, a consultancy using AI to help B2B companies modernise their accounts receivable and payable operations. Co-founder Louis Carbonnier described it without softening: _"Liquidity Lab is not a pivot or a consolation prize. It's the logical conclusion of everything we learned about where the opportunity in B2B trade credit actually lies."_

That kind of clarity, from founders who had real traction, real revenue, and real partnerships, and still couldn't make it work at scale, is arguably more useful to the UK tech ecosystem than another funding announcement. The lesson from Hokodo isn't that B2B fintech is a bad bet. It's that timing, focus, and the cost of capital all bite harder than most founders plan for, and in a UK funding environment that has contracted significantly over the past two years, those variables are less forgiving than ever.

What needs to be watched now is whether Hokodo's exit leaves a gap that a UK-based competitor fills, or whether the trade credit infrastructure play quietly consolidates around a handful of European or US-backed players with deeper pockets. Either outcome tells you something about where uk tech is actually heading beneath the trillion-dollar headline.
