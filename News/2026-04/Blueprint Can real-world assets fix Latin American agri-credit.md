Can Real World Assets Actually Fix Agricultural Credit in Latin America?
In Latam, credit is scarce. Assets, not so much.
I’ve been following rural finance in Latin America for a while now, and one thing that never stops frustrating me is how the same problem keeps reappearing decade after decade. We have land. We have production. We have farmers who know exactly what they are doing. What we consistently fail to have is credit that works for them, on terms that are actually fair, and without requiring them to jump through fifteen bureaucratic hoops first.
So when real world assets started showing up in the conversation, I paid attention. Not because I think technology solves everything — I really don’t — but because the core idea is sound. There are real assets sitting in silos, in warehouses, in invoices, in contracts, and they are not doing as much work as they could in the credit system. If you can represent those assets in a cleaner, more verifiable way, lenders get more comfortable and producers get more access. That’s the thesis, and it is not crazy.
The question worth asking is not whether tokenization sounds interesting on a slide. It is whether a soybean farmer in Argentina, a cattle producer in Brazil, or a wheat grower in Uruguay can actually borrow faster and cheaper because of it. The honest answer is: yes, in some cases, under the right conditions, if the plumbing gets built properly. That is a qualified yes, not an enthusiastic one, and I think that distinction matters.
What even is a real world asset? 
A real world asset, or RWA, is something with value in the real economy that gets represented digitally, so it can move more easily through financial systems. That’s it. The asset is the real thing — grain in a silo, an invoice someone owes you, a future harvest, a warehouse receipt. The digital representation is a kind of secure title, a way of saying: this value exists, here is the proof, and here is how to use it.
The agricultural angle is obvious once you think about it. Farming in this region is full of asset-rich, cash-poor situations. A producer might have six thousand tons of soy sitting in storage and still struggle to pay for next season’s inputs. Why? Because turning that grain into working capital through traditional banking is slow, paperwork-heavy, and often just not available at the scale they need. If the grain could function more directly as collateral or even as a means of payment, that’s real value for real people.
The credit gap is old news, but the tools are new 
The traditional rural credit model in Latin America was built for a world that suits banks, not farmers. Banks want large clients, formal documentation, standardized collateral, and predictable legal enforcement. Agricultural lending, by its nature, offers none of those things cleanly. Seasonal cash flows, weather risk, patchwork land registries, informal relationships — these are not edge cases in the sector. They are the norm.
So a lot of the real credit infrastructure ended up being built informally, through input suppliers, cooperatives, grain traders, and commodity buyers who effectively lend through their commercial relationships. That system works, up to a point. But it also concentrates power in the hands of a few intermediaries and leaves producers with limited negotiating room.
RWAs matter because they can change that equation by making agricultural assets more legible to a wider set of lenders. If grain, contracts, and receivables are digitally documented and verifiable, a regional bank in Montevideo or a foreign investor in Miami can evaluate them without having a local agent kick the tires on-site. Lower information costs mean more capital is willing to show up, and more competition usually means better terms for producers.
The best current example in the region is Agrotoken in Argentina. By April 2024 they had processed around 70 million dollars in transactions, tokenized 230,000 tons of grain, brought more than a thousand farmers into the system, and built a merchant network of over 250 participants. That is not a pilot. That is a market. And their Visa-linked model — where grain-backed digital assets can be spent directly on inputs — shows what the end product actually needs to look like for farmers: not a crypto experiment, but a practical treasury tool.
The user experience problem is just as real as the technical one 
Here is something I think gets underestimated in most of these discussions. Mass adoption in agricultural finance will not happen because farmers suddenly become interested in blockchain. It will happen when the technology becomes invisible and the product just solves a problem they already have.
Think about what a producer actually wants. Liquidity before harvest. A way to finance inputs without selling grain too early and missing a price move. Fewer trips to the bank. A payment mechanism that works the week they need it, not three weeks later after the paperwork clears. The products that will actually scale are the ones that deliver those things, whether or not the user ever hears the word tokenization.
In practical terms, that means adoption will probably flow through intermediaries that producers already trust: cooperatives, input distributors, agricultural software platforms, rural fintech apps. In Uruguay, for instance, a cooperative in Canelones or a livestock exchange in Tacuarembó is going to be a more credible distribution channel for a new financial product than any purely crypto-native startup. Tokenization spreads through existing trust, not around it.
The incumbent reaction matters 
Banks, cooperatives, grain traders, and rural lenders are not going to sit still. Their response will be some combination of resistance, quiet adaptation, and selective partnership. I’ve watched enough fintech waves to know that the disruption narrative almost never plays out cleanly. Remember fifteen years ago when people said fintech was going to kill the banks? Banks are still here. What changed is that they absorbed some of what worked and ignored what didn’t.
Some incumbents will feel threatened, which is fair. If a producer can use tokenized grain or receivables to get financing outside the traditional chain, incumbent lenders lose origination flow and client dependency. That’s especially significant in markets where commodity traders effectively act as lenders through commercial arrangements.
 But incumbents also have real incentives to engage. Better collateral visibility, cleaner audit trails, real-time pricing data — these are things that improve underwriting for anyone who cares about making good credit decisions. A large bank does not need to reject RWA infrastructure. It needs it to be compliant, structured, and legible enough to plug into existing risk systems. The most likely future is not disruption but hybridization: part of the market stays traditional, part becomes token-enabled, and the most successful models are the ones that connect both. 
For policymakers to clearly understand 
This is not a crypto story. I want to be direct about that because how regulators frame the question shapes everything that follows. If you approach RWAs as a crypto problem, you will regulate them like one: with suspicion, slow timelines, and rules borrowed from consumer speculation. If you approach them as a market design question — how do we make more agricultural assets financeable without breaking anything — you will regulate them very differently.
A workable framework needs at least five things:
 Clear legal recognition. A token is only worth something if the legal claim behind it can be enforced in court. 
 Registration and supervision of platforms. Issuance, custody, and collateralization need to sit inside a monitored perimeter. 
 Disclosure rules. Borrowers and investors need clarity on pricing, liquidation, risks, and what happens when something goes wrong. 
 Digital public infrastructure. Land registries, movable-asset registries, identity systems, and payment rails matter more than any marketing campaign. 
 Proportional regulation. A startup tokenizing receivables is not a full-service bank and should not be treated like one. 
 The risk of getting this wrong cuts both ways. Too loose and the market fills up with poor-quality issuance that burns early users and sets the whole sector back years. Too rigid and only incumbents can participate, which defeats most of the inclusion argument. Risk management is not just a compliance checkbox here. It is the foundation that makes everything else trustworthy. 
Again, risk management is one of the key pillars of adoption, and on top of the above 5 criteria regulators need to understand that risk is a benchmark measure. Therefore, trusted and safe innovation should follow the benchmark system to compare different type of assets, from different markets, not only to price at risk, but also to protect final user and financial stability.
Where things actually stand in the region 
Argentina 
Argentina is the clearest reference point for agricultural RWA experimentation in the region, and honestly it makes sense. When your currency is in perpetual crisis and your banking system creates frictions at every turn, producers get creative about how they store and deploy value. Tokenized grain is not just a financing tool in Argentina — for some producers it is also a balance-sheet strategy.
Agrotoken remains the flagship case, with 70 million dollars in transactions, 230,000 tokenized tons, and real merchant infrastructure. Matba Rofex has added grain indices linked to Rosario prices in real time, seven days a week, which matters a lot because any serious financial product built on agricultural commodities needs trusted pricing references. That is not just technical progress. That is market infrastructure.
On the regulatory side, Law 27,739 from March 2024 created the legal basis for registering and supervising virtual asset service providers under the CNV. In April 2026, the CNV’s Resolution 1125/2026 went further, allowing tokenized assets to count toward the net-worth thresholds for qualified investor classification. Argentina still has a long road to full institutional integration, but the direction is clear.
Brazil 
Brazil is probably the most strategically important market in the region for tokenized agricultural finance, and not just because of its size. The country has a functioning capital market, a sophisticated regulatory apparatus in the CVM, and a central bank that takes digital infrastructure seriously. If this is going to scale institutionally, Brazil is where the template gets built.
The CVM has placed tokenization at the center of its 2026 regulatory agenda, and reports from CVM shows crowdfunding token issuances grew 500 percent between 2023 and 2024, and the broader RWA market expanded even more dramatically — RWA Monitor reported over 1,100 percent growth in the twelve months to January 2026-. Nagro, an agrifinance platform focused on intelligent credit for agribusiness, raised 9.5 million dollars in January 2026, which signals that investors see real commercial potential in data-rich agricultural underwriting.
The Drex story is worth watching carefully. The central bank’s digital money initiative was apparently redesigned away from a blockchain-native architecture toward a more centralized model integrated with Pix, with a focus on collateral management. Whatever the technology ends up looking like, the direction — a national digital infrastructure for asset circulation and settlement — is the right one for agricultural finance.
Mexico 
Mexico is growing faster than its regulatory framework can keep up with, which creates both opportunity and real risk. Tokenized assets in the country were estimated around 400 million dollars across roughly 30 projects by early 2026, spread across real estate, fintech, logistics, and other areas. Agricultural applications are still early-stage within that broader market.
The main bottleneck is institutional. Multiple reports from 2026 describe regulatory uncertainty as the primary barrier to institutional entry, with nearly half of surveyed participants naming it the top concern. Mexico has commercial momentum but not yet the legal scaffolding that allows conservative capital to show up at scale. That gap can close, but it requires political will and some patience.
Colombia 
Colombia’s most realistic near-term opportunity is not mass consumer tokenization of farm assets but something more structural: using digital documentation and verifiable data to improve traceability, export financing, and supply-chain verification. In coffee, cacao, and other export commodities, being able to prove origin and quality in a legible, auditable way matters both for credit decisions and for premium market access.
Regulatory development is lagging behind Brazil and Argentina, and the institutional framework for tokenized assets is still taking shape. Colombia is a market to watch rather than a market already in motion. The agricultural export angle gives it a credible entry point, but it will take time.
Chile 
Chile probably will not lead in volume, but it could lead in quality. In higher-value export agriculture — wine, fruit, salmon — traceability and compliance are already central concerns. Tokenized structures that support sustainability-linked financing or proof-of-origin documentation fit naturally into that context. The CMF is working toward more specific regulatory treatment for tokenized assets, and the market remains cautious but open to experimentation.
Peru 
Peru is earlier-stage but has the agricultural export profile to eventually benefit from better documentation and receivables financing. The regulatory environment still faces significant uncertainty around tokenized assets, which delays institutional adoption. It is a plausible second-wave market if the infrastructure develops.
