FICO Shares Plummet Over 13% as Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac Accept Alternative Credit Scores
 Shares of Fair Isaac Corporation , known as FICO, fell more than 13% on Wednesday following an announcement by two federal agencies that Fannie Mae , Freddie Mac , and the Federal Housing Administration will begin accepting alternative credit scores for mortgage loans. The source of this information is a report from Yahoo Finance. 
The policy change, which had been anticipated for some time, aims to reduce FICO's longstanding dominance in credit scoring for mortgage underwriting, which officials hope will lower costs for consumers. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac back approximately 70% of the U.S. mortgage market. Starting immediately, they will accept the VantageScore 4.0 model as an alternative to FICO, as well as an updated FICO scoring model called the 10T. Federal Housing Finance Agency Director Bill Pulte and Housing and Urban Development Secretary Bill Turner made the announcement on Wednesday. HUD, which insures FHA loans, will accept the new scores soon.
FHA loans are commonly used by first-time homebuyers and are government-backed, generally requiring lower credit scores and smaller down payments compared to conventional loans. Credit scores are a critical factor in determining mortgage eligibility and interest rates. A minimum score of around 620 is typically needed for a conventional mortgage, while a score of 750 or higher is often required to obtain the lowest rates.
Both FICO and VantageScore 4.0 incorporate alternative data, such as rental payment history, to help assess creditworthiness. Pulte has repeatedly criticized FICO and the rising cost of credit pulls over recent years. FICO shares have declined nearly 50% year-to-date, making them the worst performer in the S&P 500 .
