# ClearBank founder Andrew Smith is building "the home of UK sports finance" with new banking venture Sporta

Sporta is preparing to unveil its MVP this year, with the company expecting to secure banking licence approval in Q3 2026.

[](https://www.fintechfutures.com/author/tyler-pathe)

Tyler Pathe,Reporter, FinTech Futures
January 28, 2026

schedule 3 Min Read

[](https://www.linkedin.com/sharing/share-offsite/?url=https://www.fintechfutures.com/fintech-start-ups/clearbank-founder-builds-new-banking-venture-sporta)[](http://www.facebook.com/sharer/sharer.php?u=https://www.fintechfutures.com/fintech-start-ups/clearbank-founder-builds-new-banking-venture-sporta)[](http://www.twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=https://www.fintechfutures.com/fintech-start-ups/clearbank-founder-builds-new-banking-venture-sporta)[](https://www.reddit.com/submit?url=https://www.fintechfutures.com/fintech-start-ups/clearbank-founder-builds-new-banking-venture-sporta&title=ClearBank%20founder%20Andrew%20Smith%20is%20building%20%22the%20home%20of%20UK%20sports%20finance%22%20with%20new%20banking%20venture%20Sporta)[](https://bsky.app/intent/compose?text=ClearBank%20founder%20Andrew%20Smith%20is%20building%20%22the%20home%20of%20UK%20sports%20finance%22%20with%20new%20banking%20venture%20Sporta%20-%20https%3A%2F%2Fwww.fintechfutures.com%2Ffintech-start-ups%2Fclearbank-founder-builds-new-banking-venture-sporta)

Former ClearBank technology chief and founder Andrew Smith is working on a new venture called Sporta, which he describes to FinTech Futures as "the dedicated financial infrastructure that sport has never had".

According to a WEF report presented at Davos just days ago, the sports economy is enormous, and it's growing fast. In 2025, the sector was valued at $2.3 trillion, and is forecast to reach $3.7 trillion by 2030.

Smith says the inspiration for Sporta came from his own experience at his local cricket club, where he was asked to become a benefactor. "Sport has been a big part of my life since I was a child. It’s where I learned discipline, resilience, and a sense of belonging," he explains.

However, Sporta writes on its LinkedIn page that the financial sustainability of sport "is under serious pressure".

"Clubs are struggling. Communities are losing vital infrastructure and access to digitally-enabled banking that can help them thrive, not just survive. Young and old alike are denied opportunities to be more active, healthy, and inspired," the company writes.

Related:Pitch perfect part four: Visualise it

This led Smith to explore the broader financial challenges faced by sports organisations in the UK. “Only 20% of clubs end the year with a surplus, which means the majority are under financial pressure,” he says.

Rather than making a one-off donation to his local club, Smith instead decided to create a new company to address the underlying issues affecting sports organisations nationwide, offering "something that has never existed before".

Smith says that Sporta will provide "dedicated financial infrastructure for sport". The company is currently in pursuit of a banking licence and plans to extend its focus beyond just traditional banking services.

“Because we’re AI‑native from the ground up, we can personalise products and services in a way others simply can’t,” Smith explains, while highlighting the potential to reduce costs and create new income opportunities for clubs, such as sponsorships or loans.

The venture also aims to connect the wider sports community, which includes clubs, governing bodies, businesses, and individuals involved in sports and leisure. Smith points out that 43 million people in the UK participate in organised sports each month, supported by 151,000 clubs and nearly one million businesses. “This is one of the largest, most passionate, most economically significant communities in the country,” he says, brought together through Sporta as "the home of UK sports finance".

To fund its journey, Sporta has completed two rounds of funding to date, including an initial seed round in May 2025, followed by a Crowdcube campaign closed at £122,923 in December. Funds are currently being used to deliver a minimum viable product (MVP) this year, ahead of licence approval expected in Q3.

Related:Whistle launches embedded financial advice platform to close UK advice gap

"Without revealing too much, we have several non‑banking products launching in the first half of the year," Smith says, stating that each product will aim to provide "immediate, meaningful impact for sports clubs, governing bodies, and retail customers".

He continues: "It’s a high bar, and rightly so. As a founder of ClearBank with Nick Ogden, and going through that building and licensing journey, I know how demanding the process is, and that’s why we’re so prepared. We have a great team, and we’re progressing every day."

Aside from ClearBank, founded in 2015, earlier ventures involving Smith include enterprise content management (ECM) solution WorkFile, mobile technology companies CloudZync and Tapsley, and cross-border payment fintech RTGS Group.

"We have good momentum and the opportunity ahead is enormous, for sport, for the UK economy, for clubs, and for every individual who chooses to be part of this Sporta movement," Smith concludes.

# Sporta raises £123k via Crowdcube to build AI-native sports bank

##### By Vriti Gothi

December 15, 2025

*   AI
*   Crowdcube
*   Digital Banking

##### Share

LinkedInFacebookTwitterEmailWhatsApp

Sporta, an AI-native challenger bank targeting the sport, leisure and wellbeing sectors, has closed a crowdfunding round on Crowdcube, raising £122,923 from community investors.

The raise forms part of Sporta’s second seed round and was structured to allow individuals, clubs and early supporters to invest at smaller ticket sizes, extending participation beyond traditional angel and institutional investors. According to the company, many contributors were drawn from stakeholders engaged during its early market research phase.

Sporta is a purpose-built bank for the UK’s sports ecosystem, which it estimates as a £100 billion economy encompassing around 43 million participants, but one that lacks dedicated financial infrastructure. The company describes its approach as AI-native, with technology designed from inception to support the financial needs of organisations and individuals across sport, leisure and wellbeing.

Management framed the crowdfunding round less as a capital-raising exercise and more as a mechanism to build trust and community ownership ahead of its regulatory journey. Sporta completed its initial seed round in May 2025 and plans to submit its UK banking licence application and develop its minimum viable product during 2026, with a target for licence approval in the third quarter of that year.

“This round was never about scale, it was about inclusion,” said**Andrew Smith, founder of Sporta.** “From day one, people across sport told us they wanted to be part of what we’re building, even if they couldn’t meet traditional investment thresholds. Crowdcube let us honour that.”

Sporta said its valuation has increased by 75% since the initial seed close, citing progress in proprietary technology development, senior hiring and regulatory planning. Smith added that the company’s immediate priorities remain securing a banking licence, launching its first products and continuing to build its technology stack.

Looking ahead, Sporta expects to return to crowdfunding as part of a planned Series A round, once a banking licence has been secured. The company said future community participation would be subject to restrictions and aligned with a broader strategy of balancing institutional funding with accessible retail investment — a model previously adopted by several UK FinTech challengers in their early stages.
