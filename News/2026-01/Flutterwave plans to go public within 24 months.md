Flutterwave Goes From A Butterfly to A Rocket: Going public in the next 24 months.
Flutterwave is refocusing on its core business of helping businesses collect and pay in Africa, They got into remittances to accelerate profitability not to beat WorldRemit.
 Introduction . 
 It’s Oct 2025 and I’m writing this article from a Cafe near my house, I’m opening this article like that because I may publish this sometimes in 2026, A lot may have changed including new products and new acquisitions like this one w Mono. 
 Flutterwave is at a pivotal moment. Reading its memos, LinkedIn posts, annual and half-year reports , and news coverage, one thing is clear: this is a startup not chasing hype, but meticulously building the foundation for a potential IPO. 
 “When you see a private company starting to do annual reports, you know they are going public in 24 months.” 
Here’s what I’m learning, and what I’m guessing, about the company’s strategy and direction.
 1. Annual and Half-Year Reports: IPO Signals 
Flutterwave’s reports are particularly telling. Unlike the short, high-level letters from companies like Stripe, these documents are detailed, rich in insights, and strategic. They reveal:
 What the company prioritizes internally 
 Which metrics and strategies it wants outsiders to notice 
 How it positions itself for investors and the public markets 
In short, Flutterwave’s reporting is both a roadmap for internal preparation and a signal to the market about its IPO ambitions.
 2. The Butterfly Flies to China and India 
During the pandemic, Flutterwave focused on SMEs, helping small businesses navigate digital payments. That chapter is closing. Today, the butterfly is exploring the Asian corridor, targeting markets where remittance flows are massive.
In just six months, Flutterwave processed over $1 billion, signaling that the platform is scaling rapidly in cross-border markets.
 3. What about SMEs? By Now They Might As Well Not Exist. 
The pandemic-era SME focus is now gone. Flutterwave is pivoting away from unprofitable customers and consumer products, instead doubling down on high-volume, high-margin enterprise clients.
 Flutterwave, which has secured investment from Visa and Tiger Global has built its success on processing payments for clients such as Uber and MSFT not SMEs . 
 4. Barter Is Forgotten 
Flutterwave has shut down Barter, its consumer-facing product. This move is part of a broader strategy to prune non-core, unprofitable products, keeping operations lean and margins strong.
 Earlier in 2024, Flutterwave discontinued its most famous consumer product, Barter, to focus on its more profitable enterprise and remittance services. The decision was preceded by evidence that Barter was not scaling beyond Nigeria , I agree w that 100%. 
 5. Sendy: The FX Engine 
 Flutterwave’s Send product is central to its strategy—but not for the obvious reasons. While it looks like a consumer remittance tool, its real purpose is margin magic. By collecting payments in local currencies and converting them to dollars and pounds, Send boosts FX flows, making the core business of enterprise payments more profitable—quickly. I guess this is why the underlying theme is “profitability is in sight” as they doubled their margins: 
Even though Send was discontinued in some markets for much of the past year, it’s positioned as a growth engine for sustainable margins, rather than just top-line volume.
 6. The Dream Team’s Hidden Purpose 
In a 2023 reflection, GB highlighted the “dream team” of Wall Street hires brought in 2022:
 “The CEO said: The year, 2022 was special because we had the privilege of adding more world-class leaders to our team to further our ambition. 
According to LinkedIn and other sources, all of these executives have since left and for good reason, I would guess they finished what they were hired for—- You ask, what job!
 The story isn’t about departures, it’s about purpose. The bench was a diagnostic tool: to see if Flutterwave was IPO-ready, identify gaps, and determine what needed to go—including themselves. The team delivered their assessment quickly, leaving a leaner, IPO-ready operation focused on profitability and execution. 
 7. Licenses and Compliance: The Hidden Moat 
With approvals in Ghana, Cameroon, Senegal and Zambia, The buterfly is strengthening its regulatory standing across the continent. 
 At that size and stature, You must secure licences and regulatory approvals, because to grow right, you must first earn the right to grow. 
GB, the CEO of Flutter, knows better than most of us that expansion in Africa is hard, licenses take time, and compliance is expensive but non-negotiable. Every license and compliance process is more than regulatory work, it’s a strategic moat, giving Flutterwave an edge in cross-border payments and protecting its global ambitions.
Scaling Fintech in Africa: Why Compliance is the Ultimate Competitive Advantage.
 8. The Butterfly’s Strategic Pivot, Let us say “Sticking to the original plan”. 
 In early days, Uber was one of the most important customers, in line with that Flutterwave is clearly refocusing on its core business: Sticking to the original plan of helping global businesses collect and pay in Africa. 
 The Send product accelerates profitability, while the company prunes unprofitable products and customers. Flutterwave isn’t chasing top-line growth blindly—it’s optimising for sustainable margins, particularly through FX flows. 
Contrast this with NALA, which started in remittances and is now expanding into enterprise payments and collections to scale quickly. NALA is focused on rapid expansion and high-volume acquisition, whereas Flutterwave, with already high-volume enterprise clients, is focused on margin sustainability.
 9. Competitors Are Racing but Not Public-Market Ready 
Flutterwave isn’t the only player in cross-border payments and enterprise collections, Competitors like PawaPay, Payaza, and Fincra are scaling fast and aggressively targeting similar sub-segments but not for IPOs or Acquisitions.
The difference? They’re growing for growth’s sake, at least that what we know publicly. They don’t appear to be preparing for an IPO or being acquired by a larger player, focusing instead on top-line expansion and market share.
Flutterwave, by contrast, is taking a more measured approach: pruning unprofitable products, tightening margins, and building a compliance and licensing moat—all signals that it’s thinking about long-term, public-market success in the near term.
 10. From A Butterfly to A Rocket 
Flutterwave’s journey has been like watching a butterfly take flight—nimble, strategic, exploring fertile corridors, and shedding what slows it down. Now, all signs point to a rocket in the making:
 Leaner operations and margin-focused strategy 
 FX-driven growth via Send 
 Enterprise clients and compliance moats ready for global scale 
 IPO preparation signaling a launch into public markets 
The butterfly is no longer just fluttering—it’s accelerating toward lift-off, aiming for a trajectory that could redefine African fintech on the world stage.
After Product-Market Fit (PMF): When to Go Deep and When to Go Wide in Africa. learning from Selcom, Nala & Flutterwave.
 What It All Means 
Flutterwave is:
 Pivoting from SME services to cross-border payments and remittances 
 Closing non-core products like Barter to focus into the core enterprise segment 
 Using Send to drive FX revenue margins and accelerate profitability 
 Leveraging a diagnostic executive bench to optimize operations for IPO readiness 
 Building a compliance and licensing moat 
 Focusing on sustainable margin growth rather than top-line scale alone 
In short, Flutterwave is creating a profitable, scalable, and investor-ready business—one that could withstand the scrutiny of public markets.
