Awin Group confirmed yesterday that PayPal's Honey browser extension violated publisher policies following a formal investigation into allegations of systematic affiliate commission diversion. The affiliate network suspended Honey's payments and access to new advertiser programs on January 21, 2026, according to a statement from CEO Adam Ross.
The investigation, launched immediately after public allegations emerged in December 2024, verified that Honey's browser extension practices breached Awin's publisher policies. Honey acknowledged the impact and agreed to work with Awin on remediation initiatives while implementing initial updates to address identified behavior, according to the statement published on Awin's website.
"Transparency and trust matter, especially when our industry is under scrutiny," Ross wrote in a LinkedIn post announcing the enforcement action. The statement marks the first major affiliate network response confirming specific policy violations following revelations about  Honey's commission diversion practices  exposed by tech journalist MegaLag on December 22, 2024.
Awin Group comprises Awin and Commission Factory, operating as one of the affiliate marketing industry's largest networks. The protective measures implemented include suspending payments to Honey and blocking the extension's access to new advertiser programs. "As of today, these measures remain in place," according to the statement.
The enforcement action follows similar decisions by other major affiliate networks.  Impact.com suspended Honey on January 16, 2026  for what researcher Ben Edelman characterized as "stand-down violations - and concealment from testers." Rakuten Advertising  terminated Honey from its network on January 12, 2026 , severing access to approximately 2,000 retail merchants including Walmart.
Awin's proprietary Soft Click technology has been active on Honey and most browser extensions throughout the period under investigation, according to the statement. This technology "materially reduces the risk of misattribution of publisher value (or commission) and limits direct harm to publishers and creators," the network explained. While noting that Soft Click "does not make the identified behavior acceptable," Awin stated the technology allowed the company to manage risk responsibly while establishing facts and determining appropriate remedies.
The technical mitigation represents a significant difference from other affiliate networks' exposure to Honey's practices. Soft Click functions as an attribution protection mechanism that appears to have limited commission diversion during the investigation period. The technology operates continuously across browser extensions and subnetwork publishers in Awin's ecosystem, providing baseline fraud prevention beyond standard compliance monitoring.
Honey has committed to funding and supporting several remediation initiatives as part of the path forward, according to Awin's statement. These include providing affiliate networks or mutually agreed third parties with access to relevant source code for verification purposes. The extension also agreed to implement policies enabling advertisers to manage coupon codes, including removal of unauthorized or invalid codes.
The browser extension committed to ongoing cooperation with networks and wider industry compliance teams, including advance notification of material changes to attribution or stand-down logic. Stand-down protocols represent standard practices where browser extensions temporarily halt commission diversion when detecting testing by affiliate networks or compliance monitors.
The  December 30, 2025 investigation  exposed what researchers characterized as a "selective standdown" system that allegedly adjusted Honey's behavior based on sophisticated user profiling. The system analyzed whether users were logged into affiliate network websites, specific cookies in browsers, email addresses containing "test," account age, cashback points accumulated, and login status to determine whether someone represented a legitimate shopper or compliance tester.
Awin requested that Honey finalize remediation measures and commit to meaningful support as soon as practically possible. The statement emphasized that strong enforcement coupled with constructive industry collaboration is essential to restoring trust and supporting long-term growth in the affiliate ecosystem.
Industry response has been varied. Benjamin Völk, a strategy and growth consultant, stated in response to Ross's LinkedIn announcement: "I'm disappointed by this response. What's being discussed here is not a grey area or a 'difference in interpretation', it is a credible allegation of fraud." Völk argued that partners have been removed from platforms for far less in the past, creating what he characterized as "an unavoidable impression: that compliance stops being uncompromising once enough money is involved."
The critique reflects broader frustrations within the affiliate marketing community about enforcement consistency. Content creators  filed a class action lawsuit  on December 29, 2024, seeking damages exceeding $5 million. The complaint alleges that Honey's browser extension replaced content creators' affiliate tracking cookies with PayPal's own affiliate identifiers during checkout processes, even when providing no discount codes to consumers.
A  second amended complaint filed January 5, 2026  expanded the lawsuit to ten named plaintiffs and included specific merchant contract terms documenting exact commission percentages and qualifying link definitions that Honey allegedly violated. The 101-page filing provides extensive technical documentation of how the extension allegedly used browser permissions to replace affiliate marketers' cookies with PayPal's own identifiers.
The affiliate marketing industry operates primarily on "last click attribution" models that credit the final touchpoint before purchase. Content creators and publishers argue that browser extensions exploit this system by inserting themselves as the last touchpoint during checkout, effectively erasing previous affiliate referrals regardless of whether they provide value to consumers through discounts or coupon codes.
Honey's business model centers on automatically finding and applying coupon codes at checkout while users shop online. The extension monetizes through affiliate partnerships, earning commissions when users complete purchases through retailer links. PayPal acquired Honey for $4 billion in 2019, before the alleged commission diversion practices came to public attention.
Chrome Web Store data shows  Honey declined from over 20 million users before the December 2024 investigation to 14 million users as of July 2025 . The user exodus represents over 6 million departures following revelations about systematic affiliate commission diversion and limited coupon code access.
The original investigation by MegaLag documented three mechanisms through which Honey allegedly diverted commissions: direct replacement of affiliate tracking cookies, incentivized diversion through the "Honey Gold" rewards program, and commission capture through interface elements. In one test case, Honey diverted a $35 commission on a NordVPN purchase while providing the user with 89 cents worth of Honey Gold rewards points.
When Linus Media Group, one of Honey's largest promotional partners, discovered these practices and requested changes, Honey declined to modify the behavior according to email communications reviewed in the investigation. LMG subsequently terminated their partnership after approximately 160 sponsored segments that garnered 194 million views.
The December investigation also revealed that  Honey targeted minors in advertising while leaking business codes . The extension added 146,000 online stores to its platform without their consent, representing over 80 percent of stores supported by the extension. This practice created what researchers characterized as economic coercion, where businesses faced ongoing revenue losses unless they agreed to partnership terms with PayPal.
For the digital marketing community, the Honey controversy highlights fundamental vulnerabilities in affiliate marketing attribution systems. The "last click attribution" model that enabled Honey's practices remains standard across most e-commerce platforms, creating potential for similar commission diversion schemes. Browser extensions have become increasingly important distribution channels for affiliate offers, providing convenience to consumers while generating substantial revenue through affiliate commissions and referral fees.
Marketing professionals must now consider browser extension interference when evaluating affiliate campaign performance and attribution accuracy. The technical mechanisms revealed through investigations demonstrate that extensions can manipulate attribution without detection by traditional compliance monitoring, particularly when selective standdown systems adjust behavior based on whether users represent legitimate shoppers or compliance testers.
Awin's statement emphasized that the company is "committed to enforcing its standards and using this moment to drive lasting improvements in transparency and accountability across the entire affiliate ecosystem." The network positioned the enforcement action as part of broader industry efforts to address systematic issues rather than focusing solely on one publisher.
The affiliate marketing industry faces ongoing challenges around transparency and attribution. Publishers often compete for the final click before purchase, sometimes through practices that advertisers view as interruptive or value-dilutive. Browser extensions in particular have faced scrutiny over their role in the customer journey, with questions about whether they truly influence purchase decisions or simply capture commissions by inserting affiliate links at the point of transaction.
Alternative affiliate networks exist, including CJ Affiliate and ShareASale, but replacing thousands of merchant relationships requires substantial business development effort. The loss of access to Impact.com, Rakuten Advertising, and potentially Awin represents a substantial operational disruption for Honey's business model, which depends on affiliate partnerships with major retail partners.
For Awin's retail partners and other publishers in the network, Honey's suspension could affect affiliate program performance in the short term. However, commission payments that previously went to Honey may be redistributed among other publishers in the network, including competing browser extensions, coupon sites, and cashback platforms.
Industry observers note that Awin's enforcement approach differs from other networks in its emphasis on remediation and collaboration alongside suspension. The commitment to working with Honey on source code verification, advertiser coupon management policies, and advance notification of technical changes suggests a framework for potential reinstatement if remediation measures meet network standards.
Whether this collaborative approach will satisfy critics who view the violations as fundamental fraud rather than technical policy breaches remains unclear. The debate reflects broader tensions in affiliate marketing between enforcement consistency, revenue considerations, and the practical challenges of managing large publisher networks with complex technical integrations.
The statement concludes by positioning strong enforcement and constructive industry collaboration as complementary rather than opposing approaches. "We believe strong enforcement, coupled with constructive industry collaboration, is essential to restoring trust and supporting long-term growth," Awin stated.

Impact.com Removes Honey Paypal, from Platform Following Attribution Investigation
 Network CEO, David Yovanno announced suspension of Honey browser extension due to violated platform policies as industry continues it's debate on attribution integrity 

Impact.com issued a statement and removed PayPal's Honey browser extension from its Discovery Marketplace as it temporarily suspended the affiliate account following what CEO David Yovanno described as a “thorough investigation” that uncovered policy violations related to attribution manipulation.
The action, announced via email to partners on January 16, marks the most significant enforcement response yet to mounting concerns about browser extension practices that have dominated industry conversations since a viral YouTube exposé in late December triggered widespread scrutiny of affiliate attribution integrity.
Platform Enforcement and Technical Safeguards
According to Yovanno's statement, Impact.com determined that Honey violated the network's universal stand-down requirements, policies designed to prevent attribution interference and ensure partners receive credit based on genuine contribution to transactions. The platform has temporarily suspended Honey's account “while we confirm they've made the necessary changes to comply with our terms and conditions.”
Beyond individual enforcement, Impact.com announced plans to release a software update that will “programmatically prevent the type of attribution manipulation we've uncovered during our investigation.” This technical intervention suggests the platform is moving beyond reactive enforcement toward systemic protections against similar practices.
The announcement comes as PayPal issued its own response to the controversy, with Hello Partner reporting that the payments giant defended Honey's practices while acknowledging recent removal from Rakuten Advertising's network. PayPal's statement attempted to clarify Honey's attribution methodology amid allegations that the extension manipulates last-click attribution by injecting tracking codes even when users haven't actively engaged with Honey's coupon offerings.
Industry Reckoning at Affiliate Summit West
The timing of Impact.com's announcement coincided with Affiliate Summit West in Las Vegas, where the Honey controversy dominated hallway conversations and formal sessions as Rakuten made the first move to suspend their partnership with this publisher. According to multiple attendees, affiliate networks used the gathering to signal stronger enforcement stances on attribution, publisher integrity and several platforms reportedly reviewing their policies on browser extensions and cookie-stuffing practices and monitoring stances.
Industry sources at the event indicated that networks are facing pressure from both brands and legitimate publishers who argue that attribution manipulation undermines the performance-based economics that differentiate affiliate marketing from other digital channels. Program managers expressed concern that tolerating questionable attribution practices erodes advertiser trust and threatens budget allocations.
The broader debate extends beyond Honey specifically to systemic questions about browser extension business models and whether platforms have adequate technical controls to prevent attribution interference. Several affiliate managers told colleagues at ASW that they're conducting audits of their publisher rosters to identify extensions employing similar practices.
What This Means for Affiliate Program Managers
This network action and best practice enforcement creates immediate operational considerations for brands running affiliate programs across multiple networks. Program managers should prioritise several tactical responses:
Conduct a comprehensive audit of browser extension partners across all affiliate networks. Review not just current performance data but attribution patterns for irregularities, particularly instances where extensions receive credit for transactions with no documented user interaction. Compare conversion rates and average order values for extension partners against your program baseline to identify potential attribution manipulation.
Verify that your network contracts include explicit stand-down provisions and attribution integrity requirements. Impact.com's universal stand-down policy prohibits partners from overwriting existing affiliate cookies, but enforcement varies across platforms. Program managers should confirm their networks maintain similar policies and demonstrate willingness to enforce them.
Consider implementing supplemental tracking verification beyond standard affiliate cookies. Some brands are exploring first-party tracking data, post-transaction surveys, or multi-touch attribution models that provide visibility into the customer journey beyond last-click mechanics. While these approaches add complexity, they offer protection against attribution manipulation that exploits cookie-based tracking limitations.
The incident also highlights the value of direct publisher relationships and diversified partnership strategies. Programs overly dependent on browser extensions face heightened risk from both attribution issues and potential platform enforcement actions. Building relationships with content publishers, loyalty platforms, and creator partnerships provides attribution diversity and reduces exposure to any single partner type.
Attribution Integrity as Competitive Advantage
Beyond immediate compliance concerns, this controversy presents an opportunity for affiliate programs to differentiate through transparent attribution practices and publisher quality standards. Brands that proactively communicate their commitment to fair attribution and enforce rigorous partner standards can attract high-quality publishers who've grown frustrated competing against questionable extension practices.
Several program managers at ASW indicated they're reviewing commission structures to ensure legitimate publishers driving genuine consumer value receive appropriate compensation relative to partners capturing transactions through attribution manipulation. This may involve implementing tiered commission structures that reward different partnership types based on documented consumer engagement.
The technical update Impact.com plans to release could establish new industry standards for programmatic attribution protection. Program managers should monitor whether other networks implement similar safeguards and consider platform capabilities around attribution integrity when evaluating network partnerships.
As networks signal stronger enforcement and technical protections, the competitive landscape for browser extensions will likely shift. Extensions demonstrating genuine consumer value through price discovery and shopping assistance may distinguish themselves from those primarily optimizing for attribution capture. Program managers should assess extension partners based on user engagement metrics and documented value delivery rather than solely transaction volume.
 Key Takeaways: 
Impact.com's removal of Honey and planned technical safeguards signal that major affiliate networks are moving from reactive enforcement to systemic attribution protection, creating new compliance best practices and setting hard line expectations for all partners to behave responsibly.
Program managers should immediately audit browser extension partners across all networks, reviewing attribution patterns for irregularities and verifying that network contracts include robust stand-down provisions with demonstrated enforcement.
This controversy accelerates the industry shift toward attribution transparency and publisher quality standards, presenting opportunities for programs to differentiate through rigorous partner vetting and diversified partnership strategies that reduce dependence on any single channel or partner type.
