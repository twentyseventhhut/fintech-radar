 Tools for Humanity (TFH) is a global technology company established to build for humans in the age of AI. Founded by Sam Altman and Alex Blania, It led the initial development of World Network and operates World App. 
 Q: How does Tools for Humanity (TFH) position itself in the Mexican and Central American market compared to other digital authenticity and humanity verification solutions? 
A: We are a benchmark in the proof of human field, leaving aside the traditional digital identity. Unlike traditional verification platforms that aim to confirm an individual's identity, the Worldprotocol, for which we build tools, is engineered to verify that a user is a unique human without needing to know who they are. During our verification process, we do not require any Personally Identifiable Information (PII). This approach positions our technology as a privacy-preserving substitute for systems like CAPTCHA rather than a competitor to Know Your Customer (KYC) services. Our primary objective is to enable anyone to prove their uniqueness and humanness online while maintaining full anonymity.
 Q: What key differentiators allow TFH to provide tangible value to companies and governments in the region regarding digital trust?  
A: In an environment where AI is rapidly advancing in sophistication and adoption, distinguishing between human and bot interactions has become a critical challenge. According to the latest Bad Bot Report, automated internet traffic surpassed human activity in 2024. We stand apart in the market by providing a real-time solution for platforms to verify that an interaction originates from a human. This delivers tangible value in industries like gaming, where we are working with partners to ensure fair play, and in online dating, where our technology helps prevent catfishing and other forms of fraud by confirming users are legitimate.
 Q: How has the adoption of your solutions evolved, and what lessons has the company learned to improve the experience for users and organizations? 
A: Our initial phase in Mexico and Central America has been dedicated to building a foundational user network to overcome the "chicken-and-egg" challenge in corporate adoption. Through educational efforts, such as our campaign with the luchador Místico, we successfully verified over 700,000 individuals in Mexico. The primary lesson from this process has been the critical importance of directly addressing the region's deep-seated concerns about digital privacy and security, especially given that 89% of local users have been impacted by identity theft. Consequently, our core strategy is now to emphasize how our technology provides a direct solution by enabling users to prove their humanity online without surrendering sensitive personal data, which strongly resonates with both users and organizations in this market.
 Q: How do TFH's solutions contribute to building more reliable digital ecosystems in the region?  
A: We are building essential public infrastructure for the digital age. As online interactions become increasingly saturated with AI, the ability to distinguish between human and machine becomes fundamental for trust. Our contribution is to provide a foundational layer that allows all digital entities from e-commerce sites to social platforms to operate with the certainty that they are engaging with real human beings. 
 Q: What are the main differences between a traditional digital identity solution and your proposal based on humanity verification?  
A: For the end-user, the primary benefit is privacy preservation. Traditional digital identity systems require individuals to entrust a third party with a significant amount of sensitive personal information. In contrast, our proof of human model does not require users to share any personal data to verify their human uniqueness. This is particularly valuable in a market like Mexico where concerns about data security and identity fraud are widespread. 
For businesses, the advantages are simplicity and reduced friction. Integrating our solution is straightforward via a public software development kit (SDK), which can be implemented with minimal developer hours and at no cost. For companies whose primary need is to prevent bot-driven fraud or ensure authentic user engagement, our technology offers a robust and efficient solution, eliminating the friction point of asking users for extensive personal data. While we are not a substitute for regulated KYC processes, we are a powerful complementary tool that adds a critical layer of security by ensuring the interaction originates from a verified human source.
 Q: What features of your World App solution facilitate interoperability with other technological and financial applications or platforms in the region?  
A: We are enhancing interoperability through several key initiatives. First, we have a partnership with Visa to develop a card that links directly to a user's wallet balance, which will roll out progressively across markets. We are also collaborating with Circle to integrate more stablecoins into the platform simplifying asset exchange. Second, our open ecosystem of "mini-apps" within the World App allows any third-party developer with a web app to apply for distribution.
 Q: What role has AI played in the development and operation of the World App?  
A: AI is not a feature of our product but rather the foundational reason for its existence. The entire thesis behind Worldcoin was conceived by our founders, Sam Altman and Alex Blania, who foresaw a future where advanced AI would make it nearly impossible to differentiate between humans and bots online. Our purpose is to provide the solution to this problem. While machine learning was integral to training the Orb's verification processes to detect fraud and ensure accuracy, World App itself is not an AI tool. It is a necessary piece of infrastructure for a world where AI is ubiquitous.
 Q: What trends in humanity verification and digital authenticity are transforming the global and regional digital ecosystem?  
A: The most significant global trend being adopted by the region is the integration of Privacy-Enhancing Technologies (PETs). This involves using advanced cryptographic protocols to protect user data more effectively. A key example that we utilize is zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs), which allow for the verification of a piece of information without revealing the underlying data itself. We are seeing increased adoption of these methods alongside concepts like tokenization and the use of blockchain, not for currency but as a cryptographic tool to preserve information integrity.
 Q: How is the integration of AI and blockchain influencing how companies combat digital fraud and ensure the participation of human users?  
A: Companies are increasingly adopting advanced cryptographic techniques to counter the rising threat of AI-driven fraud. One key trend is the use of anonymized multi-party computation where data is segmented, encrypted, and stored across multiple separate servers. This architecture makes it exponentially more difficult for malicious actors to breach and reconstruct sensitive information as they would need to successfully attack numerous targets simultaneously. We employ this very method for storing iris codes, which ensures the uniqueness of each user on the network.
 Q: What are the main challenges in the continued deployment of humanity verification and digital identity tools within the Mexican market?  
A: The primary challenge is the sheer scale and sophistication of automated threats. The Bad Bot Report indicates that 37% of all internet activity consists of malicious bots, and Mexico is the second most targeted country for these attacks in Latin America after Brazil. This high-risk environment is compounded by gaps in digital literacy, which can make users more vulnerable. Our own research shows the tangible impact of these threats: 58% of our users reported having seen a deepfake they initially believed was real, illustrating the potential for widespread misinformation.
Furthermore, there is a significant trust deficit among users. In our survey, 73% of respondents expressed uncertainty about whether they were interacting with a human or a bot in various online scenarios, including customer service and dating apps. This doubt erodes confidence in the digital ecosystem. The challenge therefore is not just technological but also educational.
 Q: What are TFH's main expansion or development plans for Mexico for 2026?  
A: Our plans for the next 12 to 18 months are focused on two main fronts: expanding our network and introducing new technology. We will continue to grow our networkby increasing the availability of Orbs across more regions of the country, enabling us to reach interested users we have not yet been able to serve. We are also excited about the upcoming public launch of Deep Face, a tool that allows users to verify in real-time that the person on a video call is a live human, not a deepfake. We are also actively developing new local and global partnerships and integrating more services from prominent Mexican companies into our World App mini-app ecosystem to deliver greater value to our users.

 Miguel Rocha
