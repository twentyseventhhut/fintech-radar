AI Startup DualEntry Raises $90 Million To Shake Up ERP Market
DualEntry wants to modernize financial software for mid-sized companies, promising faster migrations and less reliance on traditional consultants.
2 mins
 What’s going on here? 
AI startup DualEntry just raised $90 million in Series A funding, bumping its valuation to $415 million as it takes aim at transforming the $500 billion ERP software space.
 What does this mean? 
DualEntry, founded in New York only a year ago, isn’t standing in line behind the legacy ERP crowd – it’s trying to rewrite the rules. The company’s AI-first platform automates key financial tasks for mid-sized businesses that have outgrown QuickBooks but don’t want the complexity or cost of heavyweight systems like Oracle NetSuite or Sage. Its headline act, 'NextDay Migration,' claims to transfer entire financial histories from old setups in just 24 hours – usually a..
drawn-out job for traditional providers. With early traction from both startups and a public company, and top investors like Lightspeed, Khosla Ventures, and GV on board, DualEntry is betting that automation can cut out costly consultants and fill skill gaps in accounting teams.
 Why should I care? 
 For markets: A new challenger on the horizon. 
The ERP software market hasn’t seen this kind of disruption in years. Most advancements have focused on moving tools to the cloud, but DualEntry’s push for AI-powered automation is turning heads among investors eager for faster, cheaper implementation. With $90 million to burn and a speedy rollout plan, the startup’s arrival could kick off a new battle that forces established players to innovate quickly or risk losing market share.
 The bigger picture: Rethinking how businesses run finance. 
Finance teams everywhere are contending with ongoing staffing shortages and stricter compliance needs, making automation more appealing than ever. If DualEntry’s AI lives up to the hype, mid-sized firms could finally access advanced financial tools without big consulting bills. More broadly, a shift toward self-driving back offices could let businesses focus resources where they matter most – powering growth rather than wrangling spreadsheets.
