# Connecting the Dots: 3S Money on why fiat slows stablecoin flows

Stablecoins are going mainstream. So why are we still using them to prop up legacy money?

The US Senate recently passed the GENIUS Act, creating a regulatory framework for payment stablecoins and clearing the way for startups, banks and retailers to issue their own. Mastercard has integrated Fiserv’s US Dollar Stablecoin, FIUSD, into a global payments infrastructure that already includes Paypal’s PYUSD, Paxo’s USDG, and Circle’s USDC. And according to Fortune, Apple, X, Airbnb, and Google are the latest giants actively exploring stablecoin integration.

These are serious milestones. Standard Chartered predicts that there could be $2 trillion of stablecoins in circulation by the end of 2028 – that’s up from $250 billion currently. These aren’t fringe numbers anymore. Yet despite this deluge, I think we’ve hit a conceptual sticking point.

There’s been a lot of industry noise that stablecoin is a new take on SWIFT: payment companies take fiat, convert it to stablecoin to move money across borders, and then exchange that payment back to fiat on the other side.

This ‘fiat-crypto-fiat’ flow will be cheaper and quicker than SWIFT, but still requires a banking network. The stablecoin ends up hidden under the hood, sandwiched between fiat.

In this model, stablecoins are deployed as hidden infrastructure to prop up legacy fiat systems. But why? We should be treating them as what they actually are: just another currency.

Here’s an analogy. On the Paris metro, customers can’t just tap their phone to enter the platform. They must find a machine, tap their payment there, receive a paper ticket, then take that paper ticket back to the gates. It’s a needlessly inefficient process that contorts new technology into an older legacy form. And that’s how we’re using stablecoins today. _Fiat is the paper ticket._

On top of that, key players in the industry are promoting the ‘crypto-as-infrastructure’ idea. The Circle Payments Network, for instance, looks a lot like Ripple’s XRP, a nearly decade old model that never took off.

We’re encouraging people and businesses to stick to traditional currencies when we’re ready to offer them stablecoins. Consider the Brazilian food exporter who prefers US dollars over an unstable Brazilian real, or the digital nomad who doesn’t want invoices in Indian rupees because they’re moving to Vietnam for a few months. They can decide what currency they need and when. The same logic applies here: wake up! USDT, USDC, Bitcoin – these are all just different currencies.

We need to modernise, and fast.

Writing in Fintech Brainfood, Simon Taylor sees the future of stablecoins as a platform, on course to “collapse the distinction between FX and payments.”

“Stablecoins are about to fundamentally rewire cross-border,” he explains. “Not because they’re cheaper than SWIFT – they’re not yet. But because they’re about to combine FX and payments into a single transaction. No pre-funding, less middlemen, less fees.” If Taylor is right, this is a model that is hurtling towards inevitable obsoletion.

Above all, people deserve currency choice. Retail giants like Amazon and Walmart are looking into using stablecoins; in two years, your crypto wallet could link to Apple Pay. You won’t need legacy money to pay at the grocery store.

So why are we still pretending that stablecoins aren’t viable currencies?
