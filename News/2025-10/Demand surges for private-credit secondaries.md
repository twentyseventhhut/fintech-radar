# Private credit secondaries volume nearly doubles to $20B in 2025 – Evercore

## GP-led deals overtook LP-led deals as the source of the majority of the market’s deal flow.

January 28, 2026

| BySami Vukelj

The private credit secondaries market experienced another year of record growth, with transaction volume nearly doubling to $20 billion, from $10.9 billion in 2024, according to Evercore’s 2025 Credit Secondary Market Survey.

The market’s rapid expansion mirrors the private credit market’s growth, but also reflects the elevated demand for exit opportunities from LPs in the normally illiquid asset class with long-term holds. Its growth has also been driven by the increased adoption of continuation vehicles by private credit GPs.

The investment bank’s private capital advisory said that GP-led deals overtook LP-led deals as the source of the majority of the market’s deal flow, as GP-led volume was $12 billion in 2025, rising 202% year-over-year.

GP-led deals allow GPs to return capital to LPs, which has been particularly useful in the muted M&A environment. In these continuation fund deals, LPs are usually given the option of rolling over into the new vehicle or exiting. LP-led deals allow LPs to exit their existing equity stake in a fund by selling it to secondaries buyers, typically at a discount.

Several of the continuation vehicle deals done last year were among the largest ever done for the asset class, including Benefit Street Partners’ $2.3 billion deal and TPG Twin Brook’s $3 billion deal. Crescent Capital recently set the new record, with its $3.2 billion continuation vehicle earlier this month.

LP-led deal volume was $8 billion in 2025, a 15% yearly increase.

Pricing has continued its upward trend as transactions that used to require wider discounts in prior periods are clearing in the mid-to-high 90s, with some transactions clearing at par or even better, the firm said.

GP-led credit secondaries transactions specifically priced at an average of 98% in 2025, based on the firm’s estimates of average gross purchase price prior to post-reference date adjustments.

LCD covered the dwindling discounts in the space last year, including some of the quirks involved in secondaries pricing.

Evercore said that the firm has advised and is advising $18 billion of credit secondaries deals including active deals and those done in the last 18 months, representing a significant share of the nascent market’s volume.

While GP-leds are most well-known as a liquidity tool, these deals are increasingly used to update fund economics and terms as well, the report says.

Significant dedicated pools of capital have already grown in the space, which is estimated to have $37 billion in dry powder, according to the firm. That is poised to grow as the majority of investors in the space (83%) are raising capital in the next 12 months, according to Evercore’s 2025 secondary market survey.

Several record-breaking fundraises have closed in the past year, including Ares’ $7.1 billion fundraise for its credit secondaries strategy earlier this month and Coller Capital’s $6.8 billion fundraise last year.

Looking at target returns, 55% of buyers have a minimum net IRR target of 13-15%, while 37% of respondents have a minimum net IRR target of 10-12%.

The report attributed the secondaries market’s growth to the maturation and growth of the primary credit markets, saying that “As portfolios mature and exposures concentrate, GPs are increasingly using secondary transactions to optimize risk, generate liquidity, and recycle capital rather than relying on natural run-off.”
