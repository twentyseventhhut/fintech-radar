Digital Transformation 
 
Explained: The Tech Behind India's Credit on UPI
 
 
 

 Credit Eligible 
 
 Get Permission 
 
Over the past decade, Unified Payments Interface, UPI, has already changed how India moves money. It is one of the world's most active payment systems, processing billions of transactions each month. But the network's next evolution raises a different set of questions as the country - traditionally a debit-first, cash-on-delivery market - navigates the rise of credit on UPI.
India has over 420 million unique UPI users, at least four times its nascent credit card base of approximately 110 million cardholders. Each payment tool has a purpose: UPI for instant payments; BNPL - buy now, pay later - for low-barrier, short-term installments; traditional credit cards for high-value and international transactions; and credit on UPI, which can serve as the crucial bridge to formal credit.
A key enabler here is RuPay - a domestic alternative to international payment networks such as Visa and Mastercard. RuPay's share of India's credit card spending surged to 16% in 2025, with nearly half of these transactions routed through UPI - a leap driven by growing smartphone adoption, rapid UPI penetration and the momentum of India's fintech boom, which is projected to hit $2.1 trillion by 2030. This industry transformation has spurred a wave of startups focused on UPI-linked credit products, including Zaggle's recent acquisition of Rio Money.
One of the early movers in this space is Kiwi, a fintech that partners with banks to issue RuPay credit cards linked directly to UPI. The company, which raised $24 million in its Series B funding round this year, aims to deploy the capital by elevating its rewards program, including add-ons for credit cards, EMI enablement on UPI and partnering with multiple banks.. Each new partner bank means new APIs, new integration quirks and new compliance rules. But it also means more credit capacity and broader reach.
To sustain this, the fintech startup is investing in what it calls "sustainable unit economics" - engineering solutions that reduce marginal costs per transaction while scaling compliance and risk monitoring.
The Tech Behind the Scenes
Behind the smooth consumer experience - a QR scan, a 4-digit PIN, a green tick - sits an intricate stack of APIs, software development kits, underwriting engines and compliance layers that allow a debit-first payment ecosystem to suddenly speak the language of credit.
At the transaction level, the process looks deceptively simple. A tap or scan on UPI doesn't pull money from a savings account; instead, it taps into a pre-approved credit line. The transaction itself rides on RuPay's rails, much like a regular card payment. What doesn't change is the authorization step - the familiar UPI PIN - a clever design choice that keeps the experience seamless for users.
The complexity sits behind the scenes. Take Kiwi, for instance. Consumers expect UPI payments to go through instantly, but adding a credit check could slow things down or fail altogether. To avoid that, Kiwi has built a kind of safety net: it pre-loads credit limits, duplicates partner APIs to prevent bottlenecks and uses asynchronous calls so that even if a bank's system lags, the customer still experiences a smooth, real-time payment.
But the real breakthrough could be embedding EMI logic directly into UPI flows - a feature also being introduced by companies such as Pine Labs. When a consumer makes a purchase, the payment engine evaluates whether the transaction qualifies for instalment conversion. If it does, the system can instantly restructure the payment: instead of a single debit, the settlement is broken into equal monthly installments, sometimes even at zero cost to the user if merchants or issuers absorb the interest.
Technically, this means layering another decisioning API into the transaction flow. The same QR code triggers not just a payment but also a set of rules around tenure, interest and repayment schedule.
Clear rules back this shift. NPCI's operating circulars and a 2023 RBI notification have allowed pre-sanctioned credit lines on UPI, signaling that the move is anything but experimental. It's a smart strategy: fintechs get the flexibility to build new, API-driven products, while banks can bring their traditional card offerings onto UPI without starting from scratch.
Catalysts and Headwinds
Starting January 2025, an RBI rule required lenders to update credit bureau records every 15 days. This means responsible use of even a small credit-on-UPI loan can quickly lift a user's credit score, helping them move faster from new to credit to prime borrower.
But the ecosystem faces significant headwinds. A possible friction point is the merchant discount rate on credit-based transactions, which fundamentally conflicts with UPI's zero-cost ethos for merchants. This fee, typically 1% to 2% for transactions over ₹2,000 or $23, can disincentivize small businesses with thin margins from accepting credit-on-UPI payments. Merchant-facing apps like Google Pay for Business have implemented features to allow merchants to disable these payments.
Other challenges include the risk of consumer over-leveraging, rising non-performing assets for lenders and the potential for debt traps. On the banking side, the technical challenge of integrating modern, API-first fintech platforms with banks' outdated legacy core banking systems, many of which are built on COBOL and mainframes, is a major operational hurdle.
UPI was built for instant debit transactions, not for underwriting and carrying credit risk. Reliability, compliance and risk controls have to be re-engineered for an ecosystem where every QR code can now serve as a potential loan origination point.
