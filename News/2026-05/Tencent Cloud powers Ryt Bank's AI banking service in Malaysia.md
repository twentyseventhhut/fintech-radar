## Tencent Cloud powers Ryt Bank’s AI banking service

Share

Add TIA as a preferred source

Tencent Cloud said on May 11 it partnered with Ryt Bank, a Malaysia-based digital bank, to support the bank’s AI-driven conversational banking service in Malaysia using Tencent Cloud Chat for real-time messaging and transaction data.

The companies said the system lets users give payment instructions in plain language instead of moving through app menus.

Tencent Cloud said its chat service handled a launch-day surge from 50,000 active users without downtime.

Ryt Bank said it has reached more than 1.2 million users in a little over seven months.

Ryt AI was developed with YTL AI Labs and built on Malaysia’s ILMU AI model, according to the release.

_🔗 Source: Tencent Cloud_

## 🧠 Food for thought

Implications, context, and why it matters.

### Ryt Bank has group backing, a bank license and deposit cover

*   Ryt Bank operates under YTL Digital Bank Berhad, which YTL Digital Capital Sdn Bhd and Sea Limited, a Singapore-based technology company, set up. YTL Group also describes the bank as led by YTL Group in partnership with Sea Limited 1.
*   Bank Negara Malaysia, Malaysia’s central bank, licenses the venture. It is also a member of Perbadanan Insurans Deposit Malaysia (PIDM), Malaysia’s deposit insurance agency, which protects deposits up to 250,000 ringgit (US$64,000) per depositor 2.
*   The bank calls itself the “World’s First AI-Powered Bank,” though its website adds an asterisk that reads “*For payments” 3.

### The tie-up adds chat-based banking and local AI

*   Ryt Bank lets people make payments with plain-language instructions, which fits a broader move away from app menus toward chat-style interfaces shaped by digital services like Amazon and Netflix 4.
*   That market is growing fast. Global voice-based payments are projected to reach US$24.97 billion by 2034 5.
*   Ryt AI was built on the ILMU AI model. ILMU AI is described as Malaysia’s first homegrown large language model. The available material here does not support wider claims about a data sovereignty strategy or a contrast with generic US-based models 2.
