# Poland’s BLIK joins pan-European cashless payment push

Poland’s instant mobile payment system, BLIK, has joined a European initiative aimed at creating a common payment model that would allow transactions between different payment networks across the continent.

The platform, which currently operates in Poland and Slovakia, has entered cooperation with European companies involved in the European Payments Alliance (EuroPA) and the European Payments Initiative (EPI).

The initiatives, launched by several European banks and finance platforms, are designed to develop interoperable cashless payment systems across multiple mobile payment providers in Europe.

“The goal is to create a pan-European, connected payment network that integrates local providers and enables users to make seamless transactions across borders and currencies,” BLIK said in a statement on Wednesday.

The push to develop a European cashless payment system is driven by concerns about overdependence on US providers. Photo: Leah Millis/San Francisco Chronicle via Getty Images 

## **Transfers across borders**

By joining the initiative, BLIK said its users will be able to send transfers to recipients using other platforms within the network later this year.

Initially, this will apply to peer-to-peer payments between individuals, with in-store payments expected to follow next year.

Among other payment solutions taking part in the initiative are European platforms like Bancomat, Bizum, MB Way, Wero and Vipps MobilePay.

BLIK added that European payment interoperability could eventually be available to more than a quarter of Europe’s population, linking “many national payment methods into a broader regional payment system.”

Since its launch in 2015, BLIK has steadily reshaped Poland’s digital payments landscape and is now the country’s most widely used mobile payment system.

## **Financial infrastructure**

The push to develop a European cashless payment system comes amid concerns over the continent’s reliance on US-based providers such as Visa, Mastercard and American Express, which account for more than 60% of all card payments within the eurozone.

EU officials have warned that this dependence on foreign infrastructure leaves the bloc potentially vulnerable to sudden disruptions and poses risks to its financial security.
