 Proparco invested $2 million in Ivorian fintech Cauridor to support its expansion across West and Central Africa. 


 The investment increased Cauridor’s total funding to $13 million since its launch in 2022. 


 The deal comes as African startup funding declines and investors adopt a more selective approach to transactions. 

Proparco, the French development finance institution focused on private-sector investment in emerging markets, has invested $2 million in Ivorian fintech Cauridor, a company specializing in cross-border payment infrastructure across Africa.
The companies announced the investment during the Africa Forward summit held on May 11 and May 12 in Nairobi, Kenya.
The latest transaction increased the total amount raised by Cauridor to $13 million since Guinean entrepreneurs Oumar Barry and Abdoulaye Bah founded the startup in 2022.
The fintech said it plans to complete its Series A funding round by the end of 2026 with participation from Flourish Ventures and LoftyInc Capital.
Cauridor said it will use the funds to strengthen its technical, operational and commercial teams while accelerating its expansion across West and Central Africa. The company aims to make cross-border payments faster, cheaper and more accessible.
 “By facilitating integration among different players in the payments ecosystem, Cauridor improves the efficiency of remittance flows into Africa, particularly in the ‘last mile’ segment, by connecting international money transfer operators such as Western Union, RIA, Taptap Send, Sendwave and MoneyGram to payment networks, including mobile money operators, banks and cash-out agent networks,” Proparco said.
The investment comes as African startup financing continues to slow. According to Africa: The Big Deal , startups across the continent raised $174 million in January 2026, compared with $276 million during the same period a year earlier. The total also remained below the monthly average recorded over the previous 12 months.
At the same time, the market has recorded fewer transactions as investors have become more selective and increasingly favored startups with stronger business models and clearer growth prospects.
Nevertheless, fintech companies have continued to attract a significant share of African startup funding. Investors have supported the sector on the back of rising digital payments adoption and expanding mobile financial services, particularly in West and Central Africa, where demand for payment infrastructure remains high.
